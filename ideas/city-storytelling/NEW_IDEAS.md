### Idea 21: Planning Commission Story Feed

**PS:** PS2
**JTBD:** Job 3 (City decision-maker hears only the loudest voices)
**Problem it solves:** Planning Commission meetings are dominated by repeat participants (neighborhood association leaders, developers, attorneys). Renters, non-English speakers, and displacement-vulnerable residents are structurally absent. Commissioners review staff reports and zoning maps but never see resident stories from the neighborhoods they're deciding about.
**How it works:** A tool that surfaces relevant resident stories directly in Planning Commission meeting preparation materials. When a rezoning case is docketed for a neighborhood, the system queries the story archive by neighborhood tag and topic, produces a one-page "Community Voices" brief with 3–5 story excerpts (contributor-consented for public use), and delivers it alongside the staff report. Commissioners read anonymized story excerpts from current and former residents before the hearing. After the decision, the "What We Heard → What We Changed" tracker (Idea 13) records whether and how stories influenced the outcome.
**Data sources:** Planning Commission public docket; contributed stories tagged by neighborhood (via GeoHub boundaries) and topic; consent-tier database to filter for publicly usable stories; staff report distribution workflow.
**Research grounding:** Pain Point 1 (City collects input it doesn't use), Cross-Cutting Theme (feedback loop gap), `A2_problem_landscape_civic_storytelling.md`. Research documents: staff spend 60% of time on application review, 5% on community communication; 59% renter population invisible to notification system (mailed to owners only); no demographic data on public commenters exists.

### Idea 24: Displacement Memory Archive

**PS:** PS2
**JTBD:** Job 1 (Elder who carries neighborhood memory)
**Problem it solves:** Three public housing communities (Gilpin Court, Creighton Court, Mosby Court) are undergoing active demolition and redevelopment. Creighton Court Phase 1 cleared 504 units in 2022. The physical places are being destroyed and the residents displaced — on a compressed timeline across multiple neighborhoods simultaneously.
**How it works:** A focused, time-bound (6-month) collection project targeting residents of Gilpin Court, Creighton Court, and Mosby Court. Partners with RRHA relocation staff to offer story recording at relocation touchpoints (pre-move counseling sessions, moving day, post-move check-ins). Uses the Pop-Up Kit (Idea 17) and Voice Hotline (Idea 14) as intake channels. Stories are immediately deposited to the Preservation Escrow (Idea 19). A dedicated "Displacement Stories" section on the civic portal makes these stories discoverable and linkable to the planning processes governing each community's redevelopment.
**Data sources:** RRHA relocation program touchpoints; Gilpin Court Section 18 application timeline; Creighton Court Phase 1 demolition records (May–July 2022, 504 units — `A5_problem_landscape_neighborhood_change.md`); Mosby Court Priority Neighborhood designation; contributed resident stories.
**Research grounding:** Pain Point 3 (neighborhoods demolished faster than stories captured), `A5_problem_landscape_neighborhood_change.md`. Research documents: Fulton Urban Renewal (1970) demonstrated total community erasure when demolition happens without documentation. The same pattern is repeating across multiple neighborhoods. This is the most time-sensitive idea in the set.

### Idea 17: Community Story Pop-Up Kit

**PS:** PS2
**JTBD:** Job 1 (Elder who carries neighborhood memory)
**Problem it solves:** The residents who carry the most irreplaceable memories have the least digital access. No existing tool reaches them where they are — at churches, community centers, and neighborhood markets.
**How it works:** A portable, offline-capable recording station: a ruggedized tablet with pre-loaded recording app, a USB microphone, and a portable photo printer. Deployed at trusted community anchors (The Market @ 25th, partner churches, Peter Paul Development Center, senior centers). Residents sit with a trained facilitator who guides the conversation using photo-elicitation prompts. The tablet records audio, captures a portrait photo, and prints an instant keepsake card with the contributor's photo and a quote from their story. Data syncs to the central archive when the kit reconnects to WiFi. Consent collected on-device with the rolling consent module (Idea 16).
**Data sources:** Offline-first tablet application; community anchor site list from `B3_users_longtime_resident_storyteller.md`; photo-elicitation methodology from `rs_q1_data.md` (65+ studies show deeper accounts with photo prompts than verbal-only).
**Research grounding:** Pain Point 2 (most affected are least reachable), Pain Point 5 (trust is the hardest resource), `B3_users_longtime_resident_storyteller.md`. Research specifies: recognizable local institutions, plain-language handout, tangible return artifacts (printed summaries, CDs, USB drives) are required trust signals.
