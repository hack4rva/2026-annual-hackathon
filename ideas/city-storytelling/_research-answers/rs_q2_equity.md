# Executive Summary

Civic storytelling initiatives, particularly in communities with histories of displacement like Richmond, Virginia, hinge on establishing community trust and ensuring ethical, equitable practices. Research indicates that institutional story-collection efforts in Richmond's Fulton neighborhood were largely perceived as welcome preservation and an opportunity for narrative correction, allowing residents to tell the "true story" of their community and combat negative historical accounts. This positive reception underscores the importance of framing projects as collaborative and empowering rather than extractive. To maintain this trust, especially when dealing with traumatic topics like urban renewal, rigorous consent protocols are essential. Best practices from the Oral History Association and Voice of Witness mandate trauma-informed approaches, including rolling consent, clear communication of risks, the right to withdraw, and providing support for narrators. While collecting stories is one part of the process, connecting them to tangible outcomes is a significant challenge. The city of Santa Monica's Wellbeing Project serves as a key example of successfully creating a policy feedback loop, where qualitative resident data directly informed departmental workplans and budget allocations. Logistical and legal hurdles are also prominent. The Historic Fulton Oral History Project's online collection containing 17 interviews, despite reports of 32 being recorded, highlights potential challenges in funding, capacity, or project scope that can limit public access. Furthermore, the choice of collection modality, such as StoryCorps' high-touch facilitated model versus its self-serve digital tools, carries different implications for story quality and community trust. Projects must also cater to specific demographics, recognizing that older adults (65+) show strong engagement with photo-elicitation methods, and must adhere to strict verifiable parental consent requirements under COPPA when collecting and publicly posting any content from minors under 13.

# Trauma Informed Consent Protocols

## Protocol Name

Rolling Consent

## Description

Rolling consent is a dynamic and ongoing process of seeking and confirming a narrator's willingness to participate throughout the entire storytelling project, from the initial interview to final publication. Unlike traditional one-time informed consent, it acknowledges that a narrator's feelings and boundaries about sharing their story can change, especially when recounting traumatic events. Its importance lies in empowering the narrator, giving them continuous control over their story, and building a foundation of trust and safety. This approach helps prevent re-traumatization by ensuring the narrator is a willing and active partner at every stage.

## Source Organization

Oral History Association (OHA)

## Implementation Guideline

To implement rolling consent, interviewers should: 1. Initially explain that consent is an ongoing conversation and that the narrator has the right to pause, stop the interview, or withdraw from the project entirely at any point before public release. 2. Record the narrator's initial informed consent verbally before the interview begins. 3. During the interview, be attentive to verbal and non-verbal cues of distress and proactively offer breaks or the option to stop. 4. After the interview and during the editing process, re-engage with the narrator to review the material and confirm their consent for its specific use and public dissemination. 5. Clearly document these consent checkpoints throughout the project's lifecycle.

# Preferred Story Formats For Seniors

Residents aged 65 and over demonstrate a surprising degree of technological adoption and have shown preferences for specific, engaging formats for sharing memories. General technology trends for the 50+ demographic from 2026 indicate high comfort with digital communication; texting is their primary communication method, nine in ten use social media, and eight in ten stream video weekly. This suggests a foundational capability for engaging with audio, video, and text-based story formats. However, for the specific act of sharing memories, research points to a strong preference for methods involving photographs. Participatory photo-elicitation studies conducted with adults aged 65-94 found that using photos as prompts for storytelling yielded 'deeper, more elaborate accounts' compared to verbal-only interviews. This method was well-received, encouraged 'spontaneous, open and frank conversations,' and increased the participants' sense of agency. Therefore, a 'photo with caption' format is likely to be highly effective and preferred. Combining these insights, the most effective approach would be to offer multiple, low-barrier options, with a particular emphasis on photo-based methods. This could include simple text prompts, phone calls to record audio, facilitated in-person audio or video sessions, and a simple interface for uploading a photograph accompanied by a written or dictated caption.

# Coppa Compliance For Minors Stories

To comply with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) when handling stories and personal information from minors under 13 for public posting, you must adhere to strict guidelines. The FTC considers photos, videos, and audio recordings containing a child’s image or voice to be personal information. Therefore, before collecting or posting this information online, you must obtain verifiable parental consent. This involves providing direct notice to parents about the information being collected and how it will be used. Parents must be given the right to review the personal information collected from their child, request its deletion, and refuse to permit further collection or use. For a mixed-audience platform, you must implement an age-screen and either block users under 13 from providing personal information or obtain verifiable parental consent. Approved methods for obtaining this consent include having the parent use a credit card for a transaction, verification by trained staff via a phone or video call, answering knowledge-based challenge questions, or providing a government-issued ID. An 'email plus' method (email from the parent coupled with a confirmation step) is only permissible if the child's information will be used for internal purposes and not disclosed publicly. Relying on exceptions, such as blurring a child's face in a photo, is only compliant if all other personal and online identifiers are also removed and any persistent identifiers are used solely for internal site operations.

# Ethical Guidelines For Oral History

## Principle

Informed Consent

## Description

According to the Oral History Association, informed consent is a foundational ethical principle that establishes a transparent and trusting relationship between the interviewer and the narrator. It is not merely a legal release form but an ongoing dialogue to ensure the narrator fully understands the project's nature, its potential uses and audiences, any potential risks or benefits to them, and their rights within the process. This includes their right to maintain confidentiality, use a pseudonym, and withdraw from the project up until the point of public dissemination. The goal is to empower the narrator to make a truly informed decision about sharing their story.

## Best Practice

A concrete best practice for upholding this principle is to document consent in multiple ways. In addition to a written agreement, the Oral History Association recommends recording the narrator's verbal informed consent at the beginning of the audio or video interview. During this recording, the interviewer should restate the project's purpose and the narrator's rights, and the narrator should verbally affirm their understanding and willingness to proceed. This creates an unambiguous record of consent directly within the primary source material.

# Challenges In Civic Storytelling

## Challenge Category

Funding and Capacity

## Description

Civic storytelling projects often struggle to secure sustainable funding and maintain the necessary capacity to collect, process, archive, and disseminate stories over the long term. Initial grants or partnerships may cover collection, but not ongoing digital preservation, platform maintenance, or community outreach.

## Example

The Historic Fulton Oral History Project reportedly recorded interviews with 32 residents, but its publicly accessible online collection at VCU contains only 17 interviews. While the exact reason for this discrepancy and the project's cessation is not documented, it points to potential limitations in funding or institutional capacity to digitize and publish the full set of recordings.

## Challenge Category

Community Trust and Engagement

## Description

Building and maintaining trust with the community is paramount but challenging, especially in areas with a history of extractive research or broken promises. Projects must continually prove their value and commitment to the community's goals, ensuring residents perceive the effort as empowering preservation rather than extractive documentation.

## Example

A university launches a project to collect stories in a historically marginalized neighborhood. If the project concludes without providing residents access to the archive, or if the stories are used in academic papers without community input or benefit, future storytelling efforts by any institution will be met with suspicion and fatigue, hindering participation.

## Challenge Category

Ethical and Legal Complexity

## Description

Navigating the ethical and legal requirements for story collection is a significant challenge. This includes implementing robust, trauma-informed consent processes for sensitive narratives and adhering to strict legal frameworks like the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) for minors.

## Example

A project aiming to collect stories about housing instability must not only get initial consent but also practice 'rolling consent,' allowing narrators to withdraw at any point. If a story from a 12-year-old is submitted via a web form, the project must have a system for obtaining verifiable parental consent (e.g., via a video call with trained staff) before publicly posting the child's photo or audio, as a simple checkbox is not legally sufficient under COPPA.

## Challenge Category

Data Utilization and Impact

## Description

One of the greatest challenges is ensuring that the collected stories do not just sit in an archive but are actively used to create tangible benefits, such as influencing public policy, informing city planning, or guiding budget decisions. Creating a functional policy feedback loop is rare.

## Example

A city collects hundreds of powerful resident stories about inadequate public transit. However, without a formal process to integrate these qualitative insights into the Department of Transportation's planning and budget cycle, the stories remain anecdotal. In contrast, the city of Santa Monica created a formal framework requiring departments to use wellbeing data (including resident stories) in their annual workplans, demonstrating a successful but difficult-to-replicate model.


# Building Community Trust And Equity

Building community trust and ensuring equity are the most critical components of any successful civic storytelling project, particularly in communities like Richmond's Church Hill and Fulton, which have experienced traumatic displacement due to urban renewal. Trust is not assumed; it must be earned through transparent processes, ethical practices, and a clear commitment to empowering the community rather than extracting from it. The positive reception of the Historic Fulton Oral History Project demonstrates this principle in action. By framing the project as a partnership to 'tell the true story' and make the 'real story' accessible, the organizers aligned the project's goals with the community's desire for narrative correction and historical preservation. This collaborative framing helped overcome potential suspicion of institutional motives. 

Ensuring equity requires moving beyond simple consent forms to adopt rigorous, trauma-informed protocols. For sensitive topics like eviction and displacement, this means adhering to guidelines from the Oral History Association (OHA) and Voice of Witness (VOW). Key practices include 'rolling consent,' which gives narrators the right to pause, stop, or withdraw at any stage; providing questions in advance; being alert to triggers; and offering options for anonymity. These methods shift power to the narrator, respecting their agency and well-being and transforming the interview from a potentially extractive act into a supportive one. 

The process of collection itself influences trust. A facilitated model, like StoryCorps' Signature interviews, where a trained professional guides the conversation in a controlled environment, can build a higher degree of trust and ensure ethical standards are met consistently. This contrasts with self-serve digital tools, which, while democratizing, place the onus of quality and consent on the user and may feel more impersonal. 

Ultimately, trust is solidified when a project demonstrates its value back to the community. The most powerful way to do this is by creating transparent feedback loops where stories lead to tangible action. The city of Santa Monica’s Wellbeing Project is a prime example, where resident narratives and qualitative data were formally integrated into the city's governance, compelling departments to use the findings to shape their annual budgets and workplans. This demonstrates that the stories are not just being archived but are being heard and acted upon by those in power, completing the cycle of trust and creating a truly equitable exchange.
