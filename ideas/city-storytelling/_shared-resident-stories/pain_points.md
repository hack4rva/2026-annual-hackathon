# Pain Points — Resident Stories as Civic Insight

**Pillar:** A City That Tells Its Stories
**Problem Statement (verbatim):** Resident Stories as Civic Insight — Give residents a trusted, accessible way to share their lived experiences so community narratives can inform City decisions.
**Applies to:** Fulton Oral History Map, Richmond Stories Online, ExploreRVA (partial)

---

## Summary

Seven interconnected pain points prevent Richmond residents from sharing their stories and prevent City decision-makers from hearing them. The barriers are not primarily technological — they are structural, historical, and trust-related. The deepest pain is that the residents whose stories matter most to equitable policymaking are the ones most systematically excluded by every existing engagement channel.

---

## Pain Point 1 — The City Collects Input It Doesn't Use

Richmond's engagement tools are designed for transactional efficiency, not narrative depth. RVA311 confines input to predefined categories. Public hearings impose time limits. Surveys aggregate responses into charts and percentages. For the Diamond District, the City collected 1,300 survey responses, held four community meetings, and received 150 comments on the draft plan — but no published evidence exists of how the qualitative comments influenced the final scope or the Request for Offers.

Without a visible mechanism connecting resident input to specific policy decisions, engagement becomes performative. "Collection without use is archiving." Residents who invest emotional labor in sharing displacement experiences, neighborhood memories, or hardship stories and then see no evidence of impact will not participate again. The absence of a "What we heard → What we changed" tracker means every new engagement effort starts from a trust deficit created by the last one.

*Sources: `A2_problem_landscape_civic_storytelling.md`*

## Pain Point 2 — The People Most Affected Are the Least Reachable

The residents whose stories matter most to equitable decision-making face the highest barriers to participation. In key East End census tracts (covering Mosby, Brauers, Fairmount, Whitcomb, Upper Shockoe Valley), 66.2% of households lack broadband internet and 47.7% lack smartphones. Black Richmond residents are 115% more likely to lack internet access, 55% more likely to lack cell data, and 138% more likely to lack a bank account compared to the citywide average. Residents aged 55 and older are 73% more likely not to have internet access than younger cohorts.

This means any web-first, app-dependent, or login-required story collection tool will systematically exclude the exact population it is supposed to serve: aging Black residents from historically displaced neighborhoods who carry irreplaceable neighborhood memory. The digital divide is not a secondary concern to design around — it is the primary design constraint.

*Sources: `B3_users_longtime_resident_storyteller.md`, `B5_users_digital_equity.md`, `G1_risks_representational_bias.md`*

## Pain Point 3 — Neighborhoods Are Being Demolished Faster Than Stories Are Being Captured

Richmond is simultaneously experiencing rapid gentrification, high eviction rates, and active public housing demolition:

- **Demographic shift:** Black population decreased 7% from 2000–2016; White population increased 35%. Gentrification is concentrated in Jackson Ward, Church Hill, and the East End.
- **Eviction crisis:** Richmond has the second-highest eviction rate in the country at 11.44%. Fifteen companies drive the bulk of filings.
- **Public housing redevelopment:** Gilpin Court — RRHA submitting Section 18 application to HUD. Creighton Court — Phase 1 demolition completed May–July 2022; 504 units cleared; mixed-income build-out projected through 2027. Mosby Court — identified as Priority Neighborhood for mixed-income redevelopment.

The physical places these stories are anchored to are being demolished. The longtime residents who hold the memories are being displaced. The Fulton Urban Renewal Plan (1970) already demonstrated what happens when a neighborhood is destroyed without adequate documentation — total community erasure. The same pattern is repeating on a compressed timeline across multiple neighborhoods simultaneously.

*Sources: `A5_problem_landscape_neighborhood_change.md`, `A2_problem_landscape_civic_storytelling.md`*

## Pain Point 4 — Richmond's Oral History Assets Are Siloed and Incomplete

The city's existing oral history content is concentrated in a single neighborhood (Fulton) and scattered across institutions with incompatible access models:

- **VCU Libraries** hosts 16 publicly accessible Fulton interviews with streaming audio and searchable transcripts, but the collection lives on an academic repository (Scholars Compass) that most residents don't know exists.
- **The Valentine** holds copyright over the Fulton recordings and manages exhibit-based oral histories (Voices from Richmond's Hidden Epidemic), but long-term digital availability outside exhibits is unclear.
- **BHMVA** has physical Fulton copies but no digital portal.
- **StoryCorps** likely has Richmond content but "Richmond" searches pull California results, requiring manual QA to filter.
- **Library of Virginia** holds statewide contextual materials but no Richmond-specific neighborhood oral history sets.

Critically, Jackson Ward, Church Hill, Gilpin Court, Carver, Shockoe Bottom, and Oregon Hill have **near-zero publicly accessible digital oral histories**. The richest history is the most undocumented. Black community narratives are strong in selected thematic projects (Fulton, Rosenwald Schools) but everyday neighborhood life, immigrant and refugee stories, LGBTQ+ experiences beyond HIV/AIDS, labor stories, and contemporary faith community narratives are major collection gaps.

*Sources: `C3_services_oral_history_institutions.md`, `D4_data_oral_history_archives.md`*

## Pain Point 5 — Trust Is the Hardest Resource to Earn and the Easiest to Destroy

Civic story collection operates in a trust environment shaped by decades of institutional harm:

- **Historical displacement:** I-95 construction through Jackson Ward (1955) displaced 1,000+ residents. Fulton Urban Renewal (1970) demolished the entire historic community. These are not abstract historical facts to the residents being asked to share stories — they are lived experiences of government destroying their neighborhoods.
- **Scam exposure:** One in five aging individuals is affected by cybercriminals, representing $2.56 billion in annual losses. Older adults are cautious about unknown digital channels, especially ones that ask for personal stories.
- **Extractive research patterns:** Institutional story-collection efforts (university oral history projects, museum exhibits) can feel extractive when researchers arrive, record, and leave without returning anything tangible to the community.

Trust-building requires: recognizable local caller IDs (not unknown numbers), anchoring every touchpoint in familiar institutions (The Market @ 25th, partner churches, community centers), leading with a plain-language "what will happen with my story" handout, providing a community oversight panel with veto power on story use, and delivering tangible return artifacts (printed summaries, CDs, USB drives) so contributors have physical proof their story was valued.

*Sources: `B3_users_longtime_resident_storyteller.md`, `A5_problem_landscape_neighborhood_change.md`, `G2_risks_consent_privacy.md`*

## Pain Point 6 — Story Platforms Die, Taking Community Content With Them

The prior art is littered with platforms that launched with enthusiasm and then stranded their users:

- **Detour** (audio tour app) shut down entirely, leaving users unable to access purchased tours.
- **Neighborland** (civic engagement platform) was acquired by Nextdoor in 2020, shifting its terms of service and purpose.
- **Yahoo Upcoming** shut down in 2013 as part of corporate consolidation, destroying a social event calendar and its API.

For civic storytelling, platform death is not just an inconvenience — it is a betrayal of trust. A resident who shares a deeply personal neighborhood memory on a platform that then disappears has lost their story and their trust in digital civic tools simultaneously.

The mitigation is clear: canonical content must live on an owned domain (not a third-party SaaS), all audio/text/map data must be exportable, and institutional archival partners (VCU Libraries, The Valentine, Library of Virginia) should serve as preservation backstops. Third-party platforms (VoiceMap, ArcGIS StoryMaps, social media) should be treated as distribution channels, never as the sole custodian of community history.

*Sources: `E3_prior_art_place_based_stories.md`, `E4_prior_art_failures.md`, `G4_risks_copyright_attribution.md`*

## Pain Point 7 — Consent and Rights Are Complex Enough to Paralyze a Weekend Build

Collecting personal narratives involves a thicket of legal and ethical requirements that most hackathon teams are unprepared for:

- **Copyright:** Resident stories are the intellectual property of the contributor. The Valentine's Deed of Gift model (formal signature for rights transfer) is the gold standard but too heavyweight for a prototype submission flow.
- **COPPA:** Allowing minors under 13 to post stories publicly constitutes "disclosure" requiring verifiable parental consent. Voice recordings, photos, and videos are always PII under federal guidelines.
- **Sensitive content:** Stories about displacement, eviction, discrimination, and criminal justice can expose identifiable individuals to harm. Addresses, landlord names, employer names, and family details must be redacted or generalized before publication.
- **Trauma-informed practice:** Reflecting on past harms can trigger distress or re-traumatization. Oral history projects must build safety and respect into every step — not just collect efficiently.
- **Third-party PII:** A story about "what happened to my neighborhood" will inevitably reference other people by name. The tool must warn contributors not to include others' private information and implement editorial review before publishing.

The practical minimum for a weekend prototype: tiered consent (public named, public anonymized, staff-only), plain-language purpose/storage/rights statement, a visible withdrawal option, a mandatory "Are you under 13?" screen, and a content-warning flag for sensitive themes with human review before publication. Even this minimal set requires deliberate UX investment that competes with feature development time.

*Sources: `G2_risks_consent_privacy.md`, `G4_risks_copyright_attribution.md`, `G1_risks_representational_bias.md`*

## Cross-Cutting Theme — The Feedback Loop Gap

Every pain point traces back to a single structural failure: **no mechanism connects resident stories to City decisions.** Stories are collected in archives that planners don't consult. Public comments are gathered through surveys that produce statistics without attribution. Oral histories are preserved in institutional repositories that decision-makers never open.

The teams addressing this problem statement will be judged on whether they can credibly demonstrate — even in prototype form — that a resident's story enters the system and produces a visible signal to someone making a decision. The technology for collection, mapping, and archiving is mature. The technology for closing the story-to-decision feedback loop does not exist in any U.S. city at meaningful scale. That gap is the real problem to solve.
