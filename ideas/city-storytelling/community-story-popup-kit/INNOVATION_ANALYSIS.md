# Innovation Analysis: Community Story Popup Kit

## 1. Blue Ocean Reframing (ERRC Grid)

**Core assumption challenged:** Story collection requires either expensive professional equipment or a permanent installation.

| Action | Element | Rationale |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Eliminate** | Expensive custom kiosk rental as the default | iProbe StoryBooth kiosks are custom-quoted and require 2-4 week lead time; a $320 library-style kit (Zoom H5 + accessories) achieves archival quality at 1/10th the cost |
| **Eliminate** | Dependency on professional interviewers for every session | Pratt-Weeksville's cell-phone framework proved residents can interview one another; Smithsonian MoMS provides free facilitator guides for community-led capture |
| **Reduce** | Session length when volume matters | StoryCorps interviews run 40 minutes (1/hour/station). iProbe kiosks cap at 5 minutes (6-10/hour/station). Match session length to event context: festivals need short-form, quiet rooms need long-form |
| **Reduce** | Post-event processing bottleneck | Otter.ai Business ($19.99/user/month) provides automated transcription with speaker identification; reduces hours of manual transcription to minutes of human review |
| **Raise** | Consent sophistication at the booth | StoryCorps General Release transfers all rights permanently and irrevocably. VHP/MoMS templates use non-exclusive licenses where residents retain copyright. Default to non-exclusive for city programs |
| **Raise** | Multilingual accessibility | Boston Public Library's Oral History Backpacks include resource binders in 10 languages; Richmond's kit should include Spanish, Vietnamese, and Arabic prompts at minimum |
| **Create** | A standardized, checkable "Richmond Story Kit" available through the library system | St. Louis County Library and Princeton Public Library already lend oral history kits; Richmond Public Library should offer a purpose-built kit for city events, festivals, and community meetings |
| **Create** | A "popup-to-archive" pipeline with defined handoff points | Kit is used at event → audio backed up nightly to secure storage → automated transcription → archivist metadata review → deposit to Richmond Stories Online or VCU Libraries |

**Key reframe:** The kit is not the innovation — the **pipeline** is. Anyone can buy a Zoom H5. The value is a standardized, repeatable workflow: capture → consent → backup → transcribe → moderate → archive → reuse. Make the pipeline so simple that a library volunteer can run it.

## 3. Rapid Design Sprint

**Problem sentence:** Richmond lacks a standardized, low-cost, repeatable toolkit for capturing resident stories at public events — so story collection is ad hoc, inconsistent, and disconnected from any preservation pipeline.

**User journey (5 steps):**
1. Community organizer checks out a "Richmond Story Kit" from their local RPL branch (free, 7-day loan)
2. At their event: sets up the recorder, posts the bilingual prompts, positions the greeter to handle consent forms
3. Residents walk up, sign consent, sit down, and record a 3-5 minute story responding to a posted prompt ("What's one thing you want Richmond to remember about this neighborhood?")
4. At end of event: organizer uploads all WAV files to a shared secure folder (Google Drive or OneDrive link in the kit instructions)
5. Within 7 days: automated transcription runs; a volunteer moderator reviews; approved stories publish to Richmond Stories Online feed

**Prototype shape:** The "kit" itself: a pelican case containing a Zoom H5 recorder, 2 lavalier mics, a tabletop tripod, headphones, 2 SD cards, a printed quick-start guide (laminated), 20 consent forms (English/Spanish), 5 prompt cards, and a "backup your files" instruction sheet with upload link. Cost: ~$350-400 per kit. The "pipeline": a Google Drive folder structure (per-event subfolder) → Otter.ai automated transcription → moderation spreadsheet → approved stories pushed to Omeka S or public feed.

**First test:** Build 2 prototype kits. Lend one to a library branch for a Saturday event. Lend one to a civic association for a neighborhood meeting. Measure: stories captured per event, audio quality (usable vs. unusable), consent form completion rate, time from capture to published story.

## 4. Opportunity Mapper

### Opportunity 1: Standardized Lending Kit Through Library System

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **User** | Community organizers, civic associations, school groups, church leaders |
| **Outcome** | Check out a ready-to-use story collection kit from any RPL branch; return with captured stories that feed the city archive |
| **Workflow** | Reserve kit → check out → use at event → upload files → return kit → stories enter moderation pipeline |
| **Dependencies** | Richmond Public Library partnership; 5-10 kits ($350-400 each = $1,750-4,000 total); lending policy |
| **Richmond relevance** | St. Louis County Library and Princeton Public Library already lend oral history kits; Boston Public Library's backpacks include binders in 10 languages |

### Opportunity 2: Festival Quick-Capture as City Service

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **User** | Attendees at Richmond city festivals, cultural events, farmers markets |
| **Outcome** | 30-60 resident stories captured per festival day, feeding the public archive with diverse neighborhood voices |
| **Workflow** | City deploys 1-2 staffed quick-capture stations at major events → 5-minute prompts → greeter manages queue and consent → facilitator records |
| **Dependencies** | 2 staff per station per event; pipe-and-drape or canopy tent; event coordination with Parks & Rec |
| **Richmond relevance** | Woodstock Oral History Initiative captured 50+ interviews over 6 pop-up days; Lebanon, Indiana's repurposed phone booth captured 200+ recordings |

### Opportunity 3: Popup-to-Archive Pipeline

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **User** | Archivists, moderators, the public (as consumers of published stories) |
| **Outcome** | A defined handoff from field capture to permanent archive, preventing stories from being trapped on SD cards |
| **Workflow** | Field capture → nightly backup → automated transcription (Otter.ai) → moderator review → metadata enrichment → deposit to Omeka S / VCU Libraries |
| **Dependencies** | Otter.ai subscription ($19.99/month); volunteer moderators (2-3 people); Omeka S instance or Richmond Stories Online platform |
| **Richmond relevance** | Anti-Eviction Mapping Project warns against ad-hoc storage (Google Drive) for preservation; NYPL retired 1,000+ stories because the preservation pipeline wasn't sustained |

## 5. Problem Scoping

| Dimension | Score (1-5) | Rationale |
|-----------|:-----------:|-----------|
| **Impact** | 4 | Captures voices of residents who won't submit stories online; meets people where they gather |
| **Feasibility** | 5 | Equipment costs $350 per kit; Smithsonian MoMS guides are free; library lending model is proven |
| **Data readiness** | 3 | No existing dataset; this is a collection tool. Value depends on how many events use the kits |
| **Problem clarity** | 4 | Clear need: community events happen without story capture; ad hoc efforts lack consistency and preservation |
| **Demo-ability** | 5 | Bring the physical kit to the demo. Open the pelican case. Record a judge's story live. Play it back. Irresistible. |

**Weekend MVP shapes:**
- **Shape A (Single Kit Build):** Assemble one prototype kit. Write the quick-start guide and consent forms. Test at one event. ~4 hours build + one event.
- **Shape B (Kit + Pipeline):** Shape A + Google Drive folder structure + Otter.ai transcription setup + moderation spreadsheet + published story on a simple web page. ~8 hours.
- **Shape C (Lending Program Design):** Shape B + 3 kits built + RPL lending agreement drafted + training materials for library staff + kit return checklist. ~14 hours.

## 6. Assumption Mapping

| # | Assumption | Risk (1-5) | Uncertainty (1-5) | R × U | Validation method |
|---|-----------|:----------:|:-----------------:|:-----:|-------------------|
| 1 | Community organizers will check out and use the kits without extensive training | 4 | 3 | 12 | Test with 3 organizers of varying tech comfort; observe kit usage without intervention |
| 2 | Audio quality will be archival-grade in festival environments | 4 | 4 | 16 | Test Zoom H5 with lavalier mics at an outdoor event; grade audio quality (usable / marginal / unusable) |
| 3 | Residents will participate in 3-5 minute prompted recordings at events | 3 | 2 | 6 | Woodstock captured 50+ over 6 days; Lebanon captured 200+. High confidence in participation |
| 4 | Consent forms will be completed correctly by untrained facilitators | 4 | 3 | 12 | Simplify form to one page; test with 10 participants; measure completion accuracy |
| 5 | SD card backup workflow will be followed consistently | 3 | 3 | 9 | Include "STOP: Backup your files before returning" card in kit lid; track compliance |
| 6 | Richmond Public Library will agree to lend kits | 3 | 3 | 9 | RPL already lends technology (laptops, hotspots); story kits are a natural extension. Discuss with RPL leadership |
| 7 | The capture-to-archive pipeline can operate with volunteer labor | 3 | 4 | 12 | Track moderator throughput: how many stories/week can 2-3 volunteers process? |

**Top 3 to validate first:**
1. **Audio quality in festival environments (R×U=16):** EMU's mobile booth pilot captured only 5 stories in one day partly due to a marching band. Test recording quality at a real Richmond outdoor event. If quality is poor, add directional mics or prioritize indoor venues.
2. **Organizer kit usability without training (R×U=12):** If the kit sits in a case because the organizer doesn't know how to use it, nothing gets captured. The quick-start guide must be truly quick. Test with non-technical users.
3. **Consent form accuracy (R×U=12):** Invalid consent makes stories legally unusable (see DC Public Library's Southwest Views collection — restricted to research-only access because consent forms were missing). Simplify and test.

---

## Key Takeaway

The Community Story Popup Kit's value isn't the hardware — it's the repeatable pipeline from field capture to permanent archive. The research shows that throughput varies wildly based on session design (5-minute kiosk: 48-80 stories/day vs. 40-minute interview: 6-8/day) and that audio quality is the biggest risk in outdoor settings (EMU's marching band problem). The critical moves: (1) build a simple, loanable kit for $350, (2) partner with RPL for lending, (3) define the backup-transcribe-moderate-archive pipeline before the first event, and (4) test audio quality in a real Richmond festival environment. Consent forms must default to non-exclusive licenses (VHP model) so residents retain copyright.
