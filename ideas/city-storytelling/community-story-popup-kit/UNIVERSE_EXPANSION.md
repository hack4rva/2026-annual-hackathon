# Scaling Richmond's Community-Story Popup Kit: Funding, Partnerships, and Governance for a City-Wide Narrative Infrastructure

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Community-Story Popup Kit represents a critical opportunity to operationalize Mayor Danny Avula's "A City That Tells Its Stories" pillar, but framing it solely as a municipal project limits its potential. By expanding the universe of this idea, the kit transforms from a localized city initiative into a regional narrative infrastructure. The most viable path forward relies on a hybrid governance model where the City of Richmond and Richmond Public Library (RPL) manage the archival backend and hardware lending, while a nonprofit coalition drives community engagement, trust-building, and front-end facilitation. 

This expanded framing unlocks significant capital at the state and federal levels, moving beyond constrained municipal budgets. Opportunities like the National Historical Publications and Records Commission (NHPRC) Public Engagement grants [1] [2] and Virginia Humanities funding [3] [4] can finance the pipeline's creation. Furthermore, integrating with regional bodies like PlanRVA, which already utilizes storytelling for public engagement [5] [6], ensures the collected narratives directly inform broader Central Virginia policy decisions.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

To maximize trust, funding eligibility, and operational efficiency, the Community-Story Popup Kit should operate under a hybrid ownership model. 

* **What the City/Public Institutions Should Own:** The City of Richmond, via the Richmond Public Library and academic partners like VCU Libraries, should own the physical hardware inventory, the digital repository (e.g., Omeka S hosting), and the long-term data preservation pipeline. VCU Libraries already maintains robust digital collections and oral history archives [7] [8], making them an ideal backend partner.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A designated 501(c)(3) nonprofit or a coalition of community organizations should own the resident-facing layer. This includes managing the consent framework (utilizing non-exclusive licenses so residents retain copyright), conducting community outreach, and staffing the quick-capture stations at festivals. Nonprofits are better positioned to build trust in historically marginalized neighborhoods than government entities.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The City should not own the exclusive copyright to the stories, nor should it be the sole face of the interviewing process. A "city-owns-data" approach often suppresses participation due to privacy and surveillance concerns.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **VPM & StoryCorps "One Small Step" Richmond:** VPM and StoryCorps facilitated conversations to reconnect a divided America, selecting Richmond for a national initiative [21] [22]. *Lesson:* High-production, facilitated models work for deep engagement but are difficult to scale without significant funding. The Popup Kit must focus on lower-friction, higher-throughput capture.
* **The Valentine Museum "Richmond Stories":** The Valentine uses an interactive timeline to tell Richmond's story through objects, documents, and images [19] [20]. *Lesson:* There is already a public appetite and digital destination for Richmond history; the Popup Kit should feed into existing platforms rather than building a new silo.
* **VCU Latino Virginia Oral History:** VCU Libraries launched a collection to preserve the voices of Latinos living in Virginia [24]. *Lesson:* Targeted, community-specific oral history projects succeed when partnered with academic archives. The Popup Kit must include multilingual support to capture diverse demographics effectively.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond municipal project limits its impact and funding potential. 
* **Regional Shift:** If the frame becomes regional, partnering with PlanRVA [5], the stories become qualitative data for regional transportation, housing, and environmental planning. PlanRVA already notes that telling stories serves as a key data point in building support for policies like fare-free transit [6].
* **Statewide Shift:** A statewide framing aligns the project with Virginia Humanities [3] and the upcoming America's 250th Anniversary (VA250) initiatives [4], unlocking massive grant pools.
* **Nonprofit Front-End / City Back-End:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, trust increases exponentially. Residents are more likely to share candid experiences with a community group than a government recording device. The city benefits by receiving anonymized or consented qualitative data without bearing the liability of direct collection.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Trust and Community Legitimacy:** Residents may hesitate to share stories if they believe the City will use the data against them or monetize it. *Mitigation:* Default to non-exclusive licenses where residents retain copyright; use trusted nonprofit facilitators.
* **Data-Sharing and Privacy:** Handling unmoderated audio requires strict protocols to prevent the release of PII (Personally Identifiable Information) or defamatory content. *Mitigation:* Implement a mandatory human-review bottleneck between automated transcription and public archiving.
* **Technical Integration:** Moving audio from a Zoom H5 to Otter.ai to Omeka S requires a seamless, documented workflow. *Mitigation:* Partner with VCU Libraries [8], who already manage complex digital collections, to design the metadata schema.
* **Grant Dependence:** Relying solely on grants (like NHPRC or NEH) creates a fiscal cliff. *Mitigation:* Institutionalize the hardware costs within the Richmond Public Library's standard operating budget, using grants only for expansion and special programming.
