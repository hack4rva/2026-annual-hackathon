# Project Summary

The 'community-story-popup-kit' is a civic technology project for Richmond, Virginia, designed for city and community engagement staffers. The core job-to-be-done is to capture place-based stories and photos from elders during offline pop-up events at locations like churches and senior centers, particularly within Richmond’s East End and Southside communities. The primary motivation is to use consistent prompts and a standardized consent process to collect these narratives. The intended outcome is to convert this qualitative data into actionable civic insights that align with the city's 'Richmond 300' master plan priorities and to effectively share these findings back with residents. The project directly addresses the core pain points of the current ad hoc approach, which results in fragmented data from scattered comment cards and surveys, inaccessible audio files, weak metadata regarding location, topic, and consent, and a poor feedback loop for integrating community stories into official City planning and Capital Improvement Program (CIP) decisions.

# Executive Summary

The 'community-story-popup-kit' project in Richmond faces several primary pain points stemming from its reliance on offline engagement to reach key communities. Current methods, while necessary for equity, lead to fragmented inputs from paper surveys and pop-up events, making data synthesis difficult and costly. Digital outreach efforts have proven insufficient, as evidenced by a very small SMS opt-in database, and initial digital-first surveys have shown bias by underrepresenting BIPOC, low-income, and senior residents. This results in inaccessible audio stories with inconsistent metadata and a weak feedback loop to demonstrate how community input influences city planning. The key recommendation is to implement a structured, tiered consent model that is captured offline via a simple paper form. This model should offer participants clear choices: (A) secure archiving for internal use only, (B) public, non-commercial sharing, and (C) use in official city planning reports. This approach, inspired by best practices from the Oral History Association and models like StoryCorps, would run in parallel with a standardized metadata collection process to ensure stories are discoverable, usable, and ethically managed in alignment with Virginia's public records laws and Richmond's equity goals.

# City Staff Persona Analysis

## Persona Title

City/community engagement staffer

## Pain Points Summary

The primary challenges for a city/community engagement staffer revolve around fragmented data collection, operational inefficiencies, and ethical risks. Inputs from various offline methods like pop-ups and paper surveys are scattered and require extensive manual synthesis. Digital follow-up is ineffective, as shown by extremely low SMS opt-in rates. This leads to significant gaps in reaching key demographics, particularly seniors and BIPOC communities. Furthermore, there's a lack of standardized processes for managing metadata and consent for oral histories, rendering valuable audio assets inaccessible and unusable for long-term planning. This is compounded by weak feedback loops, making it difficult to demonstrate to the community how their input influenced city decisions.

## Detailed Pain Points

1. **Fragmented and Inefficient Data Collection:** Staffers struggle with inputs from diverse, often offline, engagement methods. The 'Richmond Connects' initiative heavily relied on pop-ups, paper surveys, and dot-voting to reach 'Communities of Concern,' which generated thousands of comments that then needed to be manually synthesized and mapped. Phase 4 specifically used paper surveys to reach Black, Hispanic, low-income, and residents aged 65+, highlighting the dependence on methods that create dispersed, unstructured data requiring significant staff time to aggregate and clean.

2. **Limited Reach and Ineffective Digital Follow-up:** Efforts to maintain contact and gather ongoing consent digitally are failing. A key piece of evidence is the SMS opt-in database for 'Richmond Connects', which contained only 46 mobile numbers mid-process despite multi-channel promotion. This underscores a heavy dependence on offline methods and the difficulty of sustaining contact with residents for things like tiered consent updates or sharing back results, breaking the feedback loop.

3. **Equity Gaps and Biased Data:** Initial digital-first collection methods often fail to reach a representative sample of the community. In the 'Richmond Connects' Phase 1, survey results over-represented white residents while under-representing low-income, BIPOC, Hispanic, and younger residents. This forced a pivot to more resource-intensive methods like in-person pop-ups and paid focus groups to correct the equity imbalance, demonstrating the risk of biased data and the cost of workarounds.

4. **Inconsistent Metadata and Unusable Oral Histories:** Audio recordings and stories collected in the field often lack the necessary metadata for rights, description, and administration. Best practices from the Oral History Association (OHA) and university programs (like the University of Richmond's) call for complete metadata and transcripts to make assets usable. Without standardized fields for location, topic, and consent, ad-hoc field recordings become inaccessible 'dark data,' losing their value for planning and archival purposes. This directly impacts the ability to use these stories to justify Capital Improvement Program (CIP) decisions.

5. **Weak Feedback Loops and Lack of Traceability:** There is a significant operational need to close the loop with the community by showing how their feedback was used. This is a prescribed practice in comparable cities like Pittsburgh, whose guide mandates engagement logs and reports demonstrating impact. While 'Richmond Connects' emphasized sharing outcomes, the lack of a structured system makes this difficult. This weakens the traceability of community input to specific planning decisions and undermines the legitimacy of the engagement process.


# Community Member Persona Analysis

## Persona Title

Elder/Community Member

## Pain Points Summary

Community members, particularly elders and those in historically marginalized groups, face significant barriers to participation and a lack of agency over their own narratives. They are often excluded by digital-first engagement strategies and face compounded barriers to access. When they do participate, they often don't see how their input is used, leading to disillusionment. Furthermore, sharing personal stories comes with privacy risks and a lack of control over how their identity, location, and voice are used, stored, and shared by the city.

## Detailed Pain Points

1. **Exclusion from Digital Engagement:** Digital-first survey methods systematically fail to capture the voices of certain community members. The 'Richmond Connects' plan explicitly notes that its Phase 1 online engagement under-represented low-income, BIPOC, Hispanic, and residents aged 65+. This means that without targeted, in-person outreach, these community members' perspectives are simply missed, leading to inequitable planning outcomes that do not reflect their needs.

2. **Lack of a Feedback Loop and Perceived Impact:** A major pain point is contributing time and personal stories without ever knowing the outcome. The strong emphasis in engagement best practices (from Pittsburgh and Richmond) on 'closing the loop' by creating reports on 'what we heard/what we did' and notifying participants when their input is used highlights that this is not the current status quo. For a community member, this lack of follow-up can feel extractive and disrespectful, discouraging future participation.

3. **Privacy Risks and Lack of Control Over Personal Stories:** Sharing personal and place-based stories carries inherent risks. Without clear, ethical consent processes, community members have no control over how their stories, images, or even their voice might be used. The detailed proposal for tiered consent (archive only, public sharing, city planning use), pseudonyms, transcript-only options, and geolocation fuzzing directly addresses this pain point. The need for 'rolling consent,' as advocated by the Oral History Association, underscores the desire for participants to retain agency and change their minds as a project evolves, a right they currently lack in ad-hoc collection processes.

4. **Compounded Barriers to Participation:** For many, especially seniors and residents in Richmond's East End and Southside 'Communities of Concern,' participation is hindered by multiple factors. The research mentions 'compounded barriers' and infrastructure needs in these areas. The city's workaround of using paid focus groups, paper surveys at churches and senior centers, and telephone town halls acknowledges that factors like limited mobility, lack of internet access, and language barriers are significant pain points that prevent equitable participation in civic life.


# Current Workflow Inefficiencies

The current process for community engagement in Richmond is marked by significant inefficiencies and breaks down at several key points. Digital follow-through is weak, as evidenced by a tiny SMS opt-in list of only 46 numbers, which severely inhibits the ability to re-contact participants for updates or tiered consent. Initial digital-first survey waves have shown bias, over-representing white residents and under-representing low-income, BIPOC, Hispanic, and younger residents, which forces a pivot to less efficient methods. The primary workarounds involve a heavy reliance on in-person, analog methods such as pop-up events, paid focus groups, paper surveys, telephone town halls, and utility mailers, particularly to reach 'Communities of Concern'. This approach generates thousands of unstructured comments and data points from scattered sources like comment cards and dot-voting exercises, which then require extensive manual synthesis. The cost of this status quo is substantial. It includes immense staff time spent aggregating and cleaning disparate inputs. More critically, it carries a high equity and legitimacy cost by risking the exclusion of seniors and BIPOC communities. Furthermore, valuable qualitative data, such as audio recordings of stories, becomes inaccessible and unusable for long-term planning due to a lack of standardized transcripts and rights metadata, weakening the traceability and justification for Capital Improvement Program (CIP) decisions.

# Consent Model Recommendations

## Legal Context

The Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (VCDPA) does not apply to state or local government bodies, as outlined in Virginia Code §59.1-576, which exempts "the Commonwealth or any political subdivision." This means the city is not bound by the VCDPA's specific controller/processor obligations for this project. However, any stories, photos, or other materials collected are considered public records. As such, they are subject to Virginia's public records laws, including retention and disposition schedules regulated by the Library of Virginia (LVA) under Code of Virginia § 42.1-85. The data may also be subject to Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests. Therefore, while VCDPA compliance is not required, a strong ethical framework for consent is necessary to build trust and manage expectations regarding the public nature of the collected information.

## Ethical Best Practices

Recommendations are anchored in the principles of the Oral History Association (OHA) and the practices of organizations like StoryCorps. The OHA's best practices mandate obtaining informed consent before an interview, which involves clearly explaining the project's purpose, potential uses, and risks to the narrator. A key recommendation is to adopt a "narrator-centered oral history agreement" that incorporates the concept of "rolling consent." This empowers participants by giving them the right to give or withdraw consent at different stages of the project, such as archiving, transcription, and public display. This approach allows individuals to change their minds later by contacting the project staff. Furthermore, the model from StoryCorps, which distinguishes between a legal release form (granting permission to archive and share) and separate privacy/visibility settings (controlling who can access the interview), provides a practical template for implementing tiered consent options.

## Proposed Consent Tiers

A tiered opt-in model is proposed to provide participants with clear and granular control over the use of their stories, designed for offline capture using paper forms with verbal explanations and icons. The model includes three main tiers: 
- **Tier A: Local archive only (restricted)**: This tier allows for the secure storage of the story and related materials (audio, photos) with access limited to internal city staff. The information would only be used in de-identified or summarized form for planning purposes, and would not be publicly posted. 
- **Tier B: Public sharing (noncommercial)**: This tier grants permission for the story to be shared publicly online (e.g., audio, photos, transcripts on a city website or digital archive) and in community exhibits. It includes sub-options for attribution, allowing the narrator to choose to be identified by their full name, first name only, or a pseudonym. It also allows for the use of excerpts, provided the context is maintained. 
- **Tier C: City planning use**: This tier specifically permits the inclusion of direct quotes, images, and anonymized summaries in official public documents, such as planning reports, Capital Improvement Program (CIP) justifications, and public presentations. It also allows for the data to be aggregated and visualized with location metadata for analysis. 
Optional sub-choices are also recommended, such as separate consent for photo use, using an identifiable voice versus a transcript only, setting the level of geolocation precision (e.g., parcel, block, or ZIP code), and placing time-based restrictions (embargoes) on release. The ability to withdraw or change tiers later (rolling consent) is a critical component.


# Data And Metadata Challenges

The core pain point for the city's community storytelling efforts is the fragmented and inaccessible nature of the data collected. Current methods, as detailed in the 'Richmond Connects' plan, rely on a mix of pop-ups, paper surveys, and dot-voting, which generated over 8,000 comments in a single phase. This creates a massive volume of disparate inputs that require manual, labor-intensive synthesis to become useful. A significant problem is that audio files from listening sessions or oral histories are often rendered inaccessible and unusable for planning purposes because they lack associated transcripts and, critically, clear rights metadata. There is no standardized intake model for capturing essential metadata related to the story's location, thematic topics, and the narrator's consent for various uses. This ad-hoc approach stands in stark contrast to the best practices demonstrated by institutions like the University of Richmond, whose oral histories include complete metadata and transcriptions, and the standards set by the Oral History Association, which mandates the collection of administrative, descriptive, technical, and rights metadata to ensure long-term usability and ethical management.

# Proposed Data Management Framework

To address the challenges of fragmented data and inaccessible files, a recommended framework for managing story data is proposed. This framework is built on four key best practices. First, it calls for the creation of a centralized archive or repository to house all collected materials (audio, photos, transcripts, notes), preventing data from being scattered across different departments or formats. Second, it emphasizes the establishment of a robust metadata schema based on the Oral History Association (OHA) guidelines. This schema should include administrative (e.g., interviewer, date), descriptive (e.g., location, topics), technical, preservation, and, crucially, rights and access metadata that documents the specific consent choices made by each participant. Third, the framework prioritizes file accessibility by recommending that audio files be transcribed. As demonstrated by the University of Richmond's oral history collection, transcripts make the content searchable and more easily usable for analysis and reporting. Finally, the framework mandates the creation of a formal feedback loop into Richmond's planning processes. This involves using engagement logs and reports, similar to the model used in Pittsburgh, to track community input and its influence on decisions. It also includes a commitment to "share back" with the community, publishing "what we heard/what we did" summaries and notifying participants when their input has informed specific recommendations or Capital Improvement Program (CIP) items, thus closing the loop and demonstrating the impact of their participation.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

The equity dimensions of this project are centered on addressing the digital divide and historical underrepresentation in civic engagement, particularly affecting seniors, residents with limited mobility, non-English speakers, and BIPOC and low-income communities in Richmond's East End and Southside. Current data fragmentation and digital-first approaches have been shown to exclude these groups, as evidenced by early survey waves in the 'Richmond Connects' plan that over-represented white residents and under-represented Communities of Concern. To counteract this, the project must prioritize inclusive, offline-first design. This includes using paper forms, providing read-aloud scripts for consent, conducting pop-ups in trusted community locations like churches and senior centers, and offering compensation for participation in focus groups. Furthermore, to protect vulnerable storytellers, the project should implement robust ethical safeguards inspired by the Oral History Association. These include offering rolling consent, the use of pseudonyms, transcript-only options to protect voice identity, and 'geolocation fuzzing' to obscure precise locations. A critical component of equity is closing the feedback loop; this involves publishing accessible summaries of findings (e.g., 'what we heard/what we did') and directly notifying participants when their input has informed city planning or Capital Improvement Program (CIP) decisions, thereby building trust and demonstrating the value of their participation.

# National Best Practice Models

## Organization Name

Oral History Association (OHA)

## Key Principles

The OHA's core principles are narrator-centered, emphasizing that informed consent must be obtained prior to an interview. The principles call for clarity about project goals, potential risks, and long-term preservation plans. A key ethical tenet is the sharing of power with the narrator throughout the process.

## Consent And Release Model

The OHA champions the concept of 'rolling consent,' which is a dynamic process where participants have the right to give or withdraw their consent at each step of a project's lifecycle (e.g., recording, archiving, publishing). This narrator-centered model allows individuals to change their minds as the project evolves, providing a strong ethical foundation.

## Metadata And Archival Standards

The OHA provides a comprehensive metadata framework, recommending the collection of five distinct categories of information to ensure long-term value and proper management. These are: administrative (project info), descriptive (content summaries), technical (file formats), preservation (archival actions), and, crucially, rights and access metadata, which governs how the material can be used.

## Organization Name

StoryCorps

## Key Principles

StoryCorps' mission is to preserve and share humanity's stories in order to build connections between people and create a more just and compassionate world. Their model is built on the foundation of recording, sharing, and archiving personal interviews.

## Consent And Release Model

StoryCorps offers a highly relevant and practical model for tiered consent by making a clear distinction between two separate actions. First, the participant signs a legal release form that grants StoryCorps permission to archive and potentially share the interview. Second, and independently, the participant uses privacy and visibility settings to control who can actually see and hear their interview online. This separation provides a clear framework for offering different levels of access.

## Metadata And Archival Standards

The entire StoryCorps platform, which archives interviews at the Library of Congress, is predicated on a robust system of metadata. This includes information about the participants, location, keywords, and the privacy settings chosen by the user, ensuring that each story is discoverable, contextualized, and managed according to the narrator's wishes.
