# Preserving Displacement Memory: A practical map of U.S. oral-history archives and platforms—with a Richmond (Jackson Ward + Fulton) spotlight

## Executive Summary

- **Multi-host ecosystems drive durability**: The most resilient projects rely on partnerships between community organizations, university libraries, and public archives. For example, the Historic Fulton Oral History Project is hosted digitally by Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU), while The Valentine holds the physical copyrights [1] [2]. 
- **Richmond possesses strong foundational coverage**: Richmond, Virginia, is comparatively well-documented. The Historic Fulton collection features 17 interviews with 32 narrators [1] [2], and Jackson Ward's history is actively preserved by The JXN Project and the Library of Virginia's "House to Highway" exhibition [3] [4] [5].
- **Spatial data amplifies policy salience**: Pairing oral histories with geographic data (like ArcGIS StoryMaps) increases their utility in urban planning. Projects like "Preserving Society Hill" and the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project successfully map testimonies to specific parcels and neighborhoods [6] [7] [8].
- **Rights and ethics dictate access**: Clear consent is critical. The DC Public Library's "Southwest Views" collection is restricted to research-only access because it lacks deed of gift or release forms [9]. Projects must align with Oral History Association (OHA) ethical guidelines to ensure narrator protection and public accessibility [10].
- **Funding requires a braided approach**: Successful initiatives combine federal grants (IMLS, NEH), philanthropic support (Mellon Foundation), and local municipal funds. The Houston Public Library secured a $100,000 IMLS grant to digitize and preserve over 1,400 audiovisual items related to Black Houstonians [11].

## 2) Richmond, Virginia Deep Dive: Leveraging Existing Assets

Richmond serves as a strong model for displacement archiving, with significant resources dedicated to its historically Black neighborhoods.

### Historic Fulton and Jackson Ward Memory Initiatives
The **Historic Fulton Oral History Project** was developed in 2011 to document life in Fulton Bottom before the city's 1970s urban renewal plan leveled 356 acres of the community [1] [2]. The collection contains 17 interviews with 32 narrators [1] [2]. VCU Libraries hosts the streaming audio and searchable transcripts, while The Valentine retains the original materials and copyrights [1] [2]. 

In Jackson Ward, **The JXN Project** (founded in 2021) works to recontextualize the neighborhood's origin story [5] [24]. They partnered with the Library of Virginia for the 2025-2026 exhibition *"House to Highway: Reclaiming a Community History,"* which explores the displacement of over 1,000 families caused by the Richmond-Petersburg Turnpike through the lens of Abraham Peyton Skipwith, the ward's first known Black homeowner [3] [4].

### Actionable Integration for Richmond
Rather than building new, isolated archives, Richmond institutions should focus on federating existing assets. A unified "Displacement Memory Portal" could cross-link VCU's Fulton transcripts, The JXN Project's historical context, The Valentine's physical archives, and the University of Richmond's "Renewing Inequality" data overlays to create a comprehensive tool for urban planners and educators.

Selecting the right platform depends heavily on the host's technical capacity and long-term preservation goals.

Universities should utilize their existing Institutional Repositories (like DSpace or CONTENTdm) for master file preservation while using StoryMaps for public engagement.

## 4) Ethics, Rights, and Community Governance

Ethical stewardship is the foundation of displacement archiving. The Oral History Association (OHA) emphasizes that the interview process must be transparent, with ongoing participation and informed consent from the narrator [10]. 

### Mitigating Access Failures
Without proper legal releases, collections become unusable for public advocacy. The DC Public Library's "Southwest Views" collection contains 56 DVDs of interviews regarding Southwest DC, but because the library lacks deed of gift or release forms, the collection is strictly restricted to on-site research access and cannot be duplicated [9]. 

To avoid this, projects must:
- Secure signed legal releases or informed consent prior to or immediately following the interview [10].
- Offer narrators options to restrict, redact, or embargo sensitive information [10].
- Utilize clear RightsStatements.org or Creative Commons licenses to dictate public use [10].

## 5) Braided Funding and Staffing Models

Displacement archives require sustainable funding to prevent link rot and platform abandonment. Successful projects braid multiple funding streams.

### The Houston Public Library Model
The Houston Public Library's African American History Research Center provides a blueprint for institutional funding. They secured a **$100,000 IMLS grant** to digitize 1,416 at-risk audiovisual items [11]. The budget specifically allocated:
- **$50,000** for outsourced digital preservation services (vendor digitization) [11].
- **$40,804** to hire a full-time contract Project Archivist to write metadata and create transcripts [11].
- **$3,196** for archival rehousing supplies [11].

Similarly, the Shockoe Project in Richmond is supported by a massive braided funding model: $25 million from the City of Richmond, $13 million from the Commonwealth of Virginia, and $11 million from the Mellon Foundation [35].

## 6) Impact Mechanisms: Influencing Policy and Memory

Oral histories move from passive archives to active policy tools when they are integrated into public discourse and environmental reviews.

### Pathways to Measurable Impact
- **Environmental Impact Statements (EIS) / NEPA**: Oral histories provide qualitative data for cumulative impact analyses. In *St. Paul Branch of the NAACP v. Department of Transportation*, residents used the history of 1970s urban renewal and I-94 displacement to challenge a new transit project's EIS [4].
- **Public Exhibitions**: The Library of Virginia's "House to Highway" exhibit translates archival data into public symposiums, walking tours, and K-12 educational touchpoints [3] [4].
- **Advocacy Coalitions**: In New Orleans, the Claiborne Avenue Alliance utilizes oral histories and mobile ethnography to advocate for highway removal and environmental justice, directly informing "Reconnecting Communities" policy debates [36].

## 8) Implementation Playbook

To launch a sustainable displacement oral history project, follow a phased approach:

**0–90 Days (Foundation & Governance)**
- Establish a community advisory board to guide project ethics and scope.
- Draft OHA-compliant informed consent and deed of gift templates [10].
- Select a digital platform (e.g., Omeka S) and secure hosting (e.g., Reclaim Hosting) [25] [28].
- Conduct 3-5 pilot interviews to test audio equipment and workflow.

**3–12 Months (Execution & Access)**
- Execute vendor contracts for digitization of legacy media (if applicable) [11].
- Generate transcripts (using AI tools with human review) and sync them using OHMS [32].
- Deposit master preservation files (WAV/BWF) into a secure Institutional Repository [11].
- Launch a public-facing StoryMap or digital exhibit and host a community listening event to drive engagement and policy awareness.
