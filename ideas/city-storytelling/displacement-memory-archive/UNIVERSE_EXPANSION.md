# From Scattered Stories to Strategic Power: Expanding the Richmond Displacement-Memory Archive

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Displacement Memory Archive cannot achieve its full policy potential if it remains solely a municipal IT project or an isolated academic repository. Richmond’s displacement history is currently fragmented across institutions like VCU, The Valentine, and The JXN Project [1] [2] [3]. To transform these scattered stories into actionable, spatially grounded evidence for urban planning, the project must expand into a federated, hybrid ecosystem. 

By shifting the frame from a "city-owned archive" to a "community-governed, city-enabled data network," the initiative unlocks significant federal and philanthropic capital. Braided funding models leveraging the USDOT Reconnecting Communities Pilot [4], the NEH Humanities Collections [5], and the African American Cultural Heritage Action Fund [6] can sustain the platform. Furthermore, integrating with regional bodies like PlanRVA [7] elevates the tool from a neighborhood-specific historical project to a Central Virginia policy instrument for equitable development.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership with Descendant Governance**

* **What the City should own:** The API layer, spatial data infrastructure (ArcGIS integration), and the formal policy pathways that allow oral histories to be submitted as evidence in environmental reviews and zoning hearings.
* **What Partners should own:** A lead cultural nonprofit (e.g., The Valentine or JXN Project) should own the community-facing portal, narrator relationships, and day-to-day curation. 
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The interpretation of the narratives and the raw audio files. Governance and interpretive authority must reside with a Descendants & Residents Advisory Council to ensure trust and prevent the weaponization of community data.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

Projects like the Barry Farm Oral History Project interviewed residents about living in stigmatized areas [32]. *Lesson:* Without proper deeds of gift and informed consent (as outlined by OHA [28]), collections can become restricted to research-only access, defeating the public policy goal.
* **City of Richmond Cultural Heritage Stewardship Plan:** A jointly funded effort by DHR and the City of Richmond (2023-2025) focusing on historic preservation and oral history [33]. *Lesson:* The city already has a mandate and active partnerships for cultural stewardship; the Archive should nest within this existing framework.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond city project limits its funding potential and policy impact. 
* **Regional Shift:** By partnering with PlanRVA [7], the Archive can overlay oral histories with regional transportation and housing data, making it relevant for broader Central Virginia transit and zoning debates.
* **State/Federal Shift:** Expanding the frame to a "Reconnecting Communities" model aligns perfectly with USDOT priorities [4] and the African American Cultural Heritage Action Fund [6]. If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides the API, the project becomes highly competitive for federal grants that require community-led equity initiatives.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Legal & Rights Management:** The biggest blocker is informed consent. If historical interviews lack proper release forms, they cannot be legally published or used in policy contexts. Adherence to OHA Principles [28] [29] is mandatory.
* **Technical Integration:** Federating data without migrating it requires stable APIs. If partner institutions change their backend systems, links will break ("link rot").
* **Community Trust:** If the city owns the narrative interpretation, marginalized communities may refuse to participate due to historical trauma. Descendant governance is a critical mitigation strategy.
* **Grant Dependence:** Building a custom platform on a one-time grant leads to abandoned software. The project must use existing, supported tools (like ArcGIS StoryMaps) rather than building bespoke software.
