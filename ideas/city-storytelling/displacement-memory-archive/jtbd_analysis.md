# Executive Summary

The 'displacement-memory-archive' project addresses critical needs for residents of Richmond's Gilpin, Creighton, and Mosby Courts facing displacement due to large-scale redevelopment. A Jobs To Be Done analysis reveals the core need for residents is to have a trusted, effective way to document their lived experiences—such as moves, housing conditions, and rights concerns—to influence redevelopment outcomes and secure their right to return. This breaks down into three key areas: a functional need for a direct documentation-to-decision channel that bypasses fragmented and ineffective current workarounds; an emotional need for a process that guarantees safety, dignity, and continuity in a low-trust environment fraught with fears of retaliation; and a systemic need for a coordinated feedback pipeline that connects resident stories to the workflows of RRHA staff, city governance, and partner organizations. The project's success hinges on resolving critical, Richmond-specific questions concerning data privacy and PII, building user trust to ensure participation from vulnerable residents, integrating the archive's insights into official decision-making bodies like the City Council and the Jackson Ward Community Plan, ensuring equity by tracking right-to-return commitments, and adapting lessons from prior local storytelling initiatives like the JXN Project.

# Project Context

The project is set against the backdrop of large-scale public housing redevelopment in Richmond, Virginia, managed by the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA). This initiative affects three key communities—Creighton, Mosby, and Gilpin Courts—each at a different stage. Creighton Court's revitalization began in 2017; Phase 1 demolition was completed in July 2022, and by Fall 2025, the first 68 families have moved into the renamed 'Creighton Renaissance' community. Displaced Creighton residents are offered several housing options, including Tenant Protection Vouchers (TPVs) and the right to return, guided by a Tenants' Bill of Rights developed with resident input. Mosby Court is in the planning phase with partner The Richman Group; as of March 2025, 98% of resident relocation assessments were completed. The redevelopment of Gilpin Court is the most complex and politically charged. While intended to be anchored by the 2023 Jackson Ward Community Plan, progress is stalled due to a major conflict between RRHA and Richmond's Mayor, Danny Avula. The Mayor has refused to provide a federally required letter of support for RRHA's plans, citing concerns over a proposal to privatize the property. He has demanded that RRHA guarantee one-for-one replacement of all 781 units, secure City Council approval, create a tenants' bill of rights, and form an oversight committee that includes Gilpin residents. This political standoff, which could reshape or halt the redevelopment, highlights the deep-seated issues of trust, governance, and resident rights that the 'displacement-memory-archive' aims to address.

# Functional Job To Be Done

## Situation

When demolition and relocation phases begin at my public housing community (Gilpin/Creighton/Mosby)

## Persona

RRHA resident

## Motivation

to easily record and submit my lived experience (moves, conditions, service needs, voucher path, rights concerns) to a trusted archive that routes to decision-makers

## Outcome

so I can influence redevelopment timelines, supports, and right-to-return outcomes

## Current Workaround

Verbal updates to relocation counselors, ad hoc texts/social posts, tenant council meetings, sporadic RRHA meetings’ public comment.

## Core Pain

Fragmented channels; unclear how stories reach RRHA, City Council, or the Jackson Ward Community Plan process; fear of retaliation; limited clarity on unit replacement and voucher options.


# Emotional Trust Job To Be Done

## Situation

When I’m asked to relocate or decide among TPV/PBV/return options

## Persona

elder, caregiver, or family in public housing

## Motivation

want assurance my story is heard without risk

## Outcome

so I can feel safe, respected, and confident I won’t be displaced from Richmond or lose community ties

## Current Workaround

Sharing concerns in hallways, with advocacy groups, or caseworkers; private social media groups.

## Core Pain

Low trust in agencies due to communication gaps, privatization concerns, and unclear tenants’ rights; stigma and fear that disclosures could affect lease compliance or voucher eligibility.


# Systems Coordination Job To Be Done

## Situation

When relocation assessments and construction phases are scheduled

## Persona

RRHA relocation staff/partner

## Motivation

want a coordinated intake-to-feedback pipeline for resident stories mapped to relocation milestones and supports

## Outcome

so I can close loops (eligibility, services, right-to-return) and surface systemic issues to RRHA leadership, the RDC, and City governance

## Current Workaround

Case notes in ACOP/relocation files, separate meeting minutes, email chains, and inconsistent data handoffs between RRHA, partners, and tenant councils.

## Core Pain

Siloed data; difficulty aggregating qualitative insight by phase/site; limited two-way transparency with City/Mayor/Council requirements and Jackson Ward Community Plan stewardship.


# Data Questions

## Question

What personally identifiable information (PII) and sensitive narrative elements must be redacted or consent-gated to comply with City privacy policy and minimize risk to residents using the archive?

## Context

Given the City of Richmond's privacy policy and the low trust residents have in agencies, it is critical to define what information is considered sensitive. Residents fear that disclosures about their housing conditions or experiences could negatively affect their lease compliance or voucher eligibility. A clear redaction and consent model is necessary to protect them.

## Target Stakeholder

City of Richmond, RRHA

## Question

How will qualitative stories be structured (tags for TPV/PBV pathway, relocation milestone, unit conditions) to align with RRHA ACOP relocation processes and Mosby/Gilpin/Creighton timelines?

## Context

For the collected stories to be useful as civic insight, the data must be analyzable and directly relevant to the ongoing redevelopment and relocation efforts. Structuring the data with tags corresponding to RRHA's official processes (like Tenant Protection Voucher or Project-Based Voucher pathways) and project-specific timelines will allow for the aggregation of actionable insights.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, Project Team

## Question

What is the retention policy, public access level, and appeals/removal process for resident-submitted materials given City open data standards and FOIA considerations?

## Context

The City of Richmond has an Open Data Portal, which implies a standard for public access. However, this archive contains personal and sensitive stories. A clear policy must be established to balance transparency with the privacy and safety of residents, including how long data is kept, who can see it, and how a resident can request to have their information removed or corrected, especially considering potential Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests.

## Target Stakeholder

City of Richmond, RRHA

## Question

Can we technically route anonymized summaries to RRHA Board packets or City Council materials without exposing PII while preserving equity signals?

## Context

A primary goal of the project is to influence decisions made by the RRHA Board and City Council. This requires a technical solution to summarize and present insights from resident stories in an anonymized format that protects individuals but still highlights systemic equity issues, such as barriers to using housing vouchers or securing the right to return.

## Target Stakeholder

Project Team, RRHA, City Council


# User Questions

## Question

What multilingual, low-bandwidth, and assisted-capture options are needed for elders and caregivers in Gilpin/Creighton/Mosby to submit voice/photo/text stories during hectic moves?

## Context

The target population includes residents who may face digital literacy challenges, have limited internet access, or speak languages other than English. To ensure equitable participation, the story submission process must be highly accessible, especially for vulnerable groups like the elderly and caregivers, who are navigating the stress of relocation.

## Target Stakeholder

Residents, RRHA relocation counselors

## Question

How will we convey no-retaliation guarantees and consent choices (public, restricted, staff-only) to build trust with residents already concerned about lease compliance and privatization risks?

## Context

The research identifies a core pain point of 'low trust in agencies' and a 'fear of retaliation' among residents. To encourage participation, the project must proactively and clearly communicate how it will protect residents, offering transparent consent options and strong guarantees that sharing their story will not lead to negative consequences for their housing situation.

## Target Stakeholder

Residents, RRHA, Tenant Councils

## Question

What training and workflows do RRHA relocation counselors need to integrate story capture during relocation assessments without adding burden?

## Context

The project plans to partner with RRHA relocation staff, who are a key interface with residents. For this collaboration to be effective, the story collection process must be seamlessly integrated into the counselors' existing responsibilities. This requires developing efficient workflows and providing adequate training so that story capture is seen as a helpful tool rather than an additional chore.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA relocation counselors, RRHA Management


# Integration Questions

## Question

How will the archive feed the Jackson Ward Community Plan governance and any Joint Gilpin Transformation/Steering Group, RRHA Board citizen comment, and City Council oversight so stories demonstrably affect decisions (timelines, replacement unit commitments, supports)?

## Context

The project's success hinges on its ability to translate resident stories into actionable insights for key decision-making bodies. As highlighted in the research, Mayor Avula has emphasized that the Gilpin Court redevelopment must be guided by the Jackson Ward Community Plan and receive City Council approval. Therefore, a clear pathway for the archive's data to inform these specific groups is essential for it to have a tangible impact on outcomes like redevelopment timelines and resident support services.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, City of Richmond, Joint Gilpin Transformation/Steering Group

## Question

What fields must align with RRHA’s relocation intake and project phasing (e.g., Creighton Phase A lease-up, Mosby relocation assessments, Gilpin unit replacement planning) to produce actionable dashboards?

## Context

The project partners with RRHA relocation staff. To be useful and integrated into their workflow, the data collected must be structured in a way that is compatible with their existing systems and timelines. The different public housing sites (Creighton, Mosby, Gilpin) are in different phases of redevelopment, requiring the data to be categorized accordingly to generate relevant, actionable reports for RRHA staff.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA

## Question

Can the platform produce meeting-ready briefs mapped to HUD Section 18 application requirements (unit replacement, services) to satisfy mayoral support conditions?

## Context

According to federal regulations cited in the research, RRHA requires a letter of support from the mayor to move forward with redevelopment plans. The mayor's support is conditional on meeting specific requirements, such as one-for-one unit replacement, which are also part of HUD's Section 18 application process. The archive's utility would be greatly enhanced if it can automatically generate reports that help RRHA demonstrate compliance with these critical political and regulatory conditions.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, City of Richmond


# Equity Questions

## Question

How will we ensure right-to-return and on/off-site replacement commitments are tracked in stories, highlighting barriers (voucher acceptance, eligibility) faced by specific households?

## Context

The 'right to return' and one-for-one unit replacement are central promises made to residents and demanded by city leadership. An equitable process requires not just making these commitments but actively tracking their implementation. This question addresses the need for the archive to serve as an accountability tool by documenting the real-world barriers, such as voucher acceptance issues or eligibility problems, that individual households face in exercising their rights.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, City of Richmond, Residents

## Question

What safeguards protect residents who report poor conditions or service gaps from adverse housing consequences, and who enforces them across RRHA/RDC/City partners?

## Context

The research identifies a core pain point for residents as 'low trust in agencies' and 'fear that disclosures could affect lease compliance.' To ensure honest participation, the project must establish and clearly communicate robust, enforceable anti-retaliation policies. This question seeks to define what those safeguards are and, crucially, which entity (RRHA, RDC, City) holds the power and responsibility to enforce them, ensuring residents feel safe sharing their experiences.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, RDC, City of Richmond

## Question

How do we ensure participation from residents least likely to be heard (non-leaseholders, youth, disabled residents) and from each site cluster, not just organized tenant leaders?

## Context

An equitable data collection effort must be intentionally inclusive. Relying only on formal channels or organized tenant leaders risks excluding significant portions of the community, such as young people, residents with disabilities, or non-leaseholding family members. This question pushes the project to develop specific outreach strategies to ensure a representative sample of stories is collected from all demographics and across all affected housing sites.

## Target Stakeholder

RRHA, Residents


# Prior Art Questions

## Question

Which Richmond storytelling efforts (e.g., JXN Project, Valentine collaborations) offer models for reparative history, consent, and community governance that we can adapt for displacement memory?

## Context

The research identifies the JXN Project as a local initiative in Richmond focused on 'reparative historic' work through narratives in Jackson Ward, the same area as Gilpin Court. It is prudent to learn from these existing local efforts to understand successful models for ethical community engagement, establishing consent, and creating community-led governance structures, rather than starting from scratch.

## Target Stakeholder

JXN Project, The Valentine

## Question

Which external displacement/oral-history archives provide applicable intake schemas and governance for integrating qualitative narratives into planning decisions without harming participants?

## Context

This project is not unique; other cities have undertaken similar efforts to document displacement and use oral histories in planning. Researching these external examples can provide proven, practical templates for data intake forms (schemas) and governance models. This helps ensure the project is built on a solid ethical and functional foundation, protecting participants while maximizing the utility of the data for decision-makers.

## Target Stakeholder

Project Team
