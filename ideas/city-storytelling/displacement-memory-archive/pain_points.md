# Executive Summary

Residents of Richmond's public housing, particularly in Creighton, Gilpin, and Mosby Courts, are experiencing significant pain points due to large-scale redevelopment projects, primarily centered on a breakdown of trust and communication. A critical issue is the conditional and ambiguous nature of the guaranteed 'right to return,' which hinges on residents remaining in 'good standing'—a status threatened by unclear rules regarding rent arrears, household composition, and administrative errors that can lead to eviction filings. Communication from the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) is fragmented across various documents and websites, creating confusion about timelines, relocation options like Tenant Protection Vouchers (TPV), and how resident feedback is integrated into decision-making. This lack of clarity is compounded by a deep-seated fear of retaliation for reporting poor conditions, which silences residents. The entire system fosters anxiety over privatization, governance, and the potential loss of community ties, forcing residents to rely on informal workarounds and advocacy groups rather than official, trusted channels to ensure their right to return and influence the future of their communities.

# Project Redevelopment Context

## Court Name

Gilpin Court

## Redevelopment Status

The redevelopment is in a complex planning phase, previously stalled by political conflict but now proceeding under a joint governance model.

## Key Updates

Progress, initially stalled by a conflict between RRHA and Richmond's Mayor over privatization concerns, is moving forward under a 'Joint Gilpin Transformation Working Group.' This group, including city and resident representatives, was formed after the Mayor demanded guarantees for a one-for-one replacement of all 781 units, a tenant bill of rights, and City Council oversight. The group is tasked with implementing the Jackson Ward Community Plan and overseeing the redevelopment.

# Functional Job Pain Points

For the RRHA resident persona, the functional job of documenting their experiences to secure their housing rights is fraught with significant pain points. Firstly, the 'right-to-return' is conditional and practically ambiguous; RRHA materials state eligibility is tied to being in 'good standing,' a term defined by vague and potentially exclusionary criteria such as being current on rent, having no pests or hoarding, and reporting all household status changes. This creates confusion and risk, as residents in communities like Creighton and Gilpin may not understand their status or the full implications of using vouchers. Secondly, communication channels are fragmented and their connection to actual decision-making is unclear. Residents must navigate multiple, shifting options (Tenant Protection Vouchers, Project-Based Vouchers, etc.) and phased moves without a single, trusted pipeline to voice concerns to City Council, RRHA, or relevant workgroups, a problem so significant the Mayor publicly demanded clearer materials on timelines and options. Thirdly, even when residents secure vouchers, they face significant barriers from landlord discrimination, which persists in the Richmond area despite state-level protections, slowing down relocation and adding immense burden. Fourthly, administrative and lease errors by the housing authority itself can threaten housing stability. Investigative reports have documented cases in Richmond where public housing tenants faced erroneous overcharges that led to eviction filings, amplifying fear and directly jeopardizing the 'good standing' required for the right to return. Finally, resident protections are often incomplete or delayed; for instance, the RRHA's page for the Mosby redevelopment shows that while relocation assessments are complete, the Tenant Bill of Rights and a Relocation FAQ are still listed as 'coming soon,' indicating critical process gaps during an active planning phase.

# Emotional Trust Job Pain Points

For elders, caregivers, and families in Richmond's public housing, the redevelopment process triggers profound emotional and trust-related pain points centered on the need for safety, dignity, and community continuity. A primary issue is the pervasive fear that disclosing housing conditions or personal needs could negatively affect their eligibility or lease status. This is not an unfounded fear; a Virginia state analysis confirms that fear of retaliation is a significant reason why people do not file housing complaints, and this is amplified in an environment where the right-to-return is explicitly tied to compliance and 'good standing' with the housing authority. Secondly, there is widespread confusion and anxiety surrounding the transition to housing vouchers and the associated protections. The Mayor of Richmond highlighted this issue, noting that residents in the Creighton redevelopment did not understand what was required to become a voucher user or the long-term implications of that transition. Thirdly, anxiety is fueled by political instability and concerns about privatization. The public standoff between the Mayor and RRHA over the Gilpin Court redevelopment, coupled with debates about a new Richmond Development Corporation, created significant uncertainty and undermined resident trust in the governance of their own homes. Fourthly, the process of phased moves, while logistically necessary, creates deep anxiety about the breaking of community and family ties. While the Jackson Ward Community Plan promises a dedicated relocation team to manage these connections, trust hinges on flawless execution, which is not guaranteed. Finally, widely publicized news reports of RRHA pursuing evictions over alleged billing errors heighten the perceived risk of engaging with the system, making residents fearful of reporting any issue that might jeopardize their 'good standing' and, consequently, their home.

# Cross Cutting Equity Dimensions

The redevelopment process has significant equity implications, disproportionately affecting and potentially excluding the most vulnerable residents. The groups most impacted include households with rent arrears or minor lease technicalities who risk losing their 'right to return' due to strict 'good standing' requirements. Elders and residents with disabilities face unique challenges related to accessibility, transportation barriers, and navigating the complex relocation process. Families with complex household compositions or those with limited English proficiency are also at higher risk. Furthermore, residents who rely on vouchers encounter systemic source-of-income discrimination, limiting their housing options. Those lacking digital access or literacy are excluded from the primary channels of information, as materials are often scattered across websites and PDFs. The mechanisms driving these inequities include the 'good standing' criteria acting as a screen, administrative billing errors that can lead to eviction proceedings, uneven landlord acceptance of vouchers, and significant time lags between demolition, voucher issuance, and the availability of new units.

# Systemic Governance And Trust Issues

## Primary Conflict

The core conflict is a political standoff over the redevelopment of Gilpin Court between Richmond's Mayor, Danny Avula, and the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA). The Mayor has refused to provide a federally required letter of support for RRHA's plans, citing major concerns over a proposal to privatize the property, the number of replacement housing units, and the lack of resident oversight. This has stalled the redevelopment project, highlighting deep-seated issues of trust and governance.

## Mayor Demands

Mayor Avula has set forth several conditions for his support, including: a guarantee of one-for-one replacement of all 781 public housing units; securing City Council approval for the plans; the creation of a comprehensive Tenant Bill of Rights for Gilpin residents; the formation of a joint oversight committee (the Joint Gilpin Transformation Working Group) that includes Gilpin residents beyond just tenant council leaders; and the development of clear, resident-facing materials that explain the redevelopment timeline, stages, and all available housing options.

## Rrha Response

In response to the Mayor's public pressure and demands, the RRHA board unanimously approved the Mayor's joint governance proposal. This led to an agreement to form the 'Joint Gilpin Transformation Working Group' to oversee the redevelopment. Furthermore, RRHA has formally agreed to develop a Tenant Bill of Rights for Gilpin Court and to address deviations from the Jackson Ward Community Plan, signaling a concession to the Mayor's conditions in order to move the project forward.

## Impact On Residents

This high-level conflict has created significant anxiety, uncertainty, and distrust among Gilpin Court residents. The public debate over privatization and the potential for the redevelopment to be reshaped or halted entirely leaves residents in a state of limbo. It amplifies their fears about displacement, the security of their right to return, and whether their voices are being heard. The standoff validates residents' low trust in the agencies controlling their future and heightens the perceived risk of engaging with the system, as their housing stability is caught in a political battle.


# Right To Return And Voucher Uncertainty

## Right To Return Condition

The right for a resident to return to the redeveloped housing is conditioned on them being 'in good standing' with their lease at the time of relocation.

## Good Standing Criteria

To be considered in 'good standing', a resident must be current on rent, have no pests or hoarding conditions, have all household members listed on the lease, and have reported all status changes to Property Management.

## Voucher Challenges

Despite state-level protections, residents using Tenant Protection Vouchers (TPV) or Housing Choice Vouchers (HCV) face significant and persistent source-of-funds discrimination from landlords in the Richmond area. This discrimination impedes their ability to find suitable alternative housing, slowing down placements and increasing the burden on displaced families.

## Evidence Of Challenges

Evidence includes recent legal actions and settlements by the advocacy group HOME of Virginia, such as a March 2025 settlement with Sylvan Homes over allegations of not renting to voucher holders. Additionally, investigative reporting from The Washington Post has documented cases in Richmond where public housing tenants have faced mistaken rent hikes and subsequent eviction filings, which directly jeopardizes their 'good standing' status required for the right to return.

# Current Workarounds And Status Quo Costs

Residents facing displacement currently rely on a patchwork of informal and often ineffective workarounds to voice their concerns, as official channels are perceived as fragmented, untrustworthy, or risky. These methods include 'whisper networks' in hallways, sharing information in private social media groups, giving verbal updates to relocation counselors, and speaking with tenant councils. When these fail, residents may turn to intermediaries like advocacy groups (e.g., HOME of VA) to contest discrimination or legal aid services to fight billing errors and eviction notices. Some also wait for sporadic official meetings to make public comments. The cost of this broken status quo is exceptionally high for all involved. For residents, it leads to immense mental stress, anxiety, and the potential loss of their fundamental right-to-return due to technical noncompliance or administrative errors. They face prolonged displacement, the risk of eviction, and the fracturing of essential family and community support networks. For the system, the costs manifest as pervasive distrust, which fuels contested redevelopment plans, slows down project timelines and lease-ups, and increases the administrative burden of litigating errors and discrimination claims. This environment of conflict and inefficiency ultimately jeopardizes the housing authority's ability to meet its commitments and risks its standing with federal funding partners like HUD.

# Analysis Of Tenants Bill Of Rights

A comparative analysis reveals a significant gap between the established 'Tenants' Bill of Rights' for Creighton Court and the ongoing struggle for similar documents at Gilpin and Mosby Courts. 

For Creighton Court, the 'Tenants’ Bill of Rights' is an existing document, reportedly created with input from the tenant council, RRHA, and the city. It is framed as the foundation for resident housing choice, support services, and communication, and it gives Creighton residents priority to return. Its existence is a tangible outcome of that redevelopment's process. However, Mayor Avula's comments that Creighton residents 'didn't know what their current status was, or what was required if they were going to become a voucher user' suggest that the document's implementation or effectiveness in creating true clarity may be flawed.

In stark contrast, the 'Tenant Bill of Rights' for Gilpin Court is not an existing framework but a central demand in the political standoff between the Mayor and RRHA. Its creation is a key condition for the Mayor's support, highlighting its perceived importance as a prerequisite for building trust and ensuring resident protection before redevelopment proceeds. RRHA has agreed to create one, but it remains a future promise born out of conflict.

Similarly, for Mosby Court, which is in the planning phase, the RRHA website lists the 'Tenant Bill of Rights' as 'coming soon.' This indicates a pattern where resident protection frameworks lag behind active redevelopment planning and relocation assessments, creating a period of uncertainty and anxiety for residents. The demand for these documents at Gilpin and Mosby is therefore not just about replicating Creighton's, but about establishing fundamental rights and accountability mechanisms at the outset of the process, a lesson learned from the perceived shortcomings and resident confusion in prior redevelopments.

# Stakeholder Demands And Conflicts

## Stakeholder

Mayor Danny Avula

## Position Or Demand

Demands joint governance of the Gilpin Court redevelopment, a one-for-one replacement of all 781 units, a robust Tenant Bill of Rights, and clear communication materials for residents before providing a required letter of support.

## Source Of Conflict

Distrust of RRHA's privatization-focused plan, concern over a shortfall of 160 units in the original proposal, and lessons learned from resident confusion during the Creighton Court redevelopment.

## Stakeholder

RRHA (Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority)

## Position Or Demand

Seeks to redevelop public housing communities, often with private partners. Defines the 'right to return' as conditional upon a resident being in 'good standing' with their lease. Has agreed to joint governance for Gilpin and to create a Tenant Bill of Rights.

## Source Of Conflict

Balancing financial and logistical redevelopment goals with public and political pressure for resident rights and accountability. The conditional nature of the 'right to return' creates uncertainty and is a point of friction with residents and advocates.

## Stakeholder

RRHA Residents (Gilpin, Creighton, Mosby)

## Position Or Demand

Demand clarity on the right to return, protection from landlord discrimination when using vouchers, fair billing practices, and a safe, non-retaliatory channel to voice concerns and influence decisions.

## Source Of Conflict

A history of low trust, fragmented communication, fear of retaliation for reporting issues, and administrative barriers that threaten their 'good standing' and housing stability. There is a fundamental power imbalance with the housing authority.

## Stakeholder

Gilpin Tenant Council

## Position Or Demand

To be a key partner in the creation of the Gilpin Court Tenant Bill of Rights, as stated in RRHA's plans.

## Source Of Conflict

Navigating the political conflict between the Mayor and RRHA while ensuring they are seen as the legitimate voice of the residents, especially as the Mayor calls for representation 'including but not limited to tenant council leaders'.

## Stakeholder

Advocacy Groups (e.g., HOME of VA)

## Position Or Demand

To represent and support residents facing housing discrimination, particularly related to 'source of funds' (vouchers), and to contest unfair practices.

## Source Of Conflict

Persistent and systemic housing discrimination by landlords in the Richmond area, which impedes the relocation process for displaced residents despite state-level legal protections.

# Data Privacy And Storytelling Risks

Residents face profound risks when sharing their personal stories for the displacement archive. There is a pervasive fear that any information disclosed—about housing conditions, household composition, or personal hardships—could be used against them by the housing authority. This could jeopardize their 'good standing' under their lease, which is a strict requirement for exercising their right to return. This fear is not unfounded, as it exists within a low-trust environment where residents feel vulnerable to retaliation, a concern validated by a Virginia state analysis on fair housing complaints. Consequently, without ironclad guarantees of data privacy, security for Personally Identifiable Information (PII), and a complete separation from lease enforcement, residents will be unwilling to participate, fearing their own stories could lead to losing their voucher eligibility or housing.

# Lessons From Prior Local Initiatives

Previous Richmond-based storytelling initiatives, such as the JXN Project, offer critical lessons for the displacement-memory-archive. The primary insight is that building trust is the most crucial prerequisite for ethical and effective community documentation. This involves creating genuinely safe, non-retaliatory channels where residents feel secure sharing their experiences without fear of reprisal. Furthermore, these projects demonstrate the power of connecting historical context to present-day struggles. For the archive to be meaningful, it must not only capture individual stories of displacement but also frame them within Richmond's broader history of housing discrimination and neighborhood devaluation, as highlighted in reports by organizations like HOME of VA. This approach ensures the archive serves not just as a repository of memory, but as a tool for advocacy and holding institutions accountable.
