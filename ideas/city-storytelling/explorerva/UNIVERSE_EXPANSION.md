# From Spoken Word to Civic Infrastructure: Scaling ExploreRVA's Living Narrative

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

ExploreRVA represents a rare "double-threat" in civic innovation: it addresses the functional failure of event discovery and the existential threat of losing community memory. While the hackathon demo was a spoken-word poem, the underlying logic aligns perfectly with the Mayor's "A City That Tells Its Stories" pillar and the regional push by PlanRVA to integrate culture into infrastructure [1]. To succeed, ExploreRVA must move beyond the traditional "app" concept—which faces a 97% failure rate in cultural contexts—and instead become a distributed digital layer for the city.

The universe for this idea expands significantly when framed as a regional tourism and humanities asset. By shifting from a Richmond-only tool to a Greater Richmond platform, it unlocks high-value funding from the Virginia Tourism Corporation (VTC) [2] and the National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH) [3]. The recommended path is a Hybrid Ownership Model: the City of Richmond provides the policy mandate and open data, while a nonprofit partner manages the sensitive work of oral history collection to ensure community trust and long-term archival stability.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Enabled / Nonprofit-Led / Regional-Scaled)**

* **What the City of Richmond Should Own**: The "Civic API." The city should provide the official data feeds for parks, permits, and public art, and integrate ExploreRVA into the Mayor's Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) reporting.
* **What a Nonprofit Partner Should Own**: The "Community Archive." An entity like CultureWorks or The Library of Virginia should manage the oral histories and community stories to ensure they are archived securely and maintain community trust.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned**: The mobile application/interface. Procurement and maintenance cycles for city software are too slow for a "live" event platform. This should be managed by a third-party vendor or a tech-focused nonprofit to avoid the "app graveyard."

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **StoryCorps "One Small Step" Richmond**: StoryCorps has successfully recorded over 500 conversations in Richmond, partnering with local institutions like the Library of Virginia [19] [22]. *Lesson*: The community is willing to share stories when facilitated by trusted, neutral platforms. ExploreRVA should act as a discovery layer for these existing archives rather than reinventing the recording wheel.
* **Library of Virginia "Virginia Untold"**: LVA received $175,392 to enhance this online project by digitizing historical registers [18]. *Lesson*: State institutions have the funding and mandate for long-term preservation. ExploreRVA should feed into these permanent archives.
* **VPM Artsline**: An existing events calendar for the region [21]. *Lesson*: Manual event entry is a bottleneck. ExploreRVA must aggregate data from sources like Artsline rather than competing with them.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **Richmond-Only Frame**: If limited to Richmond, the project risks becoming a "zombie app" competing for small local arts grants. It fails to capture the reality that residents and tourists cross municipal lines (e.g., Henrico, Chesterfield) for cultural experiences.
* **Regional Frame (Recommended)**: Expanding to Greater Richmond unlocks PlanRVA's infrastructure initiatives [1] and qualifies for the VTC Marketing Leverage Program, which requires regional DMO support [4]. It solves the discovery problem at the scale people actually live their lives.
* **Statewide Frame**: If successful, the underlying web-first, QR-triggered architecture could be white-labeled for other Virginia cities, attracting broader Virginia Commission for the Arts funding [23].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Decay (High Risk)**: The core problem identified in the demo—"ghosts from last May"—will kill the platform if it relies on manual data entry. It must use automated scrapers and API integrations.
* **Community Trust (High Risk)**: Marginalized residents may hesitate to share personal stories if the platform is strictly government-owned. Archival ownership must sit with a trusted nonprofit like LVA.
* **Procurement Lag (Moderate Risk)**: City IT procurement cycles are too slow for rapid civic tech deployment. A fiscal sponsor is required.
* **Maintenance Burden (High Risk)**: Custom native apps are expensive to maintain. The project must utilize low-code, web-first infrastructure (like ArcGIS StoryMaps) to survive long-term.
