# Executive Summary

The primary pain point for arts and culture event discovery in Richmond, VA, is severe fragmentation. Event information is scattered across multiple platforms, including Venture Richmond (downtown-only), VisitRichmond (tourism-focused), CultureWorks (arts-specific), and Style Weekly, each with its own submission process and editorial criteria. This leads to a lack of consistent and comprehensive data, creating significant work for users who must cross-reference sources to get a complete picture. Key data inconsistencies include the absence of standardized, event-level accessibility tags, forcing users to check venue-specific pages. This fragmentation creates significant equity concerns: it geographically excludes non-downtown neighborhoods like Church Hill, disproportionately burdens disabled residents trying to find accessible events, and disadvantages the approximately 9.7% of Richmond residents who lack reliable internet access and cannot easily navigate multiple online sources. Furthermore, the use of editorial discretion by platforms like VisitRichmond and Venture Richmond erodes trust, as users cannot be sure if they are seeing an authentic representation of the city's arts scene or a curated, commercially-driven selection.

# Resident Visitor Persona Profile

The 'resident/visitor' persona represents an individual in Richmond seeking to engage with the local arts and culture scene. Their functional job is defined by the need to efficiently plan their activities, as articulated in their Job To Be Done: “When I’m planning my weekend in Richmond and want to find arts events near Church Hill, I want one comprehensive, deduplicated list with filters for neighborhood, cost, accessibility, and family-friendliness, so I can quickly pick a few events and add them to my calendar.” This user is motivated by a desire for a single, reliable source of information that saves them time and effort, allowing them to easily discover events that match their specific criteria and integrate them into their personal schedule.

# Functional Pain Points

## Pain Point

Event sources are fragmented by publisher and geography, preventing a comprehensive view of all available events.

## Category

Information Fragmentation

## Evidence

Venture Richmond’s calendar exclusively lists events within the Downtown Service District, omitting events in other neighborhoods like Church Hill. Meanwhile, VisitRichmond's calendar is region-wide but uses editorial discretion to feature events that appeal to out-of-area visitors, potentially excluding more local or grassroots events. CultureWorks and Style Weekly operate their own separate calendars with unique submission processes, forcing users to check multiple sources to get a complete picture. Evidence is found in the submission guidelines: Venture Richmond states, “ONLY events taking place in Venture Richmond’s Downtown Service District will be accepted,” and VisitRichmond notes its calendar “features events that can attract out-of-area visitors… Non-visitor events… will not be included.”

## Pain Point

Filtering capabilities and event metadata are inconsistent across platforms, especially for critical information like accessibility.

## Category

Poor User Experience

## Evidence

While CultureWorks offers detailed filters for Region (e.g., RVA East End), Price, and Audience (including 'Wheelchair Accessible'), these are not universally available. VisitRichmond's main event finder lacks obvious neighborhood filters and handles accessibility through a separate guide to accessible attractions rather than as a per-event filter. This forces users with specific needs to cross-reference information from multiple pages (the event listing and a separate accessibility guide or the venue's own website), creating a significant barrier to planning.

## Pain Point

The existence of multiple, independent submission portals leads to a high likelihood of duplicate listings and out-of-sync event details.

## Category

Data Inconsistency

## Evidence

Organizations wanting to promote an event must submit it to several different platforms, including Venture Richmond, VisitRichmond, CultureWorks, and Style Weekly. Each has its own form and process. This decentralized system increases the risk of duplicate entries appearing across different calendars and makes it difficult to ensure that details like time, date, or location remain consistent if an event is updated. The evidence is the existence of these separate submission pages and their distinct criteria.

## Pain Point

Users are forced to rely on a patchwork of social media feeds and email newsletters as a workaround, indicating the absence of a single, trusted source.

## Category

Information Fragmentation

## Evidence

Community discussions reveal that Richmond locals do not rely on a single website for event discovery. Instead, they employ a multi-source strategy, subscribing to various newsletters (like Venture Richmond's) and following different social media accounts to find out about 'cool things' happening in the city. This behavior demonstrates that no single existing calendar is perceived as comprehensive or authoritative enough to be the sole source of information.


# Current Workarounds And Status Quo Cost

Currently, users looking for arts events in Richmond must employ a time-consuming, multi-platform strategy. This workaround involves bouncing between several websites, each with a different focus: Venture Richmond for downtown events, VisitRichmond for visitor-focused regional events, CultureWorks for arts-specific listings, and Style Weekly for its editorially curated calendar. This is often supplemented by monitoring social media feeds like Instagram, browsing community forums like Reddit, and subscribing to various newsletters. Once a potential event is found, users may still need to visit the specific venue's website to confirm details or check for accessibility information. After confirming, they must manually add events to their personal calendars, as not all sources offer a simple export function.

The cost of this status quo is significant. Functionally, it results in a great deal of time lost triangulating information and cross-checking details. Users experience frustration and run the risk of missing out on hyperlocal events not covered by downtown-centric or visitor-focused calendars. More critically, there are significant equity costs. The inconsistent and scattered nature of accessibility information creates barriers for disabled attendees. The geographic bias of some calendars means residents in neighborhoods like Church Hill are underserved. Finally, the reliance on navigating multiple websites and social channels disadvantages the approximately 9.7% of Richmond residents who lack a reliable internet subscription, effectively excluding them from a key aspect of the city's cultural life.

# Evaluation Of Platform Filter Capabilities

## Most Comprehensive Platform

CultureWorks

## Common Filter Gaps

Key filters are inconsistently implemented across platforms. Neighborhood-level filtering is a significant gap; for example, Venture Richmond's calendar is restricted to the Downtown Service District, excluding other areas like Church Hill, and VisitRichmond's main tool lacks obvious neighborhood filters. This forces users to search multiple sources to find events in specific parts of the city. Other desired filters like cost and family-friendliness are not universally available, contributing to a fragmented user experience.

## Accessibility Filter Analysis

The availability and standardization of accessibility-related filters are poor across Richmond's event platforms. While CultureWorks is an exception, offering a 'Wheelchair Accessible' filter, this is not a universal standard. Other platforms like VisitRichmond address accessibility through a separate, static guide to accessible attractions rather than integrating filterable, per-event metadata. Information is often scattered on individual venue websites (e.g., VMFA), requiring users to perform extra research. This lack of a unified, event-level tagging system creates a significant barrier for disabled attendees trying to find accessible events.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis Summary

The current system for discovering arts events in Richmond presents significant equity challenges, disproportionately affecting several key populations. Residents in non-downtown neighborhoods like the East End/Church Hill are at risk of exclusion due to the narrow geographic focus of prominent calendars like Venture Richmond's. Disabled attendees face a high burden as accessibility information is inconsistent, rarely tagged at the event level, and scattered across separate venue pages or general guides. The reliance on navigating multiple websites and social feeds disadvantages the approximately 9.7% of Richmond residents who lack an internet subscription, compounding their exclusion. Furthermore, small or under-resourced arts organizations may be underrepresented if they lack the capacity to meet varying submission criteria or provide high-quality images, skewing discovery towards more established, well-funded entities.

# Accessibility Information Gaps

## Gap Description

There is a significant gap in accessibility information, characterized by a lack of standardized, mandatory, and event-level accessibility tags across Richmond's various event calendars. Information is often scattered, requiring users to look for separate accessibility guides or individual venue pages rather than finding it directly within the event listing.

## Impact On Users

This information gap disproportionately burdens users with disabilities. It forces them to perform extra labor, cross-referencing event listings with separate venue or regional accessibility guides to determine if they can attend. This creates uncertainty and a significant risk of arriving at an event only to find it inaccessible, effectively excluding them from participation.

## Best Practice Example

The CultureWorks events calendar serves as a positive example by offering a 'Wheelchair Accessible' filter directly within its search function, allowing users to easily find events that meet their accessibility needs without leaving the platform.
