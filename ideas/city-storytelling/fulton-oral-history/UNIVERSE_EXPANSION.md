# From Fulton to the Commonwealth: Scaling Richmond's Oral-History Map into a Multi-Level Heritage Platform

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Fulton Oral History Interactive Map has the potential to evolve from a localized, single-neighborhood hackathon prototype into a robust, multi-jurisdictional heritage platform. By shifting the frame from a purely city-owned civic tech tool to a hybrid-governed digital humanities infrastructure, Richmond can tap into a surging national funding landscape for community-centered digital heritage. Federal programs, such as the National Park Service's African American Civil Rights Grant Program, and national philanthropic initiatives like the Mellon Foundation's Monuments Project, are actively funding projects that document and preserve marginalized histories. 

To realize this potential, the project must transcend the traditional municipal boundaries. Partnering with regional planning bodies like PlanRVA and academic powerhouses like the University of Richmond's Digital Scholarship Lab can provide the technical scaffolding and regional context necessary for scale. Ultimately, the map's success hinges on a hybrid ownership model where the City of Richmond provides data infrastructure and pilot funding, while a descendant-led nonprofit coalition governs the narrative, ensuring community trust and long-term sustainability.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Partnership with Descendant Governance**

A pure city-ownership model risks community skepticism, especially given the historical context of the City's role in the urban renewal policies that displaced Fulton residents. Conversely, a purely nonprofit-led model risks technical decay and lack of integration with broader civic data ecosystems. Therefore, a hybrid model is essential.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond should own the underlying geospatial infrastructure, API integrations with municipal open data portals, and the physical permitting for QR code signage in public rights-of-way. The City should act as a technical enabler and initial pilot funder.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A coalition of local historical nonprofits (e.g., The Valentine, Virginia Museum of History & Culture) and academic libraries (VCU Libraries, University of Richmond) should own the archival storage, metadata standardization, and copyright management. 
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The narrative interpretation, community outreach, and oral history collection processes must not be city-owned. These should be governed by a "Fulton Heritage Council" composed of descendants and community members to ensure authenticity and trust.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a "Richmond project" limits its potential. Urban renewal and the displacement of Black neighborhoods was a national phenomenon driven by federal policy (1949 Housing Act). 
* **Regional Shift:** By partnering with PlanRVA, the map can expand to include stories from Henrico and Chesterfield counties, tracking where displaced Fulton residents actually moved, thus mapping the regional impact of city policies [20].
* **State/National Shift:** Integrating with the UR Digital Scholarship Lab's "American Panorama" allows the Fulton story to serve as a qualitative case study within a massive national dataset of redlining and urban renewal [13] [21].
* **Governance Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data, the project gains immunity from municipal budget cycles and political administration changes, ensuring long-term survival.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Copyright and Legal Authority:** The Valentine holds copyrights to many Fulton transcripts. Embedding these without clear licensing agreements poses a severe legal risk.
* **Data-Sharing and Technical Integration:** Relying on VCU Libraries' Scholars Compass API requires sustained technical maintenance. If the API changes, the map breaks.
* **Trust and Community Legitimacy:** The City of Richmond was the agent of displacement in Fulton. City ownership of the narrative risks being perceived as co-optation.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Standalone civic apps frequently suffer from link rot and abandonment after initial hackathon enthusiasm fades.
