# Analysis Introduction

This Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) analysis is for the "fulton-oral-history" project in Richmond, Virginia. The initiative is aligned with the city's official strategic pillar of being a "City That Tells Its Stories" and aims to solve the problem of using resident stories as a form of civic insight. The project's objective is to develop an oral history collection tool dedicated to preserving the community narratives of residents from the Fulton neighborhood, a community profoundly shaped by urban renewal and displacement.

# Functional Jtbd

## Situation

When community memory-holders are willing to share stories about Historic Fulton and its displacement,

## Persona

community organizer or local historian/librarian in Richmond

## Motivation

capture, describe, and safely preserve those oral histories in accessible formats,

## Outcome

ensure residents’ lived experiences inform public understanding, education, and neighborhood planning.

## Current Workaround

Ad hoc recordings at reunions/churches, scattered uploads to YouTube/Google Drive, and referrals to VCU/Valentine projects; limited metadata and long-term preservation.

## Core Pain

Fragmented storage, inconsistent consent/metadata, risk of loss, and difficulty finding and reusing stories for civic processes.


# Emotional Jtbd

## Situation

When asked to share my story about Fulton’s past and the urban renewal that displaced my family,

## Persona

Fulton resident/elder or descendant

## Motivation

know my voice will be respected, context will be truthful, and I can control how my story is used,

## Outcome

contribute without fear of exploitation or retraumatization and see my experience meaningfully shape decisions.

## Current Workaround

Sharing only within trusted community spaces (churches, reunions, small-group interviews) or with known local institutions (VCU/Valentine), often declining city-led requests.

## Core Pain

Historical distrust due to past harms (RRHA-led clearance, broken promises), uncertainty about ownership, privacy, compensation, and whether stories will actually influence city action.


# Systems Jtbd

## Situation

When shaping policies and projects that affect Fulton (e.g., planning, housing, memorialization),

## Persona

City of Richmond engagement/planning staff

## Motivation

a trustworthy, searchable pipeline of resident oral histories with consent for civic use,

## Outcome

ground decisions in lived experience, honor the pillar of telling the truth, and coordinate across agencies and partners (RRHA, VCU, Valentine, Library of Virginia).

## Current Workaround

One-off listening sessions, text-heavy public comments, sporadic use of archived interviews not integrated into city workflows.

## Core Pain

No common repository or standards for discovery/usage rights, limited audio/video integration into planning processes, and difficulty surfacing stories equitably (not just the most connected voices).


# Data Questions

## Question Text

What descriptive and rights metadata standard will we use (e.g., Dublin Core + local fields for displacement context, interviewer, location, consent scope), and can it align with VCU Libraries/Valentine practices for interoperability? How will consent be captured (tiered/public, time-bound, withdrawal, sensitive-topics flags), and how will FOIA in Virginia impact access to recordings stored on city infrastructure? What are our preservation and redundancy plans (formats like WAV/FLAC masters + MP3/MP4 derivatives, checksums, cloud + local, deposit with VCU/Valentine/Library of Virginia)? How will we geolocate or otherwise situate stories when original streets/structures were demolished or renamed in Historic Fulton? Can we tag stories with policy-relevant themes (housing, schools, churches, displacement dates, RRHA interactions) without distorting narratives?


# User Questions

## Question Text

What support do Fulton elders need to participate (phone hotlines, in-home/onsite recording, transportation, stipends, trauma-informed facilitation)? What multilingual or accessibility accommodations are needed (captions/transcripts, large-print, screen-reader compatibility, TTY relay)? How will we recruit beyond well-connected networks (church rosters, school alumni, the Historic Fulton Reunion, Neighborhood Resource Center) to reach quieter or displaced voices living outside Richmond? What feedback loop will show residents how their stories influenced a specific decision (e.g., citations in staff reports, StoryMap embeds in Council presentations)?


# Integration Questions

## Question Text

Which partners will hold long-term stewardship: VCU Libraries (Scholars Compass), The Valentine, Library of Virginia, and how will deposits/ingest be automated from the tool? How will the city surface stories in public engagement tools (RVA.gov pages, ArcGIS StoryMaps, planning portals) with clear rights/attribution and without tokenizing content? Can we cross-link to existing Historic Fulton interviews in VCU/Encyclopedia Virginia so users see prior testimonies alongside new ones? What internal city workflows (planning, housing, arts/culture) will regularly consume and reference stories, and who owns maintenance?


# Equity Questions

## Question Text

What governance model ensures community control (advisory board of Fulton residents/descendants) over policies like access tiers, takedown requests, and use in contentious projects? How will we compensate interviewees and community interviewers fairly and transparently? How do we prevent harm when stories reference traumatic events or individuals (content warnings, anonymization options, redaction workflows)? How will we avoid privileging polished narratives over quiet voices—e.g., allowing low-bandwidth phone submissions, text-to-voice, or facilitated sessions?


# Prior Art Questions

## Question Text

How will we build on VCU’s Historic Fulton Oral History Project (2011–2012) and related Encyclopedia Virginia excerpts to avoid duplication and provide continuity (shared identifiers, cross-collection search)? What lessons from Richmond’s prior urban-renewal documentation (Jackson Ward, 17th Street, Carver) inform Fulton tagging, timelines, and context panels? Which local exhibits/initiatives (Valentine programs, Library of Virginia’s Virginia Memory, VPM history series) can host or amplify outputs under the city’s “tells its stories” pillar?
