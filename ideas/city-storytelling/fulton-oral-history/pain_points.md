# Project Background

The 'fulton-oral-history' project is a civic technology initiative in Richmond, Virginia, aligned with the city's strategic pillar of being a 'City That Tells Its Stories.' Its primary goal is to address the challenge of using residents' personal narratives as a valid and influential form of civic insight, particularly for public understanding and urban planning. The project focuses specifically on the Fulton neighborhood, a historically Black community that was largely demolished during the urban renewal era. According to firsthand accounts and historical records, this process involved the razing of approximately 800 homes and businesses, displacing a significant population under promises of redevelopment that took decades to materialize or never came to fruition. The project seeks to create and manage a collection of oral histories from former and current residents, elders, and their descendants to preserve this community memory. The existing collection, the Historic Fulton Oral History Project, exemplifies the complexities the new initiative must navigate: its materials are physically held by The Valentine museum, which also retains copyright, while digital streaming access is hosted by Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU) Libraries, creating a fragmented system for access and reuse.

# Summary Of Key Pain Points

The primary pain points for the Fulton oral history project stem from a fundamental conflict between the functional needs of memory workers and the deep, historically-rooted emotional needs of the community storytellers. For community organizers and historians, the key pains are operational and logistical. They face a fragmented and inefficient system where story stewardship is split across institutions (e.g., The Valentine holding copyright while VCU hosts access), making permissions and reuse a slow, high-risk process. The stories that do exist often lack consistent, detailed metadata, hindering their discovery and use in civic processes. Furthermore, reliance on ad hoc, non-archival storage like YouTube and Google Drive creates a significant risk of permanent loss due to link rot and content drift. For Fulton residents, elders, and their descendants, the pain is emotional and existential, rooted in historical trauma. There is profound distrust of city-affiliated projects due to the legacy of urban renewal, where the city labeled their neighborhood a 'slum,' razed it, and broke promises of return and redevelopment. This leads to a fear of their stories being controlled, decontextualized, or used in an extractive manner without their consent. They are skeptical that sharing painful memories will lead to any meaningful civic action, fearing retraumatization with no tangible benefit. Systemically, these issues converge into a crisis of legitimacy. The project is hampered by the absence of community-controlled governance, a lack of culturally competent and anti-racist descriptive practices, and no clear, established pathway for these oral histories to actually influence city planning and policy. The status quo results in the silencing of key memories, the underutilization of existing stories, and the perpetuation of community distrust.

# Organizer And Historian Pain Points

## Pain Point Category

Governance

## Description

Fragmented stewardship and unclear rights chains across institutions make the reuse of oral histories slow and risky.

## Evidence

The Historic Fulton Oral History Project materials exemplify this issue. The Valentine museum retains the original materials, administrative files, and copyright, while Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU) hosts streaming access. All requests to publish materials must be directed to The Valentine, creating a multi-step, non-trivial coordination process when content and authority are split between institutions.

## Impact

This fragmented authority leads to significant delays in getting clearance for reuse, making the process slow and risky for organizers who wish to incorporate these stories into new works or civic processes.

## Pain Point Category

Metadata

## Description

While stories exist, the discovery and metadata for civic reuse are inconsistent and often insufficient for practical application.

## Evidence

The VCU finding aid for the Fulton project describes 17 interviews with 32 named participants and provides only a basic scope. This level of detail falls short of oral history best practices, which call for detailed descriptions, time-based tags, and a focus on diverse voices to improve access. Many community projects lack this level of indexing.

## Impact

The lack of detailed, topic-based metadata hinders the use of these stories by city planners and researchers who need to find relevant information efficiently.

## Pain Point Category

Preservation

## Description

The use of ad hoc storage solutions like YouTube and Google Drive for oral histories creates significant risks of data loss, link rot, and privacy exposure.

## Evidence

General research on web content durability shows that 25% of web pages from 2013–2023 have vanished. Scholarly research confirms widespread 'link rot' and 'content drift', where links break or content changes over time. Storing valuable community histories on these non-archival platforms puts the materials at high risk of being permanently lost.

## Impact

There is a high risk of permanent loss of invaluable community stories due to platform instability, account loss, or broken links. It also exposes participants to potential privacy violations not covered by formal archival agreements.

## Pain Point Category

Governance

## Description

A lack of community-controlled consent and usage governance frameworks reduces participation from community members and limits the reusability of the collected stories.

## Evidence

The Oral History Association (OHA) emphasizes the importance of informed consent, enforceable agreements, and capable repositories. Tools like Local Contexts TK Labels exist to provide community-specific rules for access and future use, but these are rarely implemented in civic workflows.

## Impact

Without clear, community-governed consent models, potential narrators may be hesitant to participate, and the resulting stories may have usage restrictions that limit their value for civic engagement and planning.

## Pain Point Category

Equity

## Description

Harmful or incomplete descriptive practices in archival metadata can alienate Black communities and impede equitable search and discovery.

## Evidence

The 'Archives for Black Lives in Philadelphia' (A4BLiP) project documents the harms of anti-Black archival description and offers corrective practices. Mischaracterizing or poorly describing stories from Black communities reduces trust and makes the materials less discoverable for those who would benefit most from them.

## Impact

These practices alienate the very communities the project aims to serve, reduce trust in the archival process, and create barriers to equitable access and discovery, limiting the policy relevance of the collection.


# Organizer And Historian Workarounds

Currently, community organizers and historians employ a series of ad-hoc workarounds to manage oral histories. These include relying on personal networks to request copies and permissions from institutions, maintaining manual logs and spreadsheets for tracking, and using non-archival platforms like YouTube and Google Drive folders with basic titles for storage and access. They also often borrow generic consent templates. This status quo exists because key systems are broken: dispersed custody of materials and rights (e.g., between The Valentine and VCU) slows down permissions; inconsistent metadata prevents effective topic-based retrieval for planners; ad-hoc uploads are prone to disappearing due to link rot; there is no mechanism for granular consent for sensitive topics; and existing archival descriptions can reproduce bias. The costs of this situation are substantial, including weeks-to-months of delays for clearance, the permanent loss of recordings to link rot or account deletion, the underutilization of stories in planning and education, the potential retraumatization or withdrawal of narrators, and an inequitable archival representation that ultimately limits the policy relevance of the collected histories.

# Resident And Descendant Pain Points

## Pain Point Category

Trust

## Description

A profound and historical distrust of city-led initiatives, stemming from a history of broken promises related to urban renewal. Residents recall that 'promises were made that we could come back together but those promises never came to fruition,' leading to a deep-seated reluctance to engage with or trust new projects initiated by the city.

## Root Cause

The large-scale displacement caused by an urban renewal plan that razed approximately 800 homes and businesses in the Fulton neighborhood. The Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) ultimately demolished the entire neighborhood, and the promised new housing took over 30 years to materialize, creating a legacy of unfulfilled commitments.

## Pain Point Category

Dignity

## Description

A reluctance to share stories due to the persistent and stigmatizing official narratives that have historically framed the community in a negative light. City officials admitted to neglecting the area, such as by failing to enforce building codes, which exacerbated poor housing conditions and reinforced the harmful external perception of the neighborhood.

## Root Cause

A 1967 survey by the RRHA officially labeled Fulton 'a slum, the worst in Richmond.' This designation was used as the primary justification for the subsequent urban 'renewal' efforts that led to the community's destruction, creating a painful and alienating narrative for residents.

## Pain Point Category

Ownership

## Description

A significant fear of losing control over their personal stories, including their voice, the context in which their narrative is presented, and how it may be used in the future. This concern is heightened by the common absence of clear, community-governed consent agreements and usage restrictions in civic projects.

## Root Cause

The general lack of implementation of archival best practices, such as those from the Oral History Association (OHA) and tools like Local Contexts TK Labels, which are designed to ensure informed consent and give communities control over access and future use. Without these mechanisms, narrators are vulnerable to misuse of their stories.

## Pain Point Category

Emotional Safety

## Description

The risk of experiencing trauma or being retraumatized when asked to recount painful memories of displacement and community loss, especially when stories are solicited without sufficient care, community collaboration, or safeguards against biased or harmful contextualization.

## Root Cause

The failure of projects to follow best practices outlined by the OHA and anti-racist archival guidance, which emphasize the need for careful planning with communities, honoring narrator-stipulated restrictions, and addressing harms from biased descriptions to avoid causing further emotional distress.

## Pain Point Category

Impact

## Description

Pervasive skepticism that sharing their personal and community stories will have any real influence on current or future city decisions. This doubt is fueled by past experiences and reinforced by similar outcomes in other cities.

## Root Cause

A history of broken promises in Richmond, where resident input and community integrity were ultimately ignored. This local experience is echoed by regional precedents, such as in Durham's Hayti neighborhood, where promises of redevelopment after displacement 'never came to realization,' fueling a broader distrust that storytelling initiatives are performative rather than impactful.


# Resident And Descendant Workarounds

Due to a deep-seated distrust of city-led initiatives, which are often perceived as extractive, residents and descendants of Fulton have developed specific workarounds for sharing their stories. They primarily confine their storytelling to trusted, private community spaces such as churches and family reunions. When they do engage with formal institutions, they limit it to known local entities like The Valentine museum or Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU), with whom some level of relationship may exist. A common response to requests from city-affiliated entities is to decline participation altogether. Some also insist on retaining physical custody of copies of their recordings to maintain a degree of control. The cost of this status quo is significant: key memories are silenced and remain outside the public record, participation becomes uneven which skews the historical narrative, and valuable opportunities to inform reparative policy and planning are missed, which in turn perpetuates the cycle of distrust between the community and the city.

# Historical Context Of Distrust

## Key Actor

Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA)

## Historical Event

The 1970s urban renewal project in the Fulton neighborhood, which was primed by a 1967 RRHA survey that labeled the area 'a slum, the worst in Richmond.'

## Community Impact

The demolition of approximately 800 homes and businesses, effectively razing the entire neighborhood through the use of eminent domain and displacing its residents.

## Broken Promises

Promises made to residents that they could return to the neighborhood 'never came to fruition.' Funding for the project dried up, and the development of new housing was delayed for more than 30 years.


# Analysis Of Existing Oral History Collections

## Collection Name

Historic Fulton Oral History Project

## Managing Institutions

The Valentine and Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU) Libraries. The Valentine retains the original materials, administrative files, and copyright, while VCU Libraries hosts the streaming digital collection for public access.

## Scope And Content

The collection contains 17 interviews with 32 named participants, providing firsthand accounts of the impact of Richmond's urban renewal plan on the Fulton community.

## Access And Copyright

The collection is accessible via a streaming digital platform hosted by VCU Libraries. The copyright for all interviews was donated by the interviewees to The Valentine without restriction. Consequently, any requests to publish materials from the project must be directed to The Valentine.


# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Most Affected Groups

The groups most negatively impacted are Black Fulton elders and their descendants, whose stories require careful consent processes and culturally competent description to avoid harm. Additionally, small grassroots groups that lack the archival capacity and resources of larger institutions are also significantly affected.

## Excluded Groups

Groups excluded from participating include residents who lack broadband internet access or the technical support needed to engage with digital platforms. Furthermore, individuals with deep-seated distrust, privacy concerns, or safety fears are excluded, as they actively avoid sharing their stories on platforms that are not governed by their own community.

## Nature Of Harm

The harm is systemic and rooted in historical injustices. The Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority's (RRHA) urban renewal plan, which led to the demolition of the Fulton neighborhood and the razing of approximately 800 homes and businesses, created a foundation of distrust. This was compounded by the city's official neglect, stigmatizing labels such as calling Fulton 'a slum, the worst in Richmond,' and a history of broken promises for redevelopment that took over 30 years to materialize. This legacy of harm creates a 'durable distrust' that depresses participation in current civic storytelling projects and elevates the risk of these projects being extractive or causing retraumatization.


# Best Practices For Ethical Oral History

## Source Organization

Oral History Association (OHA)

## Informed Consent Protocols

The process should involve obtaining and documenting the informed consent of the narrator. Whenever possible, the narrator should be given an opportunity to review and approve the oral history before it is made public. This is highlighted as a crucial step to build trust and ensure narrators have control over their stories, addressing fears of misuse.

## Ownership And Use Agreements

Best practices emphasize the need for clear, enforceable agreements regarding ownership and use. For example, the Historic Fulton Oral History Project materials note that interviewees donated their copyright to The Valentine, which handles all publication requests. The OHA stresses that repositories must be able to enforce these signed agreements, which is a key concern for residents worried about losing control over their voice and context.

## Ethical Access And Preservation

Oral historians are advised to select an appropriate repository capable of long-term preservation and enforcing prior agreements, including any restrictions on access and distribution methods. The repository should honor all stipulations made with the narrator. This practice directly contrasts with the identified pain point of using ad hoc, risky storage solutions like YouTube or Google Drive, which suffer from link rot and lack formal access controls.


# Trauma Informed And Anti Racist Approaches

## Approach Name

Anti-Racist Archival Description

## Source Or Example

Archives for Black Lives in Philadelphia (A4BLiP)

## Core Principle

To combat racist structures inherent in archival description and correct harmful or incomplete metadata practices. This involves creating culturally competent descriptions to avoid alienating or misrepresenting marginalized communities, thereby improving trust, discoverability, and equitable representation.


# Solutions For Community Data Sovereignty

## Tool Or Framework

Local Contexts Traditional Knowledge (TK) Labels

## Purpose

To empower communities to manage their own digital heritage by providing a mechanism for community-specific control over their stories and data. The labels aim to address the lack of community-controlled governance over consent and usage, which can reduce participation and reusability.

## Key Functionality

The labels identify and clarify community-specific rules and responsibilities regarding access and future use of traditional knowledge. This allows for granular, community-defined usage protocols that can be attached to digital records, ensuring that the community's wishes for how their stories are used are clear and respected by institutions and researchers.


# Risks Of Current Preservation Workarounds

## Platform

YouTube / Google Drive

## Risk Type

Link Rot, Content Drift, and Data Loss

## Evidence

Research demonstrates significant digital fragility, underscoring the risks of using non-archival platforms. A study cited from BBC Future shows that 25% of web pages posted between 2013 and 2023 have vanished. Furthermore, scholarly material suffers from 'link rot,' with one in five articles experiencing 'reference rot.' A later study found that the content of three out of four references changed over time ('content drift'). Over a quarter of articles analyzed were not preserved in a formal archive, placing them at high risk for permanent loss.
