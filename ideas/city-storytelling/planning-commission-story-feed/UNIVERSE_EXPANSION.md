# “From Minutes to Neighborhood Stories: Scaling Richmond’s Planning‑Commission Feed Across Civic‑Tech Ecosystems”

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The "Planning Commission Story Feed" has the potential to transcend a simple municipal IT project and become a cornerstone of regional civic infrastructure. By shifting the frame from a city-owned website feature to a hybrid civic-journalism and open-data initiative, the universe of this idea expands dramatically. A hybrid model—where the City of Richmond provides structured data via APIs and a local nonprofit manages the resident-facing editorial layer—unlocks significant philanthropic funding from journalism and democracy funders that municipalities cannot access directly. 

Furthermore, framing this as a regional geospatial storytelling tool aligns perfectly with existing federal and state equity mandates. Programs like the USDOT Thriving Communities initiative and Virginia DHCD Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) actively fund community engagement and planning capacity. By partnering with regional bodies like PlanRVA and civic tech networks, Richmond can pilot a scalable model that translates dense legal proceedings into actionable neighborhood narratives, setting a new standard for participatory urban planning.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

To maximize trust, funding eligibility, and operational sustainability, the Story Feed should operate under a hybrid ownership model. 

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond must own the data ingestion pipeline, the Legistar API integration [1] [2], and the initial legal QA of any AI-generated summaries. The city's role is to be the authoritative, neutral provider of structured civic data.
* **What partners should own:** A Richmond-area nonprofit (or a coalition of local media and civic groups) should own the subscriber-facing front-end, the community note-taker program (e.g., a local Documenters chapter), and the editorial distribution. This ensures editorial independence, which is a strict requirement for major journalism funders like Press Forward [3] [4].
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The City should not own the final narrative distribution channels or the community note-taking apparatus. If the city owns the narrative, it risks being perceived as public relations rather than transparent civic journalism, and it immediately disqualifies the project from millions in philanthropic civic-media funding.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **City Bureau Documenters:** Trains citizens to take notes at public meetings [17]. *What worked:* Highly readable, community-focused notes that capture public comment better than official minutes. *Lesson:* Human oversight and community involvement are critical for trust and capturing the true narrative of a meeting.
* **Aware & Hamlet GovCenter:** AI platforms that process meeting videos and agendas into plain-language summaries [24] [23] [25]. *What worked:* Rapid processing of dense materials into accessible formats. *Lesson:* AI can handle the heavy lifting of summarization, but requires strict guardrails to prevent hallucinations of legal zoning terms.
* **PlanRVA Public Engagement Plan:** A regional blueprint to gather public comments on planning processes [15] [16]. *What worked:* Established funding frameworks for regional engagement [26]. *Lesson:* Richmond does not have to fund this alone; regional planning bodies already have mandates and budgets for this exact type of public outreach.
* **Bloomberg What Works Cities:** Recognizes local governments for exceptional use of data to inform policy [27] [28]. *What worked:* Leveraging data to drive innovation and respond to community challenges [29]. *Lesson:* Framing the feed as a data-driven governance initiative can attract national recognition and support from the City Data Alliance [30].

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond City IT project severely limits its potential. If the frame shifts to a **regional** model via PlanRVA, the project can tap into regional transportation and housing planning funds [16] [26], ensuring that residents understand how zoning in Richmond affects transit in Henrico. If a **nonprofit** owns the resident-facing layer, the project suddenly becomes eligible for millions in philanthropic journalism funding (like Press Forward) [10] [4] that the city cannot touch. If the city acts merely as the data provider (piloting the Legistar API extraction) [1] [31], the model can be easily replicated by other Virginia municipalities using the same legislative management software, creating a statewide standard for civic transparency.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data-Sharing & Technical Integration:** Accessing the Legistar API [1] requires technical coordination and potentially navigating state Open Government Act constraints regarding automated scraping versus official API access.
* **Accuracy & Legal Authority:** AI summarizers can hallucinate legal terms. The official minutes must remain the legal record [32] [33]. A strict dual-signoff workflow (AI draft → legal clerk verification → community editor) is mandatory to mitigate liability.
* **Grant Dependence & Maintenance:** Relying solely on grants like CDBG [5] or Press Forward [34] creates a fiscal cliff. The project must prove its value in reducing municipal staff time spent answering basic zoning queries to justify long-term operational funding.
* **Community Legitimacy:** If the feed is perceived as city propaganda, residents will ignore it. Independent editorial control via a nonprofit partner is the primary mitigation for this risk.
