# Project Analysis Summary

The planning-commission-story-feed project in Richmond, VA, addresses critical pain points across the urban planning ecosystem by bridging the gap between resident experiences and formal decision-making. An analysis reveals three primary stakeholder needs or 'Jobs To Be Done'. First, for city planners and staff report authors, the project serves a functional job by aiming to solve the inefficiency and fragmentation of sourcing resident input. Currently, planners must manually sift through scattered sources like Legistar attachments, emails, and social media, with no systematic way to verify or contextualize these stories, leading to a high risk of missing key perspectives, especially from equity-deserving communities. Second, for Richmond residents, the project addresses an emotional and trust-based job. The current process is perceived as intimidating, jargon-heavy, and opaque, leaving residents unsure how to submit feedback effectively and whether their input is actually seen or considered before deliberations. This creates a trust deficit and discourages participation. The project aims to provide a clear, trusted, and transparent channel where residents feel their lived experiences are heard and valued. Third, for the broader planning system, the project fulfills a systems-coordination job. It seeks to create a structured, machine-readable feed of community narratives tied to specific case and parcel metadata. This would replace the current ad-hoc, non-reusable data collection methods, enabling better institutional memory, pattern analysis across cases, and more robust equity analysis in line with the city's stated goals.

# City Planner Functional Job

## Persona

City planner/staff report author

## Motivation

To quickly surface verified resident stories tied to the affected parcels and neighborhoods when a rezoning case is docketed for a Planning Commission meeting.

## Desired Outcome

To produce a concise 'Community Voices' brief that complements the official staff report and aligns with strategic city plans like the Richmond 300 and the ongoing Code Refresh.

## Pain Points

1) Resident input is scattered across multiple systems and difficult to surface before hearings; the Planning Commission uses Legistar for official documents, but there is no unified synthesis of pre-hearing stories. 2) Verifying and contextualizing resident statements is a time-consuming manual process, as staff reports are structured to focus on policy consistency and case facts, not narrative validation. 3) Written comments have limited discoverability as they are often embedded within large PDF agenda packets or exist as separate links in Legistar, rather than being summarized. 4) The workflow is fragmented across different platforms, including the city's website (RVA.gov) for general information, Legistar for agendas and documents, and Microsoft Teams for meeting access, with no integrated feed for resident stories.

## Current Workarounds

Planners must manually scan Legistar attachments, search through past emails sent to the City Clerk or Council offices, rely on ad hoc notes from community meetings, monitor scattered social media posts and listservs, and occasionally make direct calls to neighborhood association leaders.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The current process results in significant costs, including wasted staff time on manual data collection, a high risk of missing input from equity-deserving communities, the production of weaker staff reports that lack the crucial context of lived experiences, and an erosion of trust between the city and its communities.


# Resident Emotional Trust Job

## Persona

Richmond resident

## Motivation

A trusted way to submit my lived experience and see that it appears in Planning Commission prep materials.

## Desired Outcome

To feel heard, safer participating, and confident my input matters.

## Pain Points

Unclear how/where to submit comments to reach the Planning Commission prior to deliberation; Intimidating, jargon-heavy process and timelines; Written comments get buried in lengthy agenda packets with a weak feedback loop; Perceived lack of representation in zoning/code refresh deliberations.

## Current Workarounds

Signing up to speak at public hearings (either in-person or via Teams), emailing the City Clerk for inclusion in Legistar, or posting on social media and neighborhood listservs.

## Cost Of Status Quo

Lower participation from less-connected residents, eroded trust in the process, and a disproportionate influence of well-resourced voices who can navigate the complex system.


# Planning System Coordination Job

## Job Summary

The primary coordination job for the planning system is to create a structured, machine-readable feed of resident narratives that are explicitly linked to specific rezoning case data and parcel metadata. This would enable better alignment between departments, facilitate systematic equity analysis across different cases, and preserve institutional memory of community feedback.

## System Failures

The current fragmented process fails to meet this coordination need in several ways. There is no shared schema or standardized format for capturing resident stories, leading to inconsistent and hard-to-analyze data. Feedback is often aggregated too late in the process to meaningfully inform staff analysis. The reliance on multiple, disconnected platforms (RVA.gov, Legistar, Teams) creates failure points and prevents a holistic view of public input. This disjointedness limits the ability to analyze public sentiment patterns across multiple cases over time.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The negative impacts of the current system are significant. It leads to a loss of valuable institutional memory, as community feedback is not stored in a reusable or analyzable format. The city is unable to perform systematic equity analysis across multiple rezonings to ensure fair outcomes. This dysfunction creates higher friction and a greater cognitive load for planning commissioners, who must piece together information from disparate sources. Ultimately, it risks under-weighting the voices from 'Priority Neighborhoods' identified in the Richmond 300 plan.


# Cross Cutting Equity Dimensions

## Affected Groups

The communities most affected and excluded are residents in historically overlooked communities, particularly those in 'Priority Neighborhoods' and areas with high social vulnerability as identified by the Richmond 300 plan's Climate Equity Index. These are often the residents most impacted by land-use and rezoning decisions but are least likely to have their voices heard in the early stages of the planning process.

## Evidence Of Exclusion

Specific evidence includes documented complaints about underrepresentation on the Zoning Advisory Council (ZAC), which led to its expansion. Before the expansion, there were significant geographic gaps, with no members from Districts 4 and 7, and limited representation from Districts 2, 8, and 9. This, combined with a 'perceived development-tilt,' highlights a structural exclusion. Furthermore, the Richmond 300 plan's call for a 'planning knowledge program' to reduce barriers for 'everyday Richmonders' implicitly acknowledges that the current jargon-heavy and complex process is exclusionary. The lack of a structured, accessible intake system for resident stories means that input from the most vulnerable is not systematically captured or considered.

## Potential Harms

Maintaining the status quo risks perpetuating historical inequities. The primary harm is that the voices and lived experiences of residents from 'Priority Neighborhoods' and other vulnerable areas are 'underweighted in deliberations.' This leads to staff analysis and commission decisions that may not reflect the full community impact of a project. It erodes trust between residents and city government, reinforces the influence of well-resourced and better-connected voices, and fails to leverage the city's own equity-focused planning documents (like Richmond 300) in a meaningful, case-by-case manner. The lack of transparent, inclusive mechanisms to document, verify, and summarize resident stories means that decision-makers may not be directly considering the most critical community perspectives.


# Richmond Public Comment Process Overview

## Channel

Legistar eComment and Email to Clerk for Agenda Packet Inclusion

## Description

Residents can submit public comments through two primary written channels. The city's official Legistar portal has an 'eComment' feature for each meeting. Alternatively, residents can email their written comments directly to the City Clerk's office, which are then supposed to be included in the official records. The main Planning Commission webpage directs residents to the Legistar calendar to find meeting details and agendas, which is the starting point for either process.

## Accessibility Notes

The process is fragmented and not consolidated on a single, easy-to-use page. The main city website instructs users to search a Legistar calendar, which can be confusing. The overall process is described as intimidating and filled with jargon, creating knowledge gaps for 'everyday Richmonders,' as acknowledged in the Richmond 300 plan.

## Effectiveness Issue

A major issue is that written comments often get buried within lengthy PDF agenda packets or exist as separate links within the Legistar system. This structure does not guarantee that the comments are synthesized or given high visibility to Planning Commissioners before their deliberations, leading to a weak feedback loop for residents.


# Barriers To Resident Participation

## Barrier Type

Process Complexity and Jargon

## Description

A significant barrier for residents is the intimidating and jargon-heavy nature of the planning and public comment process. Timelines and procedures are unclear, making it difficult for residents to know how, where, and when to submit comments to ensure they are seen by the Planning Commission before deliberations. This complexity creates knowledge gaps and discourages participation from those not already familiar with civic processes.

## Evidence Source

The Richmond 300 Master Plan serves as a primary source identifying this barrier. It includes 'Objective 5.1: Increase public knowledge of planning processes' and calls for creating a planning knowledge program for 'everyday Richmonders,' which implicitly acknowledges that the current process is too complex and inaccessible for the general public.


# Data And Integration Challenges

## Challenge Area

System Integration and Data Standardization

## Description

The core technical challenge is the absence of a structured, machine-readable data feed that links qualitative resident narratives to quantitative case and parcel metadata. The current official system, Legistar, uses static PDF documents and separate eComment links, which prevents the systematic aggregation, analysis, and integration of community feedback into planning workflows. Data is siloed and not structured for reuse or pattern analysis.

## Key Question

How can a new story feed be technically integrated with existing city systems like Legistar, and how should it fit into the established workflows of planning staff and the agenda platforms used by the Planning Commission? Furthermore, what is the appropriate data schema, including geographic and case metadata, required to make the collected stories useful for analysis and to track equity outcomes effectively?


# Governance And Prior Art From Comparable Cities

## City Name

Raleigh, NC

## Process Summary

Raleigh's rezoning process includes a mechanism for written comments gathered during neighborhood meetings to be formally submitted and included in the Planning Commission's materials. This creates a structured channel for pre-hearing input.

## Key Features

The most notable feature is the time-bound requirement for submission: written comments must be received by the Planning and Development Department at least 10 days before the first Planning Commission hearing for the case. This deadline ensures that the comments are included in the official agenda packet for commissioners to review.

## Relevance To Richmond

Raleigh's approach provides a clear, predictable, and time-bound pathway for written testimony to become part of the official record visible to decision-makers before a hearing. This directly addresses the pain point of Richmond residents who feel their written comments get buried or are not seen. Implementing a similar system in Richmond could enhance transparency, build trust, and ensure that resident feedback is formally considered during deliberations.


# Key Richmond Planning Documents

## Document Name

Richmond 300: A Guide for Growth

## Description

Richmond's master plan, adopted to guide development and growth through 2037. The plan was developed with extensive public engagement and explicitly centers equity as a core theme, acknowledging the need to correct historical planning injustices.

## Stated Goals

The plan's stated goals include increasing public knowledge of planning processes, creating a planning knowledge program for 'everyday Richmonders,' and continuously engaging with civic associations and traditionally under-represented groups. It also introduced concepts like 'Priority Neighborhoods' and the 'Climate Equity Index' to measure social vulnerability and guide equitable development.

## Source Url

https://rva.gov/sites/default/files/2025-03/R300_Amended_RRHA_lowres_20250321.pdf

## Document Name

Zoning Overhaul / Code Refresh (via Zoning Advisory Council - ZAC)

## Description

An ongoing initiative to overhaul Richmond's zoning code. The process is advised by a Zoning Advisory Council (ZAC). This initiative became a key example of representation challenges when public complaints arose that the ZAC did not adequately represent the community, lacking members from several city districts.

## Stated Goals

While not a formal document goal, the process highlighted the critical need for equitable representation in planning deliberations. In response to complaints, the city expanded the ZAC's membership, demonstrating a reactive goal to include voices from underrepresented areas and address the perceived lack of community representation in the zoning overhaul process.

## Source Url

https://www.richmonder.org/after-complaints-city-expands-zoning-advisory-group-from-17-to-21-members/
