# Executive Summary

Richmond residents face significant functional and emotional barriers when attempting to share their lived-experience stories for civic purposes. Functionally, existing channels like City Council public comments are highly restrictive, with strict pre-registration times and limits on participation frequency, making asynchronous and detailed sharing difficult. These official processes are often paired with short engagement windows and a reliance on surveys rather than narrative capture. These issues are compounded by a significant digital divide, where even with asserted 100% broadband access, barriers like reliance on mobile-only devices, lack of time, low digital literacy, language gaps, and affordability prevent consistent online engagement. Emotionally, a deep-seated distrust in government engagement persists, rooted in historical harms such as the destruction of Black neighborhoods like Jackson Ward by highway construction. This lack of trust is exacerbated by engagement practices that fail to meet residents where they are and the absence of platforms that offer privacy, control, and a trauma-informed approach to storytelling. These cross-cutting challenges mean that current civic feedback mechanisms systematically over-represent residents with time, technical resources, and trust in the system, while excluding the narratives of historically marginalized communities, perpetuating inequitable outcomes.

# Functional Persona Pain Points

For a Richmond resident whose goal is to submit their lived-experience story to inform decisions, several concrete pain points exist with current civic engagement channels. These channels are not designed for capturing nuanced, asynchronous narratives.

1.  **Time and Access Constraints on Public Comment:** The primary formal channel, City Council public comment, is highly restrictive. Residents must pre-register by 10:00 a.m. on the day of the meeting and are limited to speaking no more than four times per year and only once per three-month period. This synchronous, time-limited format severely restricts asynchronous participation and prevents the sharing of deep, nuanced stories. [Evidence: Richmond City Council Informational Meeting Guide]

2.  **Insufficient Engagement Windows:** Major projects often have very short public engagement windows, which reduces the quality and representativeness of the feedback received. For example, a multi-billion-dollar regional transportation project had a narrow two-week comment window, prompting commenters to urge for extensions to reach community members who are traditionally less involved. [Evidence: ConnectRVA 2045 Public Engagement & Outreach Report]

3.  **Digital Divide and Adoption Barriers:** While the city may assert 100% broadband access, significant barriers to adoption and use persist. City leadership acknowledges that low awareness and a lack of trust in affordability programs are major issues. Furthermore, state data reveals that many low-income households rely solely on smartphones, which are not ideal for submitting rich, geotagged, multi-media stories. These residents also often lack the time to engage online or learn new tools due to holding multiple jobs. [Evidence: Virginia Mercury, Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan]

4.  **Language and Disability Accessibility Gaps:** There are documented accessibility issues that prevent residents from contributing. These include language barriers for Limited English Proficiency (LEP) populations and lower rates of home internet subscription for people with disabilities. The high cost of assistive devices (sometimes over $1,000) presents another significant hurdle, reducing the ability of these residents to contribute nuanced stories online. [Evidence: Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan]

5.  **Emphasis on Surveys Over Narrative Capture:** Current city engagement strategies, such as the Richmond Connects transportation action plan, have relied heavily on surveys, pop-up events, and social media. While these methods are effective for gauging priorities, they are not structured to preserve and analyze place-based, multi-modal resident narratives. [Evidence: Richmond Connects 2024 Action Plan]

**Workarounds and Costs:** To overcome these barriers, residents resort to speaking at council meetings (if they can), emailing officials, posting in neighborhood social media groups, or filling out issue-centric surveys. The cost of this fragmented status quo is significant: resident stories are lost or decontextualized, participation is skewed towards those with more time and technological access, and the resulting feedback is not easily analyzable by location or topic. Consequently, policy is informed by aggregate data or transactional service tickets rather than the rich context of lived experience.

# Emotional Trust Persona Pain Points

For a resident from a historically marginalized community, the motivation is to find a trusted, safe way to share their story with control over privacy. The pain points are deeply rooted in historical inequities and current systemic failures:

1. **Erosion of Trust from Historical Harms:** Past government actions have created a deep-seated distrust. The construction of the I-95/64 highway, which 'irreparably destroyed' the thriving Black neighborhood of Jackson Ward, is a prime example cited in the Richmond 300 master plan. This history makes residents skeptical of current government-led engagement efforts, fearing their participation could lead to further harm or be ignored.

2. **Exclusionary Engagement Practices:** Current methods of engagement often fail to 'meet people where they are.' Public comment windows for city council meetings that require pre-registration and have frequency limits, along with short, one-off engagement periods for major projects, effectively exclude residents who work multiple jobs, have caregiving responsibilities, or lack the flexibility to participate in synchronous events. This reinforces the feeling that their voices are not genuinely sought or valued.

3. **Adoption and Trust Barriers to Digital Platforms:** Even when digital access is technically available, there is low awareness and significant distrust of government programs, including those for affordable internet. As noted by the Virginia Mercury, this lack of trust is a major barrier to adoption, preventing residents from using online platforms to share their stories.

4. **Accessibility Gaps Limiting Safe Storytelling:** Significant barriers exist for residents with disabilities and those with limited English proficiency (LEP). The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan highlights that many low-income households are reliant on smartphones, which are not ideal for complex tasks like crafting and submitting private, detailed, multimedia narratives. Furthermore, the high cost of assistive devices and language barriers prevent equitable participation.

5. **Lack of Control Over Personal Narratives:** Current city engagement tools, which focus on surveys and public meetings, do not offer explicit privacy controls or trauma-informed consent pathways. Residents have no clear mechanism to control how their sensitive, personal stories are used, stored, or shared, creating a major deterrent to sharing experiences of harm or neglect.

**What Breaks, Workarounds, and Costs:**
*   **What Breaks Today:** The system lacks trauma-informed and privacy-centric storytelling mechanisms. Outreach is episodic rather than continuous, consent and data use policies are unclear, and significant accessibility gaps persist, all compounded by low trust from historical harms.
*   **Workarounds:** Residents resort to sharing sensitive stories only within trusted circles like community groups or faith organizations, posting anonymously on social media, or choosing not to share their stories at all.
*   **Cost of the Status Quo:** The city suffers from a significant underreporting of harm and community needs, leading to policies that miss root-cause impacts. For residents, being forced to share traumatic experiences in public, time-limited forums without any control over their narrative carries a high risk of retraumatization.

# Cross Cutting Equity Dimensions

Overarching equity issues in Richmond's civic engagement processes systematically disadvantage marginalized communities. The current systems, which rely on synchronous participation and digital access, tend to over-represent individuals with ample time, reliable devices, and English proficiency. Consequently, narratives from Black, low-income, Limited English Proficiency (LEP), disabled, and mobile-only residents are significantly under-captured. This disparity is rooted in several interconnected problems. The digital divide presents major hurdles, including the affordability of broadband, reliance on mobile-only devices which limit functionality, and gaps in digital literacy. Accessibility barriers further exclude residents with disabilities and those who require language support. Furthermore, a deep-seated distrust in government, stemming from historical harms like the destruction of the Jackson Ward neighborhood by highway construction, creates a chilling effect on participation from historically marginalized communities. Richmond's own equity and planning documents acknowledge these shortcomings, calling for continuous, compensated, multilingual, and accessible engagement. This indicates a recognized need for a storytelling platform that enables asynchronous, multi-media submissions, provides granular privacy controls, structures narratives for analysis, and integrates comprehensive language and accessibility support to build trust and ensure more equitable representation.

The system is designed for specific, transactional service issues (e.g., pothole repair, missed trash pickup) and is not structured to capture qualitative, narrative-based feedback. The time-boxed format discourages nuanced, in-depth storytelling and favors those who can take time off work to participate, excluding asynchronous contributions.

## Evidence Or Example

# Rva311 Limitations

The RVA311 system, while useful for its intended purpose, presents significant pain points for residents wishing to share lived-experience stories. Its core limitation is its design as a transactional service request tool, not a platform for qualitative storytelling. This leads to several specific problems:

1.  **Transactional Focus:** The system is structured around discrete, actionable service requests like 'pothole in my street' or 'missed recycling pickup'. It is not designed to capture the 'why' behind an issue or the broader context of a resident's experience. A story about how a series of unaddressed infrastructure problems in a neighborhood affects a resident's daily safety and quality of life does not fit into the system's rigid, category-based reporting structure.

2.  **Lack of Narrative and Multi-Media Options:** The platform is optimized for short text descriptions of a service issue, not for rich, qualitative narratives. There are no dedicated options for submitting stories via voice recording or annotated photos, which are more accessible and expressive mediums for many residents. This limitation prevents the capture of nuanced, emotional, and detailed accounts that are crucial for understanding lived realities.

3.  **Inability to Structure Feedback for Policy Analysis:** Because RVA311 categorizes inputs as service tickets, the data is not structured for broader policy analysis. A submission is treated as an isolated incident to be resolved rather than a data point that could reveal systemic issues in a specific location or affecting a particular demographic. This prevents city planners and policymakers from identifying patterns, understanding root causes, and using resident experiences to inform strategic planning, capital projects, or policy changes.

# Public Comment Process Barriers

## Procedural Barrier

Hurdles in the public comment process are significant and include strict registration requirements. For instance, to speak at a Richmond City Council meeting via teleconference, residents must register before 10:00 a.m. on the day of the meeting. Furthermore, participation is restricted, with rules limiting an individual to speaking no more than four times per year and no more than once within a three-month period. These rules restrict asynchronous participation and limit the ability of residents to respond to timely issues.

## Time And Accessibility Barrier

Public comment is constrained by time and accessibility. Meetings are synchronous, which disadvantages residents who work multiple jobs, have caregiving responsibilities, or lack flexible schedules. Engagement windows for providing input on major projects can be extremely short; for example, a multi-billion-dollar regional transportation plan had an initial comment window of only two weeks. This is insufficient for reaching community members who are traditionally less involved and require repeated outreach. These time-limited formats are not designed for sharing nuanced, personal narratives.

## Psychological Barrier

Significant psychological barriers deter participation, particularly from residents of historically marginalized communities. A deep-seated distrust in government, fueled by historical harms such as the destruction of the Black neighborhood of Jackson Ward for highway construction, makes residents wary of engaging. The formal, public nature of council meetings can be intimidating and is not seen as a 'trusted, safe way' to share personal or potentially traumatic stories. The lack of privacy controls and trauma-informed processes in current channels creates a risk of re-traumatization and discourages the sharing of sensitive lived experiences.

## Evidence Source

Richmond City Council Informational Meeting Guide, ConnectRVA 2045 Public Engagement & Outreach Report, Richmond 300 Master Plan.


# Historical Distrust As A Barrier

## Historical Event

The construction of the I-95/I-64 highway in the 1950s.

## Affected Community

Jackson Ward, a thriving and historically significant Black neighborhood in Richmond, along with other Black communities.

## Description Of Harm

The highway construction 'irreparably destroyed' and broke apart the Jackson Ward neighborhood. This act of urban renewal, executed by the government, displaced residents, dismantled a vibrant social and economic hub, and fundamentally broke the community's trust in governmental planning and promises.

## Contemporary Impact

This historical trauma continues to shape resident attitudes today. The Richmond 300 master plan's acknowledgment of this harm and its proposal of a 'Big Move' to 're-knit the community' by capping the highway serves as an official recognition of the long-lasting damage. This history is a primary driver of skepticism and distrust toward current city-led engagement efforts, as residents from affected communities fear that new projects may inflict similar harm or that their input will be similarly disregarded.


# Digital Divide Pain Points

## Affordability Issue

The high cost of broadband service is a primary barrier to home internet use, particularly for low-income households and individuals with disabilities. The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan identifies affordability as a key obstacle. Even when affordability programs exist, low awareness and a lack of trust in these programs prevent widespread adoption. Additionally, the cost of necessary assistive devices, which can exceed $1,000, presents another significant financial barrier for people with disabilities.

## Access Issue

While physical broadband infrastructure may be widely available in Richmond, true access is hindered by a lack of adoption. According to a Virginia Mercury article, city leadership acknowledges that the core problem is not the absence of service but the failure of residents to sign up. This is driven by a lack of awareness of or trust in available programs. For certain groups, such as people with disabilities, home internet subscription rates are documented to be lower, indicating a persistent access gap.

## Device Gap

A significant portion of residents, particularly those in low-income households, rely exclusively on mobile devices like smartphones for their internet connection. The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan highlights that these handheld devices limit the ability to perform a full array of digital tasks, such as submitting detailed, multi-media stories or filling out complex forms. This 'mobile-only' access creates a functional gap that prevents full participation in online civic life.

## Digital Literacy Gap

There are significant variations in the skills and confidence needed to effectively use digital tools. The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan notes that language and literacy limitations can create a lack of awareness around helpful programs and resources. Furthermore, many residents in 'covered households' (e.g., low-income, seniors) lack the time to develop new digital skills, often because they are working multiple jobs. This skills gap is a fundamental barrier to technology adoption and online engagement.


# Privacy And Foia Concerns

A significant pain point for residents, particularly those from historically marginalized communities sharing sensitive stories, is the fear that their personal narratives and identifying information could become public record. While not explicitly named, this concern is directly related to public disclosure laws like Virginia's Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). When residents submit information to a government body, it can often be subject to FOIA requests, meaning their deeply personal, potentially traumatic stories could be exposed to the public without their direct control. This creates a powerful 'chilling effect' that discourages the sharing of critical feedback or sensitive experiences. The evidence for this is seen in the persona's core motivation to have 'control over my privacy and how it's used.' The current system, which lacks trauma-informed platforms with granular privacy controls, forces a difficult choice: either stay silent or risk public exposure. The documented workarounds—sharing stories only within trusted community organizations, posting anonymously, or not sharing at all—are direct results of this fear. The cost of this status quo is the 'underreporting of harm' and the risk of 'retraumatization when stories are forced into public, time-boxed hearings without control,' demonstrating a clear need for a trusted mechanism that guarantees resident privacy and control over their data.

# Cost Of The Status Quo

The collective cost of the current fragmented and ineffective feedback systems in Richmond is substantial, impacting both residents and city governance. For residents, the status quo leads to significant burnout, wasted time, and emotional labor as their stories are either lost, decontextualized, or ignored. Participation skews heavily towards those with the time, resources, and technical access to navigate restrictive public comment periods and digital platforms, systematically excluding marginalized voices. This results in the perpetuation of inequitable outcomes, as policies are made without a full understanding of the lived experiences in communities of concern. For residents from historically harmed communities, the cost includes the underreporting of critical issues due to a lack of trusted and safe channels, and the potential for retraumatization when forced to share sensitive stories in public, time-boxed hearings without any control over their narrative. For the city, planners and officials are forced to make decisions based on incomplete, unrepresentative, and unanalyzable data—fragmented across emails, social media, and issue tickets—which fails to capture the root causes of community challenges and leads to less effective, and often inequitable, policy and projects.

# Engagement Lessons From Richmond Initiatives

Insights from major Richmond planning efforts reveal a consensus on the need for more intensive and equitable community outreach. The 'Path to Equity' report emphasizes that equitable outreach must surpass legally-required minimums by actively meeting people where they are and engaging groups continuously throughout any planning process. This sentiment is echoed in the 'Richmond Connects 2024 Action Plan', which operationalized this by using paid Community Ambassadors, telephone town halls, and social media live streams to reach traditionally underrepresented communities. A key lesson from this initiative was the importance of compensating residents for their time and knowledge, treating them as valued partners. Furthermore, reports like the 'ConnectRVA 2045' highlight the inadequacy of short engagement windows, which limit meaningful input. Finally, planning documents such as 'Richmond 300' underscore the critical importance of building trust by acknowledging and seeking to reconcile historical harms, such as the damage inflicted on the Black neighborhood of Jackson Ward by the construction of I-95/I-64. These collective findings point towards a model of engagement that is continuous, compensated, multi-channel, and built on a foundation of trust and historical awareness.

# Key Pain Points Summary

- Restrictive and Inaccessible Official Channels: Public comment formats, such as at City Council meetings, are synchronous, time-limited, and require pre-registration, creating high barriers for asynchronous participation and the sharing of nuanced stories.
- Eroded Trust from Historical Harm: Deep-seated distrust in government engagement, stemming from historical harms like the destruction of the Black neighborhood of Jackson Ward for highway construction, prevents residents from participating authentically or at all.
- Pervasive Digital and Accessibility Divide: Significant barriers exist for many residents, including reliance on mobile-only devices, lack of time to learn new tools, language and literacy limitations, and the high cost of internet service and assistive devices.
- Lack of Narrative-Centric Tools: Current city engagement emphasizes surveys, service requests (RVA311), and pop-up events, which are not structured to capture, preserve, or analyze qualitative, place-based resident narratives over time.
- Absence of Privacy, Control, and Trauma-Informed Processes: There is a lack of trusted, safe mechanisms for residents to share sensitive stories with clear consent pathways and control over how their personal narratives are used, risking underreporting and retraumatization.
- Fragmented and Unanalyzable Feedback: Resident stories are scattered across disparate channels like emails, social media forums, and public meetings, making it impossible for city planners to analyze feedback systematically by location, topic, or demographic.
- Episodic and Inconvenient Engagement: Engagement often occurs in short, one-off windows for specific projects and fails to meet people where they are, excluding residents who juggle multiple jobs, caregiving, or have mobility challenges.

# Actionable Recommendations For Project

## Recommendation Title

Granular Privacy and Consent Controls

## Description

Implement a robust, user-friendly system that provides residents with granular control over the privacy of their submitted stories and explicit consent for how their data is used. This should include options for full anonymity, pseudonymity, or attribution. Users should be able to specify who can view their story (e.g., only internal city planners, specific departments, trusted community partners, or the general public). The platform must feature a clear, trauma-informed consent process, written in plain language, that explains precisely how the narrative will be stored, analyzed, shared, and for how long. This feature is critical for addressing the deep-seated distrust identified in the 'Emotional Trust JTBD' persona.

## Pain Point Addressed

Historical harms have eroded trust in government engagement, and current engagement formats lack clear resident control over how their narratives are used.

## Trust Building Element

By placing control directly in the hands of the resident, this feature demonstrates respect for their agency, vulnerability, and ownership of their personal story. It shifts the dynamic from data extraction to trusted partnership. For communities with a history of being harmed or misrepresented by government actions, providing this explicit control is a foundational step in rebuilding trust and ensuring that the platform is a safe space for sharing sensitive, lived experiences.
