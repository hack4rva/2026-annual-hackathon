# From App to Infrastructure: Scaling Vecina as Richmond's Civic Arts Plumbing

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Vecina's primary value is not its user interface, but its potential to serve as the authoritative "event plumbing" for the Richmond region. While the hackathon demo successfully solved the discovery friction for residents by utilizing CultureWorks data, the long-term viability of the project depends on shifting from a standalone destination site to a syndicated data utility. The recent 2024 discontinuation of the *Phillyfunguide* [1] serves as a critical warning: platforms that fail to integrate into broader institutional workflows or rely solely on manual upkeep eventually collapse. 

The research indicates a high-potential path toward regional and state-level expansion. By framing Vecina as a "Tourism and Economic Vitality" tool rather than just an "Arts" tool, the project unlocks significant funding from the Virginia Tourism Corporation (VTC) [2] and the Department of Housing and Community Development (DHCD) [3]. Vecina must prioritize automated syndication and SEO—similar to the University of Louisville's implementation of Schema.org which increased event web traffic by 648% [4] —to ensure it becomes an invisible but essential part of Richmond's digital fabric.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Funded / Nonprofit-Led / Regional-Data)**

To ensure sustainability and avoid the pitfalls of government procurement and maintenance abandonment, Vecina should adopt a hybrid ownership model:

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** The City should provide seed funding and policy backing via the Mayor's "A City That Tells Its Stories" pillar. It should act as the "Lead Applicant" for federal and state grants but should not manage the daily technical stack or data moderation.
* **What Partners should own:** **CultureWorks** should own the data backend and API maintenance, acting as the canonical source of truth. A regional entity like **Richmond Region Tourism** should own the marketing and regional expansion to ensure the tool serves visitors and residents alike.
* **What should NOT be City-owned:** The User Interface and Frontend Development. To avoid "government website" stagnation, the frontend should be maintained by a nonprofit partner or a civic tech collective (e.g., Code for America community fellows [5]) to allow for rapid iteration and modern UX standards.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

### The Phillyfunguide Collapse
* **What was attempted:** The Greater Philadelphia Cultural Alliance ran "Phillyfunguide" as the region's go-to website for cultural events [19].
* **What happened:** The Cultural Alliance discontinued Phillyfunguide on August 31, 2024 [1].
* **Lesson for Vecina:** Destination websites that require constant manual curation and marketing to drive traffic are unsustainable. Vecina must be an invisible infrastructure layer that pushes data to existing high-traffic sites rather than trying to be a standalone destination.

### Localist and Schema.org SEO Success
* **What was attempted:** The University of Louisville used Localist to promote events and implemented Schema.org markup [4].
* **What happened:** They promoted 160% more events and increased their event web traffic by 648% [4].
* **Lesson for Vecina:** Technical architecture must prioritize search engine indexability. If Vecina's events are not easily readable by Google, the platform will fail to reach the majority of users.

### Artsopolis Network Model
* **What was attempted:** Artsopolis was developed to raise the visibility of local arts and now gathers content on behalf of local partners [20] [21].
* **Lesson for Vecina:** A successful platform acts as a syndication hub, gathering data once and distributing it across a network of community partners [20].

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why Richmond-only is too small:** 
A city-only frame excludes the broader regional population that commutes into Richmond for cultural events. Furthermore, it disqualifies the project from the most lucrative state tourism grants, such as the VTC Marketing Leverage Program, which explicitly requires a minimum of three Virginia partners to financially match funds [6]. 

**The Regional and Statewide Advantage:** 
By moving to a regional frame involving Henrico and Chesterfield, Vecina can integrate with Richmond Region Tourism [15]. If the architecture is built modularly, it could eventually scale statewide by partnering with the Virginia Museum of Fine Arts (VMFA), which already works with over 1,600 partners across the Commonwealth [16]. If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data, the project avoids procurement hurdles and political branding shifts during mayoral transitions.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Rot & Dependency (High Risk):** Vecina is entirely dependent on CultureWorks. If CultureWorks changes its API or data structure, Vecina breaks.
* **Maintenance Burden (High Risk):** Hackathon projects often die when the original developers move on. Without a dedicated civic tech fellowship (e.g., Code for America [5]) or institutional owner, the codebase will stagnate.
* **The "Destination" Trap (High Risk):** As seen with Phillyfunguide [1], trying to force users to visit a new website is a losing battle. Vecina must syndicate its data to where users already are.
* **Procurement/Legal (Moderate Risk):** City Hall's IT procurement is notoriously slow. Hosting the project on nonprofit infrastructure bypasses city IT bottlenecks.
