# Executive Summary

Richmond's arts and culture event discovery landscape is characterized by significant fragmentation and systemic challenges, creating distinct pain points for both residents and event organizers. The primary user need is for a centralized, efficient, and trustworthy platform to find safe, low-cost, and geographically relevant events. Currently, information is scattered across numerous platforms (like CultureWorks, Venture Richmond, VisitRichmondVA), each with its own focus and submission process, forcing users into a time-consuming search. This fragmentation is compounded by geographic disparities, with major calendars prioritizing downtown and tourism, leaving neighborhood-level events, especially in areas like Southside, underrepresented. A significant digital divide, with nearly 15% of Richmond households lacking broadband, excludes a portion of the population from online-only listings. Furthermore, transportation barriers hinder access for residents in areas with less connectivity to downtown cultural hubs. For event organizers, the lack of a syndicated system creates a duplicative and burdensome administrative process of submitting events to multiple platforms, limiting their reach and taking resources away from their primary mission.

# Pain Points For Residents

## Persona

Southside parent

## Pain Point Type

Information Fragmentation and Lack of Detail

## Description

Residents, particularly those with limited time like a Southside parent, face a fragmented and inefficient process for discovering local arts and cultural events. Information is scattered across numerous platforms, including the CultureWorks calendar, Venture Richmond (which is primarily downtown-focused), VisitRichmondVA, Richmond.com, and various social media pages. This forces users to check multiple sources, which is time-consuming. Furthermore, major calendars often prioritize downtown or tourist-heavy areas, making it difficult to find hyper-local events in neighborhoods like Bellemeade and Swansboro. A significant issue is the inconsistent or missing details in event listings, such as cost, ADA accessibility, and public transit options, which makes it difficult for families to plan with confidence.

## Current Workaround

Currently, residents bounce between multiple websites, rely on word-of-mouth recommendations, check for physical flyers at community hubs like libraries and recreation centers, and post inquiries in local social media groups (e.g., Facebook groups) to find event information. This process is described as time-consuming and unreliable.


# Pain Points For Event Organizers

## Primary Burden

Duplicative Submission Work

## Description

Event organizers in Richmond face a significant administrative burden due to the lack of a centralized or syndicated event calendar system. They are required to manually submit their event details to multiple, distinct platforms to maximize their reach. The research identified at least five separate submission systems (VPM/Style Weekly, Visit Richmond VA, Venture Richmond, CultureWorks, and the City of Richmond's special event permit process), each with its own unique submission form, requirements, audience, and geographic focus. There is no automated system to push event information to multiple calendars simultaneously.

## Consequences

This duplicative work is time-consuming and diverts resources and focus away from the core mission of creating and presenting arts and cultural events. Because organizers may choose to prioritize only one or two platforms, their event promotion is often incomplete, limiting their reach and potentially leading to lower attendance and engagement. This fragmentation also increases the chance of data errors across different listings.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Digital Divide Impact

The reliance on web-based platforms for event discovery excludes a significant portion of the Richmond population. Nearly 15% of households in the city lack a broadband internet subscription. This digital divide disproportionately affects lower-income residents, particularly those in areas with concentrated poverty like the city's Southside, preventing them from accessing online event information.

## Transportation Barrier Impact

Residents who rely on public transportation, especially those in neighborhoods like Southside, face significant barriers to attending arts and culture events. The challenges and limitations of the public transit system, particularly for routes connecting Southside to downtown where many major venues are located, create a hurdle for residents who do not own personal vehicles. This limits their ability to participate in the city's cultural life.

## Geographic Inequity

There is a significant geographic bias in how events are promoted. Major event calendars, such as those from Venture Richmond and Visit Richmond VA, tend to focus on downtown and tourist-heavy areas. This practice marginalizes and makes neighborhood-specific events less visible, particularly in communities with a higher concentration of minority and low-income residents, such as Southside. This leads to an underrepresentation of the cultural life happening outside the city center.


# Evidence Of Information Fragmentation

The evidence for information fragmentation in Richmond's arts and culture scene is substantial and multifaceted. The primary issue is that event information is scattered across numerous, disconnected platforms, creating a frustrating and time-consuming experience for residents. Users, such as the 'Southside Parent' persona, must navigate a complex web of sources to get a comprehensive view. In addition to these formal calendars, users must also monitor social media platforms like Instagram and Facebook, where neighborhood organizations and community groups often post about local or last-minute events. The fragmentation extends beyond the digital realm, as residents also rely on word-of-mouth and physical media like flyers posted at libraries and community recreation centers. This scattered landscape is reinforced by the burden on event organizers, who must manually submit their event details to 'at least five major, distinct event calendar submission systems,' each with its own requirements, because no centralized or effectively syndicated system exists.

# Transportation And Accessibility Barriers

## Description

This creates a transportation barrier for residents in Southside and other areas who may not have access to personal vehicles.

The GRTC (Greater Richmond Transit Company) Transit Strategic Plan is an ongoing effort mentioned in the research that aims to improve transit services, including the connection from Southside to downtown, indicating that this is a recognized issue being addressed by the city.

## Impact On Residents

This transportation barrier limits access to cultural events for residents who rely on public transit. It can increase travel time, add complexity to planning, and potentially act as a cost deterrent, ultimately preventing residents without personal vehicles from participating in events held in the downtown core.


# Key Challenges For Vecina Implementation

For the 'vecina' project to succeed, it must address several critical, Richmond-specific challenges. A primary hurdle is data aggregation; the project needs a strategy to consolidate event information from a fragmented ecosystem of sources including VPM/Style Weekly, Visit Richmond VA, Venture Richmond, CultureWorks, and the City of Richmond's special event permit process. A second major challenge is establishing and standardizing metadata for event listings. This includes consistently providing crucial details often missing from current calendars, such as cost, ADA accessibility, and, critically, public transportation information like specific GRTC routes to venues, which is a known barrier for residents in Southside. Building user trust is another key challenge, particularly for the 'cautious resident' persona who needs to feel confident about attending last-minute or pop-up events. This requires a system for vetting organizers and partnering with community leaders in neighborhoods like Church Hill and Southside to ensure the platform is a reliable and reputable source. Finally, the project must confront the digital divide, as nearly 15% of city households lack broadband, necessitating a strategy that goes beyond a web-only platform to ensure equitable access for all residents.

# Strategic Recommendations For Vecina

Based on the identified pain points, several strategic recommendations can guide the 'vecina' project toward becoming an indispensable community resource. First, to address the significant burden on event organizers, 'vecina' should create a centralized, single-submission process that allows organizers to enter event details once. This system should be designed to syndicate or automatically push this information to other major local calendars, such as the VPM Artsline (Style Weekly) and Visit Richmond VA, maximizing reach while minimizing duplicative work. Second, to fill a critical gap, the project should prioritize hyper-local and neighborhood-level events, focusing on areas like Southside that are often overlooked by downtown-centric platforms. Third, to ensure equitable access and overcome the digital divide, 'vecina' must develop strategies for non-digital information dissemination, such as forming partnerships with libraries, community centers, and local print publications. Building trust is paramount; this can be achieved through active partnerships with community organizations and leaders. Finally, to enhance usability and address transportation inequities, the platform should integrate practical information into event listings, specifically details on GRTC routes and other public transit options.
