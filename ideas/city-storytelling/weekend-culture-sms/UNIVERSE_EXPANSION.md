# Text-Enabled Richmond: Scaling a Weekly Arts-SMS Digest from City Pilot to Regional Asset

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The "Weekend Culture SMS" idea possesses a massive growth trajectory that extends far beyond a standard municipal alert system. While municipal SMS programs like New York City's Notify NYC [1] and Missoula's Weekly City News Digest [2] focus primarily on civic and emergency alerts, cultural institutions like the Museum of Contemporary Art (MCA) Chicago have proven that curated, arts-focused SMS can drive immense engagement and revenue, generating over $158,000 with a 20x ROI [3]. By reframing this idea from a "city communications tool" to a "regional cultural and economic driver," its universe expands to tap into state tourism funds, federal creative placemaking grants, and regional nonprofit partnerships. 

To realize this potential, the project must not remain solely city-owned. A hybrid ownership model—where the City of Richmond provides the initial technical infrastructure and compliance framework, while a regional arts nonprofit (like CultureWorks) manages curation and subscriber relationships—is the most viable path. This structure unlocks diverse funding streams, including the Virginia Tourism Corporation's Marketing Leverage Program [4], the National Endowment for the Arts' Our Town grants [5], and Twilio.org's Impact Access Program [6]. By scaling regionally, the initiative transforms from a symptom-level fix for local event discovery into a robust, multi-jurisdictional platform for community vitality and economic development.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Initiated, Nonprofit-Led, Regionally Scaled)**

The Weekend Culture SMS should operate under a hybrid model. A purely city-owned model risks turning the digest into a sterile civic alert, while a purely nonprofit-owned model might lack the initial capital and technical procurement weight to launch securely. 

* **What the City should own:** The initial technical procurement (e.g., Twilio account setup), 10DLC (10-Digit Long Code) carrier compliance and registration [7], and the initial pilot funding. The City acts as the catalyst and infrastructure provider.
* **What partners should own:** A central arts nonprofit (e.g., CultureWorks) should own the subscriber relationship, the curation of the 3-5 weekly events, the marketing of the shortcode, and the long-term grant management. 
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The ongoing content curation and the subscriber data monetization (e.g., ticket cross-selling). The City should not be in the business of picking "winners and losers" among local arts events, nor should it bear the long-term carrier costs once the program scales.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Museum of Contemporary Art (MCA) Chicago (Attentive SMS):** MCA Chicago launched an SMS program sending 2-3 messages per week. **What worked:** The cadence was perfect, generating over $158,000 in revenue and a 20x ROI [3]. **Lesson:** Curated, limited-frequency SMS is highly monetizable and drives massive engagement compared to email.
* **SFMOMA "Send Me SFMOMA":** An SMS service allowing users to text a keyword to receive artwork. **What worked:** It went incredibly viral, receiving over 3.7 million text requests by July [27] and over 6 million total [28]. **What failed:** The massive influx caused mobile carriers to blacklist the number [29], and running the short code became too expensive to sustain [28]. **Lesson:** Carrier compliance (10DLC) and throughput planning are mandatory; a sustainable funding model for carrier fees is required from day one.
* **City of Missoula Weekly City News Digest:** A municipal SMS/email opt-in for city news. **What worked:** Established a direct line to residents [2]. **What failed (for arts):** It functions as a general civic utility rather than a curated cultural experience. **Lesson:** Arts discovery requires dedicated, curated channels, not just inclusion in a generic city newsletter.
* **Notify NYC:** New York City's emergency and event SMS alert system (text NOTIFYNYC to 692-692) [1]. **What worked:** Simple, memorable shortcode onboarding [30]. **Lesson:** The onboarding mechanism ("Text RVAARTS to XXXXX") must be frictionless.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a "City of Richmond" project artificially limits its potential. 
* **Regional Shift:** Cultural consumers do not care about municipal boundaries; a resident of Henrico will travel to Richmond for a gallery opening. By framing this as a Central Virginia or Richmond Region initiative, the project becomes eligible for Richmond Region Tourism Foundation grants [11] and VTC Marketing Leverage funds [4]. 
* **Statewide/National Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the platform can be packaged as a replicable "Creative Placemaking" model, making it highly competitive for NEA Our Town grants [5]. 
* **Operational Shift:** If the city only pilots the infrastructure and a partner scales it, the city avoids the long-term financial burden of carrier fees (which crippled SFMOMA's initiative [28]) while still achieving its MAP pillar goals.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Compliance and Carrier Blacklisting (High Risk):** As seen with SFMOMA, carriers will block high-volume traffic that looks like spam [29]. 10DLC (10-Digit Long Code) registration is legally required to comply with the TCPA and CTIA guidelines [7] [31]. Mitigation: Register the campaign with The Campaign Registry immediately and ensure strict opt-in consent [32].
* **Financial Sustainability (High Risk):** Sending millions of texts is expensive [28]. Mitigation: Cap the pilot audience size, utilize Twilio.org Impact Access credits ($100) [6], and build a revenue model (e.g., ticket cross-sells) early.
* **Data-Sharing and Curation Trust (Medium Risk):** The city should not be the sole arbiter of what constitutes "culture." Mitigation: Formalize an API and curation partnership with CultureWorks to maintain community legitimacy.
