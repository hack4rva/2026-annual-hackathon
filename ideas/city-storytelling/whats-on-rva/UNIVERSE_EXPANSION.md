# Richmond's Cultural Concierge: Scaling "What's On RVA" from Hackathon Demo to Regional Infrastructure

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

"What's On RVA" represents a critical shift from passive event listing to an active cultural concierge service. While the hackathon demo excels in user experience (UX) and conversational AI, its long-term viability depends entirely on solving the "existential data gap" identified in initial reviews. To move beyond a "ghost calendar" fate, the project must transition from a City-owned tool to a **Regional Hybrid Model**. 

By positioning the platform as the digital front door for the Richmond Region Tourism Improvement District (TID) [1] and leveraging CultureWorks as the primary data steward [2], the project can tap into sustainable hotel-tax assessments and state-level marketing grants [3]. This expansion transforms the idea from a local utility into a regional economic engine that drives overnight stays and neighborhood-level spending across the five TID jurisdictions [4].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Regional Hybrid Model**

The platform should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Municipal procurement for custom AI software is notoriously slow, and a city-only boundary ignores the regional nature of arts audiences. 

* **What the City Should Own (Data Catalyst):** The City should act as a policy enabler and data catalyst. Through its Non-Departmental Grant Program, the City already funds major cultural institutions [5]. The City should mandate that all arts sub-grantees provide machine-readable event feeds as a condition of funding, creating a sustainable data pipeline.
* **What Partners Should Own (Product Owner):** A regional nonprofit, such as CultureWorks or Venture Richmond [6], should own the resident-facing application and AI layer. This allows for agile software updates, private-sector sponsorships, and rapid UX iteration that municipal governments cannot easily execute.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The proprietary recommendation algorithm, the AI chat interface, and the day-to-day content moderation of the "Neighborhood Stories" should remain outside city ownership to avoid maintenance burdens and legal liabilities regarding AI hallucinations.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Artsline (CultureWorks):** The current standard for Richmond arts listings. *Lesson:* It has the data but lacks the conversational and map-based UX of What's On RVA. It must serve as the backend API.
* **BLK RVA (Richmond Region Tourism):** A successful niche tourism campaign designed to support Black-owned businesses and cultural attractions [21] [22]. *Lesson:* Community-driven content (like Neighborhood Stories) drives higher engagement than generic listings.
* **PlanRVA GeoHub:** A regional open data portal for boundaries, business, and emergency management [17]. *Lesson:* Do not rebuild mapping infrastructure; integrate directly with PlanRVA's ArcGIS backend for spatial accuracy.
* **Yahoo Upcoming / EveryBlock (Defunct):** Pioneers in event aggregation that died due to data decay. *Lesson:* Without an automated API pipeline, the platform will become a "ghost town" within 6 months. Manual entry is a guaranteed failure path.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why Richmond-only is too small:** 
The arts ecosystem is inherently regional. A resident in Henrico won't download an app that artificially stops showing events at the city line. Furthermore, restricting the platform to the City of Richmond cuts off access to the Richmond Region Tourism Improvement District (TID) funds, which are collected across five jurisdictions (Richmond, Chesterfield, Hanover, Henrico, and Ashland) [4]. 

**What changes if the frame becomes regional:**
Moving to a regional frame unlocks TID funding, which has already generated $1.7M in room revenue in just six months [1]. If a nonprofit like CultureWorks owns the resident-facing layer while the City provides data mandates, the platform can scale its AI concierge to serve both local residents and out-of-town visitors, qualifying for VTC Marketing Leverage grants [3].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Decay (Existential Risk):** If the platform relies on manual scraping of Instagram or individual venue sites, it will break. *Mitigation:* Establish a formal data-sharing MOU with CultureWorks and the City to use Artsline as the primary API feed.
* **Maintenance Burden:** AI models require constant tuning to avoid hallucinating event dates or locations. *Mitigation:* Use a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) approach that strictly limits the AI's answers to the verified event feed.
* **Procurement Blockers:** City procurement for custom software is restrictive. *Mitigation:* House the platform's ownership within a regional nonprofit to ensure agility.
* **Trust and Equity:** AI tools can inadvertently skew toward popular, well-funded venues. *Mitigation:* Actively tune the AI to surface events in underrepresented neighborhoods and utilize multilingual capabilities to serve Richmond's diverse populations.
