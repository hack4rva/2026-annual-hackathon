# Report Summary

Discovering arts and cultural events in Richmond, VA, is a significant challenge for residents due to a highly fragmented information landscape. The primary user pain point is the need to consult numerous disparate sources—including tourism sites, media calendars, venue pages, and social media feeds—to get a complete picture of what's happening. This process is time-consuming and often results in missed events. Current workarounds, such as following popular Instagram curators like 'The Richmond Experience' and 'Miss Elaneous' or subscribing to newsletters, are considered incomplete and limited by users themselves. A major deficiency across existing platforms is the lack of standardized and essential filters for accessibility (ADA), public transit options, and price, which creates significant barriers for individuals with disabilities and those reliant on public transportation. Furthermore, the information found is often unreliable, with warnings from sources like VPM's Artsline that events may be rescheduled, placing the burden of verification on the user. These issues create significant equity gaps, disadvantaging residents without reliable broadband, those who do not use social media, and people with specific accessibility needs. The 'whats-on-rva' project aims to directly address these pain points by creating a centralized, authoritative platform. It will aggregate, deduplicate, and standardize event listings from all major sources. Crucially, it will enrich this data with normalized tags for neighborhood, price, and family-friendliness, and add vital, often-missing details such as ADA compliance information and proximity to GRTC transit routes, thereby creating a single, reliable, and equitable resource for the entire community.

# Project Overview

The 'whats-on-rva' project is a civic technology initiative designed to address the fragmented and frustrating experience of discovering arts and cultural events in Richmond, VA. It operates under the civic pillar of 'City That Tells Its Stories.' The core problem it solves is the scattered nature of event listings, which are currently spread across numerous sources like Style Weekly, VisitRichmond, RVAHub, Eventbrite, and individual venue websites. The project's primary goal is to function as an arts and cultural event aggregation platform. It will pull event data from this multitude of sources into a single, unified, and searchable interface. Beyond aggregation, the platform will process the raw data to deduplicate identical events, standardize taxonomies for genres and neighborhoods, and enrich listings with crucial but often-missing details like accessibility and public transit information. The ultimate aim is to provide a comprehensive and reliable central resource for residents and visitors to discover all arts and cultural happenings in the Richmond area.

# Richmond Resident Persona Analysis

## Persona Description

This persona is a Richmond resident, located either within the city itself or in the nearby surrounding counties. They are actively engaged in planning their social and cultural activities, particularly for the upcoming weekend.

## Job To Be Done

When it’s Thursday afternoon and I’m planning my weekend in Richmond, I want to see all arts and cultural events in one searchable place, so I can quickly pick something near me that matches my interests and budget.

## Pain Points

1. **Fragmented Discovery:** Event information is scattered across numerous sources, including tourism websites (VisitRichmond), media calendars (Style Weekly, RVAHub), and individual venue pages. Evidence from Reddit shows users asking for a single, reliable source because they "usually just stumble upon these things."
2. **Incomplete Workarounds:** Residents rely on social media and newsletters as a workaround, but this is insufficient. Users on Reddit follow accounts like The Richmond Experience and Miss Elaneous but note that the information is "still limited."
3. **Inconsistent Filtering:** Key filters that users need, such as accessibility (ADA), public transit options, and price, are not standardized across the various event calendars. For example, the VMFA's calendar has some filters but not for ADA or transit, while VisitRichmond highlights categories but lacks on-page filters for these criteria.
4. **Information Reliability Gaps:** Event listings can be outdated or inaccurate, leading to issues with duplication or unannounced changes. VPM’s Artsline explicitly warns users to "check with organizations" because events are "sometimes rescheduled," which implies the listings require external verification.
5. **Missing Integrated Transit Context:** Information about GRTC bus services, including the Pulse line, is located on a separate website and is not integrated into event listings. While venues list parking information, they do not show GRTC routing or station proximity, forcing users to manually cross-reference this information.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The current situation results in several costs for the resident. The planning process is extremely time-consuming due to the need to check multiple sources. This fragmentation leads to missed events and reduces opportunities for spontaneous attendance. The workarounds involve following dozens of Instagram accounts and newsletters (like The Richmond Experience and Miss Elaneous), manually checking multiple calendars (VisitRichmond, Style Weekly, RVAHub), and resorting to crowdsourcing recommendations on platforms like the r/rva subreddit. Furthermore, this system creates inequitable access for residents who do not use social media, have limited or unreliable broadband access, or require specific accessibility information that is hard to find.


# Newcomer And Host Persona Analysis

## Persona Description

This persona is a 'neighborhood connector' or a 'newcomer host'. They are socially-minded individuals who take on the role of curating cultural experiences for friends, family, or new residents, aiming to showcase the authentic character of Richmond.

## Job To Be Done

When I’m taking friends or family to an event that reflects Richmond’s culture, I want to find curated, reliable recommendations that feel authentically RVA.

## Pain Points

1. **Partial Curation:** This persona desires authentic, curated recommendations, which leads them to rely on human curators on Instagram and in newsletters. However, this coverage is partial and crowdsourced. Evidence from Reddit shows users citing The Richmond Experience and Miss Elaneous but calling them 'limited,' and curators like Miss Elaneous are seen asking followers to flag events they missed.
2. **Tourist-Focus vs. Local Feel:** Major portals like VisitRichmond are geared towards tourists, highlighting large festivals and general attractions. This contrasts with the host's desire to find weekly or monthly neighborhood-level happenings (like First Fridays) that provide a more local feel but are harder to discover comprehensively.
3. **Verification Burden:** There is a significant burden on the host to verify event details. For instance, VPM Artsline's caution that events may be rescheduled forces hosts to double-check information directly with organizers to avoid mishaps.
4. **Lack of Standardization:** There is no consistent, cross-platform tagging system for crucial details like neighborhood, price, or accessibility. While a single venue like the VMFA might have its own robust filters, this doesn't apply across the entire ecosystem, requiring the host to manually synthesize information from disparate sources.

## Emotional Cost

The primary cost is emotional and social. The status quo requires the host to spend significant extra time pre-hosting to research and verify events. This creates a risk of recommending an event that is ultimately canceled, sold out, or not inclusive (in terms of price, accessibility, or transit options). Such an outcome can be embarrassing for the host and can erode the trust their guests have in their recommendations, undermining their role as a reliable connector.

# Cost Of The Status Quo

The current fragmented event ecosystem in Richmond imposes significant costs on users. For a resident simply trying to plan their weekend, the status quo results in time-consuming planning, a high likelihood of missing events, and reduced opportunities for spontaneous attendance. This is due to the need to check multiple, disparate sources like tourism sites, media calendars, and individual venue pages, none of which provide a complete picture. The workarounds, such as following dozens of Instagram accounts and newsletters, are acknowledged by users as being 'limited' and incomplete. This fragmentation also creates a 'verification burden,' as event listings can be outdated or rescheduled, forcing users to double-check details directly with venues, as warned by VPM's Artsline. For a 'neighborhood connector' or host, the costs extend to social capital; there is a risk of recommending events that are canceled, sold out, or not inclusive (in terms of price, accessibility, or transit), which can erode trust with their guests. Furthermore, the reliance on digital-first, social media-heavy workarounds creates an equity gap, disadvantaging residents without reliable broadband or those who do not use platforms like Instagram, effectively excluding them from community happenings. This digital divide is a recognized issue in Richmond, with active policy efforts to address it.

## User Impact

# Accessibility Information Gap

A significant pain point in Richmond's event discovery landscape is the severe lack of standardized and easily accessible information for people with disabilities. Key filters for accessibility are not standardized across the various event calendars (such as VisitRichmond, Style Weekly, or RVAHub). While a venue like the VMFA might have its own internal filters, these do not apply across the broader ecosystem. Consequently, crucial details regarding ADA compliance, wheelchair access, sensory-friendly options, and available parking for disabled patrons are not present on aggregated event listings. This forces individuals who need this information to engage in a burdensome, multi-step process of hunting for it. They must navigate away from the event listing and find the specific, often separate, 'Accessibility' or 'ADA' page on each individual venue's website. The research cites examples of this siloed information on the websites for the Dominion Energy Center, Altria Theater, and the VMFA. This creates significant extra work and barriers for disabled patrons and their caretakers, making spontaneous event attendance difficult and potentially excluding them from cultural participation.

# Transportation And Logistics Challenges

Users face considerable challenges in planning transportation and logistics for events in Richmond due to a lack of integrated information. A primary issue is the general absence of public transit details within event listings. Information about GRTC's Pulse and bus services, including routes and station proximity, is not surfaced on event calendars or venue pages. Instead, this information resides on the separate GRTC website, forcing users who rely on or would consider public transit to manually cross-reference maps and schedules to determine the best way to get to a venue. This problem is compounded by a 'transit vs. parking bias.' Venue websites, such as the one for the Dominion Energy Center, tend to foreground information about driving directions and parking. By prioritizing car-centric information and failing to integrate GRTC services—which have been fareless since 2020 under the 'Open Access' program—the current system disadvantages transit riders and makes it harder for all attendees to consider transportation options beyond their personal vehicles.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Affected Group

Residents with limited digital access (due to affordability or adoption barriers), individuals with disabilities and their caretakers, and public transit riders.

## Barrier

Event information is primarily disseminated through online platforms and social media, creating a 'digital divide'. Accessibility details (like ADA compliance) are not standardized in event listings and are siloed on individual venue websites. Similarly, public transit information (GRTC routes/stations) is not integrated into event listings, which tend to prioritize driving and parking directions.

## Consequence

These barriers result in inequitable access to cultural and community events. Residents without stable internet or social media accounts are more likely to miss events. People with disabilities face a significant extra burden to verify accessibility, potentially discouraging attendance. Public transit users are disadvantaged by the lack of integrated transit information, making it harder for them to plan travel to and from events.


# Digital Divide Impact

The digital divide significantly exacerbates the problem of event discovery in Richmond. The primary workarounds for fragmented information involve following numerous accounts on Instagram and subscribing to digital newsletters, which inherently excludes residents without reliable, affordable broadband access or those with lower digital literacy. The research highlights that the city's own policy actions, including the creation of a Digital Equity Assessment and Implementation Plan and the Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan, underscore that internet affordability and adoption remain significant barriers. Consequently, residents who rely on non-digital sources or who simply avoid social media platforms are systematically disadvantaged and more likely to be unaware of community and cultural events.
