# "From Closed Sidewalks to Open Access: Scaling Richmond's ADA-Detour Publisher Across Government & Civic Partners"

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The ADA Detour Digital Publisher represents a critical leap from physical compliance to digital equity, addressing a gap where no U.S. city currently publishes machine-readable, ADA-attributed pedestrian detour geometries. While the idea originated as a Richmond-specific solution to prevent GRTC CARE paratransit no-show penalties and assist visually impaired residents, its true universe extends far beyond municipal boundaries. Recent regulatory shifts, including the DOJ's 2024 Title II web accessibility rule and the U.S. Access Board's PROWAG final rule, have implicitly mandated that if cities provide digital notice of closures, those notices must be fully accessible [1] [2] [3]. 

To scale effectively, this initiative must transition from a siloed city project into a hybrid ecosystem. By leveraging federal safety grants like the USDOT Safe Streets and Roads for All (SS4A) [4] [5], state-level disability funding from the Virginia Board for People with Disabilities [6] [7], and regional data-sharing infrastructure through PlanRVA [8] [9], Richmond can pioneer a national standard. The most viable path forward is a hybrid ownership model: the City of Richmond mandates and supplies the raw permit data, a regional body like PlanRVA hosts the standardized feed, and a disability-advocacy nonprofit manages the public-facing, screen-reader-optimized portal to ensure absolute trust and WCAG compliance.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit-Regional Partnership**

To maximize compliance, funding access, and community trust, the ADA Detour Digital Publisher should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Instead, it requires a federated ownership model:

* **What the City Should Own (Data Engine):** The City of Richmond (specifically DPW) must own the regulatory mechanism and the raw data pipeline. This includes updating Work-in-Street permit requirements to mandate digital submission of pedestrian routing plans and maintaining the authoritative GIS layer of active closures.
* **What Partners Should Own (Public Portal & User Experience):** A disability-advocacy nonprofit (e.g., Ability First or Access Virginia) should own the public-facing web interface. Nonprofits are better positioned to conduct continuous WCAG user testing, build trust with the accessibility-dependent community, and iterate on screen-reader-first text directions without being bottlenecked by municipal IT procurement.
* **What the Region Should Own (Data Hub):** PlanRVA (the Richmond Regional Transportation Planning Organization) should own the data syndication layer. By integrating the detour data into their existing regional frameworks and WZDx feeds [8] [10], the system can scale to neighboring counties (Henrico, Chesterfield) seamlessly.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Seattle Accessible Route Planner:** SDOT created a map identifying sidewalks, curb ramps, and APS signals to help users plan routes based on mobility preferences [30] [31]. It offers automated routing to avoid hills and construction [32]. *Lesson:* While highly successful in user adoption, it relies heavily on a visual map interface. Richmond's tool must prioritize text-based, screen-reader-compatible directions to serve visually impaired users effectively.
* **Esri Road Closures Solution:** Esri allows agencies to share road closure updates directly with consumer mapping providers like Apple Maps, TomTom, and Waze [25] [33] [26]. *Lesson:* The distribution rails for vehicle closures exist and are highly automated. The ADA Detour Publisher must aim to create an equivalent, standardized feed (like WZDx) for pedestrian pathways so it can eventually plug into major navigation apps.
* **Work Zone Data Exchange (WZDx):** USDOT's specification harmonizes work zone data for third parties, recently adding features like `DetourRoadEvent` [10] [28] [29]. *Lesson:* Richmond should not invent a proprietary data schema. It should adapt WZDx and OpenSidewalks standards to ensure the data can be consumed nationally.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential and ignores the reality of regional transit. GRTC CARE paratransit operates across jurisdictional lines (Richmond, Henrico, Chesterfield). If a wheelchair user travels from Richmond to Henrico, a digital detour tool that stops at the city border is fundamentally broken. 

By shifting the frame to a regional model hosted by PlanRVA, the project unlocks economies of scale. PlanRVA already manages regional STBG and CMAQ funding [8] [9]. A regional approach allows multiple municipalities to feed their permit data into a single, standardized WZDx-compatible hub [10]. Furthermore, if a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, they can scale the interface statewide without needing to navigate the distinct IT procurement processes of every individual city, provided the cities supply the standardized data.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Silos & Format Constraints:** DPW currently relies on static PDF traffic control plans. Converting these into machine-readable LineStrings requires workflow changes. *Mitigation:* Update permit requirements to mandate digital submission of routing plans from contractors.
* **Legal & Compliance Liability:** Under the DOJ's 2024 Title II rule, any digital detour information published by the city must meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards [3]. *Mitigation:* Offload the UI presentation to a specialized disability nonprofit that builds screen-reader-first text interfaces, reducing the city's direct digital liability while improving user experience.
* **Trust and Community Legitimacy:** Historically marginalized and accessibility-dependent communities may distrust municipal tech tools that have previously failed them. *Mitigation:* Co-design the platform with groups like Ability First [6] and ensure the tool is branded and operated by trusted community advocates.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Grant funding (like SS4A) covers initial development but not perpetual hosting. *Mitigation:* Embed the data feed into PlanRVA's existing regional transportation data infrastructure, which is sustained by recurring STBG allocations [8].
