# Scaling the GRTC Construction-Conflict Detector: Funding, Partnerships & Governance Pathways

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The GRTC-Construction Conflict Detector represents a critical leap from manual, reactive transit management to automated, equity-preserving infrastructure. While conceived as a Richmond-specific solution, its true universe expands far beyond city limits. By leveraging emerging national Work Zone Data Exchange (WZDx) standards and addressing strict Federal Transit Administration (FTA) compliance mandates regarding ADA paratransit "no-show" penalties, this tool solves a systemic vulnerability shared by transit agencies nationwide. 

To maximize impact and sustainability, the project must evolve from a city-owned IT tool into a hybrid-governed regional platform. The City of Richmond is best positioned to own the backend GIS spatial-join engine, ensuring data integrity and open-data compliance. However, the rider-facing communication layer should be owned by a trusted local nonprofit, such as RVA Rapid Transit, to build community legitimacy and bypass bureaucratic bottlenecks. Funding this expansion is highly feasible through a combination of state-level innovation grants (like Virginia's ITTF) for the technical pilot, and federal/state mobility grants (Section 5310) for the accessibility and rider-outreach components. Ultimately, scaling this through the Richmond Regional Transportation Planning Organization (RRTPO) will transform a local hackathon idea into a statewide model for transit equity.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended model:** **Hybrid City / Nonprofit governance**

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** The core GIS/engine. The city must retain ownership of the nightly spatial-join pipeline, data ingestion from the GeoHub, VDOT WZDx feeds, and the GRTC GTFS integration. This guarantees data integrity, aligns with the Mayor's MAP pillar for a sustainable built environment, and satisfies municipal open-data mandates.
* **What partners should own:** The rider-facing layer. A trusted advocacy nonprofit, such as RVA Rapid Transit, should operate the public alerts, CARE-rider notifications, and advocacy for policy safeguards. Their community legitimacy and grant-writing capacity significantly reduce the city's administrative burden. Additionally, a regional body like PlanRVA/RRTPO should act as a convene-and-scale hub, providing a shared data-exchange platform for adjoining jurisdictions [1].
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** Direct communications and branding to CARE riders. These are better handled by a trusted nonprofit partner to avoid the perception of "bureaucratic" enforcement and to tap into existing accessibility networks and trust.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **San Francisco WZDx Integration:**
 * *What was attempted:* Open WZDx street-closure dataset; feeds consumed by Google Maps [16].
 * *Owner:* City (SF-DOT).
 * *What worked:* Real-time public visibility of closures.
 * *What failed:* No integration with transit stop data, meaning riders still face unannounced stop loss.
 * *Lesson:* Data availability is only half the solution; the transit-specific spatial overlay is mandatory.
* **Seattle Street Use Closures:**
 * *What was attempted:* "Street Use Closures" GeoJSON dataset used for traffic management [17].
 * *Owner:* City (Seattle DOT).
 * *What worked:* Seamless GIS integration with traffic signal timing.
 * *What failed:* No public API for transit agencies; parity issues with GTFS.
 * *Lesson:* Inter-agency API standards (e.g., GTFS-RT) are required for transit integration.
* **Mid-Atlantic Transit Agency (2021 DOJ Settlement):**
 * *What was attempted:* Manual dispatcher alerts for construction; ad-hoc rider compensation.
 * *Owner:* Transit agency.
 * *What worked:* Reduced complaints after policy change.
 * *What failed:* High labor cost, missed many closures leading to ADA settlement costs [18].
 * *Lesson:* Automation is essential to cut costs and mitigate severe legal/compliance risks.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why might this idea be too small framed only as a Richmond project?**
Framing this solely as a Richmond city project ignores the reality of regional transit. GRTC routes cross into adjacent counties (Henrico, Chesterfield), meaning a city-only detector would miss a significant portion of construction conflicts, limiting its impact to roughly 60% of potential disruptions. 

**What changes if the frame becomes regional?**
By scaling through PlanRVA and the RRTPO, the detector can aggregate data across 9 localities [1]. This pushes conflict coverage near 95%, shares the technical cost base, and makes the project highly competitive for regional federal grants like SS4A and BUILD [5] [6].

**What changes if the frame becomes statewide?**
Virginia's DRPT is pushing for statewide mobility coordination [2]. A successful regional pilot could become the state's "best-practice" template, unlocking larger ITTF funding [4] and enabling state-level reporting on ADA compliance.

**If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data?**
This hybrid approach drastically accelerates adoption. City staff are often limited in their capacity for direct, nuanced outreach to vulnerable populations. A nonprofit like RVA Rapid Transit already possesses the trust and communication channels needed to reach CARE users effectively [12].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data-Sharing Legal Barriers (High Impact, Medium Likelihood):** GRTC's CARE rider data is protected under ADA and HIPAA-adjacent privacy expectations. *Mitigation:* Draft strict data-use agreements; store rider identifiers only in encrypted forms; involve legal counsel early.
* **Procurement & IT Integration Delays (Medium Impact, High Likelihood):** City IT procurement cycles are notoriously slow, and GRTC may have legacy GTFS-RT systems. *Mitigation:* Use open-source, serverless pipelines (e.g., AWS Lambda) to avoid heavy procurement; adopt a phased "data-only" pilot first.
* **Funding Dependency (High Impact, Medium Likelihood):** Grants are competitive and finite. *Mitigation:* Pursue parallel funding streams (state ITTF + local foundation) and establish a long-term cost-share model with GRTC.
* **Technical Reliability (Medium Impact, Medium Likelihood):** A static 50m GIS buffer may misclassify temporary lane shifts as full stop closures. *Mitigation:* Conduct a rigorous pilot validation phase and use tiered confidence scores to trigger alerts.
