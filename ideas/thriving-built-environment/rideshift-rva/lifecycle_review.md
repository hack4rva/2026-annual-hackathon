## What Was Fundamentally Missed

RideShift built a good product for the wrong problem statement. The stated problem is "help residents find and understand transportation and infrastructure projects happening in their neighborhoods." RideShift's answer to this is an embedded link to Richmond Connects — a tool that already existed before the hackathon. That's not a solution; it's a link.

What RideShift actually built is a mode-shift behavior change platform incentivized by local business rewards. This is a legitimate civic product that addresses a real challenge (RVA Green 2050, transportation equity, local economic development). But it belongs under Thriving Economy, not Thriving Built Environment.

The barrier data collection feature is the most genuinely novel contribution in either Built Environment problem statement — structured, voluntary data about why Richmond residents don't use transit or active transportation is valuable to planners and has never been systematically collected in this way. But this data has no defined City recipient, which means it may never be acted on.

---

## Blockers

1. **Problem statement misalignment** — This is a structural issue, not a fixable one. RideShift solves a transportation behavior change problem; the Built Environment pillar asks for a project visibility solution. A judge scoring this against the rubric should weight the Impact category low for Built Environment alignment.

2. **Barrier data recipient undefined** — The most valuable feature (barrier reporting) has no City stakeholder who commits to receiving and acting on it. Without this, the data collected disappears.

3. **CONTACTS.md gap** — The right SME outreach for RideShift is to GRTC (Greater Richmond Transit Company) or the Office of Sustainability. Neither is in CONTACTS.md. Add contacts before drafting outreach.

---

## Recommended Next Steps

**Priority 1:** Re-enter RideShift for the Thriving Economy pillar award. The local business partnership model, minority-owned business alignment, and 36-hour partner acquisition is strong economy pillar work. This is where RideShift belongs.

**Priority 2:** Identify a City stakeholder (GRTC, Office of Sustainability, or Richmond Department of Transportation) to receive and act on barrier data. Without this, the most novel feature has no impact.

**Priority 3:** Add the "construction affects my route" notification feature — the one announced as future-state in the demo. This is the one feature that would genuinely address the Infrastructure Project Visibility problem statement and differentiate RideShift from a simple Richmond Connects link.

**Priority 4:** Add CONTACTS.md entries for GRTC and the Office of Sustainability. Draft outreach email about barrier data ownership.

## Scoring Guidance (for judges)

For the **Built Environment Pillar Award**: Score Impact low (product does not address the stated problem statement). Score Innovation, User Value, and Equity higher — the mode shift mechanic and local business partnership are genuinely original.

For the **Thriving Economy Pillar Award**: Score Impact, User Value, and Feasibility all high. The 5 real business partners in 36 hours is an exceptional execution signal. The barrier data collection could be valuable for economic and transportation planning.

For the **Mayor's Choice Award**: Score Feasibility high — real business partnerships, real product, deployed. Score Impact moderate — the impact is on transportation behavior change and local economy, not on infrastructure visibility as the pillar intended.
