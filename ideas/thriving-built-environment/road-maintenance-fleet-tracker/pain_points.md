# Pain Points — Fleet Operations & Street Service Tracking

**Applies to:** All Your Bass (Stay Informed) AND Road Maintenance Fleet Tracker
**Source:** Web research, documented 311 equity studies, Toronto PlowTO analysis (2025), Boston 311 data (2026), University of Iowa 311 equity research (2024)

---

## Job 1: The Stranded Resident — Pain Points

**Pain 1.1 — No self-service status check**
Residents have no way to check if their street has been plowed or cleaned without calling 311. During a snow emergency, 311 wait times spike to 30-60+ minutes. Residents must either wait on hold, guess, or ask a neighbor — all unreliable.
*Evidence: Boston Globe analysis of 311 data, February 2026 — Dorchester residents reported streets uncleared for multiple days with no status information available.*

**Pain 1.2 — System says "done" when it isn't**
Toronto's PlowTO system (2025) showed route completion status as "done" while sidewalks were physically uncleared. Residents who trusted the app made decisions (driving to work, sending children out) based on false data. Trust collapses after one such experience.
*Evidence: Toronto City Council background file, February 2025 storm response.*

**Pain 1.3 — High cost of uncertainty for hourly workers**
A resident who works hourly and cannot work from home faces a real economic decision: do I risk driving on an unplowed street, or do I call in and lose a day's pay? Without reliable status information, they must choose between financial and physical safety. This cost is concentrated among lower-income residents.
*Evidence: Inference from University of Iowa 311 equity research, 2024.*

**Pain 1.4 — Notification lag**
Even where notification systems exist, they are often reactive (triggered after completion, not before service starts). A resident who knows "your zone will be plowed at 2 PM" can make plans. A notification that arrives at 4 PM saying "your zone is complete" is useful but misses the highest-value decision window.
*Evidence: Structural analysis of plow notification systems.*

## Job 2: The DPW Supervisor — Pain Points

**Pain 2.1 — No real-time fleet picture**
DPW supervisors currently manage fleet status via radio contact, phone calls, and informal tracking. During a 12-hour snow emergency shift, a supervisor may not know the real-time location or completion status of 20+ trucks without individually contacting each driver.
*Evidence: Inference from demo transcripts; no Richmond-specific survey of DPW supervisor workflows was conducted by either team.*

**Pain 2.2 — Reporting is manual and delayed**
Reporting fleet status upward to the Director or Mayor's Office requires manual aggregation at shift end. By the time a report is compiled, the situation has changed. Decision-making is retrospective, not real-time.
*Evidence: Inference from demo transcripts and general DPW operational context.*

**Pain 2.3 — No anomaly detection**
Without a real-time system, a truck breakdown or a missed route segment may not be discovered until a resident calls 311 to complain — sometimes hours later. There is no proactive alert that says "Truck 7 has been stationary for 45 minutes on Route 12."
*Evidence: Structural analysis; no Richmond-specific documentation.*

## Job 3: The Accountability-Seeking Resident — Pain Points

**Pain 3.1 — Reports go into a void**
When a resident calls 311 to report a missed plow or street cleaning, they receive no confirmation that the report was received, no tracking number they can reference, and no update on whether the issue was addressed. The report disappears.
*Evidence: Structural analysis; consistent with documented 311 experience in multiple cities.*

**Pain 3.2 — Systematic deprioritization of underserved neighborhoods is invisible**
Research (Boston, 2026) shows that affluent neighborhoods receive faster snow service than lower-income ones. But there is no tool or dataset that makes this disparity visible at the neighborhood level in real time. Residents cannot see the pattern; they can only experience it.
*Evidence: Boston Globe 311 data analysis, February 2026.*

**Pain 3.3 — 311 participation gap reinforces inequity**
Lower-income and minority communities use 311 at lower rates than wealthier ones, despite greater need. This means service allocation based on 311 volume systematically under-serves high-need neighborhoods. A tool that only improves service visibility for active 311 users will reproduce this inequity.
*Evidence: University of Iowa research on equity challenges in 311 systems, 2024.*

**Pain 3.4 — No community-level data**
Individual residents can report individual issues, but there is no way for a neighborhood association to see the pattern: "Our block is the last to be plowed, consistently, every storm." Aggregated neighborhood-level data would be powerful for accountability, but it doesn't exist in a publicly accessible form.
*Evidence: Structural analysis.*

## Cross-Cutting Equity Pain Points

**Language access:** No Richmond DPW communication tool is available in Spanish, despite a significant Spanish-speaking resident population. Pittsburgh's plow tracker supports 10+ languages.

**Smartphone dependency:** All existing notification and tracking approaches require smartphone or email access. Richmond's lowest-income residents, who face the most significant consequences of missed service, are least likely to have reliable digital access.

**Trust deficit:** Years of unresponsive 311 calls and no feedback loops have created a trust deficit. Even a working tool faces adoption challenges in communities that have been ignored before. This is not a technology problem; it requires community engagement to rebuild trust.
