# Snow-Tier Compass - From City Pilot to Regional-Scale Expectation Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Snow Tier Compass represents a paradigm shift in municipal winter operations: replacing expensive, failure-prone real-time plow tracking with a low-cost, high-impact "expectation engine." By digitizing static snow removal tiers and publishing them year-round, Richmond can proactively manage resident expectations and reduce 311 call volumes, mirroring the approximately 30% reduction seen in Syracuse [1]. However, framing this solely as a Richmond Department of Public Works (DPW) IT project severely limits its potential. 

To truly expand its universe, this idea must evolve from a city-owned pilot into a regional hazard-mitigation asset. By adopting a hybrid ownership model—where the City of Richmond maintains the core Geographic Information System (GIS) data while a civic nonprofit owns the public-facing portal—the project bypasses municipal procurement bottlenecks and unlocks significant philanthropic and federal funding. Regional conveners like PlanRVA [2] and state partners like the Virginia Department of Transportation (VDOT) [3] can then scale this proven data pattern across Central Virginia, transforming a simple address lookup into a multi-jurisdictional resilience tool eligible for major federal grants like USDOT SMART [4] and FEMA BRIC [5].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended model: Hybrid City ↔ Non-profit partnership**

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond must retain ownership of the raw tier data, the GeoHub geocoder infrastructure, and the "source of truth" authority. The City is responsible for funding the one-time digitization of legacy PDF maps into GeoJSON and maintaining the internal service-level agreement (SLA) for data accuracy [6].
* **What partners should own:** A newly chartered 501(c)(3) or an existing civic tech partner (e.g., a local Code for America brigade) should own the public-facing web application, user interface (UI), community outreach, and grant-writing efforts. Regional bodies like PlanRVA should own the multi-jurisdictional data-sharing agreements [2].
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The City should not own the resident-facing portal's branding, community-engagement staffing, or the agile software development lifecycle. Housing these in a nonprofit protects the initiative from municipal procurement delays and qualifies the project for philanthropic funding streams that are closed to government agencies [7] [8].

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Syracuse, NY (Winter Weather Operations):** Utilized Esri's ArcGIS Velocity to track plows and update a public map every 5 minutes. **What worked:** Achieved better transparency and reduced citizen calls. **Lesson:** Transparency builds public trust and reduces 311 burden [1].
* **Pittsburgh, PA (Snow Plow Tracker):** Deployed a GPS-based tracker that faced heavy criticism. **What failed:** The system did not register trucks moving under 5 mph or in reverse, leading to public frustration and accusations of inaccuracy. **Lesson:** Incomplete real-time telemetry erodes credibility; static expectation-setting is safer than flawed live tracking [16].
* **Washington, D.C. (Citizen Insights):** Uses Geotab to provide public visibility into snow operations. **What worked:** Implemented a configurable delay (e.g., 15 minutes) to avoid showing exact live locations. **Lesson:** Intentional data latency protects operational privacy while maintaining public awareness [15].
* **Philadelphia, PA (StreetSmartPHL):** City-run platform showing treated street status. **What failed/worked:** Explicitly warns users that status updates take 15 to 45 minutes to appear. **Lesson:** Clear communication of data latency is essential to manage user expectations [17].
* **New York City (PlowNYC):** Uses unique physical IDs (CSCL) to track when a street segment was last visited by a plow. **What worked:** Highly granular, segment-level data. **Lesson:** Robust GIS digitization of street segments is a non-negotiable foundation [18].

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **City-only:** If framed only as a Richmond project, the tool remains a localized convenience. It solves a specific DPW communication issue but misses economies of scale and larger federal grant opportunities.
* **Regional (PlanRVA):** Shifting to a regional frame allows the tool to serve the 22 localities under PlanRVA's Emergency Management Alliance [2]. This standardizes data, pools maintenance costs, and makes the project highly competitive for FEMA BRIC hazard mitigation funding [5].
* **Statewide:** If VDOT adopts the underlying data standard, it creates a uniform emergency-response platform across Virginia, though this requires significant inter-agency coordination [3].
* **Nonprofit-led UI / City-led Data:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the project can iterate rapidly and access philanthropic capital (e.g., Community Foundation [7]), while the City maintains the authoritative data feed, ensuring accuracy without procurement bottlenecks.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Procurement and Legal Authority:** Traditional municipal procurement for software development is slow. A hybrid model mitigates this, but requires a clear Service Level Agreement (SLA) between the City and the nonprofit partner.
* **Data Quality:** The foundational step relies on accurately digitizing legacy DPW PDF maps [6]. If the underlying GeoJSON is flawed, the public tool will fail immediately.
* **Public Trust and Latency:** As seen in Pittsburgh, residents will reject a tool they perceive as inaccurate [16]. The system must explicitly communicate that it is an "expectation setter" (static tiers) rather than a real-time tracker, and any operational status updates must clearly state their refresh latency (e.g., Philadelphia's 15-45 minute warning [17]).
* **Inter-agency Data Sharing:** Scaling regionally requires neighboring jurisdictions to agree on data standards and tier definitions, which may face bureaucratic resistance.
