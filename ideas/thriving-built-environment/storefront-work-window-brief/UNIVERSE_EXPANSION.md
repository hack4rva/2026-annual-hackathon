## 2. Expanded Ownership Recommendation

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Partnership with Regional Data Integration**

The most sustainable and politically viable path for the Storefront Work-Window Brief is a hybrid ownership model, mirroring successful public-private mitigation efforts seen in cities like San Francisco [7] [8]. 

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond (specifically the Department of Planning & Development and IT) must own the data stewardship, the API endpoints, and the underlying policy. The city should codify a "Construction Notice Standard" that mandates the data fields required for the brief, ensuring the tool has legal enforceability and contractors are held accountable for providing accurate timelines.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A Richmond-based economic development nonprofit (such as Venture Richmond) should own the public-facing user interface, the community outreach strategy, and the physical distribution of the briefs. They are best positioned to bundle the brief with existing small-business aid, such as marketing grants or signage subsidies [5] [9].
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own the merchant-facing marketing, the door-to-door canvassing, or the UI refresh cycles. Relying on municipal IT for front-end updates often leads to stagnation, and merchants are more likely to trust a business-advocacy organization when seeking continuity support.
* **Regional Expansion:** PlanRVA should act as the regional data broker, utilizing their existing GeoHub infrastructure to eventually scale the tool's data feeds across the nine-locality metropolitan area [10] [11].

## 5. Existing Efforts / Prior Art

Municipalities across the country have attempted to solve the construction-communication gap, yielding valuable lessons for Richmond's approach.

* **NYC DDC Community Construction Liaisons (CCLs):** NYC deploys dedicated liaisons to go door-to-door, issuing 72-hour and 24-hour notices to businesses [34] [35]. *Lesson:* While highly effective, this human-heavy approach is expensive. The Work-Window Brief automates the creation of these notices, allowing a smaller team to distribute them or enabling merchant self-service.
* **SFMTA Construction Mitigation Program:** San Francisco pairs business liaisons with a $5 million Small Business Impact Mitigation Fund to provide directed support and grants to impacted merchants [7] [36] [37]. *Lesson:* Information alone isn't always enough. Richmond should pair the brief with micro-grants (via Venture Richmond) to maximize goodwill and survival rates.
* **VTA BART Silicon Valley Phase II (CEOP):** VTA mandates 2-week advance email alerts, 1,000-foot radius flyer drops, and weekly updates [38] [39]. *Lesson:* Strict, codified notification radiuses work. Richmond's tool should automatically generate briefs for all addresses within a defined radius of the GeoHub centerline data.

## 6. Scale Shift Analysis

Framing this idea solely as a Richmond DPW project severely limits its potential. 

* **City-Only Frame:** If owned entirely by the city, the tool guarantees quick initial data access but ties its maintenance to municipal IT budget cycles. It risks being viewed as a temporary mayoral initiative that could lose funding after an election cycle.
* **Regional Frame (PlanRVA):** By shifting the frame to the regional level, the tool becomes a "Metropolitan Business Continuity Platform." Because PlanRVA already aggregates transportation projects for nine localities [10] [40], integrating their GeoHub data allows the brief to serve businesses in neighboring Henrico and Chesterfield counties without duplicating software development.
* **Nonprofit-Led UI Frame:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides the data, the tool gains agility. Nonprofits can iterate on the UI faster than city procurement allows, and they can apply for philanthropic grants (like the Community Foundation) that the city cannot easily access [21].
* **Federal Frame:** Positioning the tool as a scalable prototype for USDOT SMART or SS4A grants transforms it from a local fix into a nationally recognizable "Construction Impact Transparency Toolkit," opening pathways for massive federal funding [1] [3].
