# From Data Silos to Resident Trust: Scaling StreetLights as Central Virginia's Infrastructure Interface

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

StreetLights represents a critical "translation layer" between technical government GIS data and resident lived experience. While the hackathon prototype is Richmond-centric, the true "expanded universe" for this idea lies in a Regional Hybrid Model. By moving beyond the City of Richmond's borders to include PlanRVA (the regional planning body) and VDOT (state infrastructure), StreetLights can solve the "fragmented commute" problem where residents cross jurisdictional lines but expect a single source of truth. 

The strategic path forward involves leveraging federal USDOT SMART or SS4A grants to fund a professionalized version of the "Plain English" engine [1] [2]. Ownership should transition to a Hybrid Governance structure: the City and PlanRVA provide the "Data Pipe" (via GeoHub and Virginia Roads), while a civic-tech nonprofit or a university-led lab manages the "Resident Interface" to ensure the UX remains agile, human-centric, and free from the procurement lag of traditional government IT.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Regional Governance**

To scale effectively, StreetLights must transition from a standalone hackathon project to a multi-stakeholder utility. 

* **What the City of Richmond Should Own**: The **Data Source (GeoHub)**. The City must focus on "Data Hygiene"—ensuring Project Managers update the GIS layers in real-time. The City should *not* own the frontend code, as procurement cycles will stifle UX innovation.
* **What PlanRVA (Regional Body) Should Own**: The **Regional Integration**. PlanRVA is mandated to build a "Regional Mobility Metrics Dashboard" in FY26 [3]. PlanRVA should host the backend that aggregates Richmond, Henrico, Chesterfield, and VDOT data, providing a single API for the StreetLights interface.
* **What a Nonprofit/Civic Partner Should Own**: The **Resident Interface (StreetLights UI)**. A partner like Code for America (Code for RVA) or a dedicated Civic Tech Lab should manage the website, SMS alerts, and the "Plain English" translation engine [4] [5]. This keeps the tool "community-owned" and more trustworthy to skeptical residents.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Boston Capital Projects Explorer**: Owned by the City's Digital Service. **Lesson**: They link directly to 311, allowing residents to see "Project vs. Problem" in one view [22].
* **NYC Capital Projects Dashboard**: High-detail, but very technical. **Lesson**: It struggles with "Plain English," proving that a nonprofit-led "translation layer" is a unique value add for StreetLights [23].
* **Philadelphia Atlas**: Combines permits, property data, and construction. **Lesson**: Residents use maps for *everything* related to an address; StreetLights should eventually include "Permits" to show private construction too [24].
* **Richmond's RVA311 Launch (2021)**: Replaced SeeClickFix. **Lesson**: The city prioritized "backend integration" over "frontend friendliness." StreetLights can be the "friendly face" that RVA311 lacks, provided it connects to the backend [25].

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why Richmond-only is too small**: Residents in the "Fan" or "Church Hill" often commute into Henrico or Chesterfield. A tool that stops at the city line is only partially useful for a daily commuter. Furthermore, state-managed roads (I-95, I-64, Route 1) are not in GeoHub, leaving massive blind spots in the user experience.

**The Regional Frame**: If PlanRVA adopts this, it becomes a Regional Infrastructure Utility. This unlocks federal MPO funding (RSTBG/CMAQ) that the City cannot access alone [26]. 

**The "Data vs. Interface" Split**: If the City owns the data and a nonprofit owns the interface, the tool can be updated weekly without a 6-month city procurement process. This "Agile Frontend" model is how the best civic tech succeeds, allowing the city to focus purely on data accuracy.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **The "311 Void"**: Without a real API connection to RVA311, the "Community Reporting" feature is a "dark pattern" that misleads residents into thinking they've filed a formal report. This will destroy community trust [25].
* **Data Hygiene**: If DPW project managers don't update GeoHub, StreetLights will show "In Planning" for a project that finished last month. The tool is only as good as the city's internal data governance.
* **VDOT Silos**: VDOT data is in a different format (Virginia Roads/SYIP) and is updated annually based on the approved SYIP [27]. Merging this with City GeoHub data is a significant technical hurdle.
* **Procurement & Legal**: The City may resist a nonprofit "owning" the resident interface due to liability concerns regarding "incorrect information" being displayed to the public.
