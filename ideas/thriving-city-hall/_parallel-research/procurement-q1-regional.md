# Executive Summary

The procurement technology landscape across the nine surveyed mid-sized US cities—Richmond, Baltimore, Pittsburgh, Louisville, Norfolk, Raleigh, Durham, Chattanooga, and New Orleans—is characterized by the adoption of dedicated e-procurement portals for managing solicitations and vendor relationships. Platforms such as OpenGov, Bonfire (Euna), and Workday are prevalent, supplemented by city-specific portals like New Orleans' BRASS system. A significant and universal finding is the absence of technology designed to help staff identify and validate contracts across different jurisdictions (city, state, federal). There is no public evidence that any of these cities have implemented AI-powered contract analysis, OCR-based data extraction, or procurement intelligence dashboards for aggregating external contract sources. While most cities offer some level of searchability for their own contracts, these functions are confined to their internal systems. Vendor compliance checking is addressed in a minority of cases through specialized third-party systems like B2Gnow (used by Richmond and Baltimore), indicating that this function is often separate from the core procurement platform. All identified systems are currently live and operational.

# Key Findings

A detailed review of the procurement technology in the nine specified cities reveals several key takeaways:

*   **Dominance of E-Procurement Platforms:** A few Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) vendors are prominent in this space. OpenGov is the chosen platform for Richmond, Pittsburgh, and Norfolk. Louisville utilizes Bonfire (now Euna Procurement), and Baltimore integrates procurement into its city-wide Workday ERP system. Other cities like New Orleans (BRASS), Raleigh, Durham, and Chattanooga rely on different, often city-specific, portals.

*   **Lack of Cross-Jurisdictional Discovery Tools:** A central finding is that none of the nine cities publicly document the use of procurement aggregation platforms like Pavilion or CoProcure. Staff in these cities do not appear to have a tool that allows them to search for compliant contracts from other cities, state agencies, or federal sources from a single interface.

*   **Absence of AI and Advanced OCR:** The research found no public, verifiable evidence of these cities piloting or implementing AI-powered contract analysis or dedicated OCR-based data extraction projects for the purpose of analyzing and discovering contracts across different jurisdictions. While vendor platforms may use OCR for internal document indexing, it is not being leveraged as a strategic tool for external contract intelligence.

*   **Fragmented Vendor Compliance Systems:** The management of supplier diversity and compliance is often handled separately from core procurement operations. Richmond and Baltimore have both implemented the B2Gnow Diversity Management System, a third-party platform, for this purpose. The other seven cities do not have a visibly promoted third-party compliance portal, suggesting this process may be less systematized or handled internally.

*   **Siloed Contract Visibility:** While most cities provide a mechanism to view their own contracts—such as Richmond's Open Data Portal, New Orleans' dedicated contract search, or listings within the Bonfire portal for Louisville—this visibility is strictly limited to their own jurisdictional data. There are no dashboards or tools that unify this data with state or federal contract repositories.

*   **System Status:** All identified procurement portals, compliance systems, and data portals mentioned across the nine cities are currently active and live. The research found no official notices of these systems being sunset or replaced.

# Cross Jurisdictional Contract Search Analysis

The procurement technology ecosystems in the surveyed cities—Richmond, Baltimore, Pittsburgh, Louisville, Norfolk, Raleigh, Durham, Chattanooga, and New Orleans—are highly fragmented and do not support integrated, cross-jurisdictional contract searches. The research found no evidence of any of these cities implementing aggregation platforms like Pavilion or CoProcure that would allow procurement staff to identify valid contracts from city, state, and federal sources through a single interface. Instead, each municipality relies on its own primary e-procurement or ERP portal. For example, Richmond, Pittsburgh, and Norfolk use OpenGov Procurement; Louisville uses Bonfire (Euna); Baltimore uses Workday; and New Orleans uses its proprietary BRASS portal. While these platforms centralize opportunities within a single city, they do not extend to other governmental levels. Some cities provide access to their own contract data, such as Richmond's 'City Contracts' dataset on its Open Data Portal and New Orleans' 'Search City Contracts' function, but these are strictly limited to their own municipal agreements. The current landscape forces government staff to manually hunt for contracts on separate city, state (e.g., Virginia's eVA, North Carolina's NC eProcurement), and federal websites, confirming the persistence of the problem of siloed information and the absence of a unified discovery tool.

# Ai Ocr And Advanced Analytics Assessment

Across all nine surveyed cities, there is a notable absence of implemented or publicly documented advanced technologies for procurement, such as AI-powered contract analysis, dedicated OCR-based data extraction tools, or procurement intelligence dashboards. The research found no evidence of any active or pilot projects in Richmond, Baltimore, Pittsburgh, Louisville, Norfolk, Raleigh, Durham, Chattanooga, or New Orleans that utilize AI to analyze contract terms or OCR to systematically extract data from contract documents for cross-jurisdictional discovery. While some of the underlying e-procurement platforms (like OpenGov or Workday) may incorporate basic analytics or allow for searching within uploaded documents (which might involve native OCR), none of the cities have publicized a strategic initiative to leverage these technologies to solve the problem of identifying compliant contracts across different government sources. The procurement systems in place appear to function primarily as transactional and bidding platforms rather than as advanced analytical or intelligence-gathering tools. The lack of these technologies means that tasks like comparing contract terms or extracting key data points from PDFs across different jurisdictions remain a manual process for city staff.

# Challenges In Procurement Modernization

The research into the procurement technology of these nine mid-sized cities highlights several common challenges and limitations that hinder modernization, particularly in the context of cross-jurisdictional contract discovery:

*   **Data Fragmentation and Silos:** The primary challenge is the severe fragmentation of procurement data. Each city operates its own e-procurement system (e.g., OpenGov, Bonfire, Workday, BRASS), which is inherently siloed. These platforms are not designed to interoperate with systems from neighboring cities, counties, or state and federal agencies, making a unified search impossible with current tools.

*   **Systemic Gaps in Functionality:** There is a fundamental gap between the procurement staff's need to find external, compliant contracts and the capabilities of the implemented software. The existing platforms are designed for internal process management—posting bids, managing vendor registration, and processing purchase orders—not for external market intelligence or contract discovery.

*   **Disjointed Compliance Management:** The need for separate, third-party compliance systems, as seen with B2Gnow in Richmond and Baltimore, demonstrates that core procurement and ERP platforms often lack robust, integrated modules for tracking supplier diversity and compliance. This forces procurement and compliance officers to work across multiple, disconnected systems, increasing administrative burden and the risk of data inconsistencies.

*   **Inferred Budget and Resource Constraints:** The complete absence of publicly documented AI-powered analysis tools, aggregation dashboards, or advanced OCR projects suggests that mid-sized cities likely face significant budget and resource constraints. They appear to prioritize foundational e-procurement capabilities over investing in more advanced, and often costly, intelligence and analytics technologies.

# Vendor Registration And Portals Overview

Vendors seeking to do business with the surveyed cities interact through a variety of distinct, city-specific portals. There is no single platform for registration or bidding across these municipalities. The landscape includes:

- **OpenGov Procurement:** Used by Richmond, Pittsburgh, and Norfolk. Vendors must register on each city's respective OpenGov portal to receive notifications, view solicitations, and submit bids electronically.
- **Workday Supplier Self-Service:** Implemented by Baltimore as its primary online procurement system and vendor portal. Suppliers use it to manage their information, access opportunities, and handle invoicing and payments.
- **Bonfire (Euna Procurement):** Louisville Metro uses the Bonfire platform for its procurement portal, where vendors register and participate in open bidding opportunities.
- **Proprietary & Other Portals:** New Orleans operates its own comprehensive 'BRASS' Supplier Portal for all supplier management functions, from registration to payment. Chattanooga maintains a dedicated 'City of Chattanooga Supplier Portal'. Durham uses a bids page powered by CivicEngage, while Raleigh directs vendors to its own city procurement pages for opportunities.

In addition to these primary procurement portals, some cities use specialized third-party systems for vendor compliance and diversity tracking. Both Richmond and Baltimore have launched the B2Gnow Supplier Diversity Management System to manage their MBE/ESB/MWBE directories, certifications, and compliance reporting. For the other cities surveyed, no similar third-party compliance platforms were prominently identified on their main procurement websites.

# Future Outlook

Looking ahead, the evolution of procurement technology in these mid-sized cities is likely to be incremental rather than revolutionary. Several trends can be anticipated:

*   **Increased Platform Consolidation:** The trend of cities adopting established SaaS platforms like OpenGov and Euna (Bonfire) is likely to continue. As cities look to replace legacy or homegrown systems, they will gravitate towards these standardized solutions that offer core e-procurement functionalities, vendor support, and regular updates.

*   **Slow Adoption of Advanced Analytics:** Widespread implementation of AI for contract analysis or cross-jurisdictional discovery is unlikely in the near term. Adoption will probably be slow and cautious, potentially emerging first as embedded features within the major SaaS platforms rather than as standalone intelligence tools purchased by cities. Cost and a focus on core operational needs will remain significant barriers.

*   **Push for Internal Integration:** A more immediate focus for cities may be on achieving better integration between their procurement platforms and other internal administrative systems. This includes linking procurement portals with city-wide financial/ERP systems (like Workday) and compliance management tools (like B2Gnow) to create a more seamless internal workflow and reduce data silos within their own government.

*   **Reliance on Traditional Cooperative Purchasing:** In the absence of sophisticated aggregation technology, cities will continue to rely heavily on traditional cooperative purchasing agreements and state-level marketplaces (such as North Carolina's eProcurement or Virginia's eVA) to leverage contracts from other jurisdictions. The process of discovering and validating these opportunities, however, is likely to remain a largely manual task for procurement staff.
