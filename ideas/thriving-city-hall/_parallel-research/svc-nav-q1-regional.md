# Executive Summary

The civic technology landscape in the specified mid-sized US cities is characterized by a universal adoption of unified 311 service portals and accompanying mobile apps as the foundational tool for managing resident service requests. All nine cities—Richmond, Baltimore, Pittsburgh, Louisville, Norfolk, Raleigh, Durham, Chattanooga, and New Orleans—operate live digital platforms to centralize and track non-emergency issues. Key innovations are emerging beyond this baseline, with New Orleans leading the way through its live AI-powered chatbot, "Jazz," which facilitates requests via text and web. Other cities are following this trend, with Durham and Pittsburgh having planned or procured chatbot technology. SMS-based service requests are also live in Durham and New Orleans. A significant trend is the strategic replacement of older systems (e.g., in Pittsburgh, Raleigh, and Chattanooga) with more intuitive, integrated platforms that incorporate plain-language navigation, smarter features like duplicate detection, and clearer service level agreements to ensure requests are routed correctly and efficiently the first time.

# Key Findings Overview

The most important trends in civic technology across the surveyed cities are the widespread adoption of unified 311 portals, the emergence of AI and SMS-based services, and the strategic replacement of legacy systems. Firstly, unified service portals and mobile apps are a standard, live feature in all nine cities, providing a central point for residents to submit and track non-emergency requests. Secondly, a key innovation is the emergence of AI chatbots. New Orleans is a frontrunner with its "Jazz" AI chatbot, launched in 2021 and accessible via text and web, which integrates with the city's CRM to provide 24/7 answers and route requests. This trend is growing, as Durham has issued an RFP for a chatbot and Pittsburgh has announced plans for one as a future enhancement. Complementing this, direct SMS-based service requests are live in Durham and New Orleans. Finally, there is a clear trend of strategic modernization, with several cities replacing older platforms to improve user experience and efficiency. Notable examples include Pittsburgh sunsetting its 'MyBurgh' app for the new 'PGH311' system, Raleigh replacing 'SeeClickFix' with its 'Ask Raleigh' portal, and Chattanooga transitioning from 'CHATT311' to the new 'CHA 311' platform.

# Unified Service Portals Overview

All nine cities examined have implemented live, unified service portals to act as a single point of contact for residents to submit and track non-emergency service requests. These platforms are central to modernizing civic engagement and improving service delivery. For instance, Richmond's RVA311 offers a 24/7 self-serve web portal at rva311.com. Baltimore provides a similar online 311 portal accessible on any device, which allows users to attach supporting documents. Pittsburgh launched a new 311 intake system in February 2025 with an updated website designed for efficiency. Louisville's Metro311 portal assists residents with address lookups and 'Find Services' features to identify the correct department. Norfolk's 'MyNorfolk' Citizen Services Portal enables users to create and track requests in real-time, with the data also available through an open data portal for transparency. Raleigh recently launched 'Ask Raleigh' in beta, a portal designed to be more intuitive and replace the previous SeeClickFix tool. Durham's 'Durham One Call' hub serves a similar function, offering an online request form among other contact methods. Chattanooga announced its new CHA 311 web portal in April 2025, featuring improved categorization and mapping. Finally, New Orleans' NOLA-311 site (nola311.org) provides a channel for non-emergency requests and information, complementing its phone and social media outreach.

# Technology And Vendor Landscape

The civic technology landscape in these cities is marked by a clear trend of modernization and platform replacement. Several municipalities are moving away from older or less integrated solutions. For example, Raleigh replaced its 'SeeClickFix' tool with the new 'Ask Raleigh' portal as of July 2025. Similarly, Pittsburgh sunset its 'MyBurgh' app in favor of the new 'PGH311' system, and Chattanooga is actively transitioning from its legacy 'CHATT311' system to the modern 'CHA 311' platform, transferring open requests in the process. While specific vendors like Salesforce or Accela are not mentioned in the provided text, the use of underlying technologies like Customer Relationship Management (CRM) platforms is evident, as New Orleans' AI chatbot 'Jazz' integrates directly with the 311 center's CRM. The adoption of artificial intelligence is another key technological aspect, with New Orleans having launched its AI-powered chatbot in 2021. Other cities are following this path; Durham issued an RFP for a comprehensive chatbot in February 2025, and Pittsburgh has planned a chatbot as a future enhancement to its new 311 system.

# Impact On Request Routing

These modern civic tech tools incorporate several key features designed specifically to improve the accuracy of service request routing and prevent misdirection. In Richmond, the RVA311 system functions as a central intake to ensure requests are delivered to the correct city team for investigation. Chattanooga's new CHA 311 platform helps residents describe their needs more accurately through 'refined and expanded categories' and ensures precise location data by allowing users to 'drop a pin on a map to auto-populate your service request location' using GPS. Raleigh's 'Ask Raleigh' portal introduces intelligent features to prevent errors upfront; it uses geofencing to alert a user if their reported issue is outside city limits and also notifies them if a similar issue has already been reported to avoid duplicates. In New Orleans, the AI chatbot 'Jazz' is explicitly intended to 'reduce misrouting and handle multi-department cases' by leveraging a knowledge base that integrates information from the 311 CRM and various city websites. Louisville's Metro311 portal aids routing by allowing residents to use keyword searches and 'Find Services' navigation to identify the appropriate department before submitting a request.

# Plain Language And Accessibility Features

The cities are incorporating various features to enhance accessibility and make their service portals easier to navigate using plain language. Louisville's Metro311 portal uses intuitive navigation aids like a 'Find Services' search and an 'I Want To…' section to help residents quickly find popular content. Richmond's RVA311 site provides FAQs and 'How do I…' guidance to answer common questions directly. Pittsburgh's new 311 system was designed with a 'simpler, more efficient way for residents to submit service requests' and now includes Service Level Agreements (SLAs) to give residents clear expectations on timelines. Raleigh's 'Ask Raleigh' portal was explicitly 'designed to be faster, smarter, and more intuitive'. Chattanooga's platform uses 'refined and expanded categories' to help residents better describe their requests without needing to know internal city jargon. Furthermore, accessibility is enhanced through multiple channels, such as Durham's 24/7 SMS service and New Orleans' AI-powered chatbot 'Jazz', which is available 24/7 via text and the city website to provide immediate answers.

# Cross City Comparison

A comparative analysis of the nine cities reveals a widespread commitment to digital transformation, though the strategies and levels of technological adoption vary. All cities have successfully implemented unified service portals and/or mobile apps, establishing a foundational digital channel for resident services. However, the adoption of more advanced technologies shows greater divergence. New Orleans stands out as a leader in artificial intelligence with its 'Jazz' AI-powered chatbot, which has been live since 2021 and is accessible via text and web. In contrast, Pittsburgh and Durham are in the planning or procurement stages for similar chatbot technology, while the other cities have not yet indicated plans for AI implementation. SMS-based service is another area of differentiation; Durham and New Orleans are the only two cities offering live SMS intake channels. A significant common trend is the active replacement of older platforms. Pittsburgh (MyBurgh to PGH311), Raleigh (SeeClickFix to Ask Raleigh), and Chattanooga (CHATT311 to CHA 311) have all undertaken major system migrations, indicating a collective move towards more modern, integrated, and user-friendly solutions rather than maintaining legacy software.
