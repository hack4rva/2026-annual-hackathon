# Executive Summary

For the 2025-2026 period, the City of Richmond's Department of Procurement Services (DPS) is staffed with 32 positions, an increase from prior years, though specific caseloads per staffer are not publicly reported. The department faces challenges with lengthy procurement cycles, with the average Request for Proposal (RFP) taking 307 days to complete in FY2023, against a future target of 150 days. In terms of equity, Richmond's Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) has a performance target of 9% for minority spend in FY2025, with historical performance hovering around 10%. This is distinct from the broader Virginia state goal of achieving a 42% utilization rate for Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned (SWaM) businesses. The city maintains a high degree of transparency, publicly reporting contract data, bid outcomes, and sourcing documents. However, the procurement function is under significant oversight, evidenced by a recent Inspector General report on MBE compliance, a major 'p-card reset' in 2025 that drastically reduced the number of purchasing cards to tighten controls, and an ongoing focus from the City Auditor on unresolved high-risk issues related to internal controls citywide.

# Staff Caseload And Workload Analysis

The City of Richmond does not publish an official caseload statistic, such as contracts per staffer, for its Department of Procurement Services. While the city's Open Data portal provides monthly updates on active and inactive contract records, a total annual number of contracts awarded is not reported, which prevents the calculation of a precise average caseload. However, the department's workload can be inferred from its performance metrics on contract processing times. In FY2023, the average time to complete a Request for Proposal (RFP) was 307 days, and the average time from advertisement to award for an Invitation for Bid (IFB) was 118 days. These lengthy timelines suggest a significant workload and potential backlogs. The city has set ambitious targets for FY2025 to reduce these times to 150 days for RFPs and 90 days for IFBs, indicating an effort to improve efficiency and manage the existing workload more effectively.

# Public Reporting Of Equity Data

The City of Richmond tracks and publicly reports its procurement spending with Minority Business Enterprises (MBE) and Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned (SWaM) businesses. The Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) reports on the "% of Minority Spend" in the city's budget performance section. For FY2025, the performance target was set at 9%, with historical spending ranging from approximately $27 million to $50 million annually. For public transparency, the city maintains a Procurement Transparency Dashboard. This dashboard directs users to two key resources: the Open Data Portal, which provides monthly updated datasets of active and inactive contracts including agency, contract number, dollar amount, and supplier; and the OpenGov portal, which manages all formal invitations for bids (IFBs) and requests for proposals (RFPs). The OpenGov portal also publishes bid results, including the names of successful and unsuccessful bidders and their bid amounts.

# Richmond Performance Against Equity Goals

The City of Richmond's performance in directing procurement dollars to minority-owned businesses generally meets its own internal targets. Historically, the city has reported that approximately 10% of its spending goes to Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs), which aligns with its historical target of 10%. For fiscal year 2025, the city has set a new target of 9% for minority spend. While the city meets its own goals, it is important to note the distinction between the city's target and the state's objective. The Commonwealth of Virginia has established a more ambitious goal of a 42% utilization rate for the broader category of Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned (SWaM) businesses. The city's MBE-specific goal of 9-10% is substantially lower than the state's comprehensive SWaM goal. The provided information tracks Richmond's performance against its MBE goal but does not provide a figure for the city's total SWaM utilization to directly compare against the 42% state target.

# Barriers For Small And Minority Vendors

While the provided documents do not contain a formal disparity study explicitly listing all barriers, they indicate potential challenges for small and minority-owned vendors within Richmond's procurement process. A City Inspector General report on MBE Compliance highlights that common barriers discussed in local oversight include complexities related to "documentation and goal-verification processes." This suggests that the administrative requirements for proving eligibility and compliance can be burdensome. In an effort to address this, the city has adopted B2GNow, a compliance monitoring and goal-setting software, to streamline tracking. Additionally, the city's own performance metrics reveal long procurement cycle times. In FY2023, the average time from initiation to completion for a Request for Proposal (RFP) was 307 days, and the average time from advertisement to award for an Invitation for Bid (IFB) was 118 days. These extended timelines can act as a significant barrier for smaller firms that may lack the financial resilience and operational capacity to endure such a lengthy process before a contract is awarded.

# Public Data Release Considerations

The City of Richmond already practices a high level of procurement transparency, making a significant amount of data public. Contract records, including agency, dollar amount, and supplier, are published monthly on its Open Data Portal. Furthermore, all formal Invitations for Bids (IFBs) and Requests for Proposals (RFPs), along with their outcomes, are managed through the OpenGov portal. This includes competitively sensitive information such as the names of unsuccessful bidders and their bid amounts for awarded contracts. The primary legal constraint governing the release of further data is the Virginia Freedom of Information Act (FOIA). FOIA dictates what information can be legally redacted, typically pertaining to proprietary or confidential details submitted by bidders that could harm their competitive position if released. The city's current public-facing information does not specify any additional local privacy constraints beyond those established by state procurement law and FOIA.

# Recent Oversight And Media Coverage

## Planned Procurement Assessment

## P Card Audit Summary

On April 30, 2025, the city administration announced a 'p-card reset' to tighten internal controls over its purchasing card program. This action, which followed reviews highlighting control weaknesses, resulted in a material reduction in the number of active p-cards citywide, from 320 down to 67. This measure was implemented to improve oversight and reduce the risk of misuse.

## Unresolved Audit Issues

The Office of the City Auditor (OCA), in its FY2025 Annual Report, highlighted unresolved high-risk issues across city departments. The report noted findings of 'inadequate internal controls, oversight,' which provides the broader context for the increased scrutiny on financial and procurement-related activities. While not exclusively focused on the Department of Procurement Services, these citywide findings directly impact procurement functions and signal continued oversight in this area.

# Office Of Minority Business Development Role

## Mission

Based on city ordinance, the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) is charged with the verification of Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) and ensuring compliance with the city's Minority Business requirements.

## Vision

The provided documentation does not contain a stated vision for the Office of Minority Business Development.

## Key Objectives

The primary objectives of the OMBD include: tracking and reporting on the percentage of city procurement dollars spent with minority-owned businesses, with a performance target of 9% for FY2025; verifying the status of businesses as Minority Business Enterprises; ensuring compliance with minority business goals on city contracts; and utilizing compliance monitoring and goal-setting software (B2GNow) to streamline these processes.
