# Executive Summary

As of 2025-2026, Richmond's RVA311 system experienced a significant surge in usage, with total requests jumping to 208,216 in 2025 from 83,000 in 2024. The phone remains a critical channel, with call center agents resolving over 116,000 concerns directly over the phone in 2025 without needing to create a formal service request. A precise breakdown of usage across phone, mobile app, and web portal is not publicly available. From an equity perspective, a notable digital divide exists, as U.S. Census data (2020-2024) indicates that 13.8% of households in Richmond lack a broadband internet subscription, potentially limiting their access to digital 311 channels. To address language barriers, the city has a formal Language Access Plan (effective July 2023) and provides free professional interpretation services. However, its website relies on Google Translate for initial translation, with a disclaimer advising users to call for professional services. No formal, published usability studies or pain point reports specific to the RVA311 system were found, though the city initiated a general Community Survey in early 2026 to gauge satisfaction with overall city services.

# Documented User Pain Points

While no formal report documenting user pain points was found, several challenges and difficulties for residents can be inferred from the available data and policies:

1.  **Digital Access Barrier:** The most significant documented barrier is the digital divide. With 13.8% of households lacking a broadband internet subscription, a substantial portion of the population may find it difficult or impossible to use the RVA311 mobile app or web portal. This forces reliance on the phone channel, which may not be convenient for all users or situations, and limits access to the benefits of digital self-service, such as tracking requests online.

2.  **Language Access Complexity for Web Users:** For Limited English Proficient (LEP) residents, navigating the system presents a multi-step challenge. The RVA.gov website, including the 311 pages, uses Google Translate for initial translation. However, the city itself posts a disclaimer stating this is a "generic translation" and is not a substitute for professional services. This means an LEP user must first navigate the machine-translated site and then understand that to get accurate help, they must call the 311 phone number. This creates an extra burden and a potential point of confusion or failure for non-English speakers trying to access services digitally.

3.  **Lack of Transparency in Channel Performance:** The absence of a publicly available, detailed breakdown of request volume by channel (phone, app, web) is a systemic issue. It prevents residents, community advocates, and researchers from fully understanding how the system is being used, which channels are most effective, and where investment or improvements may be needed. This lack of data can be a pain point for those seeking to hold the system accountable or advocate for change.

# Policy On Machine Translation

## Policy For Vital Documents

The city's policy requires that 'vital documents' be professionally translated to ensure meaningful access to city programs and services. This is coordinated by city departments and the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE). Machine translation is not considered a substitute for this requirement.

## Website Disclaimer Text

RVA.gov usa Google Translate™ para proveer una traducción genérica en el idioma de su preferencia.
