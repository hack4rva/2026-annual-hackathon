# Executive Summary

As of 2025-2026, mid-size U.S. cities comparable to Richmond, VA, are actively modernizing their 311 and resident service systems by adopting robust, multi-channel platforms. A primary trend is the implementation of enterprise-grade Customer Relationship Management (CRM) systems as the backbone for 311 services. Cities like Pittsburgh and Baltimore have adopted Salesforce, identifiable by their Salesforce Experience Cloud web portals. Raleigh made a significant move in 2026 by replacing its SeeClickFix tool with a system built on ServiceNow. Richmond itself launched its RVA311 service on AvePoint Citizen Services, which is built on Microsoft technology. This indicates a strategic split between adopting highly customizable, general-purpose enterprise platforms (Salesforce, ServiceNow) and specialized GovTech solutions that may be built on major cloud infrastructure (AvePoint on Microsoft). The core strategic priority is to provide residents with multiple access channels, including web portals, native mobile apps (iOS/Android), and traditional phone access, as seen in Charlotte, Baltimore, and Norfolk. The stated goals for these modernizations, as noted in Pittsburgh's launch, are to enhance resident engagement and make it easier to report issues and track their progress. Notably, despite interest in the topic, there is no verifiable evidence in the provided sources of successful, outcome-documented implementations of AI-powered chatbots, LLM-based navigators, or deterministic wizard-style tools in these cities.

# Platform Comparison Summary

The 311 platform strategies observed in comparable cities reveal a key divergence between two main approaches: leveraging a highly customizable, general-purpose CRM versus adopting a purpose-built government technology (GovTech) solution.

1. **General-Purpose CRM (e.g., Salesforce, ServiceNow):** This approach involves building the 311 system on a broad, enterprise-grade platform. 
   - **Pittsburgh** explicitly migrated its 311 CRM to Salesforce in February 2025, with its public portal running on a Salesforce Experience Cloud site. 
   - **Baltimore** also utilizes a Salesforce Experience Cloud portal for its 311 services. 
   - **Raleigh** provides a compelling case study by replacing its former tool, SeeClickFix (a purpose-built solution), with 'Ask Raleigh' in 2026, which is implemented on the ServiceNow platform. This move suggests a strategic decision to favor the integration and customization capabilities of a larger enterprise platform over a more narrowly focused tool.

2. **Purpose-Built GovTech Solutions (e.g., AvePoint Citizen Services, SeeClickFix):** This approach uses software specifically designed for citizen service requests and government workflows.
   - **Richmond** deployed its RVA311 system using AvePoint Citizen Services. This solution is itself built on top of Microsoft Azure and Dynamics 365, illustrating a hybrid model where a GovTech vendor provides a tailored experience on top of a major technology stack.
   - **Raleigh** previously used SeeClickFix, a well-known player in this space, before its transition to ServiceNow.

In summary, while purpose-built solutions offer a focused, out-of-the-box experience for citizen requests, there is a clear trend towards adopting major enterprise platforms like Salesforce and ServiceNow. This shift is likely driven by the desire for greater flexibility, deeper integration with other city departments and systems, and the ability to create a more unified technology ecosystem.

# Role Of Data And Analytics

Modernized 311 systems are fundamentally designed to be data-driven, enabling cities to capture, track, and analyze resident service requests to improve operations and transparency. The platforms adopted by cities like Pittsburgh, Baltimore, and Norfolk serve as central repositories for vast amounts of service data.

A key function highlighted across these systems is the ability for both residents and city staff to track the status of requests in real-time. For instance, Pittsburgh's 2025 launch of its new CRM was aimed at making it easier for residents to 'track progress.' This tracking capability generates valuable data on request types, submission times, departmental response times, and resolution rates.

The most direct example of leveraging this data for public transparency comes from **Norfolk, VA**. The city maintains an open data portal specifically for its 'MyNorfolk' citizen services system. This dataset explicitly 'provides data from MyNorfolk, a citizen services portal that delegates and tracks requests and questions about the city of Norfolk.' By making this data public, Norfolk enables residents, researchers, and internal analysts to identify trends in service needs, pinpoint geographic hotspots for certain issues (e.g., potholes, missed trash collection), and hold departments accountable for service level agreements. While the provided sources do not detail specific operational efficiencies gained, the infrastructure for such analysis is a core component of these modernized 311 systems. The data collected is the raw material for improving resource allocation, managing departmental workloads, and making informed decisions about city services.

# Future Trends In 311 Modernization

Based on the current state of 311 modernization in 2025-2026, several future trends can be anticipated. The most significant emerging area involves the application of artificial intelligence, though its practical implementation remains nascent. The provided research found no verifiable, citable examples of cities successfully deploying AI-powered chatbots or LLM-based tools with documented outcomes. This gap suggests that while AI is a major topic of interest, cities are still in the exploratory phase, and the near future will likely involve pilot programs and initial experiments to determine how to effectively use these technologies to classify requests, assist residents, and reduce call center volume.

A more immediate and observable trend is the move towards **enterprise platform consolidation**. The decision by Raleigh in 2026 to replace a specialized GovTech tool (SeeClickFix) with an enterprise service management platform (ServiceNow) is indicative of a broader strategic shift. Cities are likely to increasingly favor platforms that can not only handle 311 requests but also integrate deeply with other municipal systems (e.g., asset management, finance, HR). This allows for a more holistic view of city operations and creates a foundation for more complex, cross-departmental workflows, moving beyond simple service request ticketing.

Finally, as these platforms become more integrated, the potential for **proactive and predictive service delivery** will grow. With years of service request data collected in systems like Salesforce or ServiceNow, cities will be better positioned to use predictive analytics to anticipate infrastructure failures (e.g., water main breaks in a specific area), forecast service demand, and allocate resources proactively rather than reactively. This represents the next frontier beyond the current focus on streamlining reactive service requests.
