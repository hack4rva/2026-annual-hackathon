# Pain Point Research — Procurement Risk & Opportunity Review

**Pillar:** A Thriving City Hall
**Problem Statement:** Help City staff identify valid, compliant contracts across City, state, and federal sources without manual PDF hunting.
**Applies to:** CivicPulse AI, Mira, Vendor Contract Mgmt, RVA Contract Lens
**Research Date:** March 31, 2026

---

## Pain Points by JTBD

### Job 1 — The Procurement Officer Facing a Renewal Deadline

**P1.1: The "Three Days to Verify One Contract" Problem**
RVA Contract Lens's demo explicitly cites a real Richmond case: staff spent three full days cross-referencing PDFs and databases to confirm a single recurring software renewal was still valid. With ~1,400 active contracts, this manual verification model is physically unscalable. Staff are forced to prioritize by deadline urgency, which means lower-urgency contracts receive no proactive review — they only surface when something goes wrong.

**P1.2: Fragmented Data Across Incompatible Sources**
Contract information relevant to a single decision is scattered across: the contract PDF itself, SAM.gov (federal compliance), VITA (state contracts), internal spreadsheets, vendor correspondence, and department file shares. No single system connects these. An officer checking compliance must open multiple browser tabs, different portals, and PDF readers — each with its own search interface and data format. This is not a technology gap but an architecture gap.

**P1.3: The Missed Renewal Window**
When a contract renewal deadline passes without action, the city faces three bad options: emergency procurement (expensive, reduced competition), contract lapse (service interruption), or retroactive approval (compliance risk). The RVA Contract Lens demo claims $28 million in potential savings from better competitive bidding — a figure grounded in the reality that missed windows default to incumbent renewal without price competition.

**P1.4: Vendor Concentration Risk**
Without portfolio-level visibility, procurement decisions are made contract-by-contract. This creates vendor concentration: a single vendor may hold a disproportionate share of a department's budget without anyone noticing until it becomes a risk. RVA Contract Lens explicitly flags this, showing departments with high vendor concentration and calculating the financial exposure.

### Job 2 — The New Staff Member Who Inherited a Portfolio With No Context

**P2.1: Institutional Amnesia Is the Default**
Municipal procurement is a field with chronic knowledge loss. When a veteran procurement officer retires or transfers, the reasoning behind contract decisions — why this vendor, why this term, why this price — disappears. Successors must reconstruct context from incomplete records, or more often, simply start fresh. This leads to repeated mistakes: vendors who were previously problematic get renewed because no one remembers the history.

**P2.2: No Decision Audit Trail**
Most municipal procurement systems track the contract itself (dates, amounts, parties) but not the decision process (why it was renewed, what alternatives were considered, what compliance issues were flagged). Without a decision trail, new staff have data without context — which is often worse than no data at all.

**P2.3: Onboarding Costs Compound Over Time**
Every staff transition creates a period of degraded procurement quality. The new person makes decisions with less information, which means worse outcomes. Over a career of 5-10 staff transitions touching the same contract portfolio, the cumulative cost of lost context is enormous — but invisible because no one tracks it.

### Job 3 — The Resident or Watchdog Asking "Where Does the Money Go?"

**P3.1: Public Procurement Data Is Technically Public, Practically Invisible**
Procurement data in most cities is "open" in the sense that it's theoretically available via FOIA. It is not open in the sense that a resident can find, read, and understand it. The data is locked in PDFs, buried in meeting minutes, formatted in procurement jargon, and scattered across multiple systems. The gap between legal transparency and practical transparency is the entire problem.

**P3.2: FOIA as the Only Access Path**
When the only way to see procurement data is to file a FOIA request, the system advantages those with the time, knowledge, and persistence to use FOIA — primarily journalists, lawyers, and advocacy organizations. Regular residents who wonder "how is my tax money being spent?" have no practical path to answers.

**P3.3: MBE/SWaM Participation Is Hard to Track**
One of the most asked equity questions in municipal procurement — "are minority-owned businesses getting a fair share?" — is often impossible to answer without manual analysis. Certification databases, contract award records, and spending data live in different systems. No city-facing tool aggregates this in real time.

## Cross-Cutting Equity Dimension

**Procurement Equity Is Budget Equity**
How a city spends its procurement dollars directly affects which businesses thrive, which communities benefit from city contracts, and whether wealth circulates locally or leaks to out-of-state vendors. Tools that make procurement more transparent and efficient also have the potential to surface inequity — or, if poorly designed, to reinforce it by automating biased patterns.

**AI Risk in Procurement**
AI-powered contract review introduces a specific equity risk: if the scoring model penalizes small or minority-owned vendors for characteristics correlated with size rather than quality (e.g., fewer past contracts, smaller insurance policies), the automation can systematize disadvantage. Every demo that uses AI for procurement decisions must document how its scoring model handles this.
