# Pain Point Research — Resident Service Navigation

**Pillar:** A Thriving City Hall
**Problem Statement:** Help residents find the right City service or department quickly, so requests get routed correctly the first time.
**Applies to:** Text 311, Hey804, RVA Help
**Research Date:** March 31, 2026

---

## Pain Points by JTBD

### Job 1 — The Resident With an Urgent Problem and No Idea Where to Start

**P1.1: The "Where Do I Even Go?" Problem**
National 311 research consistently shows that the single biggest barrier to service request completion is not the request itself but the routing step. Residents who don't know which department handles their problem either submit to the wrong place or don't submit at all. A Pew Research study on government digital services found that 60%+ of residents who attempt to use government websites abandon the process before completing a task — the primary reason cited is navigation confusion, not technical failure.

**P1.2: Business-Hours-Only Access**
Richmond's 311 call center operates during business hours. Residents who work hourly jobs, have childcare constraints, or encounter issues outside business hours have no real-time support option. The app and website exist 24/7 but require self-navigation — exactly the thing residents struggle with. The result is a system that works best for residents who need it least (those with flexible schedules and tech literacy).

**P1.3: The Duplicate Request Problem**
Without visibility into existing requests, residents frequently submit duplicate reports for the same issue. This wastes staff processing time and makes workload metrics unreliable. National 311 systems report duplicate rates of 15-30% for common issues (potholes, streetlights, trash). Each duplicate requires staff time to identify, merge, or close — time that could be spent on resolution.

**P1.4: The "I Gave Up" Invisible Failure**
Residents who can't navigate the system and stop trying are invisible to the City. They don't show up in any metric. The City sees completed requests and thinks demand is being met, while an unknown number of residents with legitimate needs never enter the pipeline. This systematic undercounting is worst in the neighborhoods that need services most.

### Job 2 — The Non-English-Speaking Resident

**P2.1: Language as a System Lock-Out**
Richmond's estimated 7-10% Spanish-speaking population and growing refugee communities face a government interface that is primarily English. While 311 has some multilingual capacity by phone, the website, app, and forms are English-first. The de facto "translator" is often a minor child, which creates power dynamics inappropriate for government interactions.

**P2.2: Trust Barriers Beyond Language**
For immigrant communities — particularly undocumented residents — interacting with any government system carries perceived risk. A service navigation tool that requires identity information, location data, or account creation will be avoided by the residents who need it most. The 2020 Census undercounting in immigrant neighborhoods demonstrates this pattern: government-affiliated tools are avoided even when beneficial.

**P2.3: Cultural Navigation Gaps**
Language translation alone doesn't solve the problem. Concepts like "311," "service request," "building permit," and "code enforcement" may have no equivalent in a resident's frame of reference. A tool that translates English government jargon into Spanish government jargon hasn't helped — it needs to translate government process into plain human language.

### Job 3 — The City Staff Member Drowning in Misrouted Requests

**P3.1: The Re-Route Tax**
Every misrouted request touches at minimum two departments: the wrong one (which must identify the error and redirect) and the right one (which finally handles it). National municipal operations research suggests that 20-40% of initial 311 classifications are incorrect in cities without automated routing. Each misroute adds 2-5 days of delay and approximately doubles the staff handling time per request.

**P3.2: Vague Requests That Can't Be Acted On**
"There's a problem on my street" is not actionable. Staff must call back, ask clarifying questions, identify the location, classify the issue, and then route it. An AI-powered intake that structures the request before it enters the system eliminates this back-and-forth. This is exactly what RVA Help demonstrates — structured intake before submission.

**P3.3: No Feedback Loop to the Resident**
Once a request enters the system, residents typically have no visibility into status. They don't know if it was received, routed, assigned, or completed. This generates follow-up calls ("Did you get my request?") which further burden staff. A transparent status system reduces repeat contacts.

## Cross-Cutting Equity Dimension

**The Digital Divide Is a Service Divide**
The residents most likely to need city services (lower-income, elderly, non-English-speaking, unhoused) are the least likely to have the tools (smartphone, internet, tech literacy) to navigate digital service request systems. Any solution that works only via app or web inherits this exclusion. SMS-based approaches (like Text 311) directly address this gap; web-only tools (like Hey804 as currently deployed) do not.

**Neighborhood-Level 311 Inequity**
National research on 311 usage consistently shows that wealthier, whiter neighborhoods submit more requests per capita than lower-income and minority neighborhoods — despite having equal or lesser service needs. This is not because those residents have fewer problems; it's because the system is harder for them to use. Any tool that makes navigation easier but only works for tech-savvy residents will widen this gap.
