# From Data Silos to Civic Trust: Scaling Richmond's City-Activity Transparency System

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The City Activity Transparency System must evolve from a localized civic hackathon concept into a regional governance initiative. Research indicates that monolithic "smart city" dashboards fail when grant funding expires, whereas systems embedded in daily operations and executive review—such as STAT-style programs—achieve lasting impact. By shifting the focus from merely publishing data to governing with data, Richmond can leverage this tool to fulfill Mayor Danny Avula's "A Thriving City Hall" mandate. 

Expanding the universe of this idea reveals that it should not be solely a city-owned IT project. Instead, it requires a split-stack architecture: an internal Common Operating Picture (COP) managed by the City for real-time emergency and operational response, paired with a batch-updated public scorecard hosted by a civic nonprofit or regional body. This hybrid approach unlocks federal funding streams like USDOT SMART Grants [1] and EPA Exchange Network Grants [2], mitigates municipal procurement bottlenecks, and builds community trust through independent oversight.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Ownership**

* **What the City Should Own:** The internal Common Operating Picture (COP). The City of Richmond must retain ownership of the secure, near-real-time GIS layers integrating 911, 311, and fleet data. This ensures compliance with privacy regulations and operational security for emergency management.
* **What Partners Should Own:** The public-facing transparency scorecard. A local nonprofit (e.g., Community Foundation for a greater Richmond) or a regional body (e.g., PlanRVA) should own the resident-facing dashboard. This reduces the City's long-term IT maintenance burden and increases public trust by having an independent entity curate the equity and performance metrics.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The underlying open-data hosting infrastructure for the public view. Relying entirely on internal city servers for public transparency creates bottlenecks; leveraging state-level platforms like the Virginia Open Data Portal [3] or regional hubs ensures better uptime and interoperability.

* **City of Richmond Capital Project Dashboard:** An interactive map exploring active capital improvement projects [17]. *Lesson: Richmond already has the foundational GIS capability; the new system should integrate with, rather than replace, this tool.*
* **Smart Columbus Operating System:** A monolithic smart city OS that hosted 3,000 datasets but failed after its grant expired. *Lesson: Avoid building a massive, grant-dependent "operating system" that lacks integration with daily municipal workflows.*

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential. 
* **Regional Shift:** If the frame becomes regional (partnering with PlanRVA), the system can track cross-jurisdictional issues like transit (GRTC GTFS feeds) [18] and environmental resilience [10]. This unlocks larger state and federal resilience grants.
* **Statewide Shift:** Aligning with the Virginia Open Data Portal [3] standardizes data formats, making Richmond eligible for state-level IT modernization funds.
* **Nonprofit Ownership Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the City is relieved of UI/UX maintenance and public marketing, allowing municipal IT to focus strictly on data pipeline reliability.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Silos & Technical Integration:** Richmond's existing systems (e.g., 311 and fleet data) may have "data walls." Mitigation: Use tiered latency (batch updates for public, real-time for internal) to reduce API load.
* **Sustainability & Grant Dependence:** Building a custom platform with a one-time grant leads to abandonment. Mitigation: Build on existing commodity tools (ArcGIS Hub) [19] and secure a nonprofit partner for ongoing public maintenance.
* **Community Trust & Data Fatigue:** Publishing hundreds of raw datasets overwhelms residents. Mitigation: Curate the public view to 15-20 high-impact, equity-sensitive KPIs.
* **Procurement:** Traditional municipal procurement for custom software is slow. Mitigation: Use the hybrid model where a nonprofit partner procures the public-facing web development.
