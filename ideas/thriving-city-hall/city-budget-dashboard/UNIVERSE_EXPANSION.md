# From Static Data to Civic Power: Scaling Richmond's Budget Transparency Universe

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Richmond City Budget Dashboard is currently a high-quality "historical artifact" rather than a live governance tool. While the hackathon demo proves technical feasibility using FY14–FY22 data, its true value lies in transitioning from a static Tableau workbook to a dynamic fiscal infrastructure that bridges the gap between the Mayor's Action Plan (MAP) and actual spending. To survive beyond the hackathon cycle, the project must move toward a Hybrid Ownership Model: the City of Richmond must own the "Data Pipeline" (backend), while a non-partisan civic partner (e.g., a regional nonprofit or academic institution) owns the "Narrative and Engagement" layer (frontend).

Expanding this universe requires shifting the frame from "transparency for transparency's sake" to "fiscal accountability as a service." By leveraging federal SLFRF (ARPA) flexibility for data infrastructure [1] and aligning with regional data governance initiatives, Richmond can transform this dashboard into a regional benchmark. The goal is not just to show where the money went, but to enable participatory oversight—allowing Council and residents to simulate budget trade-offs in real-time.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Provisioned / Nonprofit-Led)**

* **What the City Should Own:** The Data Pipeline and API. The Department of Finance and the Office of Budget & Strategic Planning must own the "Single Source of Truth." They should provide a live, machine-readable API of budget vs. actuals to prevent the current issue of stale data (last updated July 2021).
* **What a Partner Should Own:** The User Experience and Narrative. A nonprofit (e.g., Code for RVA or a local university) or regional body like PlanRVA should host the dashboard. This prevents the sanitization of data and ensures the tool remains active even across mayoral transitions.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The Community Engagement/Advocacy layer. If the City owns the feedback mechanisms, residents may perceive it as a black hole. An external partner can synthesize public feedback into independent reports for City Council.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Checkbook NYC (New York City):** Operated by the Office of the New York City Comptroller, this is a first-of-its-kind online transparency tool providing unprecedented access to track how the government spends its annual budget [13]. It has been ranked the top transparency tool in the nation [14]. *Lesson:* Independent oversight (Comptroller vs. Mayor) ensures trust and longevity.
* **New Orleans Office of Performance and Accountability:** Integrates performance-based budgeting, which relates funding to expected results [16], and actively encourages residents to participate in the budget process to provide feedback on priorities [15]. *Lesson:* Dashboards must be tied to active community feedback loops.
* **Norfolk Balancing Act (Contextual):** Uses simulation tools to let residents simulate budget tradeoffs. *Lesson:* Moving from passive viewing to active participation increases civic engagement.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **Why it might be too small as a Richmond project:** The dashboard is currently a symptom-level fix for data silos. If framed only as a Richmond project, it risks dying when the Tableau license expires or the hackathon momentum fades.
* **Regional Frame:** If PlanRVA adopts this, it becomes a Regional Fiscal Health Monitor. PlanRVA already collects data to make sure goals for the region are met [9]. A regional frame allows Chesterfield, Henrico, and Richmond to see where they are duplicating services and where regionalization saves money.
* **Statewide Frame:** Scaling the LLM Pillar Mapping logic could create a Commonwealth Budget Explorer, aligning with state data governance goals.
* **Nonprofit Ownership:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data, it ensures the tool remains an independent accountability mechanism rather than a PR tool.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Rot and Integrity (High Risk):** Richmond's Socrata budget dataset was last updated in July 2021. If the dashboard relies on stale data, it loses all credibility.
* **Political Feasibility (High Risk):** Visualizing sensitive spending (e.g., Police vs. Social Services) is inherently political. The Mayor's office may be hesitant to fund a tool that makes unfavorable trends easy to see.
* **Maintenance Burden (Medium Risk):** Tableau requires active management. If the hackathon team disappears, the dashboard breaks the next time the City changes its account codes.
* **Trust and Legitimacy:** If the City owns the feedback loop, residents may not trust that their input is being objectively reviewed.
