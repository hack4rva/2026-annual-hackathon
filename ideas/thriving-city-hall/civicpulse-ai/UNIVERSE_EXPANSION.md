# From PDF Scraper to Procurement Engine: A Strategic Roadmap for CivicPulse AI in Richmond and Beyond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

CivicPulse AI currently exists as a high-potential "capability demo" that solves a symptom (manual PDF review) rather than the systemic disease (fragmented procurement intelligence). To move beyond a hackathon project, the idea must pivot from a standalone tool to a **Regional Procurement Intelligence Layer**. By integrating with Virginia’s eVA system and federal SAM.gov exclusions, and by aligning with Richmond’s Administrative Regulation 2.13 on AI governance, this tool can transition from a "nice-to-have" dashboard to a mandatory compliance and efficiency engine. 

The "universe" for this idea is not just Richmond City Hall; it is the broader regional footprint. The most viable path forward is a hybrid ownership model where the City of Richmond pilots the workflow, but a regional body or a civic-tech nonprofit maintains the open-source core to allow for multi-jurisdictional cost-sharing and data aggregation.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Piloted / Regional-Owned / Open-Source Core)**

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** The **Data Policy and Workflow Integration**. The City should define the "Urgency Flags" and "Risk Indicators" specific to Richmond’s charter and AR 2.13.
* **What a Regional Partner (e.g., PlanRVA) should own:** The **Hosting and Multi-Tenant Infrastructure**. By hosting at the regional level, surrounding counties and Richmond can share the cost of the AI tokens and API maintenance. PlanRVA already manages regional programs and conducts procurements on behalf of the region [1] [2].
* **What should NOT be City-owned:** The **Software Development Lifecycle**. To avoid the "zombie software" trap, the code should be maintained by a civic-tech collective or a nonprofit partner (e.g., U.S. Digital Response) under an open-source license.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **eVA (Virginia's eProcurement Portal):** The Department of General Services utilizes eVA as its primary electronic procurement portal, linking buyers and sellers across state agencies and local governments [25] [26] [27]. *Lesson:* CivicPulse AI should not try to replace eVA, but rather pull data from it or act as a local intelligence layer on top of it.

* **Open Contracting Partnership (Lift Accelerator):** Supports US cities in leveraging procurement data to promote stronger local economies and transparency [18] [30]. *Lesson:* Transparency and open data are key drivers for external funding and support.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why is this idea too small framed only as a Richmond project?**
Procurement staff manually search SAM.gov, eVA, and local portals separately. If CivicPulse AI only serves Richmond, it remains a siloed PDF reader. 

**What changes if the frame becomes regional?**
By shifting to a regional frame with PlanRVA, which already facilitates the recognition and analysis of regional opportunities [14], the tool becomes a shared utility. Henrico, Chesterfield, and Richmond can share the cost of AI infrastructure, and the system can flag "Cooperative Purchasing" opportunities across jurisdictions.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Silos & Integration:** The hackathon demo lacks real data. It must connect to the SODA API and external registries. Without SAM.gov API integration (which any user can request for public data [31] [32]), the tool fails its core compliance mission.
* **Procurement "Catch-22":** The City may struggle to "buy" a tool built by a hackathon team without a formal RFP. Partnering with USDR [16] can help bypass this by providing pro-bono modernization support.
* **AI Hallucinations & Trust:** Extracting contract values incorrectly could lead to budget errors. The system must maintain its "Two-Tile Layout" for human-in-the-loop verification to comply with AI governance standards.
