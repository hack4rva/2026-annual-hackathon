## 1. Executive Summary

The Contract-Payment Drift Scanner represents a critical evolution in municipal fiscal oversight, transforming static open data into an active audit intelligence layer. While originally conceived as a Richmond-specific tool to support Mayor Danny Avula's "A Thriving City Hall" pillar, the universe of this idea extends far beyond city limits. By cross-referencing contract values with actual payment registers, the scanner addresses a systemic vulnerability that plagues local governments nationwide: procurement overruns, unauthorized order splitting, and chronic payment delays that disproportionately harm nonprofit service providers. 

Expanding this idea requires shifting from a purely city-owned utility to a hybrid civic infrastructure model. Evidence from peer cities demonstrates that while the city must retain data sovereignty, partnering with civic-tech nonprofits and regional data trusts accelerates development and unlocks diverse funding streams. ## 2. Expanded Ownership Recommendation

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Data + Nonprofit-Operational Lead)**

To maximize agility, funding potential, and community trust, the Drift Scanner should adopt a hybrid ownership structure. 

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the underlying data pipelines, the legal authority to publish the Socrata datasets (`xqn7-jvv2` and `5y73-enav`), and the internal policy enforcement mechanisms. The city acts as the data steward, ensuring that the information feeding the scanner is accurate, timely, and compliant with municipal regulations.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A civic-tech nonprofit (such as the Richmond Spend Mirror Coalition or Code for America) should own the analytical layer, the user interface, and the community outreach strategy. This allows the platform to iterate rapidly outside of slow municipal procurement cycles and positions the tool to receive philanthropic funding that governments cannot directly access.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The resident-facing dashboard and the open-source code repository should not be exclusively city-owned. Keeping the codebase in the civic commons ensures it can be adopted by other jurisdictions and maintained by a broader community of developers, reducing the city's long-term technical debt.

## 5. Existing Efforts / Prior Art

Analyzing peer cities reveals that while the mathematical logic of drift scanning is simple, the operational execution requires careful data governance.

* **Portland, OR:** Portland successfully implemented the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS) to make procurement data accessible [13] [14]. They mapped original contract amounts directly to total invoiced amounts. *Lesson:* Standardized data schemas are critical for automated overrun detection.
* **New York City, NY:** NYC faced a massive backlog of unpaid nonprofit contracts. Mayor Adams launched the "Clear the Backlog Initiative," unlocking over $4.2 billion in contractual dollars for nonprofits [9]. *Lesson:* Payment drift is a political crisis that requires executive sponsorship to resolve; framing the scanner as a tool to protect nonprofits builds political will.
* **Philadelphia, PA:** A Pew study found that 90% of Philadelphia's government contracts were paid late, forcing nonprofits to dip into reserves [1] [2]. *Lesson:* Lack of real-time payment visibility directly harms the civic ecosystem.
* **Austin, TX:** Austin publishes its authorized spending limits alongside inception-to-date orders on its Socrata portal [15] [16]. *Lesson:* Exposing the gap between authorized limits and actual spend in plain sight acts as a deterrent to overruns.
* **San Francisco, CA & Chicago, IL:** Audits in these cities uncovered improper single-payment vouchers and split purchase orders designed to evade oversight thresholds [17] [18]. *Lesson:* The scanner must include rule-based flags for split POs (e.g., multiple purchases just below the $10,000 threshold).

## 6. Scale Shift Analysis

Framing the Drift Scanner solely as a Richmond project limits its potential impact and funding viability. 

* **Regional Expansion:** Expanding to include regional bodies (like GRTC or PlanRVA) increases the detection surface for anomalies. However, this requires complex Data-Sharing Agreements and harmonized contract identifiers across different ERP systems.
* **Statewide Integration:** Linking the scanner to Virginia's eVA procurement system and the Commonwealth Data Trust [5] creates a unified procurement backbone. The Virginia Open Data Portal already hosts over 31,000 datasets [19], providing a rich environment for cross-jurisdictional analysis.
* **Nonprofit-Led Front-End:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides the data, the project can scale much faster. Nonprofits can iterate on the UI/UX without being bogged down by municipal IT procurement rules, while the city maintains the authoritative data source.
