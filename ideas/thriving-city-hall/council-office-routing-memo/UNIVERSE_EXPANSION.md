# From Switchboard to Service Hub: Expanding the Council-Office-Routing-Memo for Richmond and Beyond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Council Office Routing Memo is currently framed as a localized, tactical fix for Richmond's district offices, but its underlying architecture addresses a systemic municipal failure: the "human switchboard" problem. Across the country, city council offices act as inefficient middlemen for constituent services, leading to massive rework. In Los Angeles, for instance, 311 agents are forced to transfer over 40% of calls to other departments [1] [2]. By transforming this manual triage into a structured, API-ready routing memo, Richmond can eliminate duplicate tickets and establish a standardized knowledge base. 

Expanding the universe of this idea means shifting it from a standalone city IT project to a scalable, open-source civic asset. Funding streams from the USDOT SMART program [3] and HUD's CDBG public services allocations [4] offer federal backing for data-driven local government tools. Furthermore, by partnering with national civic innovation networks like the Beeck Center's Intergovernmental Software Collaborative [5] or Code for America [6], Richmond can co-develop a tool that not only serves its own Mayor's Action Plan but becomes a replicable standard for municipalities nationwide.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Civic-Tech Partnership**

The Council Office Routing Memo should not be entirely city-owned, nor should it be fully outsourced. A hybrid model ensures sustainability, continuous improvement, and regional scalability.

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** The underlying data taxonomy, the Open311 API integrations, and the governance policies (such as Service Level Agreements). The city must own the "truth" of which department handles which issue and the performance metrics associated with resolving them.
* **What partners should own:** A civic-tech nonprofit (such as the Beeck Center's Intergovernmental Software Collaborative or Code for America) should own the open-source codebase and the resident-facing/staff-facing application layer. This allows the software to be maintained, updated, and shared across jurisdictions without burdening Richmond's IT department with custom software maintenance.
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The proprietary application interface. Custom-built, city-owned platforms often become legacy burdens. By keeping the tool open-source and partner-maintained, Richmond avoids the trap of funding endless software updates and can focus purely on service delivery.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Los Angeles 311 Audit:** LA found that 40% of 311 calls required transfer to other departments because the first-tier call center could not resolve them [1] [2]. *Lesson:* Without a unified knowledge base and direct routing capabilities, frontline staff become bottlenecks. The routing memo must bypass this by providing direct, accurate departmental contacts immediately.
* **Houston Administrative Policy 2-23:** Houston implemented strict Citywide Service Request Response Standards, mandating SLAs for the 311 helpline [23]. *Lesson:* Technology alone doesn't fix routing. Richmond must pair the routing memo with a mayoral administrative policy that enforces response times and accountability.
* **San Diego 311 Planning:** A draft report highlighted that council offices frequently acted as switchboards, transferring calls due to a lack of centralized information [24]. *Lesson:* Council staff need a curated, easily accessible taxonomy (like the proposed memo tool) to prevent ad-hoc, error-prone transfers.
* **Lawrence, KS SeeClickFix Relaunch:** By revamping their CRM workflows, Lawrence achieved a 0.5-day average response time and a 7.1-day average closure time [25]. *Lesson:* Streamlined, well-configured intake tools drastically reduce resolution times and increase resident engagement.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond City Council project limits its potential. 
* **Regional Shift:** If the frame expands to the Greater Richmond area (PlanRVA), the tool could utilize a shared regional taxonomy. Residents often don't know if their issue falls under city or county jurisdiction; a regional routing memo could seamlessly direct Henrico or Chesterfield residents who mistakenly contact Richmond offices.
* **Statewide/National Shift:** By building the tool as an open-source module within the Intergovernmental Software Collaborative [5], Richmond transitions from a consumer of software to a producer. The city pilots the tool, but the partner scales it to hundreds of municipalities facing the exact same "switchboard" problem.
* **Nonprofit Ownership of the Interface:** If a nonprofit owns the application layer while Richmond provides the API data, the city is insulated from UI/UX maintenance costs and can focus purely on fulfilling the service requests generated by the tool.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Equity and Bias:** Relying heavily on digital 311 data can privilege wealthier, whiter neighborhoods that complain more frequently [6]. *Mitigation:* The routing memo must include a manual intake flag for council staff assisting low-digital-access residents via phone or walk-ins, ensuring their issues are tracked with equal priority.
* **Procurement Bottlenecks:** Traditional government procurement can delay software pilots by months or years. *Mitigation:* Structure the project as a technical assistance partnership (e.g., via NLC [18]) or utilize existing CDBG administrative funds [10] to bypass lengthy RFP processes for small-scale pilots.
* **Siloed Adoption:** If only one council district adopts the tool, the city's overall data remains fragmented. *Mitigation:* The Mayor's Office must mandate the tool's use across all districts through an administrative policy similar to Houston's AP 2-23 [23].
* **Maintenance Burden:** Custom software rots. *Mitigation:* Do not build a proprietary CRM. Build a lightweight, open-source generator hosted by a collaborative like the Beeck Center [5].
