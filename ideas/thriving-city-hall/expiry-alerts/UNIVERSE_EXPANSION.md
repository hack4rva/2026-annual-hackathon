The city must also own the internal policy mandate that requires procurement officers to acknowledge and act upon T-30 alerts.

Relying solely on city staff to manually recruit M/WBEs for every expiring contract is unscalable; this function is better suited for community partners and chambers of commerce who can use the alert data to mobilize their networks.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project leaves significant value on the table. 

* **Regional Shift:** If expanded to the Richmond region (e.g., via PlanRVA, which already coordinates regional resilience and transportation planning [17] [18]), the alert system could harmonize procurement timelines across adjacent counties. This allows M/WBEs to see a consolidated pipeline of upcoming regional opportunities, rather than navigating fragmented city-by-city portals.
* **Statewide Shift:** Virginia's Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity (SBSD) manages the SWaM certification program [19]. If the alert system's logic is adopted statewide, it could automatically cross-reference expiring contracts with the SWaM database, proactively notifying certified vendors of upcoming solicitations months in advance.
* **Nonprofit Layering:** If a nonprofit owns the resident/vendor-facing layer, the city avoids the administrative burden of vendor matchmaking. The city simply publishes the "expiring soon" data feed, and the nonprofit uses it to host targeted vendor fairs and capacity-building workshops.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Alert Fatigue:** If notifications are not tiered or if they lack actionable pathways, procurement officers will ignore them. Escalation logic (T-180, T-90, T-30) is critical.
* **Policy Alignment:** Alerts are useless without a policy mandate. If there is no consequence for ignoring a T-30 alert, emergency procurements will continue.
* **Data Quality:** While the `xqn7-jvv2` dataset currently has 0% missing expiration dates, maintaining this hygiene requires strict data governance.
* **Procurement Regulations:** Any automated default to open re-solicitation must comply with the Virginia Public Procurement Act (VPPA) and local ordinances regarding competitive negotiation and sealed bidding [20].
