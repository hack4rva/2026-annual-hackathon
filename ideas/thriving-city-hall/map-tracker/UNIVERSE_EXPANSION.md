# From Prototype to Policy: Scaling the RVA MAP Tracker into a Governance Operating System

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The RVA MAP Tracker is more than a dashboard; it is a high-signal prototype of institutional buy-in. While built in 48 hours, its success in securing real-time data from department heads suggests it has solved the "data desert" problem that plagues most municipal transparency projects. However, to survive beyond the current administration, the project must shift from a personality-driven "Mayor's tool" to a permanent "City Hall utility." The expanded universe for this idea lies in a Hybrid Ownership Model. By anchoring the data governance within the City's Information Technology or Performance office while partnering with an external academic or nonprofit "Accountability Steward" (like the VCU Wilder School), Richmond can ensure the platform remains a permanent fixture of civic life. 

Funding should transition from hackathon "sweat equity" to a sustainable mix of Bloomberg-backed technical assistance and local philanthropic capacity-building grants. This strategic positioning will not only fund the tracker but also help Richmond achieve What Works Cities Certification, a standard already met by 104 cities including neighboring Newport News [1] [2]. Ultimately, the MAP Tracker's true potential is realized when it evolves from tracking a single administration's promises to becoming the foundational operating system for regional civic accountability.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Ownership (City-Owned Backend, Partner-Owned Public UI)**

To ensure longevity and trust, the MAP Tracker should adopt a hybrid ownership model. 

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the data generation, the administrative interface, and the API infrastructure. Department heads must remain the "Metric Owners," and the data must flow directly from city systems (or the Richmond Open Data Portal) to ensure accuracy and operational alignment [3]. The City should also own the integration of this data into the annual budget process.
* **What Partners Should Own:** An external partner—ideally an academic institution like the VCU Wilder School Center for Public Policy or a civic tech nonprofit—should own the public-facing dashboard and the "auditing" of the data [4]. This provides a layer of academic neutrality and trust, ensuring that the public interface remains live and unscrubbed even during political transitions or when metrics trend negatively.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The City should not own the exclusive narrative rights to the public-facing dashboard. If the City controls the public UI entirely, there is a high risk of "political repudiation" where a future administration quietly sunsets the dashboard if performance lags.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **What Works Cities Certified Municipalities (e.g., Glendale, AZ; Cambridge, MA):** These cities achieved Platinum Certification by excelling in data-driven governance and turning data into real impact [24]. *Lesson:* Certification is a milestone, not an endpoint; continuous improvement and community engagement are required to sustain data practices.
* **Richmond Procurement Transparency Dashboard:** The City recently launched a dashboard to provide insights into city contracts, bids, and suppliers to build accountability and trust [25]. *Lesson:* The City is already actively investing in transparency tools; the MAP Tracker should integrate with this existing dashboard to show which vendors are funded to achieve specific MAP goals.
* **PlanRVA Near Miss Dashboard:** PlanRVA collects data on near misses and collisions to incorporate into project scoring [26]. *Lesson:* Regional bodies are successfully managing specialized data dashboards; this architecture could be adapted for broader strategic goals.
* **Virginia Open Data Portal:** Provides centralized access to over 31,000 datasets, fostering community engagement and standardizing data formats [21]. *Lesson:* The MAP Tracker should not silo its data; it must feed into or pull from established open data portals to maximize usability and discoverability.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing the MAP Tracker solely as a Richmond City Hall project limits its potential impact and funding opportunities.

### Expanding the Frame to the Richmond MSA
Richmond's core challenges—such as housing affordability, transit connectivity, and environmental sustainability—do not stop at the city limits. PlanRVA already facilitates holistic and coordinated planning across the region's nine localities [17] [18]. If the MAP Tracker evolves into a "Regional Vital Signs Hub," it can track cross-jurisdictional goals. This shift unlocks federal funding from agencies like the EDA, which supports Economic Development Districts with planning grants [12].

### The "Nonprofit-Led, City-Fed" Model
If the City owns the entire stack, the dashboard is vulnerable to political cycles. A mayor may choose to stop updating a dashboard if the metrics reflect poorly on their administration. By shifting to a model where the City provides the raw data via API, but a nonprofit or academic partner (like VCU) owns the resident-facing layer, the platform gains resilience. The partner ensures the dashboard remains live and holds the City accountable for continuous data provision, transforming the tool from a political asset into a permanent civic utility.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

Several critical blockers could derail the transition from prototype to permanent infrastructure:

* **Funding Deadlines:** Federal SLFRF (ARPA) funds, which are highly suitable for IT capacity and government services, must be expended by December 31, 2026 [7] [8]. Missing this window leaves a massive funding gap.
* **Data Fatigue and Maintenance Burden:** The hackathon relied on real-time, manual data entry by department heads. Over time, manual entry fails. The system must automate data feeds from the Richmond Open Data Portal or ERP systems to reduce the reporting burden [3].
* **Political Repudiation:** Dashboards that highlight failing metrics are often quietly decommissioned by leadership. Without a legal mandate (like a City Council ordinance) or an external partner holding the administration accountable, the tracker may not survive the next election cycle.
* **Procurement Lag:** Transitioning from a single-file web app to an enterprise-grade solution can take months or years through standard municipal procurement processes. The City must use the prototype as a bridge while navigating these hurdles.
