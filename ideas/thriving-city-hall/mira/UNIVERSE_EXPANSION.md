# Scaling Mira: From Richmond Hackathon to Virginia's AI Procurement Standard

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mira's transition from a 48-hour hackathon prototype to a permanent civic fixture requires shifting its identity from a "PDF Reader" to a "Regional Compliance Engine." While the current build excels at data extraction, its true strategic value lies in automating the cross-referencing of local contracts against state (eVA) and federal (SAM.gov) registries—a massive manual pain point for Richmond's procurement staff. 

To succeed, Mira should move toward a Hybrid Ownership Model. The City of Richmond should retain data governance, while a regional entity (like PlanRVA) or a civic tech nonprofit manages the software product. This prevents the "innovation silo" effect, allows for regional cost-sharing, and opens doors to federal SMART grants [1] and private philanthropic funding. By framing Mira as a "Compliance Checklist Accelerator" rather than a replacement for human auditors, the project can bypass the legal skepticism that has stalled similar AI efforts in jurisdictions like King County [2] and New York City [3].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Owned Data / Nonprofit-Led Product)**

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** Data governance, security protocols, and the "Final Decision" authority. The City must remain the "Customer Zero" and provide the subject matter expertise (SME) to define the compliance rules Mira checks against.
* **What a Partner (Nonprofit/Regional) should own:** The codebase, Azure environment, and feature roadmap. A nonprofit or a regional body like PlanRVA can iterate faster than City IT and can apply for grants (like the Microsoft Tech for Social Impact [4]) that the City cannot.
* **What should NOT be City-owned:** The core software development lifecycle. Municipalities often struggle to maintain custom-built software after the initial developers depart. By housing the "product" in a regional or nonprofit entity, Mira can be shared with surrounding counties and agencies like GRTC, creating a sustainable user base.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Oakland AI Contract Compliance Tracker:** Oakland issued an RFI for AI pilot projects, specifically seeking tools for contract-compliance tracking [17] [18]. *Lesson:* There is validated municipal demand for compliance tracking, not just text extraction.
* **NYC Comptroller MyCity Audit:** An audit of the MyCity portal revealed discrepancies between vendor accuracy claims and actual performance [3]. *Lesson:* AI tools must have a Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) verification layer to maintain trust and auditability.
* **King County AI Redaction:** King County successfully used AI for document processing [19], but the Prosecuting Attorney's Office later instructed police not to use AI for official reports due to error and perjury concerns [2] [20]. *Lesson:* Position Mira strictly as an advisory dashboard, not a generator of official legal determinations.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **The "Richmond-Only" Frame (Current):** Mira is a tool for a few staff members to read PDFs faster. It is a "nice-to-have" that risks being abandoned when the Azure credits run out or the hackathon team moves on.
* **The Regional Frame (PlanRVA/GRTC):** Mira becomes a Shared Services Portal. By aggregating contracts from Richmond, GRTC, and surrounding counties, the AI can identify regional efficiencies. This shifts the value from "time saved" to "regional cost avoidance."
* **The Statewide Frame (eVA Integration):** If Mira is built to ingest eVA open data [21], it could be marketed to all 900+ Virginia local governments [22] as an "eVA Plus" dashboard. This moves the project from a "hack" to a potential State-Standard Tool.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Privacy & Security:** Uploading unredacted contracts to a cloud-based AI requires formal security assessments.
* **The "Black Box" Problem:** If Mira flags a contract as "High Risk," procurement officers need to know why. Without explainable AI, legal counsel may prohibit its use, similar to the pushback seen in King County [2].
* **Maintenance Burden:** AI models evolve rapidly. Without a dedicated product owner, the extraction logic will break.
* **Procurement Policy:** Richmond's own procurement rules may make it difficult for the City to adopt Mira if it isn't funneled through a nonprofit partner or a regional grant.
