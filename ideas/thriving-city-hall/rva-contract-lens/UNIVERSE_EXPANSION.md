# RVA Contract Lens — Scaling AI-Powered Procurement Transparency from City Hall to the Region

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The RVA Contract Lens idea addresses a critical municipal pain point: managing nearly $7 billion in public contracts across fragmented systems. However, framing this solely as a City of Richmond internal tool limits its potential and ignores the realities of modern civic technology funding and maintenance. To truly succeed, the project must evolve from a localized hackathon concept into a regionally integrated, hybrid-owned platform. 

By adopting the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS) and leveraging regional cooperative purchasing networks, RVA Contract Lens can attract significant national funding from organizations like the National Science Foundation and Bloomberg Philanthropies. The most viable path forward is a hybrid ownership model where the City of Richmond maintains authority over its data and procurement policies, while a civic nonprofit or regional body manages the AI infrastructure, public portal, and API integrations. This approach mitigates the city's technical maintenance burden while maximizing public transparency and regional scalability.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Partnership**

The platform should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. A hybrid model distributes risk, leverages external funding, and ensures long-term sustainability.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the underlying procurement data, the policy decisions regarding vendor equity (MBE/SWaM), and the internal staff dashboard's final decision-making authority. The city is responsible for publishing its data in a standardized format (like OCDS).
* **What Partners Should Own:** A civic tech nonprofit or regional coalition (such as PlanRVA) should own the AI scoring engine, the public-facing transparency portal, and the maintenance of external API integrations (e.g., SAM.gov, FCC). 
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own or maintain the custom data aggregation pipelines or the machine learning models. Building a custom cross-level procurement aggregation platform from scratch is inefficient when vendor-led solutions and cooperative networks already exist.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing RVA Contract Lens solely as a Richmond project makes it too small for major federal grants and ignores the reality of regional vendor ecosystems. If the frame shifts to a **regional model** (e.g., partnering with PlanRVA or GRTC [17] [25]), the platform can deduplicate vendor vetting across neighboring counties, increasing its value proposition. If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the city is shielded from the continuous UI/UX maintenance burden, allowing city IT to focus purely on secure data publication. A pilot in Richmond that is designed for regional scaling is highly attractive to funders like the NSF CIVIC program [1].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **API Rate Limits & Terms of Service:** The demo's reliance on live federal APIs is a major risk. SAM.gov restricts non-federal users to just 10 requests per day [26], and its terms of use prohibit automated scraping [27]. The platform must build a cached query service rather than relying on real-time, per-click federated searches.
* **Equity Bias in Scoring:** The proposed transparent scoring logic (+/- points) risks penalizing vendor concentration and rewarding extensive compliance documentation, which inherently favors large, established vendors over small, minority-owned businesses (MBE/SWaM). The model requires explicit equity calibration.
* **Data Verification:** The hackathon demo claimed 8 live data source integrations, but none were verified as running against live APIs. Bridging the gap between a UI mockup and a live, secure data pipeline is the project's largest technical hurdle.
