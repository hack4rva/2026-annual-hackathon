# Beyond the Chatbot: Scaling RVA Help into a Regional Triage Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

RVA Help represents a critical shift from "automated answering" to "structured triage." While the hackathon demo successfully identified the "Intake Gap"—where vague resident reports lead to departmental friction—its long-term viability depends on moving away from a standalone app toward an integrated middleware layer. By connecting Richmond’s AvePoint/Dynamics 365 backend with a confidence-aware AI frontend, the project can transition from a student experiment to a robust civic utility.

The expanded universe for RVA Help lies in Regional Interoperability. By reframing the tool as a bridge between City 311 (infrastructure) and Regional 211 (human services), the project unlocks state-level funding and national innovation grants. The recommended path is a Hybrid Ownership Model: the City of Richmond maintains the data authority and API integrations, while a regional nonprofit or digital service coalition manages the resident-facing interface to ensure rapid iteration and cross-jurisdictional scaling.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Authorized / Nonprofit-Managed)**

To successfully scale RVA Help without creating a "Shadow IT" burden for city staff, ownership must be strategically divided:

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the **Data Authority and API Gateway**. The City must control the "Source of Truth" (AvePoint/Dynamics 365) and the security protocols for how data enters the municipal firewall. The city should not manage the AI model itself.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A regional body (like PlanRVA) or a civic tech nonprofit should own the **Resident Experience (UX) and AI Training**. A nonprofit partner can iterate on the chat interface, language models, and accessibility features much faster than city procurement allows, ensuring the tool remains responsive to resident needs.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The **Hosting Infrastructure for the AI Model**. To avoid high compute costs and technical debt, the AI processing layer should be managed by a third-party civic tech provider or a regional cloud instance shared across jurisdictions.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Nashville Auto311:**
 * *What was attempted:* A confidence-guided automated system for non-emergency calls.
 * *What worked:* Achieved an overall average accuracy of 94.49% for categorizing incidents by dynamically adjusting to shifting incident types and reducing follow-up conversations [20] [21].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* RVA Help must implement strict confidence thresholds. If the AI's confidence is low, it must ask clarifying questions rather than guessing.
* **Atlanta 311 Chatbot:**
 * *What was attempted:* A public service chatbot for a metropolitan city.
 * *What failed:* The chatbot demonstrated poor alignment with resident needs, returning Municipal Court address change forms in response to questions about police accountability [22].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* Unconstrained AI scope leads to poor user experiences. The taxonomy must be strictly disciplined.
* **NYC MyCity AI Chatbot:**
 * *What was attempted:* An AI chatbot to assist residents and business owners.
 * *What failed:* The chatbot malfunctioned and provided false information, encouraging illegal behavior [23].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* RVA Help must utilize a dual-intent classifier that hard-routes policy or legal questions to official static pages, reserving AI strictly for actionable service requests.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

### The Richmond-Only Frame
Currently, RVA Help is framed as a better frontend for RVA311. While it solves a local usability issue (vague requests), it struggles with the high cost of custom API maintenance for a single city and creates a parallel ticketing system that burdens staff.

### The Regional Frame
If RVA Help becomes a regional portal managed by PlanRVA, a resident in the City can report a pothole, while a resident in a neighboring county can report a drainage issue through the same interface. This spreads the maintenance cost across multiple localities and creates a seamless experience for residents who cross jurisdictional lines daily.

### The Statewide Frame
By framing this as an AI Intake Layer for Virginia, the project can integrate with 211 Virginia (operated by United Way). This allows the system to triage both infrastructure issues (311) and human service needs (211), significantly reducing the burden on human operators statewide and unlocking VITA funding [1] [14].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Technical Integration & Procurement:** The fundamental miss of the hackathon demo was building a standalone ticketing system. Richmond’s contract with AvePoint may restrict third-party "write" access to the database. This requires a staging API where the City reviews AI-structured requests before committing them.
* **Trust and Hallucination:** As seen in NYC, AI chatbots can provide false or illegal advice [23]. RVA Help must be constrained to only provide status updates pulled directly from AvePoint, with no generative text regarding timelines or policy.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Built by high school students, the project lacks enterprise support. The codebase must be transitioned to a professionalized entity (like PlanRVA or a contracted vendor) to ensure long-term sustainability.
