# Scaling the Service-Wizard: Funding, Partnerships, and Governance Pathways for a City-Wide, Deterministic 311 Navigator

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Deterministic Service Wizard represents a highly scalable, low-risk solution to resident service navigation that extends far beyond a simple Richmond city government IT project. While recent municipal trends favor AI chatbots, deterministic tools out-perform AI on safety, auditability, and equity by eliminating hallucination risks and relying on human-translated, version-controlled logic. By reframing this tool from a "city website feature" to a "regional civic infrastructure asset," its universe expands significantly. 

This expansion unlocks substantial funding streams, including the $100 million annual federal SMART Grants program [1] and Virginia's Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) [2]. Furthermore, by leveraging regional bodies like PlanRVA [3] and national civic tech networks like Code for America [4] and the Beeck Center's Digital Service Network [5], the Service-Wizard can transition from a localized hackathon prototype into a shared, state-wide digital public good.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City / Regional Nonprofit Model**

To maximize agility, funding eligibility, and long-term sustainability, the Service-Wizard should not be solely owned and operated by the City of Richmond's IT or 311 departments. Instead, it requires a hybrid governance structure:

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must retain ownership of the core routing logic and data—specifically, the version-controlled JSON taxonomy. This ensures that the city maintains authoritative control over service definitions, department contacts, and policy-based routing, satisfying legal and audit requirements.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A local civic nonprofit (e.g., a Richmond Innovation Hub) or a regional body like PlanRVA should own the front-end user interface, the hosting infrastructure, and the continuous integration/continuous deployment (CI/CD) pipeline. This allows for rapid iteration, community-led translation efforts, and agile user testing without triggering lengthy municipal procurement cycles.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own the custom web hosting or the community outreach layer. Relying on city IT for front-end updates often leads to maintenance fatigue and version-control drift.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **GOV.UK Smart Answers (UK):** A proven deterministic model at a national scale. It succeeds due to rigorous content design and version control. *Lesson for this idea: Maintain a strict, code-based or JSON-based version control system for all routing logic.*
* **Decatur Direct (Decatur, IN):** An AI chatbot launched in March 2026 powered by Ordinal Connect. *Lesson for this idea: AI chatbots carry hallucination risks and lack the strict auditability required for complex eligibility routing.*
* **MyDecaturGa (Decatur, GA):** Uses a structured SeeClickFix portal based on a category taxonomy, not a decision tree. *Lesson for this idea: Traditional taxonomies can be overwhelming; a guided, step-by-step wizard reduces cognitive load.*
* **Ask Raleigh (Raleigh, NC):** Structured intake via SeeClickFix. *Lesson for this idea: Without a guided decision tree, misrouting remains a challenge.*

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing the Service-Wizard solely as a Richmond project artificially caps its impact. 
* **Regional Shift:** If expanded regionally via PlanRVA [3], the tool could serve the entire Richmond metropolitan area, standardizing service navigation across adjacent jurisdictions and reducing per-municipality hosting costs.
* **Statewide Shift:** By leveraging VITA's statewide IT contracts [12], the underlying architecture could be offered as a "shared service" to any Virginia municipality, transforming it into a state-wide digital public good.
* **Ownership Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides the data, the tool can iterate at the speed of a startup while maintaining the authoritative trust of government data.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Procurement & Legal Authority:** The city may face hurdles in procuring external development services. *Mitigation: Utilize existing VITA contract vehicles [12] or partner with a nonprofit to bypass direct municipal procurement.*
* **Maintenance Burden:** 311 teams historically lack dedicated staff for taxonomy upkeep, leading to link rot. *Mitigation: Secure a CDBG Planning Grant [2] to fund a part-time curator and implement automated Git-based CI/CD testing.*
* **Grant Dependence:** Relying on a single grant source risks project stalling. *Mitigation: Layer funding by combining federal SMART grants [1] with local foundation support [8] [9].*
* **Technical Integration:** Linking to multiple disparate 311 forms may break when underlying systems update. *Mitigation: Use URL-agnostic redirect IDs and automated link-checking scripts.*
