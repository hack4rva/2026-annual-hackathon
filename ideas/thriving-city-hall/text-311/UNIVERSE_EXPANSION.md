# Scaling Text-311: A Regional Strategy for Digital Equity and Civic Access

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Text-311 presents a compelling vision for lowering the barrier to civic participation by offering an SMS-based "front door" to Richmond's existing 311 service. However, framing this solely as a Richmond City Hall efficiency tool severely limits its potential and ignores critical structural realities. The project currently faces a hard technical bottleneck: the lack of a confirmed public API for Richmond's AvePoint/Dynamics 365 platform [1] [2], which reduces the tool to a disconnected chatbot rather than a true service delivery engine. Furthermore, research from Boston's 311 system reveals an "equity paradox" where digital-first channels can inadvertently widen the reporting gap between high-income and low-income neighborhoods if not paired with targeted community outreach [3] [4]. 

To truly expand the universe of Text-311, the initiative must be reframed from a municipal IT project to a "Broadband-Alternative Service Delivery" initiative. By shifting the scale to a regional level—potentially under the umbrella of PlanRVA, which is already exploring regional shared services [5] —the project can unlock significant federal and state digital equity funding, such as the NTIA's Digital Equity Competitive Grant Program [6] and Virginia's DHCD Digital Opportunity funds [7] [8]. This regional, hybrid-ownership approach allows a nimble nonprofit or civic tech partner to manage the rapidly evolving AI and SMS interface, while local governments retain control over the underlying data and physical service fulfillment.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Public-Nonprofit Partnership (The "Civic Interface" Model)**

The most viable path forward for Text-311 is a hybrid model where the interface and the underlying data infrastructure are decoupled and managed by different entities based on their comparative advantages.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond (DIT/311 Operations) must retain absolute ownership of the **Data Layer** (the AvePoint/Dynamics 365 system) [1] and the **Service Fulfillment** (dispatching crews to fix the pothole). The city should not be in the business of maintaining a custom AI-SMS codebase, which requires rapid iteration, specialized talent, and constant updates to AI models.
* **What a Partner Should Own:** A regional nonprofit (e.g., a Code for America Brigade, a university-affiliated lab, or a regional body like PlanRVA) should own the **Interface Layer**. This allows for faster experimentation with AI models, multilingual translations, and user experience testing without the constraints of municipal procurement cycles.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The SMS gateway (e.g., Twilio) and the AI orchestration logic. By keeping this as a "third-party interface" that talks to the city's API, the city avoids the long-term technical debt of maintaining a bespoke software product and navigating the complex, evolving A2P 10DLC carrier registration requirements [9] [10].

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

Understanding previous municipal SMS and AI chatbot implementations is critical to avoiding known failure modes.

* **New Orleans (AI-Powered 311 Chatbot):**
 * *What was attempted:* Launched an AI-powered textable 311 chatbot in 2021 to deliver faster answers to residents who lack time to wait on calls or devices for websites [25].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* AI can successfully handle intake and reduce call volume, but it requires strict guardrails to prevent hallucinations when dealing with non-standard address descriptions or local slang.
* **Denver (Sunny the Chatbot):**
 * *What was attempted:* SMS-based 311 where users text "hello" to 439311 (HEY311) to report issues on the go [26].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* Denver faced issues with SMS scams mimicking city parking invoices [27] [28]. Text-311 must establish clear, verified branding and educate the public on what official city texts look like to maintain trust.
* **Boston (Digital Channels and Equity):**
 * *What was attempted:* Studied the correlation between digital technology use and 311 system participation [3] [4].
 * *Lesson for this idea:* The study found that as residents used digital channels more frequently, their request volume increased, potentially exacerbating disparities if digital tools are only adopted by already-engaged, higher-income residents [3]. Text-311 must actively target low-income and LEP populations to avoid this trap.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **Why the Richmond-Only Frame is Too Small:** If framed only as a Richmond tool, Text-311 is merely a "nice-to-have" accessibility feature competing for a tiny slice of the municipal General Fund. It places the entire burden of software maintenance, AI API costs, and carrier compliance on a single city IT department.
* **The Regional Frame (The Sweet Spot):** If PlanRVA adopts this as a "Regional Service Portal" [5], it becomes a shared infrastructure project. This allows neighboring counties like Henrico and Chesterfield to "opt-in," sharing the costs of the AI API and SMS gateway while providing a unified experience for residents who cross county lines daily.
* **The Nonprofit-Led Interface Frame:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides the data API, the nonprofit can pilot experimental features (like photo-to-text reporting or rapid multilingual translation) that a risk-averse City IT department could not easily procure or test.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Technical Integration (The API Bottleneck):** The most critical blocker is the lack of a confirmed public API for Richmond's AvePoint/Dynamics 365 platform [1] [2]. Without a "write" API, the system cannot automatically create tickets, requiring manual data entry by city staff, which defeats the purpose of automation.
* **Legal and Compliance (A2P 10DLC):** Federal regulations require all organizations using 10-digit long codes for application-to-person messaging to register their brand and campaigns [9]. Failure to register the "Text-311" brand with an EIN will result in major carriers (Verizon, AT&T) filtering and blocking the messages immediately [10] [29].
* **The Equity Paradox:** As seen in Boston, introducing a new digital channel can inadvertently widen the gap between high-income and low-income neighborhoods if the tool is primarily adopted by those who are already civically engaged [3].
* **AI Hallucination and Trust:** Using generative AI for location ascertainment carries the risk of misclassifying dangerous situations (e.g., a gas leak reported as a minor street issue). The system must rely on structured menus for final confirmation rather than pure free-text conversation.
