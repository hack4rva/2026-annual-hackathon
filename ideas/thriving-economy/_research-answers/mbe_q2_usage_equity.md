# Executive Summary

Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) in Richmond, Virginia, primarily learn of contracting opportunities through official city channels, including notifications from the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) and listings on electronic procurement portals like OpenGov and Virginia's eVA. The city provides a fully digital registration path for MBE certification through its Supplier Diversity Management System, mitigating the need for in-person visits to City Hall. While specific data on the primary barriers perceived by Richmond MBEs is not publicly available, the services offered by the OMBD—such as training, business counseling, and financial referrals—suggest an alignment with nationally recognized challenges, including informational, procedural, and financial hurdles. Similarly, there is no published data on the primary languages of MBE owners, but the city's Title VI notice indicates a commitment to language access, with Spanish being the most likely priority after English. To address digital equity, Richmond has adopted a plan to improve internet access and explicitly uses non-digital outreach methods like signs and in-person ambassadors to accommodate business owners with limited digital literacy or broadband. The research found no evidence of any U.S. city, including Richmond, having deployed a city-run, AI-powered, plain-language contract matching tool with published outcomes for MBEs.

# Perceived Barriers For Mbes

## Informational Barrier Summary

Informational barriers for MBEs in Richmond relate to the awareness and navigation of contracting opportunities. While the city provides multiple channels for information, this can create complexity. MBEs can learn about opportunities through notifications after registering with the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD), by monitoring the city's OpenGov e-Procurement Portal, and by checking Virginia's eVA/Virginia Business Opportunities (VBO) site. The OMBD also holds workshops and seminars. However, national research from the Urban Institute confirms that general challenges with awareness and navigating complex procurement systems are common for MBEs, suggesting that simply having multiple sources does not eliminate the barrier.

## Procedural Barrier Summary

Procedural barriers involve the complexity of registration, paperwork, and bidding processes. For Richmond, a key procedural complexity is the city's ongoing transition to a new e-Procurement system, OpenGov. This has resulted in a hybrid system where some solicitations require electronic submission via the portal, while others still use a paper-based process requiring physical submission at City Hall. Although a fully digital path for OMBD registration exists through the B2GNow/DiversityCompliance portal, the bidding process itself can be inconsistent. These complexities align with national findings that difficult documentation and compliance requirements are significant hurdles for MBEs.

## Financial Barrier Summary

Financial barriers are a significant challenge for MBEs seeking to secure city contracts. These primarily revolve around the ability to meet capital requirements, including securing the necessary bonding and insurance. While the provided context lacks specific data on Richmond MBEs' perceptions, the services offered by the city's OMBD strongly indicate these are recognized local issues. The OMBD provides 'Financial Options, Counseling and Referrals,' a service designed to help businesses overcome these exact hurdles. This is consistent with a national study by the Urban Institute, which identifies difficulties in obtaining bonding, insurance, and sufficient capital as primary barriers for MBEs in government procurement.

# Financial Barriers Deep Dive

A detailed examination of financial barriers faced by Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) in Richmond reveals significant challenges related to securing bonding and insurance for city contracts. While specific local data on the prevalence of these issues is not available, national research from the Urban Institute highlights that obtaining adequate bonding, insurance, and working capital are primary obstacles that prevent MBEs from successfully competing for and executing public sector contracts. The City of Richmond implicitly acknowledges these challenges through the support structure of its Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD). The OMBD's mission includes advancing opportunities for minority businesses, and to this end, it provides 'Financial Options, Counseling and Referrals.' This service is designed to directly assist businesses in navigating and overcoming these financial hurdles, indicating that local officials recognize access to capital, bonding, and insurance as critical barriers to entry and success for MBEs in the city's procurement landscape.

# Procedural And Informational Challenges

Beyond financial hurdles, MBEs in Richmond face significant procedural and informational challenges that can hinder their participation in city contracting. 

Informational gaps persist despite multiple channels of communication. MBEs can learn of opportunities via OMBD email notifications, a public events calendar for workshops, the city's OpenGov procurement portal, and the state's eVA portal. However, this fragmented system requires vendors to actively monitor several different sources, which can be burdensome and lead to missed opportunities. There is no single, centralized, and easily digestible source for all relevant information.

Procedural complexities are particularly evident in the city's current bidding process. The Department of Procurement Services is in the midst of a transition to the OpenGov electronic system. This has created a hybrid environment where some solicitations require electronic submission through the portal, while others still mandate a traditional paper submission process at City Hall. This inconsistency creates confusion and adds a layer of complexity for vendors. While there is a fully digital path to register as an MBE with the OMBD through its dedicated Supplier Diversity Management System (B2GNow), this is separate from the actual bid submission process. Furthermore, the city acknowledges that digital-only processes can be a barrier. The Richmond City Council's resolution on digital equity and the 'Richmond Connects' plan's call for non-digital outreach methods—such as signs, information kiosks, and in-person ambassadors—demonstrate an institutional awareness that not all business owners have equal digital literacy or broadband access, adding another dimension to procedural accessibility challenges.

# Richmond Digital Equity Plan

The City of Richmond's 'Digital Equity Implementation Plan' is a formal initiative mandated by a City Council resolution. The resolution declared that 'equal access to high-speed gigabit internet access in every home; educational and training facility; and business of the City is a public necessity.' The plan's purpose is to develop and implement strategies to close the digital divide for all residents and business owners in the city, which includes providing access to high-speed internet and related training programs.

# City Of Richmond Procurement Process

The City of Richmond's procurement workflow is currently in a hybrid state as the Department of Procurement Services transitions to a new e-Procurement system. The new central platform is the OpenGov portal, where vendors can submit bids and proposals electronically. However, during this transition, the city operates a dual system. Some solicitations are designated for electronic submission via OpenGov, while others still require the traditional paper submission process, which involves physical delivery. For solicitations requiring electronic submission, they are typically posted on Virginia's eVA portal, which then directs vendors to the City's specific OpenGov portal (https://procurement.opengov.com/portal/rva) to register and submit their response. Vendors must carefully review the instructions for each specific solicitation to determine the correct submission method.

# Key Recommendations For Improvement

Based on the research, several actionable recommendations can be made for the City of Richmond to enhance MBE contracting equity. First, the city should conduct a formal disparity and vendor experience study to gather quantitative, Richmond-specific data on barriers, contracting timelines, and drop-off points, which would enable more targeted interventions. Second, in the absence of specific data on MBE owner languages, the city should prioritize language access by first ensuring all materials are in plain English, then developing Spanish-language versions of key portals and documents, and finally establishing a process for on-demand translation consistent with its Title VI obligations. Third, Richmond should continue to strengthen its multi-channel outreach strategy, leveraging both digital notifications from OMBD and procurement portals, and the non-digital methods outlined in the 'Richmond Connects' plan, such as information kiosks and in-person ambassadors, to ensure all potential vendors are reached, regardless of their digital literacy. Lastly, to address informational barriers, the city could explore piloting a vendor-facing, AI-powered tool that simplifies contract language and matches opportunities to firm capabilities, potentially learning from private sector tools to become a municipal leader in this area.
