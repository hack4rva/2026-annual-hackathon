# Skilled Trades Licensing Navigation

Skilled trades workers in Richmond, Virginia, must navigate a multi-layered licensing and registration process that involves state and city agencies in a specific sequence. The typical path begins with fulfilling state-level requirements before proceeding to city-level approvals. First, the contractor must obtain the appropriate contractor firm license (Class A, B, or C) from the Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), which involves completing an eight-hour pre-license education course. Concurrently, the business must address Virginia's Workers' Compensation requirements. The Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission mandates coverage for any business that regularly employs more than two workers, and critically for contractors, this count must include the employees of any subcontractors. After securing the state DPOR license and ensuring compliance with workers' compensation laws (often proven with Form 61A), the contractor can then engage with the City of Richmond. The next step is to obtain a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) from the city's Zoning Administration Office to ensure the business location is properly zoned for its intended use. The CZC is a mandatory prerequisite for the final step: applying for the City of Richmond Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL). When applying for the BPOL, the city requires the contractor to provide proof of their valid state DPOR contractor's license and demonstrate compliance with the workers' compensation regulations. This sequential process ensures that contractors are properly licensed and insured at the state level before being granted a license to operate within the city.

# Digital Divide And Registration Access

## Registration System

Multi-Agency Business Registration (VA SCC, IRS, City of Richmond)

## Online Method

Virginia's State Corporation Commission (SCC) provides an online filing system that it notes is quicker for approvals. The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) offers an online application for an Employer Identification Number (EIN). The City of Richmond utilizes its RVA Business Portal as the central hub for local business license filings and tax payments.

## Paper Or In Person Fallback

For state-level registration, the Virginia SCC accepts paper forms submitted by mail. For a federal tax ID, the IRS provides Form SS-4, which can be mailed or faxed as an alternative to the online application. The City of Richmond has transitioned to a digital-first model for its Business Personal Property, Machinery and Tools (BPOL) tax, stating that for 2025 filings, 'There are no paper forms for manual filing.' However, it maintains non-digital support by offering in-person and virtual assistance. Furthermore, the initial New Business License Application process appears to still rely on an in-person path at City Hall using a fillable PDF checklist.

## Assistance Resource

The City of Richmond offers direct support for its digital portal through in-person and virtual appointments, as well as assistance via the RVA 311 call center. For entrepreneurs lacking personal internet or computer access, all Richmond Public Library branches provide public computers and printing services, acting as a crucial public access point to complete necessary online registrations.

# Summary Of Identified Equity Barriers

The primary equity barriers for small business startups in Richmond, Virginia, can be consolidated into three main categories: regulatory complexity, digital access, and information gaps.

1.  **Regulatory and Procedural Complexity:** Aspiring entrepreneurs face a fragmented and multi-layered compliance landscape. A key hurdle is the sequential nature of registrations, where a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) is a mandatory prerequisite for obtaining a city business license (BPOL). For skilled trades, this complexity is amplified, requiring navigation of at least three separate governmental bodies: the state-level Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR) for contractor licensing, the Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission for insurance compliance (a requirement checked by the city), and finally, the City of Richmond's own zoning and business license departments. This multi-agency process presents a significant barrier for those with limited time, resources, or experience in navigating bureaucracy.

2.  **Digital Access Barriers:** There is a growing digital divide in the registration process. While federal (IRS for EIN) and state (SCC for entity formation) agencies still provide paper-based or fax-based fallbacks, the City of Richmond is moving towards a digital-only environment. The RVA Business Portal, as of 2025, has no paper forms for manual filing. This policy creates a substantial barrier for entrepreneurs without reliable broadband access or digital literacy. Although the city offers in-person assistance and public libraries provide computer access, these are workarounds that require additional effort and may not be as efficient, placing digitally disconnected individuals at a distinct disadvantage.

3.  **Information and Language Gaps:** A significant barrier is the lack of accessible, centralized data and guidance. The city does not publish analytics on common failure points in the registration process, making it difficult for entrepreneurs to anticipate challenges. The fact that the city's primary contact center, RVA 311, is a designated starting point for business questions—but does not report on the volume or nature of these inquiries—highlights a systemic information gap. Furthermore, while census data indicates a large Spanish-speaking population, the research did not uncover specific data on the languages spoken by aspiring entrepreneurs or the availability of multilingual support throughout the complex licensing and registration process, suggesting a potential language barrier.
