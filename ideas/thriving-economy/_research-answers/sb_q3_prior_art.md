# Executive Summary

An analysis of business startup guidance tools reveals a significant gap between the resources available in Virginia, particularly Richmond, and the more advanced, interactive models implemented in cities like New York, Boston, and Chicago. Virginia's cities currently lack a comprehensive, interactive 'wizard' similar to NYC's 'MyCity Business Step-by-Step' tool, which generates a personalized list of all city, state, and federal requirements. Instead, cities like Richmond and Alexandria provide static checklists, narrative guides, and separate transactional portals, such as Richmond's EnerGov-based Online Permit Portal for zoning and permit applications. The statewide Virginia Business One Stop (VBOS) functions primarily as a resource directory, and while it references a future 'New Business Wizard,' it does not currently offer an integrated, sequenced guidance system. In contrast, successful platforms like NYC's MyCity and Chicago's Business Direct demonstrate sustained adoption due to their transactional nature, allowing users to apply, pay, and track status in one place, and their cross-agency integration backed by strong executive sponsorship. Boston presents another model of success with its high-touch, in-person 'Neighborhood Business Managers' assigned to specific geographic areas, a targeted service delivery approach that Richmond has not implemented. While Richmond's Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) offers technical assistance, its services appear centralized downtown without a documented, recurring presence in other neighborhoods like the East End or Southside. Lastly, efforts to streamline applications by pre-populating forms face hurdles; while using State Corporation Commission (SCC) data is technically feasible pending data-sharing agreements, leveraging federal EIN data from the IRS is largely prohibited by federal confidentiality laws.

# Virginia Permit Wizard Attempts

Based on a review of municipal websites, no city in Virginia has successfully implemented and sustained a personalized, step-by-step permit and license wizard comparable to NYC's MyCity Business. While NYC's tool generates a customized list of requirements based on user input, Virginia cities have taken a different approach. For example, the City of Richmond offers an Online Permit Portal (powered by EnerGov), which functions as a transactional portal for applying and paying for specific permits like the Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC), rather than a guidance wizard that helps entrepreneurs first identify all their obligations across agencies. Similarly, the City of Alexandria provides resources like a narrative New Business Guide and checklists, which are static informational tools, not interactive, personalized engines. The outcome is that Virginia cities currently emphasize transactional permitting portals and static guides over the development of personalized requirement-finding wizards.

# Virginia Business One Stop Analysis

The Virginia Business One Stop (BOS) portal, despite its claim of serving over 33,000 users, currently functions primarily as a resource directory and aggregation hub rather than a fully interactive, sequenced guide for starting a business. The portal's 'About' page indicates an intention for future enhancements, stating, 'Going forward, more features will be added to the Business One Stop web page and the New Business Wizard online registration system.' This suggests an aspiration to develop more advanced features. However, the public-facing site today does not offer an interactive, question-driven experience that spans state and local requirements in a manner comparable to platforms like NYC's MyCity. It allows users to create an account to organize steps but mainly provides links and information. There is no publicly available evidence, such as Requests for Proposals (RFPs) or strategic roadmaps on the Virginia Small Business and Supplier Diversity (SBSD) or Department of Planning and Budget (DPB) websites, to indicate a concrete, active attempt to upgrade the portal into a comprehensive, interactive sequenced guide.

# Richmond In Person Guidance Analysis

The City of Richmond, VA, has not implemented or publicly considered a model for in-person business guidance directly comparable to Boston's Neighborhood Business Managers program. The Boston model is characterized by assigning named managers to specific geographic neighborhoods to provide localized, hands-on assistance with permitting, licensing, and location selection. In contrast, Richmond's support structure is more centralized and network-based. The city directs entrepreneurs to resources through its economic development authority, the Small Business Development Center (SBDC) network, and service counters at City Hall for permitting, finance, and the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD). While these entities provide crucial support, there is no evidence of a program where dedicated staff are assigned to and operate within specific neighborhoods like the East End or Southside to act as a primary local navigator. A review of the city's official website and the Richmond EDA's pages found no mention of proposals or pilot programs to establish such a neighborhood-assigned manager role.

# Successful Tool Adoption Factors

Business navigation tools that achieve sustained adoption are distinguished from those that stall or fail by a combination of key features and strategic implementation. Based on the analysis of successful examples like NYC MyCity Business and Chicago Business Direct, the primary distinguishing factors are:

1.  **Transactionality over Static Information**: Successful platforms are not just informational directories. They allow users to complete critical tasks such as applying for permits, paying fees, and checking the status of their applications directly within the tool. Chicago Business Direct exemplifies this by consolidating licensing and tax services into one transactional portal.

2.  **Cross-Agency Integration and User Profiles**: These tools orchestrate processes across different government agencies and utilize stored user profiles. This functionality, as noted in a comptroller audit of NYC's MyCity, allows users to save their personal and business information once and have it reused for multiple applications, significantly reducing the friction and repetitive data entry associated with navigating bureaucracy.

3.  **Strong Executive Sponsorship and Connection to Reform Initiatives**: Sustained tools are often backed by strong, visible executive leadership (e.g., a mayor's office) and are framed as part of a larger government reform initiative. Case studies on Chicago's licensing and permitting reform highlight that the tool's development was driven by a primary goal of energizing business growth through smarter, business-friendly regulation, providing the political will and resources for a comprehensive build-out.

In contrast, tools that stall, like the current version of Virginia Business One Stop, typically remain as link directories or resource aggregators without offering integrated, transactional, or personalized guidance, thus failing to become an indispensable part of the business startup and management process.

# Data Prepopulation Feasibility Analysis

## Overall Assessment

The feasibility of pre-populating local business applications, such as Richmond's BPOL and CZC forms, is mixed. It is considered technically feasible to pull data from state-level sources like the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC) Clerk's Information System (CIS), provided that the necessary system integrations, APIs, and intergovernmental data sharing agreements are established. However, pre-populating forms using federal data from an Employer Identification Number (EIN) application is legally constrained and largely infeasible for routine business onboarding due to strict federal confidentiality laws governing tax information.

## Technical Challenge Summary

Technical barriers primarily involve a lack of integration and interoperability between different government systems. For example, the City of Richmond uses separate systems for its Certificate of Zoning Compliance (EnerGov Online Permit Portal) and its BPOL/BPP filings (RVA Business Portal). Integrating these would be a prerequisite. Furthermore, while the Virginia SCC's Clerk’s Information System (CIS) is the authoritative registry for business entities, a public, real-time API for this system is not advertised, which would hinder automated data transfer. Accessing IRS data via APIs is also technically challenging, as it requires vetted use cases and specific authorization, which is not typically granted for general business onboarding purposes.

## Legal Constraint Summary

The most significant legal barrier concerns the confidentiality of federal tax information. Data from an EIN application is considered federal tax return information and is protected by law. Its disclosure to state and local governments is tightly restricted and generally limited to specific statutory programs, not for the routine pre-population of local business license or permit forms. Sharing data from the state-level SCC would also require legal frameworks, such as Memorandums of Understanding (MOUs), and compliance with SCC's data policies.

## Key Governing Statute

The primary legal statute that governs the confidentiality of federal tax and EIN application data is 26 U.S.C. § 6103, which broadly prohibits the disclosure of returns and return information by the IRS except under specific, limited circumstances.


# Richmond Ombd Geographic Outreach

The free technical assistance provided by Richmond's Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) is available in principle to all city entrepreneurs, but its documented service delivery appears to be highly centralized, which may create geographic access limitations for those outside the downtown core. The OMBD's main office is located in City Hall, and its official web pages and workshop schedules promote events and services without listing any permanent or recurring satellite offices in neighborhoods such as the East End or Southside. While the office provides a 'broad range of technical assistance' and promotes workshops through centralized calendars and social media, there is no public evidence on its official sites of a structured outreach program with a routine, staffed presence in these specific communities. This centralized model suggests that entrepreneurs from the East End, Southside, and other areas distant from City Hall may face challenges in accessing in-person assistance, unless they can travel downtown or the OMBD engages in pop-up or partner-hosted events not detailed on its primary web pages.

The Boston Office of Small Business operates a Neighborhood Business Manager (NBM) program that provides localized, in-person guidance to entrepreneurs. The program's structure is based on assigning named, dedicated managers to specific geographic districts within the city. These managers act as direct points of contact and can provide answers and assistance on a wide range of critical business-related matters, including navigating permitting and licensing processes, developing a business plan, and selecting a suitable location. The program's goal is to offer tailored, accessible support by embedding business experts within the communities they serve, as evidenced by the public listing of managers and their assigned neighborhoods, such as 'Duamarius Stukes — Hyde Park, Lower Mills, Roxbury'.

# Chicago Business Direct Overview

Chicago Business Direct is a comprehensive web portal that serves as an integrated, one-stop system for the city's business licensing and tax needs. A key factor in its successful adoption is its transactional capability, which goes beyond being a simple information directory. Users can perform ongoing transactions such as applying for licenses, paying fees, and checking application statuses through a single, account-backed platform. The portal provides clear, step-by-step guidance for the license application process, including zoning approval and inspections. The system's success is also attributed to strong executive sponsorship under a mayoral initiative aimed at business-friendly regulation and process streamlining, which consolidated services and created a more intuitive online application system.

# Technical Barriers To Data Integration

The specific technical challenges in integrating data from federal (IRS), state (SCC), and local (city) systems are multi-layered. At the local level in Richmond, business application data is siloed; the Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) is handled through the EnerGov Online Permit Portal, while the Business Professional and Occupational License (BPOL) is managed via a separate 'RVA Business Portal'. Pre-population would require technical integration between these distinct platforms. At the state level, the Virginia SCC's Clerk’s Information System (CIS) is the authoritative source for business entity data, but there is no advertised public real-time API to facilitate seamless data pulls into city applications. While some SCC-related datasets are on Virginia’s Open Data Portal, they are not a substitute for the full entity profiles in CIS. At the federal level, while the IRS provides APIs, access is not open; it requires a client ID, a vetted use case, and specific authorization, making it an unsuitable mechanism for general, automated pre-population of local business forms.

# Legal Barriers To Data Sharing

The primary legal and privacy constraint on sharing business data for pre-populating local applications stems from federal law governing tax information. Federal tax return information, which includes data submitted during an Employer Identification Number (EIN) application, is strictly protected by 26 U.S.C. § 6103. This statute establishes the confidentiality of such data and generally prohibits the IRS from disclosing it. While there are exceptions for disclosure to state and local governments, they are for specific, statutorily defined purposes (e.g., tax administration) and do not typically cover routine use for pre-populating general business license or permit applications. Any such sharing would require a specific legal exception and a formal interagency agreement. At the state level, while data from the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC) is not governed by the same strict federal confidentiality law, intergovernmental sharing of authoritative data from the Clerk's Information System (CIS) would likely require formal Memorandums of Understanding (MOUs) and must comply with the SCC's own policies on data use and distribution.
