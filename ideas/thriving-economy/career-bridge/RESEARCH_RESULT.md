# From Job Boards to Pathways: City-Grade Platforms That Map Skills to Quality Jobs

## Executive Summary
US cities and Workforce Development Boards (WDBs) are increasingly shifting from generic job boards to comprehensive career pathway navigation platforms. These systems integrate skills assessments, training program matching, and career pathway visualization to connect job seekers with family-sustaining careers. However, while the software features are maturing, the evidence shows that **technology alone does not lift wages**. 

The most successful implementations—such as New York City's sector-focused centers and the City of Birmingham's employer-aligned Guild partnership—embed digital navigation within robust program designs featuring funded training, 100-hour externships, and intensive coaching. Rigorous meta-analyses of 46 career pathway evaluations reveal that while these approaches drive large gains in educational progress and industry-specific employment, short-term earnings gains are often small, and long-term earnings gains require deep employer integration [1] [2]. To prove ROI, cities must mandate standardized data plumbing, linking platform telemetry to Unemployment Insurance (UI) wage records via the State Wage Interchange System (SWIS) to track 180-day retention and pre/post wage growth.

## Landscape of City/WDB Pathway Platforms
The current landscape features a mix of custom-built municipal systems, vendor platforms integrated into local portals, and AI-driven skills mapping tools. While most platforms offer robust assessment and matching features, very few publicly report independent outcomes on placements, retention, or wages.

## Deep Dive: NYC's Career Pathways and Workforce1
New York City’s Workforce1 system represents one of the most mature, heavily evaluated career pathway models in the US. Rather than relying solely on a digital portal, NYC integrates its technology (Worksource1 case management and the NYC Training Guide) with physical, sector-focused career centers [12]. 

The system uses Individual Training Grants (ITGs) to fund tuition for in-demand occupations [13]. A rigorous independent evaluation by Westat demonstrated the power of combining sector focus with training: participants in sector-focused centers *with* training achieved average annual earnings of $22,726, compared to $19,943 for sector-focused centers without training, and $15,128 for general career centers [3]. Furthermore, NYC's Customized Training program for incumbent workers yielded an average wage gain of 9% (approximately $3,286 per year), with even larger percentage increases for workers starting at $15 per hour or less [3]. NYC tracks retention rigorously at 30, 60, 90, 180, and 365-day intervals [3].

## Evidence Synthesis: What Rigorous Studies Say
When evaluating the impact of career pathway navigation and training, the evidence base suggests cautious optimism. Abt Associates conducted a meta-analysis of 46 career pathways impact evaluations, including 27 randomized controlled trials and 19 quasi-experimental designs [2] [14]. 

The meta-analysis revealed that the career pathways approach leads to:
* **Large gains** in educational progress and industry-specific employment [2].
* **Small gains** in general employment and short-term earnings [2].
* **No meaningful gains** in medium/longer-term earnings on average across the 46 studies [2].

*Takeaway: Digital navigation tools alone will not transform earnings. They must be paired with longer-term, employer-tethered training programs (like Project QUEST or Year Up) to achieve sustained wage growth.*

## Measurement Framework for Pathway Platforms
To move beyond vanity metrics (e.g., "platform logins" or "course completions"), cities must adopt a standardized measurement framework aligned with the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) and best practices like NYC's Common Metrics [15] [16].

### Standard Metrics and Definitions

| Metric | Definition | Source | Interval |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Placement (Offer + Start)** | Unduplicated hires tied to program referral | Case system + employer HRIS | At hire; 30 days |
| **Retention (Q2/Q4)** | Employed in 2nd/4th quarter post-exit | UI wage records (SWIS) | 6 & 12 months [16] |
| **30/90/180/365-day retention** | Employed at same or any employer | Employer HRIS + self-report + UI | Monthly/quarterly cadence [3] |
| **Median earnings Q2** | Median wages in 2nd quarter post-exit | UI wage records | 6 months [16] |
| **Wage growth (12 mo)** | % change vs baseline (Q-3/Q-4) | UI wage records | 12 months [3] |
| **Credential attainment** | Industry-recognized/acad. degrees | Provider SIS/LMS | During/within 1 year |

*Takeaway: By standardizing around WIOA Q2/Q4 retention and median earnings, WDBs can compare the efficacy of different vendor platforms (e.g., Guild vs. Lightcast) using apples-to-apples data.*

## Data Plumbing & Governance
Tracking these metrics requires robust data plumbing. Cities must link their front-end navigation platforms (like PathwayU or Career Coach) and case management systems (like CalJOBS or Salesforce) to backend wage data [17] [18] [19]. 

The State Wage Interchange System (SWIS) facilitates the interstate exchange of wage data for assessing WIOA performance [20]. Cities should execute Data Sharing Agreements (DSAs) that comply with FERPA and UI confidentiality regulations to securely route this data into performance dashboards [21]. 

## Design Patterns That Move Outcomes
The City of Birmingham's partnership with Guild and UAB Medicine highlights the program design required to make navigation platforms work. Facing a shortage of healthcare workers, they co-designed a Certified Medical Assistant pathway based on real employer demand [4]. 

The model included:
* A **100-hour externship** at UAB to provide real-world experience [4].
* High-quality coursework from Guild's Learning Marketplace [4].
* Clear role pathways and transparent job opportunities [4].
As a result, jobseekers with no prior clinical experience are being hired into family-sustaining roles, and previously rejected applicants are finding pathways to employment [4].

## Evaluation by Design
To isolate the incremental effect of digital navigation tools versus broader program components, WDBs should embed evaluation into their rollouts.

### Practical Evaluation Designs

| Design | Use Case | Sample Size | Pros/Cons | Data Needed |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **A/B (feature toggle)** | Pathway visualization on/off | 1,000+ | Clear attribution; requires UX control | Telemetry + UI wages |
| **Stepped-wedge rollout** | Multi-site phased launches | 6–12 sites | Politically feasible; temporal controls | Case + UI wages |
| **Regression discontinuity** | ITG score cutoffs | Varies | Strong internal validity | Historic scoring + UI wages |
| **Matched diff-in-diff** | Vendor vs non-vendor sites | Large | Broad inference; confounding risk | PIRL + wages; site covariates |

*Takeaway: Do not launch a platform without a pre-registered evaluation plan. Use stepped-wedge rollouts across different career centers to measure if the software actually improves Q2 retention.*

## Implementation Plan (6–12 Months)
1. **Months 0–2 (Alignment & Plumbing):** Select 2-3 high-demand sectors. Execute SWIS/UI Data Sharing Agreements. Draft procurement language requiring vendors to map telemetry to WIOA PIRL elements.
2. **Months 2–4 (Configuration):** Configure the platform (e.g., BuildWithin, Lightcast) [11] [10]. Secure employer MOUs for 100-hour externships and first-look hiring commitments.
3. **Months 4–9 (Launch & Monitor):** Launch cohorts using an A/B or stepped-wedge design. Track 30-day and 90-day placement and retention via employer HRIS feeds.
4. **Months 9–12 (Evaluate):** Pull Q2 UI wage records. Conduct an independent evaluation to assess median earnings and retention against control groups.

## Risk & Mitigation
The greatest risk is deploying a "black-box" software tool without employer buy-in or data access. Vendors often claim high placement rates, but without UI wage linkages, these claims are unverifiable. Mitigate this by requiring data-export capabilities in vendor contracts, mandating employer hiring commitments (like UAB Medicine's [4]) before launching a pathway, and ensuring independent third-party evaluation of wage gains.
