# Executive Summary

First-time entrepreneurs in Richmond, VA, face significant functional, emotional, and systemic challenges when navigating the city's business startup process. The primary pain point is the lack of a clear, unified, and sequential path to legal operation. Entrepreneurs struggle with an unclear order of operations, particularly the critical dependency of obtaining a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) on prior zoning approval, which can be either a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) or a Certificate of Occupancy (CO) depending on the business type—an exception that is easy to miss. This confusion is compounded by a fragmented digital ecosystem requiring users to navigate multiple, non-integrated city portals with separate logins, including the Online Permit Portal (OPP) for permits and zoning, the RVA Business Portal for licenses and taxes, and B2GNow for supplier diversity. Entrepreneurs are further burdened by long and unpredictable timelines for plan reviews and inspections, which can range from days to over a month, creating significant financial risk in the form of rent paid on unopened locations. Additionally, valuable financial incentives like the CARE and Enterprise Zone programs are difficult to discover and access due to complex eligibility rules, specific documentation requirements, and strict application windows, causing many to miss out on substantial rebates. These functional hurdles create significant emotional distress, including anxiety over investing savings, fear of penalties for missed steps, and uncertainty due to conflicting information. The system disproportionately impacts entrepreneurs with limited capital, digital access, or English proficiency, as most core city portals are English-only, further widening the equity gap.

# Functional Job Pain Points

Entrepreneurs in Richmond face significant functional and process-related struggles when starting a business, primarily stemming from an unclear and fragmented system. A core pain point is the ambiguous sequence of operations and dependencies between different city departments. For instance, the city mandates that zoning compliance must be verified before a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) is issued. The official checklist explicitly directs applicants to obtain an approved Zoning Certificate (CZC) or Certificate of Occupancy (CO) before applying for the BPOL. However, a critical and easily missed exception exists for 'assembly uses' like restaurants and daycares, which require a CO instead of a CZC. This lack of clarity causes applicants to start the BPOL process prematurely, leading to delays. The process is further complicated by the existence of multiple, non-integrated digital portals: the EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP) for CZCs and COs, the RVA Business Portal for BPOL and tax filings, and B2GNow for supplier diversity. This fragmentation forces entrepreneurs to navigate multiple logins and interfaces. Timelines and review lags add another layer of difficulty, with plan reviews taking anywhere from 5 to over 30 business days, and health department inspections requiring a 7-10 working day scheduling window after a complete submittal. Finally, discovering and applying for financial incentives like the Enterprise Zone and CARE programs is fraught with friction; these programs have specific eligibility criteria, documentation requirements, and narrow time windows for application, and often require the applicant to already possess a city business license and be current on all taxes and permits, creating a catch-22 for new businesses seeking startup support.

# Emotional Job Pain Points

The process of starting a business in Richmond inflicts a significant emotional toll on entrepreneurs, characterized by anxiety, fear, and uncertainty. A primary source of stress is the fear of financial loss, especially after making a major commitment like signing a commercial lease. Entrepreneurs invest their savings and take on debt without a predictable timeline or clear assurance that they are on the right track. This anxiety is compounded by receiving conflicting or incomplete guidance from various city sources, forcing them to rely on community word-of-mouth or make repeated calls to city staff for clarification. There is a pervasive uncertainty about special rules that might apply to their specific business type, such as the unique Certificate of Occupancy requirements for restaurants or the specific constraints on home-based businesses. This opacity leads to a fear of penalties, re-submittal fees, or catastrophic delays. The 'cost of the status quo' manifests as tangible financial burdens—like paying rent on an unopened location while waiting for plan reviews or inspections—which directly translates to emotional distress. For home-based entrepreneurs, the fear of their application being denied could mean an unexpected and crippling need to lease commercial space. This psychological impact of navigating an opaque and unforgiving system is a major deterrent and a source of constant worry for aspiring business owners.

# Systems Job Pain Points

The friction for entrepreneurs in Richmond is fundamentally a systems problem, caused by fragmented and disconnected city platforms that lack a unified workflow. Entrepreneurs and their advisors (such as those at SBDC or OMBD) must contend with a constellation of separate portals, including the EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP) for permitting and zoning, the RVA Business Portal for business licenses (BPOL) and taxes, the B2GNow portal for supplier diversity registration, and additional, separate portals for Economic Development Authority (EDA) incentives like CARE and Enterprise Zone. This fragmentation forces duplicative data entry across multiple systems and makes it exceedingly difficult to get a holistic view of an applicant's progress. Advisors spend significant time manually tracking prerequisites, reconciling statuses between departments, and creating their own checklists to guide clients. A common failure point is an entrepreneur starting in the wrong system (e.g., applying for a BPOL before securing zoning approval), leading to delays and rework. This disjointed ecosystem also leads to missed opportunities. Without integrated prompts or a unified pathway, entrepreneurs often fail to discover or apply for valuable incentives like the CARE program (offering up to $25k/building) or Enterprise Zone rebates. Similarly, registration for supplier diversity programs (MBE/ESB) on the separate B2GNow portal is an extra step that can be easily overlooked, hindering efforts to promote equity.

# Current Start A Business Workflow

The current workaround for starting a business in Richmond is a convoluted, self-directed process that requires entrepreneurs to piece together information from multiple city and state websites, phone calls, and peer advice. The inferred sequence is as follows:

1.  **Zoning and Occupancy Verification:** The first, and most critical, step is to determine the correct zoning and occupancy path. The entrepreneur must use the EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP) to apply for either a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) for most commercial and home-based businesses, or a Certificate of Occupancy (CO) for 'assembly uses' like restaurants. This distinction is crucial and often discovered through painful trial and error or by calling the Permit Center.

2.  **Permitting for Construction/Renovation:** If the business location requires a build-out or renovation (common for retail and restaurants), the entrepreneur must submit plans and apply for building permits, also through the OPP. This step can trigger long plan review delays of several weeks or more.

3.  **Specialized Departmental Approvals (If Applicable):** For specific industries, additional steps are required. For example, a food business must take its approved CO to the Richmond City Health District (RCHD) to apply for a Health Permit. This involves a separate application, fees, plan review, and a final inspection that must be scheduled 7-10 working days in advance.

4.  **Business License Application (BPOL):** Only after securing zoning/occupancy approval (and other required permits like the Health Permit) can the entrepreneur apply for their Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL). This is done through a different system, the RVA Business Portal. The Department of Finance cross-references with the Department of Planning and Development Review to verify zoning compliance before issuing the license.

5.  **Incentive and Diversity Program Applications:** After obtaining a business license, the entrepreneur can then pursue incentives. They must navigate to the separate Economic Development Authority (EDA) website and portals to apply for programs like CARE or Enterprise Zone, ensuring they meet strict documentation and timing deadlines. To be certified as a minority-owned business, they must register on yet another portal, B2GNow, managed by the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD).

Throughout this entire process, entrepreneurs rely heavily on workarounds like making direct phone calls and walk-in visits to the Permit Center, emailing RCHD staff, consulting with SBDC advisors, and seeking guidance from other business owners to navigate the fragmented steps.

# Fragmented City Portals And Systems

## System Name

EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP)

## Primary Function

Submitting applications for Certificates of Zoning Compliance (CZCs), Certificates of Occupancy (CO/H-CO), and building permits.

## User Pain Points

Users face confusion over the correct application path, such as needing a Certificate of Occupancy for assembly uses (restaurants) instead of a standard CZC. The system is associated with long and unpredictable timelines, with initial plan reviews taking anywhere from 5 to 30+ business days and general processing times of 2-3 weeks. Incomplete submittals can pause these timelines, adding further delays.

## Integration Gaps

This portal is entirely separate from the RVA Business Portal, where business licenses are obtained. Although a zoning certificate from OPP is a prerequisite for a business license, the systems are not connected, forcing users to manually manage this dependency. It also does not integrate with incentive application portals or the supplier diversity system.

## System Name

RVA Business Portal

## Primary Function

Applying for and renewing the Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) and filing Business Personal Property (BPP) taxes.

## User Pain Points

The portal enforces a critical dependency—verifying zoning compliance before issuing a license—but does not prevent users from starting the application out of sequence. This leads to rework and delays when an applicant applies for a license before securing their CZC or CO. It requires a separate login from the EnerGov OPP.

## Integration Gaps

The portal does not automatically check or pull data from the EnerGov OPP to confirm zoning status. It is also disconnected from the B2GNow supplier diversity portal and the EDA's incentive programs, meaning user data must be re-entered in multiple places.

## System Name

B2GNow (Supplier Diversity Management System)

## Primary Function

Registering a business with the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) for Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) certification and other supplier diversity opportunities.

## User Pain Points

This is another standalone system that entrepreneurs must discover and navigate. It adds to the overall 'portal fatigue' by requiring a separate registration and login, increasing the administrative burden, especially for underrepresented founders who could benefit most from these programs.

## Integration Gaps

The system is not integrated with the primary business licensing (RVA Business Portal) or permitting (OPP) workflows. There are no prompts or data handoffs from other portals to encourage registration, leading to a risk of low uptake and missed opportunities for entrepreneurs.

## System Name

EDA Application Portals (Enterprise Zone/CARE)

## Primary Function

Applying for local business incentives, such as the Commercial Area Revitalization Effort (CARE) and Enterprise Zone (EZ) programs, which provide rebates for investments and fee waivers.

## User Pain Points

Applicants face complex eligibility rules, including needing an active City business license and being current on all taxes and permits. Eligibility for these programs is directly tied to activities in the OPP (permits, CO) and RVA Business Portal (business license), but there is no data sharing or integration to streamline the application or pre-screen applicants.

# Available Incentive And Support Programs

## Program Name

Commercial Area Revitalization Effort (CARE) Program

## Program Type

Financial Incentive (Reimbursement Grant)

## Benefits Summary

Provides a reimbursement grant equal to 50% of eligible expenses for investments in the exterior or interior improvements of commercial structures. The maximum benefit is up to $25,000 per building or $50,000 per applicant for multiple buildings.

## Eligibility Criteria

The applicant must be the legal entity or individual operating the business at the property. The business must be located in a designated CARE area, hold a City of Richmond business license, and be current on all city permits, licenses, and taxes.

## Program Name

Virginia Enterprise Zone (EZ) Program

## Program Type

Financial Incentive (Grants and Rebates)

## Benefits Summary

Provides multiple incentives for businesses in designated zones, including: a Development Fee Rebate up to $3,000; a Business Relocation Rebate of 50% of eligible costs up to $10,000; a Loan Fee Rebate up to $1,000; and rebates for machinery and equipment (M&E).

## Eligibility Criteria

Businesses must locate, invest, and grow within specific Enterprise Zone boundaries in Richmond. Applicants must have a City of Richmond business license and be current on all permits and taxes. Specific incentives have their own deadlines, such as applying for the Development Fee Rebate within nine months of receiving the Certificate of Occupancy.

## Program Name

Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) Registration

## Program Type

Certification (Supplier Diversity)

## Benefits Summary

Registration in the Supplier Diversity Management System provides access to city contracting and supplier diversity opportunities. It is managed by the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD).

## Eligibility Criteria

The program is for minority-owned businesses. Registration is handled through the separate B2GNow online portal. The current process is fragmented from other business startup steps, creating a risk of low uptake.

## Program Name

Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) Tax Threshold Increase

## Program Type

Financial Incentive (Tax Relief)

## Benefits Summary

Effective January 1, 2026, the BPOL tax threshold doubles from $250,000 to $500,000. This exempts businesses with gross receipts below $500,000 from paying this local tax.

## Eligibility Criteria

Applies to all businesses operating in the City of Richmond that are required to have a business license. The relief is automatic for businesses with annual gross receipts under the $500,000 threshold starting in 2026.
