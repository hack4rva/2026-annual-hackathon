# iBuild — Expanding Richmond’s Permit‑Pre‑Application Engine into a Regional, Multi‑Sector Innovation Platform

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The iBuild initiative—originally conceived as a Richmond-specific pre-application permit wizard—possesses a universe of potential that extends far beyond a siloed municipal IT project. By reframing iBuild from a city-owned software patch to a hybrid-owned, regionally scalable civic innovation platform, Richmond can unlock significant federal and state funding while drastically reducing permit-processing labor. Pre-application wizards are becoming the de-facto front door for building permits across the country; similar tools have yielded massive ROI, such as Salt Lake City saving over 2,100 staff hours with a 95% self-service rate. 

To maximize impact, iBuild should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Instead, it requires a hybrid ownership model where the City provides authoritative data (zoning, fee schedules) while a local nonprofit partner (such as Startup Virginia or the Greater Richmond SBDC) hosts the user interface, manages updates, and drives community outreach. This structure bypasses municipal procurement bottlenecks, leverages abundant digital service modernization grants (like the EDA's EDGE program and HUD CDBG funds), and positions the tool for regional expansion across the PlanRVA footprint.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership with Regional Scaling Potential**

The most viable path for iBuild is a hybrid ownership model structured as a "service-delivery contract." 

**What the City Should Own:**
The City of Richmond must retain legal stewardship and data custodianship. Virginia law requires the municipal government to retain record-keeping of permit applications. Therefore, the City should own the underlying data feeds, including GIS layers, published fee schedules, and eventual API connections to the EnerGov backend. The City's GIS Office and Planning Department must also own the quarterly data-audit process to ensure fee estimates remain accurate and do not erode public trust.

**What Partners Should Own:**
A Richmond-based nonprofit (e.g., Startup Virginia) should own the resident-facing intelligence layer. This includes hosting the web wizard, managing the UI/UX, conducting multilingual user testing, and running community outreach. Nonprofits can iterate faster than municipal IT departments and are eligible for civic innovation grants that the City cannot access directly.

**What Should NOT be City-Owned:**
The City should not own the front-end software development or the direct marketing of the tool to first-time entrepreneurs. Keeping the wizard entirely in-house risks the project stalling due to competing IT priorities or backend API delays, as seen in other jurisdictions.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

### Local and State Efforts
* **Richmond Online Permit Portal (OPP):** The City currently uses an online portal for commercial and residential building permits [28] [29]. However, this system handles post-application submission and lacks a pre-application guidance layer.
* **Virginia Business One-Stop:** A state-level portal for business registration that organizes steps in one place for a $20 fee [5]. It does not provide localized, sequenced permit discovery or fee estimation.
* **Henrico County "Build Henrico":** Henrico moved all permitting online in 2021 [30] [31]. While it offers digital filing, it lacks a front-end wizard to triage users before they create an account.

### National Implementations
* **Philadelphia Permit Navigator:** A pilot project helping residents find required permits for residential and commercial uses [32]. 
* **Los Angeles County EPIC-LA:** Uses a decision engine to assist in identifying permits and estimating turnaround times [33] [34]. 
* **Washington, DC DOB Permit Wizard:** Identifies permits, estimates total costs, and informs anticipated reviews [35].

*Lesson for iBuild:* Success hinges on static data accuracy and clear disclaimers. Failures often stem from reliance on unavailable backend APIs and inadequate data-governance, which can lead to fee variances and eroded public trust.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why might this idea be too small framed only as a Richmond project?**
Framed solely as a Richmond IT project, iBuild competes for limited municipal budget and risks creating siloed data. It also fails to address the reality that contractors and small business owners frequently operate across municipal lines (e.g., Richmond, Henrico, Chesterfield).

**What changes if the frame becomes regional?**
By integrating with PlanRVA—which provides Geographic Information Systems (GIS) data across nine localities including Richmond, Henrico, and Chesterfield [36] [37] —iBuild can scale to serve the entire Greater Richmond region. This multiplies the user base and makes the project highly competitive for federal EDA EDGE grants [1] and CDBG economic development funds [4], which prioritize regional impact.

**What if a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer?**
If a nonprofit (like Startup Virginia) owns the UI while the City provides data, the project gains agility. The nonprofit can rapidly iterate the user experience, implement multilingual support, and secure philanthropic funding (e.g., Community Foundation grants [7]) that the City cannot easily access.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Legal Authority:** Virginia law mandates city custodianship of permit records. A pure private ownership model conflicts with this; the City must retain data ownership while outsourcing the UI.
* **Data Governance & Trust:** Out-of-date fee tables lead to public trust loss. The City GIS Office must institute strict quarterly audits to ensure the wizard's estimates match actual EnerGov outputs.
* **Technical Integration:** Deep API access to EnerGov may be delayed. The MVP must rely on static fee tables and published zoning codes to avoid being blocked by backend IT constraints.
* **Procurement:** City procurement rules may prohibit direct contracts with for-profit vendors for this type of agile development. A nonprofit partnership circumvents this blocker.
* **Political Feasibility:** Competing city initiatives could drain resources. iBuild must be framed as an economic development tool (aligned with the Mayor's Action Plan) rather than just an IT upgrade.
