# Executive Summary

First-time entrepreneurs in Richmond, Virginia, face significant hurdles when starting a business, primarily stemming from a fragmented and unclear city process. Key pain points include scattered information across various departmental websites, confusion over the specific zoning and occupancy permits required (CZC vs. CO/HCO), and a historical reputation for severe delays in permit processing. This lack of a centralized, transparent system creates anxiety, leads to costly delays in business openings, and increases the administrative burden on founders. In response, the city has acknowledged these shortcomings and initiated significant improvements. These include hiring a project facilitator to offer a 'one-stop shop' for applicants and achieving substantial reductions in permit turnaround times, which now average within five business days. Despite these positive changes, the legacy of past difficulties and the remaining fragmentation continue to be a source of stress for new business owners.

# Detailed Pain Point Report

### Founder Persona: Navigating Zoning and Permitting

**Job to be Done:** As a first-time entrepreneur choosing a location in Richmond, I want to know if my proposed address is zoned for my use and what city approvals I need (CZC or occupancy), so I can avoid costly delays and secure my city business license (BPOL) on time.

**Pain Points & Evidence:**

1.  **Fragmented and Unclear Information:** Entrepreneurs struggle to find a single, clear source of information outlining the entire process. Information is scattered across different city department websites, such as Planning and Development Review and the Department of Finance, requiring users to piece together the full sequence of requirements.
2.  **Confusion between CZC and CO/HCO:** There is a significant lack of clarity on when a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) is sufficient versus when a more involved Certificate of Occupancy (CO) or a change of occupancy/use permit (HCO) is necessary. A city guide notes that while a CZC often suffices for Business or Retail Use, a CO is mandatory for Assembly, Education, and Institutional Uses, a distinction not immediately apparent to new entrepreneurs.
3.  **Uncertain Timelines and Delays:** Richmond's permitting office has a historical reputation for major backlogs, with past processing times stretching from 45 days to two months. Although a 2022 Richmond BizSense report indicates dramatic improvements with turnaround times now averaging within five business days, the memory of these past delays, which the city itself acknowledged as a 'pretty poor reputation,' continues to create anxiety for founders.

**Workarounds and Costs:**

*   **Current Workaround:** The existing process is manual and convoluted, requiring entrepreneurs to search parcel and zoning maps, contact the Zoning office for clarification, file separately on the Online Permit Portal, and then follow up with the Finance department to verify licensing needs.
*   **Cost of Status Quo:** This fragmented system imposes tangible costs, including lost revenue from delayed openings, a heavy administrative burden that diverts focus from core business activities, and significant stress and uncertainty for entrepreneurs.

### Anxious New Business Owner Persona: Seeking Guidance and Transparency

**Job to be Done:** When I’m submitting my first applications to the City and State, I want clear, official, step-by-step guidance with status visibility.

**Pain Points & Evidence:**

1.  **Lack of a Centralized, User-Friendly Portal:** The city's Online Permit Portal (OPP) has been described as 'ineffective.' The city's exploration of updating or replacing its Energov permitting software is an acknowledgment that the current system does not adequately meet user needs for a simple, intuitive interface.
2.  **Limited Status Visibility:** The current system fails to provide clear, real-time visibility into the status of applications. This opacity is a major source of anxiety, leaving business owners unsure of where they stand in the process.
3.  **Desire for a Single Point of Contact:** The city's recent action of hiring a project facilitator to create a 'one-stop shop' for applicants directly indicates that the previous multi-departmental system was a significant pain point, causing confusion and frustration.

**Workarounds and Costs:**

*   **Current Workaround:** Entrepreneurs are compelled to repeatedly and proactively contact various city departments by phone or email to check on their application status, a time-consuming and frustrating endeavor.
*   **Cost of Status Quo:** The lack of clear guidance and visibility leads to high levels of anxiety and frustration. It also results in wasted time as entrepreneurs are forced to act as their own project managers, tracking down information that a modern system should provide automatically.

### Cross-Cutting Equity Dimensions

The difficulties in navigating Richmond's business startup process disproportionately impact minority-owned, women-owned, and entrepreneurs from lower-income backgrounds. These groups often have less access to the financial resources, professional networks (e.g., lawyers, consultants), and available time required to overcome a complex and fragmented bureaucratic system. A 2026 city report highlighted 'significant economic disparities between minority and non-minority communities.' The complexity of the current process can function as a significant barrier to entry, stifling entrepreneurship within these communities and exacerbating existing economic inequalities.

### Evidence from Comparable Cities

*   **Pittsburgh, PA:** Provides clear guidance on its website about when a Certificate of Occupancy is required and offers an online tool to verify a property's certificate status.
*   **Baltimore, MD:** Offers specific guidance in its permit FAQs, such as when to add a lessee to a Use & Occupancy permit, demonstrating a focus on clarifying common points of confusion.
*   **Louisville, KY:** Features a comprehensive 'Doing Business in Louisville, KY' guide, which serves as the type of centralized, step-by-step resource that is currently missing in Richmond.

# Zoning And Occupancy Process Analysis

The zoning and occupancy approval process in Richmond, Virginia, presents significant challenges for first-time entrepreneurs due to fragmented information and procedural ambiguity. The core of the issue lies in determining the necessary approvals for a specific business location and use. Entrepreneurs must navigate the distinction between a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC), a Certificate of Occupancy (CO), and a change of occupancy/use permit, often referred to as a Home-based CO (HCO). According to a city guide mentioned in the research, a CZC is often sufficient for properties intended for Business or Retail Use. However, for uses classified as Assembly, Education, and Institutional, a full Certificate of Occupancy must be obtained through the more involved HCO process. This distinction is a primary source of confusion and potential delays. The application process for these permits is handled through the city's Online Permit Portal (OPP), which has been described by some as 'ineffective.' The current workaround for entrepreneurs is a manual, multi-step process that involves searching city parcel and zoning maps, directly contacting the Zoning office (part of Planning and Development Review) for clarification, and then filing on the OPP. This fragmented approach is compounded by historical anxiety over processing times. While a 2022 report indicated significant improvements with permit turnaround times averaging within five business days, the city's past reputation for severe backlogs—with waits of 45 days to two months—still influences entrepreneurs' perceptions and creates stress.

# Business Licensing Bpol Process Analysis

The Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) is a critical final step for legally operating a business in Richmond and is managed by the city's Department of Finance. A significant prerequisite for obtaining a BPOL is securing the appropriate zoning and occupancy approvals from the city. An entrepreneur cannot successfully apply for their business license without first having an approved Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) or Certificate of Occupancy (CO), depending on the business type and location. This dependency makes the BPOL process directly vulnerable to the delays and confusion inherent in the preceding zoning approval stages. The research highlights that the process is not seamlessly integrated. The current workaround for entrepreneurs involves separately contacting the Department of Finance to verify licensing requirements after they have navigated the zoning and permitting system. This lack of a unified, streamlined system contributes to the overall administrative burden and uncertainty for new business owners. The city's acknowledgment of these issues is evidenced by its hiring of a project facilitator to create a 'one-stop shop' experience, aiming to reduce the need for entrepreneurs to interact with multiple departments like Finance and Planning and Development Review separately.

# Recommendations For Ibuild Project

Based on the identified pain points for first-time entrepreneurs in Richmond, the 'ibuild' project should focus on creating a centralized, transparent, and user-friendly navigation tool. Actionable recommendations for the project include:

1.  **Develop a Unified, Step-by-Step Checklist:** Create a single, comprehensive digital checklist that guides an entrepreneur through the entire process, from initial idea to obtaining a Business License (BPOL). This would consolidate information currently scattered across the Planning and Development Review, Finance, and other city department websites, directly addressing the core pain point of fragmented information.

2.  **Implement a 'CZC vs. CO' Decision Tree:** Build an interactive questionnaire or decision tree. By answering a series of simple questions about their business type (e.g., retail, restaurant, office), proposed activities, and location, the tool would clearly advise the user whether they need a simple Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) or must go through the more complex Certificate of Occupancy (CO/HCO) process. This would eliminate a major source of confusion and potential delays.

3.  **Integrate Real-Time Status Tracking:** Provide a dashboard where users can see the live status of their submitted applications (zoning, permits, licenses). While this may require integration with the city's backend systems like Energov, it would directly solve the 'Limited Status Visibility' pain point and reduce the anxiety and administrative burden of having to constantly contact city departments for updates.

4.  **Provide Timeline Estimations:** Use historical data or city-provided targets to give users an estimated timeline for each step of the process. This would help manage expectations, reduce anxiety around 'Uncertain Timelines', and allow entrepreneurs to plan their opening and finances more effectively.

5.  **Create a Centralized Document Hub:** Allow users to upload and manage all required documents in one place, with clear explanations for each, reducing the risk of incomplete or incorrect submissions.
