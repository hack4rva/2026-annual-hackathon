# From Data to Deals: Scaling Richmond's OMBD Proactive Match Dashboard Across the Commonwealth and Beyond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The OMBD Proactive Match Dashboard has the potential to fundamentally shift how Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) interact with municipal procurement, but framing it solely as a City of Richmond IT project severely limits its impact. The universe of this idea expands significantly when viewed as a regional economic mobility engine powered by state-level data and federal innovation funding. By leveraging the Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity's (SBSD) existing SWaM directory [1] and integrating with the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS) [2], the dashboard can transcend city limits. 

To maximize adoption and sustainability, the dashboard should not be entirely city-owned. Instead, it requires a hybrid governance model where the City of Richmond provides the open data infrastructure, while a coalition of regional nonprofits and economic development groups—such as the Metropolitan Business League and the Greater Richmond Partnership—manage the vendor-facing experience and outreach. This approach unlocks federal funding streams like the EDA's Build to Scale program [3] and the MBDA Business Center Program [4], transforming a local civic hack into a scalable, federally backed procurement innovation hub.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

The most viable and scalable ownership model is a hybrid structure dividing responsibilities between the City of Richmond and a regional nonprofit coalition. 

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond (via OMBD and Procurement) must own the underlying data pipelines, API endpoints, and the publication of contract data in standardized formats (like OCDS). The city is responsible for ensuring data accuracy, enforcing 5-digit NIGP/NAICS code requirements for internal solicitations, and maintaining the secure infrastructure that feeds the dashboard.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A nonprofit partner (or coalition, such as the Metropolitan Business League or ChamberRVA) should own the vendor-facing dashboard interface, user experience, and the proactive outreach campaigns. Nonprofits possess the community trust and agility required to onboard MBEs, conduct training, and iterate on the software without municipal procurement bottlenecks.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The City should not own the user-facing application layer or the direct vendor coaching programs. Municipal ownership of the UI often leads to stagnant software, poor user experience, and a lack of trust from historically marginalized business owners who may be skeptical of city-run portals.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Virginia eVA and SBSD SWaM Directory:** The state already maintains a comprehensive directory of certified SWaM and DBE firms [1]. eVA utilizes NIGP commodity codes for vendor registration and automatic email notifications [26]. *Lesson:* Richmond does not need to build a new directory from scratch; it must pull from and align with the state's existing SWaM database and enforce strict NIGP code hygiene to prevent alert fatigue.
* **Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS):** Implemented by over 30 governments globally, OCDS provides a free, non-proprietary standard for publishing public contracting data [23]. *Lesson:* Adopting OCDS ensures Richmond's contract data is interoperable, allowing third-party developers and nonprofits to easily build and maintain the matching dashboard without relying on proprietary vendor lock-in [27].
* **Metropolitan Business League's Amplify Program:** MBL launched a six-month executive-level initiative to equip small business owners to secure procurement awards [14]. *Lesson:* Technology alone is insufficient. The dashboard must be paired with high-touch, cohort-based training programs like Amplify to ensure MBEs are ready to bid when a match occurs.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential. The underlying problem—MBEs struggling to find relevant contracts—is universal across the Commonwealth. 

* **Regional/Statewide Shift:** If the frame shifts to the regional (PlanRVA) or state level, the dashboard can tap into the Virginia SBSD's SWaM directory [1] and eVA procurement data [26]. This creates a unified portal where an MBE in Richmond can be matched with contracts in Henrico, Chesterfield, or state agencies, vastly increasing the volume of opportunities and making the platform stickier for users.
* **Nonprofit Ownership of the Resident-Facing Layer:** If a nonprofit owns the UI while the city provides data, the platform can iterate faster, bypass municipal procurement delays for software updates, and build deeper trust with minority business owners who may have historical grievances with city procurement offices.
* **Pilot to Scale:** The City of Richmond can serve as the initial sandbox to prove the matching algorithm's efficacy using Socrata data. Once validated, a partner like the Virginia APEX Accelerator [19] or ChamberRVA [17] can scale the tool to other municipalities.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Alert Fatigue (High Risk):** Using broad 3-digit NIGP codes leads to irrelevant alerts and vendor fatigue. Mitigation requires enforcing 5-digit or 7-digit commodity code precision during vendor onboarding [28].
* **Data Integration & Quality (High Risk):** The dashboard relies on the timely and accurate publication of city contract data. If Richmond's internal systems (e.g., B2Gnow, ERP) do not expose clean APIs or if data is siloed, the matching engine will fail.
* **Trust and Adoption (Medium Risk):** MBEs may be hesitant to invest time in another government portal. Partnering with trusted entities like the Metropolitan Business League [13] is critical for legitimacy.
* **Grant Dependence (Medium Risk):** Relying solely on federal grants (like EDA Build to Scale [3]) creates a fiscal cliff. The platform must eventually integrate into the operational budgets of regional economic development authorities or be sustained by corporate sponsorships via ChamberRVA.
