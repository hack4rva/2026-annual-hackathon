# Executive Summary

The primary pain points in Richmond, VA's city contracting process create significant barriers for both Minority-Owned Business (MBE) owners and city staff. MBE owners face a fragmented and inefficient system where they must manually monitor multiple, non-integrated platforms—including OpenGov for solicitations, eVA for statewide leads, iSupplier for vendor registration, and B2Gnow for MBE certification. This fragmentation leads to missed opportunities and duplicative administrative effort. Furthermore, the notification systems on these platforms often produce low-relevance alerts due to inconsistent commodity code taxonomies (NIGP vs. NAICS) and a lack of precise filtering, eroding vendor trust. For city staff in the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) and Department of Procurement Services (DPS), the core challenge is data silos. Information is scattered across these same systems, preventing a unified view of vendor capabilities and contract opportunities. This makes proactive and targeted outreach to MBEs difficult, hinders the ability to accurately measure and report on participation gaps against disparity study findings, and complicates the overall management of solicitations and compliance.

# Mbe Owner Persona Analysis

## Pain Points

1. **Fragmented Systems and Sign-up Overhead**: MBE owners must navigate and maintain profiles on at least four separate platforms: OpenGov for solicitations and submissions, eVA/VBO for statewide leads, iSupplier for vendor registration, and B2Gnow for OMBD certification and directories. This fragmentation forces them to constantly monitor multiple sites.
2. **Low Signal-to-Noise in Notifications**: Alerts are often irrelevant. OpenGov requires users to 'Subscribe' to the portal and 'Follow' individual projects to get updates, which are tied to commodity codes (e.g., NIGP) that may not align with the vendor's self-identified capabilities (e.g., NAICS). Furthermore, eVA sends out daily statewide 'Leads' emails, adding high volume without precise local filtering.
3. **Tight Timelines and Heavy Document Parsing**: The process demands significant time investment. State guidance emphasizes the need to read 'all solicitation documents,' which are often large and numerous. This is a substantial burden for small firms that lack dedicated administrative or proposal staff.
4. **Registration and Account Friction**: The city's iSupplier portal presents significant barriers to entry. It requires submission of W-9 and ACH forms, has a strict 2-minute inactivity timeout that can cause loss of unsaved data, involves a 3-5 business day approval process, and sends system emails from a 'RAPIDS Workflow Mailer' that frequently land in spam folders.

## Current Workarounds

To find opportunities, MBE owners engage in a highly manual and time-consuming process. Their primary workaround involves manually monitoring multiple portals, including the City's OpenGov portal, the statewide eVA Virginia Business Opportunities (VBO) site, and communications from the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD). They also rely on informal channels like word-of-mouth. To stay eligible and visible, they must periodically update their business capability codes across the different systems they are registered in, such as the iSupplier and OMBD directories.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The current system imposes significant costs on MBEs. The primary cost is missed revenue opportunities, which occur due to missed bid notices, being filtered out by misaligned commodity codes, or being unable to complete registration in time for a solicitation window. There is also a high administrative overhead, with countless hours lost to monitoring multiple systems and parsing irrelevant notifications. This disproportionately affects the smallest firms with limited resources. Finally, the experience erodes vendor trust; delays in onboarding, misrouted communications, and a high volume of irrelevant alerts from 'spray and pray' tactics lead to frustration and skepticism about the fairness and efficiency of the city's procurement process.


# City Staff Persona Analysis

## Pain Points

1. **Siloed Data Across Systems**: Staff must contend with critical data being spread across multiple, disconnected platforms. MBE directories and commodity codes are in B2Gnow, solicitations and awards are in OpenGov, the vendor master file is in iSupplier/ERP, and some discovery happens in eVA. This fragmentation prevents a unified view of the procurement landscape.
2. **Difficulty Measuring Participation Gaps**: The city's 2023 Disparity Study confirmed a gap between the availability and utilization of MBEs. However, translating this finding into actionable, operational dashboards is difficult for staff due to the siloed data and a lack of consistent definitions and canonical datasets for measurement.
3. **Ineffective and Risky Vendor Outreach**: Without a reliable way to match vendor capabilities (from B2Gnow) to specific solicitations (in OpenGov), staff risk conducting 'spray and pray' email campaigns to large, undifferentiated lists. This approach is inefficient and can damage relationships with the vendor community.
4. **High Process and Support Burden**: Procurement staff (DPS) manage solicitations in OpenGov, but they must also provide support to vendors who are unfamiliar with that system and must use the separate iSupplier portal for registration. This adds an extra layer of coordination and support burden to their workflow.
5. **Reporting Latency and Lack of Real-Time Insight**: Key transparency datasets, such as contract awards, are only updated monthly. This latency means analysts and OMBD staff cannot see real-time participation metrics, forcing them to be reactive rather than proactive in their supplier engagement and gap analysis.

## Current Workarounds

City staff rely on inefficient and manual workflows to bridge the gaps between their disparate systems. The executive summary explicitly mentions a reliance on 'spreadsheets and disparate data sources'. This involves manually reconciling data from B2Gnow, OpenGov, and iSupplier to track outreach and compliance. For outreach, the current workaround is to pull large lists from the B2Gnow directories and send mass emails, a 'spray and pray' tactic that lacks precision.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The status quo results in significant operational inefficiency, as staff spend valuable time on manual data reconciliation instead of strategic outreach and analysis. This inefficiency directly undermines the city's ability to achieve its equity goals; the lack of real-time, integrated data makes it difficult to strategically address the participation gaps identified in the Disparity Study or to prove the impact of their programs. Furthermore, the reliance on imprecise, mass-email outreach leads to 'alert fatigue' among vendors, eroding trust and reducing the effectiveness of communications. This can also lead to lower competition on bids if outreach is not targeted effectively, ultimately impacting the value the city receives.


# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Most Affected Groups

The groups most negatively impacted are the smallest Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs), new entrants to the city contracting market, and firms that lack dedicated administrative or proposal staff. These businesses bear the brunt of the administrative burden required for multi-portal monitoring, multiple registration processes, and parsing extensive solicitation documents. The summary also notes that language access needs can further compound these barriers.

## Exclusionary Factors

Exclusionary mechanisms include: mismatches between different code taxonomies (e.g., NIGP, NAICS) used across various systems, which leads to missed opportunities; monthly latency in data updates which limits the ability for targeted and timely outreach; reliance on email-based notifications that require specific user actions (like 'Subscribe' or 'Follow') and are susceptible to being caught in spam filters; and significant lead times for vendor registration approval, which can cause businesses to miss solicitation windows.

## Evidence Of Systemic Issues

The primary evidence of systemic issues is the city's own '2023 Disparity Study'. The City Council officially accepted the study's findings, which concluded that there are significant disparities between the availability of qualified MBEs and their actual utilization in city contracts. This official acknowledgement validates the anecdotal evidence and pain points identified in the research.

# Systemic Challenge Commodity Codes

A critical systemic challenge in Richmond's procurement process is the lack of a standardized commodity code taxonomy across its various platforms. Systems like OpenGov use their own category codes for subscriptions and matching, while vendor profiles in other databases like the OMBD's B2Gnow directory or state-level systems may be organized by different standards, such as the National Institute of Governmental Purchasing (NIGP) codes or North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) codes. This inconsistency is a primary driver of failure in the discovery process. For MBEs, it means that automated alerts from platforms like OpenGov or eVA are often irrelevant, as the codes selected by the city for a solicitation may not align with the codes the vendor used to register their capabilities. This 'code mismatch' leads to vendors receiving a high volume of low-relevance opportunities, forcing them to spend valuable time parsing useless information and simultaneously risking that they will miss relevant solicitations that were coded differently. This ultimately lowers competition and creates significant barriers for small firms with limited administrative resources.

# Systemic Challenge Data Fragmentation

The procurement and vendor management ecosystem for the City of Richmond is highly fragmented, with essential data siloed across at least four distinct, non-integrated systems. Solicitations and bid submissions are managed through OpenGov. Vendor registration and payment details are handled in a separate Oracle-based system, iSupplier. MBE/ESB certification and directories are maintained in B2Gnow. And statewide opportunity leads are distributed via the state's eVA platform. This data fragmentation creates significant inefficiencies for all parties. MBE owners are forced to create and maintain profiles on multiple sites and must actively monitor each one to ensure they don't miss notices. For OMBD and DPS staff, the silos make it nearly impossible to get a holistic, real-time view of the procurement landscape. They cannot easily match vendors from the B2Gnow directory to new solicitations in OpenGov or track an MBE's journey from initial outreach to final award. Furthermore, key transparency data on contracts is only updated monthly on the Open Data Portal, introducing a critical lag that prevents proactive, data-driven engagement with suppliers and makes it difficult to measure the impact of equity initiatives in a timely manner.

# Evidence From Richmond Disparity Study

2023 Disparity Study

## Conducting Firm

MGT

## Key Finding

The study concluded that significant disparities exist between the availability of qualified minority business enterprises (MBEs) and their actual utilization in City of Richmond contracting.

A City Council resolution was passed to formally accept the findings of the disparity study. This resolution also directed the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) to develop and implement a comprehensive action plan to address the identified disparities and mandated that a new disparity study be conducted every five years.


# Current Workarounds And Costs

Minority-owned businesses (MBEs) in Richmond employ laborious workarounds to navigate the fragmented procurement landscape. Their current process involves manually monitoring multiple platforms daily, including the city's OpenGov portal, the statewide eVA VBO system for email leads, and OMBD communications, while also relying on informal word-of-mouth networks. This multi-system monitoring is a significant time and resource drain, disproportionately affecting the smallest firms that lack dedicated administrative or proposal staff. The costs of this status quo are substantial. Functionally, it leads to missed revenue opportunities when notices are overlooked, misrouted to spam, or discovered too late to meet tight submission deadlines. The registration process on iSupplier alone can take 3-5 business days, potentially excluding a vendor from an active solicitation. Financially, MBEs suffer from inefficiency, wasting time parsing high-volume, low-relevance statewide emails from eVA or dealing with duplicative alerts. Systemically, this friction contributes to the underutilization of available MBEs, a fact substantiated by the city's 2023 Disparity Study, and erodes vendor trust in the fairness and accessibility of the procurement process.

# Comparable City Approaches

## City Name

Baltimore, MD

## Approach Description

The City of Baltimore uses Workday as its primary online procurement system, where vendors must register and select specific commodity/service codes to receive email notifications about new bids. Concurrently, the city uses a separate B2G Diversity Management System specifically to monitor MBE and WBE participation goals and send related compliance emails.

## Relevance To Richmond

Baltimore's approach is highly relevant because it mirrors the fragmented system pattern found in Richmond. The use of a main procurement portal (Workday, analogous to Richmond's OpenGov) alongside a separate diversity compliance system (B2G, which Richmond also uses) demonstrates that this siloed structure is a common municipal challenge. This parallel reinforces the need for a solution in Richmond that can integrate data and workflows across these distinct platforms to reduce vendor confusion and administrative burden.


# Failure Of Current Notification Systems

The existing notification mechanisms intended to alert vendors to new opportunities are largely ineffective and contribute to vendor frustration. The daily 'Leads' emails from the statewide eVA system, for example, are not filtered specifically for Richmond City opportunities, inundating local vendors with a high volume of irrelevant, statewide solicitations. This creates a significant 'signal-to-noise' problem. The city's primary solicitation portal, OpenGov, relies on a passive notification model where vendors must proactively find the portal, 'Subscribe' to general categories, and then 'Follow' individual projects to receive updates. This places the burden entirely on the vendor and is prone to error; if a vendor fails to take these specific actions, they receive no updates. Compounding the issue, critical system emails, such as those from the iSupplier registration portal, are known to be flagged by spam filters, meaning vendors may miss crucial communications regarding their account status. For city staff, this reliance on flawed, automated systems without precise matching capabilities leads them to conduct manual outreach that can feel like 'spray and pray' emails, which further causes 'alert fatigue' among vendors and erodes their trust in the city's commitment to equitable opportunity.
