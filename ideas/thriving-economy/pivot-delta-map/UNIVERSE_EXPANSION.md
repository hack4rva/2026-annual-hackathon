# Pivot-Delta Map: Turning Richmond's Business-Flow Data into a City-wide Economic-Signal Engine

## Executive Summary

The Pivot-Delta Map represents a critical shift in how Richmond can understand and support its local economy, moving from lagging state-level aggregates to real-time, neighborhood-level business flow intelligence. Research into leading municipalities demonstrates that local business-license data significantly outperforms state filings for neighborhood mapping, as seen in New York City, San Francisco, and Philadelphia. For instance, NYC's Storefront Activity report maps over 150,000 storefronts, providing granular visibility into vacancy and turnover [1]. However, unlocking this potential in Richmond requires navigating Virginia's strict tax confidentiality laws (Va. Code § 58.1-3), which restrict the sharing of raw local tax returns [2] [3]. 

To succeed, the Pivot-Delta Map must expand beyond a purely city-owned IT project. A hybrid governance model—where the City of Richmond provisions anonymized, aggregated data while a nonprofit or regional partner (such as PlanRVA or LISC) manages the public-facing platform—mitigates legal and procurement risks. Furthermore, framing this as a regional economic-signal network unlocks substantial funding pathways, including GO Virginia Region 4 grants (which recently announced $6.2 million for regional growth projects) [4] and HUD Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) [5]. By layering equity metrics, such as Seattle's approach with its Race and Social Equity Index [6], Richmond can transform raw business data into a targeted engine for inclusive economic development.

## 1. Policy Context & Alignment

The Pivot-Delta Map directly advances Mayor Danny Avula's Mayoral Action Plan (MAP), specifically the "A Thriving Economy" pillar. Currently, economic development staff and small business support organizations (like SCORE, SBDC, and LISC) lack the neighborhood-level business dynamics required to target outreach effectively. By visualizing where entrepreneurial activity is growing, shifting, or declining, the city can move from reactive support to proactive intervention. This tool challenges the core assumption that entrepreneurs only need help starting businesses; it highlights that understanding where businesses are dying, shifting, and consolidating is equally vital for market opportunity and preventing community displacement.

## 2. Data Landscape & Legal Framework

Richmond has access to public state registration data, but city-level license data and privacy laws dictate how it can be operationalized.

### 2.1 Existing Data Sources

The foundational data layers are readily available but insufficient on their own. Virginia SCC business registration data provides public entity formation records. GeoHub parcel data allows for geocoding business addresses, and Census Business Builder / ACS economic data provides demographic context. However, these federal and state sources operate with significant lag and lack the block-level granularity required for real-time corridor health assessments.

### 2.2 Data Gaps & Acquisition Pathways

To achieve the fidelity seen in leading cities, Richmond must integrate local business license data. The critical missing fields include precise license start and end dates, ownership strings (for entity resolution), and "administratively closed" flags to identify "zombie" businesses that have ceased operations but never formally dissolved. Acquiring this requires a structured data-sharing agreement or FOIA process with the Richmond Finance Department to extract and geocode this data to the parcel layer.

### 2.3 Confidentiality & Compliance

Virginia statutes impose strict limitations on the use of local tax data. Va. Code § 58.1-3 explicitly declares local tax records and returns as confidential, limiting disclosure primarily to audit purposes [2] [3] [7]. Local tax officials are heavily restricted in what they can disclose [8]. Therefore, the Pivot-Delta Map must employ a privacy-by-design architecture. The system must aggregate data to obscure Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and rely on the statute's allowances for statistical use, ensuring that the public-facing map displays corridor-level trends rather than individual tax liabilities.

## 6. Ownership & Governance Models

A hybrid city-nonprofit structure balances public accountability with agile development and mitigates procurement bottlenecks.

### 6.1 City-owned (full)
* **Pros:** Ensures strict data sovereignty, direct integration with city policy (like the MAP), and seamless access to internal finance databases.
* **Cons:** Subject to slow procurement cycles, heavy IT staffing loads, and potential public trust issues regarding surveillance or tax enforcement.

### 6.2 Nonprofit-led
* **Pros:** Highly agile, eligible for diverse philanthropic grants, and often viewed with higher trust by community members and small business owners.
* **Cons:** Struggles with long-term financial sustainability and lacks the legal authority to mandate data feeds from city departments.

### 6.3 Hybrid / Regional Consortium (Recommended)
The optimal model is a joint venture. The City of Richmond retains ownership of the raw data and manages the secure pipeline (ensuring compliance with Va. Code § 58.1-3). A nonprofit partner (e.g., LISC) or a regional body (PlanRVA) owns the public-facing application and user interface. This shares costs, expands geographic relevance, and allows for rapid feature iteration outside of municipal procurement constraints.
