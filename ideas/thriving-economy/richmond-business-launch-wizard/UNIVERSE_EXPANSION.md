# Richmond Business Launch Wizard – Expanding the Ownership, Funding, and Partnership Universe to Power a Regional “One‑Stop Start‑Up” Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Richmond Business Launch Wizard has the potential to transcend a standard municipal IT project and become a regional economic engine. By shifting the frame from a city-owned static guide to a hybrid-owned, address-aware decision engine, the universe of this idea expands significantly. True interactive wizards drive massive self-service rates (95-98%) and save thousands of staff hours by preventing downstream rework and surprise costs. 

To realize this, the project must look beyond Richmond's municipal budget. The funding landscape at the state level is highly favorable, with GO Virginia allocating millions to digital innovation and entrepreneurship [1] [2], alongside federal SSBCI funds managed by the Virginia Small Business Financing Authority [3] [4]. By partnering with established local ecosystem players like Startup Virginia [5] and the Capital Region SBDC [6], the Wizard can leverage agile non-profit development cycles while utilizing the City's authoritative GeoHub zoning data [7] [8].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

Pure city ownership risks procurement delays, slow feature iteration, and limited scalability beyond municipal borders. Conversely, pure nonprofit ownership lacks the legal authority to access and maintain authoritative zoning and fee data. A hybrid model is the optimal path forward.

* **What the City should own:** The underlying data infrastructure. The City of Richmond must own and maintain the APIs for GeoHub zoning layers [7], permit fee schedules, and historical processing data from the Online Permit Portal [9]. The City also owns the regulatory authority and final application processing.
* **What partners should own:** The resident-facing layer. A nonprofit partner (such as Startup Virginia) should own the UI/UX, product development, user testing, and multilingual localization. This allows for agile development outside of municipal procurement cycles.
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The software code base and the community outreach strategy. Open-sourcing the code under a nonprofit steward allows other regional counties (e.g., Henrico, Chesterfield) to adopt the tool, creating a unified regional experience.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Richmond Online Permit Portal (OPP):** 
 * *What was attempted:* A digital portal for submitting plans, applying for permits, and requesting inspections [9].
 * *What worked:* Digitized the workflow and enabled online payments via Tyler Technologies [29].
 * *What failed/Lesson:* It is a transactional backend, not a discovery frontend. Users must already know what permits they need. The Wizard must sit *in front* of the OPP to sequence the requirements.
* **Virginia Business One Stop:**
 * *What was attempted:* A state-level portal providing resources to plan, develop, or expand a business [30].
 * *What worked:* Centralized state-level registration links.
 * *What failed/Lesson:* Lacks local, address-aware zoning checks. The Wizard should bridge the gap between state registration and local municipal zoning/permitting.
* **Richmond GeoHub Zoning Map:**
 * *What was attempted:* Publicly accessible GIS data for zoning districts [7] [31].
 * *What worked:* The data exists and is maintained by the City.
 * *What failed/Lesson:* It requires users to interpret zoning codes manually. The Wizard must consume this data via API to automate the eligibility signal.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this idea solely as a Richmond municipal project limits its potential. 

* **Why Richmond-only is too small:** A city-only frame restricts funding to municipal IT budgets and limits the data's usefulness to city limits, ignoring the reality that entrepreneurs often evaluate sites across county lines (e.g., Henrico, Chesterfield).
* **Regional Shift:** By aligning with PlanRVA's Comprehensive Economic Development Strategy (CEDS) [32], the Wizard can serve as a regional data-collection tool, tracking permit demand by geography to support broader workforce and infrastructure funding requests.
* **Statewide Shift:** Positioning the tool as a localized "last-mile" engine that integrates with the Virginia Business One Stop [30] opens access to VSBFA and GO Virginia funding [1] [33].
* **Hybrid Impact:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the platform can be white-labeled and scaled to adjacent jurisdictions rapidly, while the City focuses solely on maintaining accurate data feeds.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

| Risk Factor | Description & Mitigation |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Data Freshness & Legal Authority** | *Blocker:* If fee tables or zoning rules change and the Wizard is outdated, it creates liability. *Mitigation:* The City must expose live APIs (via GeoHub [8] and Tyler Tech [29]) rather than static data dumps. |
| **Procurement Delays** | *Blocker:* City IT procurement can take 12-18 months. *Mitigation:* Utilize the hybrid model where a non-profit partner (e.g., Startup Virginia) receives grant funding to build the UI independently. |
| **Technical Integration** | *Blocker:* Connecting a modern frontend to legacy municipal databases. *Mitigation:* Scope the MVP to only read data (zoning checks, fee estimates) rather than write data (submitting the actual application). |
| **Equity and Trust** | *Blocker:* English-only tools exclude minority entrepreneurs. *Mitigation:* Mandate multilingual support (EN, ES) from day one, funded by SSBCI Technical Assistance grants [3]. |
