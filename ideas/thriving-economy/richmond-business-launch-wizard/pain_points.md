# Executive Summary

This Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) analysis for the Richmond Business Launch Wizard identifies the significant challenges faced by first-time entrepreneurs in Richmond, Virginia. The current process of starting a business is fragmented, requiring individuals to navigate numerous city (Planning and Development Review, Finance) and state (State Corporation Commission) websites, forms, and offices. This disjointed landscape leads to critical errors, such as failing to secure a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) before applying for a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL), a key dependency. Entrepreneurs grapple with uncertainty regarding timelines for permits and reviews, which can span weeks or months, and lack a unified way to track their progress across different agencies. The core challenges are not merely functional; they are also deeply emotional, rooted in the anxiety of investing limited savings, the fear of making costly mistakes that lead to financial penalties, and a feeling that the city's processes are an obstacle rather than a supportive guide. The goal of the proposed wizard is to address these pains by providing a single, accurate, and dependency-ordered checklist, empowering entrepreneurs to open their businesses legally, on time, and with a sense of confidence and control.

# User Persona Profile

The primary user persona is a first-time entrepreneur in Richmond, Virginia, who is investing their limited personal savings to launch a new venture. This individual is preparing to open either a physical storefront business, such as a retail shop or restaurant, or a home-based business, like a consultancy or studio. Lacking prior experience with municipal and state regulations, they are navigating the complex web of permits, licenses, and compliance for the first time. This user group is particularly vulnerable to the financial and temporal costs of the current system; they include low-capital individuals, immigrants who may face language barriers, and micro-entrepreneurs for whom every dollar and day counts. Their primary goal is to successfully and legally establish their business without exhausting their startup capital on unforeseen delays, penalties, or professional expediter fees.

# Functional Job To Be Done

The primary functional job for the entrepreneur is to obtain a single, accurate, and correctly sequenced checklist of all city and state requirements needed to launch their business. This includes understanding and completing tasks in the correct dependency order, such as state-level registration with the State Corporation Commission (SCC), followed by city-level applications for a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC), any necessary building or trade permits, and potentially health or ABC permits for food and beverage establishments. The final critical step is securing a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) within 30 days of opening. The ultimate outcome the user seeks is to successfully open their business legally and on schedule, without encountering costly rework, failed inspections, or financial penalties for non-compliance.

# Emotional Job To Be Done

The entrepreneur's emotional job is to gain confidence and reduce the significant anxiety associated with starting a new business. As they invest their limited personal savings, they need to feel in control of the process and secure in the knowledge that they are not making critical, costly mistakes. This involves moving from a state of fear about unknown requirements, potential penalties for late filings (such as the 10% penalty for a late BPOL), and long, uncertain delays (like the 60-90 day ABC license processing time) to a feeling of empowerment. The user wants to feel that the city's process is a supportive and transparent guide, not an 'onerous' and punitive barrier. Ultimately, they need to build trust in the system so they can focus on building their business, rather than worrying about compliance pitfalls.

# Key Entrepreneur Pain Points

## Category

Dependency

## Description

Entrepreneurs struggle with the non-obvious and cross-departmental sequencing of required approvals. A critical and often missed dependency is the requirement to secure a Certificate of Zoning Compliance (CZC) and/or a Certificate of Occupancy (CO) from the Planning and Development Review department before the Department of Finance will issue a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL).

## Evidence

Must secure CZC/CO before BPOL; sequencing is non-obvious and cross-departmental. City BPOL page requires CZC prior to license; CO/CZC prerequisites are scattered across PDR guides.

## Category

Timeline

## Description

The process is fraught with significant and unpredictable delays, creating multi-week uncertainty. Initial plan reviews can take from 5 to over 30 business days depending on project complexity, and processing for some applications can take an additional 2-3 weeks. This makes it extremely difficult for entrepreneurs to plan opening dates and manage carrying costs.

## Evidence

Permit/plan review and inspections introduce multi-week uncertainty: online submittal 3–5 business days; initial plan review commonly 5–30 business days depending on project size; processing may take 2–3 weeks for some application types.

## Category

Financial

## Description

There is a strict deadline for new businesses to obtain a BPOL within 30 days of opening. Missing this deadline due to process confusion or delays results in a steep financial penalty of 10% of the tax due (or a $10 minimum), plus 10% annual interest on the unpaid amount.

## Evidence

Tight BPOL deadline: new businesses must obtain license within 30 days of opening; late penalty 10% (min $10) and 10% annual interest.

## Category

Dependency

## Description

Specific industries, particularly food and beverage, face additional lengthy dependencies that create critical path risks. Restaurants must undergo health plan reviews and obtain ABC licenses for alcohol sales, a process that adds an average of 60-90 days to the timeline, delaying a key revenue stream.

## Evidence

Health and ABC timelines add 60–90 days for alcohol and additional time for health plan review, creating critical path dependencies for restaurants.

## Category

Informational

## Description

The digital application process itself is a significant hurdle. Entrepreneurs report that the multi-step process on the city's Online Permit Portal (OPP) is confusing, especially for home-based businesses applying for a Residential CZC. Usability issues and the need for specific, non-obvious attachments can stall applications.

## Evidence

Must apply for a Residential CZC via OPP with specific affidavits/attachments; multi-step digital process can be confusing. OPP/portal usability hurdles reported anecdotally.

## Category

Financial

## Description

Entrepreneurs, particularly in service industries, face uncertainty in financial compliance. They must estimate their gross receipts for their initial BPOL application, which may require later adjustments. Furthermore, misclassifying business activities can lead to incorrect tax rates, requiring time-consuming re-filings.

## Evidence

Must estimate gross receipts for BPOL and adjust later ('Beginner’s Adjustments'); misclassification across multiple activity types can affect rates.


# Current Process And Workarounds

The current process for an entrepreneur in Richmond is manual, fragmented, and requires significant self-directed research. Entrepreneurs must piece together information from a variety of disparate city and state websites, including the Richmond Department of Planning and Development Review (PDR), the Department of Finance, the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC), and the Virginia Business One Stop portal. This involves navigating confusing online systems, downloading and interpreting PDF forms, and making direct phone calls to different agencies. Due to the system's complexity, entrepreneurs have developed numerous workarounds. Formally, they can use the city's Online Permit Portal (OPP) for applications, escalate stalled or complex issues via the city's 'TechDesk', and schedule in-person or virtual assistance. To accelerate timelines, they can also hire third-party plan reviewers or use the city's 48-hour third-party inspection policy. Informally, many rely on free mentorship from organizations like SCORE Richmond, seek advice from peers on forums like Reddit, or hire professional permit expediters to manage the entire process for them.

# Cost Of Status Quo

## Cost Type

Financial Penalty

## Description

The confusing and delay-prone process of obtaining necessary permits and licenses directly exposes new businesses to financial penalties. The failure to secure a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) within the mandated 30-day window after opening results in significant fines and interest, adding financial strain to a new venture. This is just one of several costs, which also include extended lease payments during unexpected review delays (2-12+ weeks), fees for temporary occupancy certificates ($263), and the opportunity cost of lost revenue, such as months of missed alcohol sales for restaurants waiting on an ABC license.

## Example

A 10% penalty on the BPOL tax due (with a $10.00 minimum) plus 10% annual interest for late filing.

# Business Licensing Bpol

## Issuing Department

Department of Finance

## Filing Deadline

Within 30 days of opening

## Late Filing Penalty

10% of the tax due or $10.00, whichever is greater, plus 10% annual interest.

## Cost Structure

Based on gross receipts, with rates varying by business type. For example, the rate for a restaurant with gross receipts of $250,000 or more is $0.36 per $100.


# Building And Occupancy Permits

## Permit Name

## Purpose

To legally occupy a commercial space at the end of the inspection process for a building permit.

## Prerequisites

Passed final inspections on all associated Trade Permits, including Building and Zoning, and resolution of any special inspections or audits.

# Equity And Accessibility Dimensions

## Affected Group

First-time, low-capital, immigrant, and home-based micro-entrepreneurs.

## Barrier

Navigating complex and non-obvious sequencing of multi-departmental requirements (e.g., CZC before BPOL), usability hurdles in digital portals, and language access gaps for non-English speakers.

## Impact

Amplified financial risk due to penalties and delays, which can delay market entry or lead to noncompliance, disproportionately affecting low-margin and minority-owned startups.
