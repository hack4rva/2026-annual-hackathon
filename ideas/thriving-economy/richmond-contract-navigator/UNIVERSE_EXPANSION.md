# Scaling Equity: Strategic Expansion of the Richmond Contract Navigator

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The Richmond Contract Navigator addresses a critical information asymmetry that prevents Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs) from accessing city procurement pipelines. While the hackathon prototype proves technical feasibility, its long-term viability depends on shifting from a standalone "city tool" to a regional infrastructure asset. By transitioning to a Hybrid Ownership Model—where the City of Richmond provides the data pipe and a trusted intermediary like the Metropolitan Business League (MBL) manages the user interface—the project can bypass the trust gap and procurement jargon that currently alienates first-time vendors. 

The universe for this tool expands significantly when framed as a Regional Procurement Gateway. Funding should not rely solely on the city's general fund but rather on a combination of GO Virginia grants, corporate philanthropic arms like Capital One, and federal MBDA Capital Readiness funds. This expansion transforms the tool from a simple search engine into a vendor readiness engine that prepares businesses for state and federal opportunities, ensuring Richmond's economic initiatives create lasting, multi-level wealth.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION
**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Fueled, Nonprofit-Hosted)**

The Richmond Contract Navigator should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Instead, it requires a hybrid model that separates the data infrastructure from the user-facing application. 

* **What the City Should Own (The Data Pipe):** The City of Richmond, specifically the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) [1] and the IT department, must own the data pipeline. Their responsibility is to negotiate API access with procurement vendors (like OpenGov or BidNet) and maintain a clean, real-time feed of active solicitations.
* **What Partners Should Own (The Front Door):** A trusted community organization, such as the Metropolitan Business League (MBL), should own the frontend application, branding, and user experience. The MBL recently received a $250,000 grant from Richmond's Economic Development Authority for small business support [2], making them an ideal host. 
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The resident-facing interface and the AI matching logic should live outside the `.gov` ecosystem to mitigate the "insiders-only" perception and allow for agile updates without municipal procurement delays.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Virginia eVA Procurement Portal Training:** The state provides a blended learning approach ("Learning as a Journey") and extensive manuals for its eVA platform [23] [24]. *Lesson:* While comprehensive, these manuals are dense. The Navigator must translate this density into actionable, plain-English steps.
* **VDOT DBE Supportive Services:** VDOT provides training and assistance specifically for Disadvantaged Business Enterprises to help them grow and participate in federal/state contracts [25] [26]. *Lesson:* Sector-specific support works; the Navigator could eventually feature a transportation-specific module.
* **Commercial Platforms (OpenGov, Bonfire):** Cities overwhelmingly buy commercial platforms that focus on internal workflow rather than vendor-facing AI matching. *Lesson:* Do not try to build a full procurement system; build a lightweight intelligence layer on top of open data.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential. Richmond's procurement volume alone may not justify the ongoing AI maintenance costs. 

If the frame becomes regional (Central Virginia), the project can leverage PlanRVA, which develops the Comprehensive Economic Development Strategy (CEDS) with local governments and businesses [15]. A regional approach solves procurement discovery for adjacent counties (Henrico, Chesterfield) simultaneously, creating a larger marketplace that attracts more vendors and qualifies for GO Virginia regional collaboration grants [3]. 

If the frame becomes statewide, the tool must integrate with the Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity (SBSD) and the eVA system. SBSD manages the Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned Business (SWaM) certification [18]. Matching a business to a contract is useless if they lack this certification; therefore, scaling requires deep-linking the Navigator's readiness roadmap directly into SBSD's certification workflows.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data-Sharing and API Access:** The most critical blocker. If the city uses platforms like BidNet or OpenGov without public APIs for active solicitations, the tool remains a "rear-view mirror" using historical Socrata data.
* **The Trust Deficit:** MBEs often view government portals with skepticism. Hosting the tool on a city domain may suppress adoption compared to a community-hosted alternative.
* **Certification Drop-off:** Users may find a match but abandon the process when faced with the complex SWaM or DBE certification requirements [18] [27].
* **Maintenance Burden:** AI models and LLM tokens require ongoing funding. Without a dedicated maintenance fund (e.g., via the Community Foundation), the tool will become obsolete as procurement laws change.
