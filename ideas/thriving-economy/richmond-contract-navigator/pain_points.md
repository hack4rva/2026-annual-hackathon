# Executive Summary

Minority-owned Business Enterprise (MBE) owners in Richmond, VA, face significant pain points when trying to secure city contracts, primarily stemming from a fragmented and inconsistent procurement information ecosystem. Key challenges include the need to navigate at least four separate city systems to get a complete picture of opportunities: OpenGov for active solicitations, a different open data portal for historical awards, a static PDF for forecasted projects, and the iSupplier portal for vendor registration and management. This disjointed approach makes it difficult and time-consuming to discover, assess, and track opportunities. Further complicating the process are inconsistent instructions, such as conflicting guidance on electronic versus hard-copy submissions, and opaque vendor management processes, like the active vendor deactivation process, which can erode trust and create uncertainty. These barriers disproportionately affect micro and small MBEs, which often have limited administrative capacity, leading to lost time, missed deadlines, and a reduced ability to compete for city contracts.

# Mbe Pain Points And Evidence

## Pain Point Summary

Fragmented Discovery of Contracting Opportunities

## Process Breakdown

Key information for finding and assessing contracts is scattered across at least four different, non-integrated systems. New solicitations are on OpenGov, historical contract awards are in a separate Open Data portal, future opportunities are in a static PDF 'Forecast Calendar', and vendor registration is managed in iSupplier. This lack of a single, unified view forces business owners to piece together a complete picture of the opportunity pipeline manually.

## Evidence And Source

Evidence is found across Richmond's official sites: OpenGov is for new bids ('All bids and proposals are to be submitted electronically through OpenGov'), a separate dataset lists past contracts ('list of open and closed city contracts for the past 5 years'), a PDF calendar lists forecasts ('Forecast Calendar for Anticipated Solicitations'), and iSupplier is for registration ('All suppliers and prospective suppliers must register on iSupplier'). Source: Richmond Procurement Solicitations, City Contracts open data, Forecast Calendar PDF, Supplier Portal.

## User Workaround

Users must manually check each portal individually, set up separate alerts, download and monitor the forecast PDF, browse the historical contracts dataset, and rely on informal networks like word-of-mouth from prime contractors to stay informed.

## Pain Point Summary

Inconsistent Instructions and Ambiguous Submission Processes

## Process Breakdown

There is conflicting guidance on how to submit bids, which erodes trust, especially for new vendors. While the main solicitations page mandates electronic submission via OpenGov, the Invitation for Bid (IFB) page still contains language referencing 'official, hard copy' documents located at City Hall. This ambiguity creates confusion and risk, as a vendor may not be sure if their electronic submission is sufficient or if a physical copy is also required.

## Evidence And Source

The conflict arises from different city web pages. The Solicitations page states submissions are electronic via OpenGov, but the IFB page states, 'The official, hard copy, solicitation document is located in the Procurement Services office...'. This creates uncertainty for bidders. Source: Richmond Procurement Solicitations page, Richmond IFB page.

## User Workaround

To mitigate the risk of a non-compliant submission, users are forced to contact Procurement or OMBD staff directly for clarification, attend supplier outreach events to ask questions, or take screenshots of their electronic submissions as personal proof of receipt.

## Pain Point Summary

Opaque Vendor Status and Data Hygiene Issues

## Process Breakdown

The process for managing a vendor's status with the city, including activation and deactivation, is not transparent. This can lead to a firm's submission being rejected or their eligibility being disrupted without clear, proactive communication. A vendor may believe they are in good standing only to find out their status has changed, jeopardizing their ability to bid on contracts.

## Evidence And Source

A Richmond City Auditor report titled 'Follow-Up - Active Vendor Deactivation Process' indicates that there are formal processes for deactivating vendors, which, if not communicated clearly, can cause surprise deactivations and rejected submissions. Source: Richmond City Auditor Reports Issued list and the specific 2024-09 Follow-Up report.

## User Workaround

Business owners must proactively contact city departments to confirm their vendor status, adding another administrative task. They may also rely on manual email confirmations and save screenshots to self-verify their interactions and submissions with the city.

## Pain Point Summary

Systemic Barriers and High Administrative Burden

## Process Breakdown

The cumulative effect of fragmented information, inconsistent metadata, complex compliance steps, and multi-portal navigation creates a significant administrative burden. This is not just an inconvenience; it's a systemic barrier that requires a high investment of time and resources simply to find and apply for opportunities, which smaller firms often lack.

## Evidence And Source

A 2024 Urban Institute report documents these systemic barriers in public procurement nationally, noting that 'time-consuming discovery, complex compliance steps, and capacity constraints' disproportionately affect entrepreneurs of color. This national finding validates the local experience in Richmond. Source: Urban Institute (2024) report on procurement barriers.

## User Workaround

Firms must dedicate significant non-billable hours to navigating these systems. This often means the business owner themselves, rather than dedicated staff, must perform these tasks, taking time away from core business operations and growth activities.


# Information Fragmentation

## Solicitation Platform

New bids and proposals are required to be submitted electronically through the OpenGov portal. The city's procurement solicitations page states, 'All bids and proposals are to be submitted electronically through OpenGov'.

## Historical Award Data Source

Historical contract award data is located in a separate system, the Richmond Open Data portal. This dataset contains a 'list of open and closed city contracts for the past 5 years' and is updated on a monthly basis, isolating past performance data from current opportunities.

## Procurement Forecast Source

Information on future or anticipated solicitations is published in a separate PDF document known as the 'Forecast Calendar for Anticipated Solicitations'. The document itself notes that the 'Information [is] subject to change at anytime' and is 'Not an all inclusive list,' making it an unreliable and disconnected source for planning.

## Vendor Information Systems

Vendors must manage their information across at least two different systems. The primary registration system is iSupplier, where 'All suppliers and prospective suppliers must register'. This is separate from the OpenGov platform where bids are submitted. Additionally, the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) is involved in vendor data, and City Auditor reports on processes like 'Active Vendor Deactivation' suggest another layer of data management that can impact a vendor's status.


# Data Discoverability Challenges

## Metadata Issues

A core pain point identified is that 'inconsistent metadata' across the fragmented systems hinders a vendor's ability to get a complete and accurate picture of an opportunity. This problem is compounded by the fact that information is spread across OpenGov, a separate open data portal for historical awards, and a PDF forecast, each with potentially different data standards and update cadences.

## Commodity Code Alignment

The challenge of 'category/code alignment' is explicitly mentioned as a factor that 'hinder[s] precise filtering.' This indicates that the use of standard codes (like NIGP or NAICS) is inconsistent across postings and systems, making it difficult for a business to reliably find all relevant solicitations for their specific trade, such as construction or professional services.

## Search Filter Limitations

The current search and filtering tools are ineffective, forcing users into manual workarounds. The persona of an MBE owner details their process as 'Manually check[ing] OpenGov’s Richmond portal search/advanced search, [and] skim[ming] multiple categories.' This demonstrates that the available filters are not precise enough to reliably surface relevant opportunities, costing users significant time and effort.


# Process And Transparency Barriers

## Instructional Clarity Issues

There are direct contradictions in the city's instructions for bidders. While the main solicitations page mandates that 'All bids and proposals are to be submitted electronically through OpenGov,' the Invitation for Bid (IFB) page simultaneously references an 'official, hard copy, solicitation document' located at City Hall. This ambiguity creates confusion and risk for bidders, especially those new to the process.

## Lack Of Submission Feedback

A key motivation for new vendors is 'To have clear, consistent instructions and confirmation that my registration and submissions are received and compliant.' The absence of this confirmation is a major pain point. As a workaround, vendors resort to 'manual email confirmations and screenshots to self-verify submissions,' indicating a lack of a trusted, automated feedback mechanism within the system.

## Trust And Opacity Factors

Several factors create an opaque process that erodes trust. These include ambiguous instructions, the need to navigate multiple systems (OpenGov for submission, iSupplier for registration), and a lack of transparency around vendor status. A City Auditor's follow-up report on the 'Active Vendor Deactivation Process' suggests that critical vendor lifecycle actions can occur without clear communication, potentially leading to 'surprise deactivations or rejected submissions.' This, combined with peer-city evidence of historical discrimination in contracting, creates a high barrier to trust for new and minority-owned firms.


# Current Workarounds And Cost Of Status Quo

## Workaround Description

Users employ a manual, multi-step process to find and verify information. This involves manually checking the OpenGov portal with advanced search filters, setting up email alerts, downloading and monitoring a separate PDF forecast, browsing the City Contracts open data portal for historical context, and cross-referencing information on OMBD pages. For clarification and trust, they must reach out directly to city staff, attend outreach events, and rely on informal networks like prime contractors for guidance.

## Time And Effort Cost

The status quo imposes a significant time and effort cost. Business owners lose valuable hours manually checking disparate systems and re-keying search filters across multiple portals. New vendors face 'extra onboarding time' to navigate the confusing landscape. This administrative burden, described by the Urban Institute as a key barrier, diverts resources from core business functions to cumbersome, repetitive searching and verification tasks.

## Opportunity Cost

The high friction in the process leads to significant opportunity costs. These include missed deadlines due to non-synchronized notices between the forecast and actual solicitations, reduced overall bid throughput because so much time is spent on discovery rather than proposal writing, and lower bid confidence for new vendors. Ultimately, qualified MBEs miss out on contracts they could have won, hindering their growth and revenue.

For small MBE firms, such process gaps can result in surprise deactivations or rejected submissions, creating significant barriers to participation and eroding trust in the city's procurement system.

The provided context does not cite a specific, formal disparity study conducted directly by the City of Richmond. However, it establishes a context of underutilization and structural barriers for minority-owned businesses by referencing an audit of the city's Office of Minority Business Development and by drawing parallels with findings from peer cities like Baltimore, which conducted a disparity study revealing past discrimination. The challenges documented in Richmond's fragmented procurement system are presented as contributing factors to the types of inequities that disparity studies typically uncover.

## Platform Analysis

The analysis of Richmond's digital procurement platforms reveals significant fragmentation and usability issues. Information is scattered across at least four separate systems with different interfaces and update schedules: 1) New solicitations are posted on OpenGov; 2) Vendor registration and profile management are handled through a separate iSupplier portal; 3) Historical contract award data is located in a distinct Open Data portal dataset, updated monthly; and 4) Future opportunities are listed in a static PDF Forecast Calendar that is explicitly noted as being subject to change and not all-inclusive. This creates a disjointed user experience, as there is no single, unified view to search across solicitations, awards, and forecasts. Furthermore, there are critical inconsistencies in instructions; for example, while the main solicitations page mandates electronic submission via OpenGov, other pages still reference the need for 'official, hard copy' documents, creating ambiguity and potential compliance risks for vendors.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Most Affected Populations

The burdens of the current system disproportionately affect the most vulnerable businesses. This includes micro and small MBE/ESB firms that lack dedicated administrative or procurement staff, first-time bidders and new-to-city vendors navigating the complex process for the first time, and firms not yet connected to the informal networks of prime contractors. Evidence from peer cities like Durham suggests that Black-owned businesses in particular report lower success rates, indicating they are among the most affected groups.

## Exclusionary Mechanisms

The identified pain points function as systemic barriers that exclude MBEs. The fragmented information, inconsistent instructions, and opaque processes create a 'steep learning curve' and demand a high level of administrative capacity that small firms lack. These hurdles are not just inconveniences; they are exclusionary mechanisms that filter out businesses without the resources to overcome them. This effectively limits the pool of bidders and reinforces the status quo, where it is easier for larger, more established firms to participate.

## Impact On Economic Equity

These barriers have a direct and negative impact on economic equity. By making it harder for MBEs to win city contracts, the system limits their ability to generate revenue, grow their businesses, and create wealth. This perpetuates historic underutilization and discrimination in public contracting, as evidenced by studies in peer cities like Baltimore. When minority-owned firms are systematically disadvantaged in accessing public funds, it curtails economic opportunity and mobility within minority communities, undermining broader goals of a thriving and equitable economy.
