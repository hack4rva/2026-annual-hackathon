# Scaling Richmond Spend Mirror: Funding, Partnerships & Governance Pathways for MBE Procurement Transparency

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Richmond Spend Mirror has the potential to transcend a simple municipal dashboard and become a regional digital equity infrastructure. By shifting from a pure directory model to a data-driven discovery platform focused on historical spending patterns and renewal cycles, the tool directly addresses the institutional knowledge gap that holds back Minority-Owned Business Enterprises (MBEs). However, keeping this tool strictly within the confines of Richmond's city government limits its funding potential, adoption rate, and geographic impact. 

Expanding the universe of this idea requires a hybrid approach. By leveraging state-level procurement frameworks like Virginia's SWaM (Small Business and Supplier Diversity) [1] and federal ecosystem builders like the MBDA Business Center [2], the Spend Mirror can attract significant outside capital. Furthermore, partnering with national civic tech organizations such as the Open Contracting Partnership [3] and Code for America [4] provides the technical assistance needed to scale this from a local hackathon project into a nationally replicable open-data standard.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Ownership**

To maximize trust, funding eligibility, and sustainability, the Richmond Spend Mirror should adopt a hybrid ownership model. 

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond must retain ownership of the underlying data pipelines, the Socrata API integrations, and the data governance policies. The City is responsible for ensuring the data is fresh, accurate, and published in a standardized format.
* **What partners should own:** A trusted local nonprofit or quasi-public entity (such as the Capital Region Minority Supplier Development Council, which operates the Virginia MBDA Business Center [5]) should own the vendor-facing front-end portal, user experience, and community outreach. 
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The user interface and vendor registration/tracking mechanisms should not be city-owned. MBE vendors often harbor skepticism toward municipal procurement portals due to historical barriers. A nonprofit-led front-end removes the "government gatekeeper" friction and allows for agile feature development outside of slow municipal IT procurement cycles.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Boston (Inclusive Quote Contract & Buying Plan):** Boston successfully combined open directories with a public buying plan and targeted procurement methods, resulting in 12% of discretionary spend ($155.6M) going to M/WBEs. *Lesson:* Discovery must be paired with actionable forecasting and targeted award channels.
* **New York City (PASSPort & SBS Directory):** NYC has massive datasets and 11,382 certified M/WBEs, but only 21.77% won new contracts in FY25. *Lesson:* A directory alone is insufficient; vendors need to see spending patterns, not just active RFPs.
* **Portland (Open Contracting Data Standard):** Portland publishes data from tender to payment, including subcontractor roles. *Lesson:* End-to-end transparency is the gold standard, allowing firms to analyze historical pipelines.
* **Virginia eVA / SWaM Portal:** Virginia's state procurement portal uses NIGP commodity codes and tracks SWaM certification [1] [27]. *Lesson:* Richmond Spend Mirror must map its plain-language inputs to these existing state codes to ensure vendors can seamlessly transition from discovery to bidding.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why is this idea too small framed only as a Richmond project?**
If restricted to Richmond, the tool only captures a fraction of the regional economic engine. MBEs in Central Virginia do not restrict their business to city limits; they operate across Henrico, Chesterfield, and state agencies. 

**What changes if the frame becomes regional or statewide?**
By expanding to a regional frame, the project becomes eligible for GO Virginia grants, which require multi-jurisdictional collaboration [13]. A statewide frame aligns the tool with the Virginia Department of Small Business and Supplier Diversity (SBSD) and the eVA procurement system [1], potentially allowing the Spend Mirror to become the default discovery layer for all SWaM-certified businesses in the Commonwealth.

**What if a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer?**
If a nonprofit (like the MBDA Business Center) owns the front-end, the tool can scale nationally as a white-label open-source product (supported by the Beeck Center's Intergovernmental Software Collaborative [23]), while individual cities simply plug in their Socrata data feeds.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Freshness & Technical Debt:** If the Socrata dataset is not updated automatically, the tool will display stale data, instantly destroying vendor trust. Mitigation requires automated API pipelines.
* **Subcontracting Blind Spots:** A massive amount of MBE opportunity lies in subcontracting, yet data is often missing (as seen in NYC's $33B blind spot). The tool must eventually integrate with systems tracking subcontractor payments.
* **Community Legitimacy:** MBEs may view this as just another empty government transparency initiative. Partnering with trusted entities like the Capital Region Minority Supplier Development Council [28] is required to build legitimacy.
* **Procurement & Legal Authority:** The City must ensure that publishing historical spend and renewal cycles does not violate any confidentiality agreements with incumbent vendors.
