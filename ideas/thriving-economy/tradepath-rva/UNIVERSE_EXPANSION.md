# Scaling TradePath RVA: From City Pilot to Regional Workforce Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
TradePath RVA is more than a digital directory; it is a financial and logistical "last-mile" solution for Richmond’s skilled trades pipeline. While the $5,000 City of Richmond pilot and $50,000 People’s Advantage FCU commitment provide a strong foundation, the project’s true potential lies in its ability to unlock massive, underutilized federal and state funding streams—specifically Virginia's SNAP E&T 50-50 reimbursements [1] and WIOA supportive service funds [2]. 

To achieve sustainable scale, TradePath RVA should transition from a city-owned tool to a regional hybrid model. By positioning a nonprofit intermediary as the operator, the initiative can bypass municipal procurement hurdles, serve the broader regional labor market, and leverage private philanthropic grants that are unavailable to government agencies. The ultimate universe for TradePath is a regional ecosystem where the City provides data and policy support, while private and federal capital fund the barrier removal.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION
**Recommended Model: Hybrid Regional Nonprofit-Led (The "Intermediary" Model)**

* **What the City of Richmond Should Own:** Data integration (permitting/business licenses), Mayoral "bully pulpit" for employer recruitment, and the initial $5,000 pilot seed investment.
* **What a Regional Partner (e.g., United Way or Capital Region Workforce Partnership) Should Own:** The resident-facing platform, case management for barrier removal, and the administration of the Revolving Loan Fund.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The loan fund itself (to avoid municipal debt/legal liability) and the long-term technical maintenance (better handled by a nimble nonprofit or tech partner).

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART
* **Virginia G3 & FastForward:** These state programs cover tuition for high-demand trades, but they do not comprehensively cover "life costs" like childcare or tools. *Lesson:* TradePath must explicitly market itself as the "Gap Filler" to these state programs to avoid redundant efforts.
* **GRTC Transit Access Partnership (TAP):** Provides fareless transit service in Richmond [7], but lacks "last-mile" connectivity to industrial job sites. *Lesson:* TradePath should integrate with GRTC’s transit data to provide a "commute feasibility score" for every trade path listed.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS
* **From Richmond to Regional:** A Richmond-only tool ignores the fact that the Apprenticeship Training Centers (JATCs) for many trades are located in Henrico or Chesterfield. A regional frame allows for a single "Common App" for trades across the MSA.
* **From "App" to "Financial Product":** If framed only as an app, it is a cost center. If framed as a CDFI-backed financial product, it becomes a sustainable asset that attracts private investment.
* **From Symptom to System:** TradePath currently fixes the "symptom" (lack of cash for boots). A "Statewide" frame could address the "system" by lobbying for state programs to include a "Supportive Service Stipend" as a standard feature.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS
* **Loan Default Risk:** The target population is financially vulnerable. *Mitigation:* Use a "Pay It Forward" model where repayment is a percentage of post-graduation income, not a fixed debt.
* **Data Decay:** Training costs and application windows change monthly. *Mitigation:* Require training providers to "claim" their profiles and update data to remain listed.
* **Procurement/Legal:** The City of Richmond may struggle to "gift" $5,000 to a private loan fund. *Mitigation:* Structure the $5,000 as a "Service Contract" for a pilot study on barrier removal.
