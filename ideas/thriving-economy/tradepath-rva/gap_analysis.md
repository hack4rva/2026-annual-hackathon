# Gap Analysis — TradePath RVA

**Pillar:** Thriving Economy
**Problem Statement (verbatim):** "Small Business Navigation — Help first-time entrepreneurs understand what steps to take, in what order, to start and register a business in Richmond."
**Demo:** TradePath RVA (Team presentation)
**Primary User:** Richmond resident considering a skilled trades career but blocked by gap costs (boots, childcare, transportation, lost wages) that traditional funding doesn't cover.
**Pain:** Training programs, employers, and openings exist — but the last-mile costs of entering the pipeline (childcare deposits, tools, transportation, lost wages during training) are not covered by grants or scholarships, and traditional credit is inaccessible to this population.
**Desired Outcome:** A mobile-first platform where a Richmond resident can explore trades careers, assess personal barriers, and access micro-loans ($500–$2,500) specifically designed for gap costs — with repayment deferred until employment.

---

## Problem Grounding: Alignment to PS2

TradePath RVA addresses **workforce development and career pathways for skilled trades**, not "what steps to take, in what order, to start and register a business in Richmond." These are different problems.

The PS2 problem is about regulatory fragmentation — five government systems (SCC, IRS, Virginia Dept. of Taxation, City Zoning, City Finance) that don't cross-reference each other, no documented sequencing, and terminology that excludes first-time entrepreneurs. TradePath RVA does not touch any of these systems. It does not mention business registration, BPOL, zoning compliance, or the SCC. It does not address the startup journey at all.

**The honest alignment assessment:** TradePath RVA is closer to **Thriving Families — Youth Employment Pathways** ("Give Richmond youth one clear place to explore summer jobs, internships, and first-job guidance") than to Thriving Economy PS2. The platform's core function — explore career paths, assess barriers, connect to training and funding — maps almost exactly to the Youth Employment Pathways problem statement, expanded to adults.

**Where the connection exists:** Skilled trades workers who complete training and become independent contractors (electricians, plumbers, HVAC technicians) DO eventually need to navigate business registration. Virginia requires DPOR licensing, Workers' Compensation insurance, and City contractor licensing for non-city contractors performing $25k+ of work annually. TradePath RVA could be the on-ramp to a business navigation tool — but it is not itself that tool.

**What IS genuinely aligned:** The barrier assessment pattern and the revolving loan fund address a real problem that the PS2 research documents but that no other demo tackles: the financial barriers to economic participation. The pain points research identifies cost and timeline uncertainty as a universal barrier (Pain Point 5), and the JTBD analysis documents that entrepreneurs transitioning from employment face cash-flow pressure during a process of unknowable duration. TradePath RVA attacks the financial barrier dimension of economic participation, even if it doesn't attack the regulatory navigation dimension.

- **Training program data:** Which specific Richmond-area training programs are in the system? Are completion rates, costs, and outcomes sourced from the programs themselves or estimated?

## Gap Analysis by JTBD

### Job 1: First-time entrepreneur who doesn't know where to start
> "I want to see every permit, license, and registration I need — in the right order, from the right agencies — so I can move from idea to legally operating business without missing a step."

**Coverage: Not addressed.**

TradePath RVA does not address business formation, registration, permitting, or licensing in any way. The platform is about career exploration and barrier removal for entering the trades workforce — it does not begin where Job 1 begins (the decision to start a business) and does not end where Job 1 ends (a legally operating business).

**Gap fixable or fundamental?** Fundamental misalignment. TradePath RVA would need to add an entirely new product surface to address this job — essentially building a second platform for the subset of trades workers who go independent.

### Job 2: Resident transitioning to self-employment who can't afford mistakes
> "I want a clear, sequenced path specific to my situation, so I don't waste limited savings on wrong-order filings, missed deadlines, or penalties."

**Coverage: Partial — addresses the financial barrier dimension only.**

TradePath RVA directly addresses the "can't afford mistakes" dimension through its barrier assessment and revolving loan fund. The $500–$2,500 micro-loans for gap costs (boots, childcare, tools) solve a real financial barrier that Job 2 documents. The deferred repayment structure (starts after job placement) addresses the cash-flow pressure that the JTBD analysis identifies.

What's missing: everything about the regulatory sequence. TradePath RVA does not help a trades worker understand SCC registration, DPOR licensing, Workers' Comp requirements, or City contractor licensing. The "clear, sequenced path" portion of this job is entirely unaddressed.

**Gap fixable or fundamental?** The financial barrier work is real and valuable. The regulatory navigation gap is fundamental — it would require building the core PS2 solution on top of the existing platform.

### Job 3: SBDC counselor or City staff answering the same questions
> "I want a self-service tool that handles the common path so I can focus my limited time on complex cases."

**Coverage: Not addressed.**

TradePath RVA is a resident-facing tool with no staff-side component. It does not reduce the burden on SBDC counselors, SCORE mentors, or OMBD staff. It does not capture or codify the tacit knowledge that the JTBD analysis identifies as the critical staff-side gap.

**Gap fixable or fundamental?** Fundamental misalignment. This job is about the business registration advisory ecosystem, which TradePath RVA does not participate in.

## Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: The Revolving Loan Fund as a Reusable Pattern

The Trade Pathway Revolving Loan Fund is the most innovative element across both PS2 demos. The structure — credit union partnership, micro-loans sized to real gap costs, no credit score requirement, repayment deferred until outcome achieved — is a **financing pattern that could be applied to business startup costs**, not just trades training costs.

Consider: a first-time entrepreneur in Richmond faces $300–$500 in registration fees (SCC filing, CZC application, BPOL), plus costs for legal counsel, accounting setup, and compliance. These are the same kind of "gap costs" that TradePath identifies for trades workers — too small for traditional lending, too large for someone at the financial edge, and occurring before the business generates revenue.

A "Business Launch Revolving Loan Fund" using the same People's Advantage FCU partnership could directly address the PS2 financial barrier while a separate tool addresses the navigation/sequencing problem.

**Dependencies:** FCU willingness to extend the model; data on actual startup cost distribution in Richmond; integration with a business navigation tool that identifies the costs.

### Opportunity 2: The Barrier Assessment as a Reusable UX Pattern

TradePath RVA's barrier assessment — "Do you need childcare? Transportation? Help with tools? How will you cover lost wages?" — is a **design pattern worth extracting**. Most government service tools start with "what do you need to do?" TradePath starts with "what's in your way?"

Applied to business navigation, a barrier assessment could ask:
- Do you have internet access to complete online filings?
- Do you read English fluently?
- Do you have $500–$1,000 available for registration fees?
- Can you visit City offices during business hours?
- Do you have access to legal or accounting advice?

The answers would route the user to the appropriate support path — SBDC counseling, OMBD assistance, library resources, or self-service tools — rather than dumping everyone into the same generic checklist.

**Dependencies:** Integration with the Richmond support ecosystem directory; validation with SBDC/OMBD staff.

### Opportunity 3: Trades-to-Business Pipeline

TradePath RVA's most natural PS2 contribution would be a **post-placement module** for trades workers who complete training and want to go independent. The platform already has the user's trust, contact information, and barrier profile. Adding a "Ready to start your own business?" pathway that connects to actual Richmond business registration steps would create a genuine end-to-end workforce-to-entrepreneur pipeline.

This would require: the business registration sequencing engine that PS2 asks for (which TradePath does not have), DPOR licensing guidance specific to trades, and Workers' Comp / contractor licensing navigation. It's a substantial build, but TradePath's existing user relationship is the asset.

**Dependencies:** The PS2 navigation tool itself; DPOR licensing data; Workers' Comp requirements by trade; partnership with SBDC for trades-specific counseling.
