## What the Demo Does Well

**Barrier-first design.** Most workforce and entrepreneurship tools start with "here's what you need to do." TradePath starts with "what's in your way?" This inversion is significant because it acknowledges that the people who most need these pathways are the ones least able to pursue them under existing program structures. The barrier assessment (childcare, transport, tools, lost wages) is grounded in real resident conversations, not hypothetical personas.

**Real financial partner with real capital.** People's Advantage Federal Credit Union committed $50,000 to the Trade Pathway Revolving Loan Fund, leveraging a $5,000 city pilot. This is not a concept — it's a capitalized financial product with a named originator, specific loan terms ($500–$2,500), and a concrete underwriting model (no credit score, repayment after placement). Among all demos in this pillar, this is the only one with a committed deployment partner and funding.

**Mobile-first, multilingual.** The platform is explicitly designed for phone-first access and claims multilingual support. This reflects the reality that Richmond's trades-eligible population is disproportionately mobile-primary and includes non-English speakers. The design philosophy — "meets you where you are on your phone, in your language, at whatever hour you finally have a minute to breathe" — is the right accessibility posture.

**Coherent narrative from problem to solution to funding.** The demo moves cleanly: Richmond has 53k trades jobs → barriers prevent access → here's a platform to explore paths and assess barriers → here's a revolving loan fund to cover gap costs → here's a real credit union to originate the loans. Each element supports the next. The story is complete.

## What Was Fundamentally Missed

TradePath RVA does not address the PS2 problem statement. Small Business Navigation asks for help with "what steps to take, in what order, to start and register a business in Richmond." The demo addresses none of this:

- No business registration guidance (SCC, EIN, state tax, CZC, BPOL)
- No permit/license sequencing
- No regulatory navigation of any kind
- No mention of the five-agency fragmentation that defines the PS2 pain
- No staff-side efficiency component

The demo solves a different problem — workforce access barriers for skilled trades careers — and solves it well. But judges evaluating against PS2 will need to decide whether to score on alignment to the stated problem or on the broader pillar goal.

The JTBD analysis explicitly flags this: Job 1 (startup sequence) is not addressed, Job 3 (staff efficiency) is not addressed, and Job 2 (financial/legal steps for self-employment) is only partially addressed through the financial barrier dimension. The regulatory, legal, and sequencing dimensions are absent.

## Blockers

| Blocker | Severity | Specific Ask |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| Training program data not validated | **High** | Identify which Richmond-area training programs are in the system. Verify program costs, duration, completion rates, and outcomes with each program directly (Reynolds Community College, Carver Career Center, union apprenticeships). Stale or generic data undermines the "personalized path" promise. |
| Wage and employment data not cited | **Medium** | Source all wage figures from BLS OEWS or Virginia Employment Commission data at the Richmond MSA level. The "53,000 workers" and "$40k–$60k" claims are plausible but need citations. Judges and city partners will ask. |
| Loan fund terms unconfirmed as final | **Medium** | Clarify with People's Advantage FCU: are the stated terms ($500–$2,500, no credit score, repayment after placement) finalized or indicative? What is the expected default rate? What happens to the revolving fund if repayment rates are low? |
| Multilingual support unvalidated | **Medium** | Which languages are actually supported? Has the platform been tested with non-English-speaking Richmond residents? Spanish should be the minimum validated language given Richmond's LEP demographics. |
| No employer pipeline demonstrated | **Low** | The demo claims employers exist and have openings. Are there specific employer partnerships, or is this based on general labor market data? A named employer partnership would significantly strengthen feasibility. |

## Recommended Next Steps (Priority Order)

1. **Validate and cite all data sources.** The demo's credibility depends on Richmond-specific data. Source wage figures from VEC/BLS at the MSA level. Confirm training program details directly with providers. Document employer demand with specific openings or partnerships. This is the difference between "we built a platform" and "we built a platform grounded in Richmond's actual labor market."

2. **Formalize loan fund terms with People's Advantage FCU.** The revolving loan fund is TradePath's strongest asset. Get the terms in writing: loan amounts, underwriting criteria, servicing model, default management, revolving mechanism. A one-page term sheet makes this fundable. Without it, it's a concept with a handshake.

3. **Build the trades-to-business bridge.** The highest-value PS2 contribution TradePath could make is a post-training module for trades workers going independent. After someone completes HVAC certification and gets placed, the next question is: "How do I start my own shop?" Connect the barrier assessment and loan fund to actual DPOR licensing, Workers' Comp requirements, and City contractor registration. This closes the gap between what TradePath does and what PS2 asks for.

4. **Validate multilingual support with real users.** Recruit 5–10 Spanish-speaking Richmond residents to test the full flow — trade exploration, barrier assessment, loan application. Document where the platform fails linguistically. Fix before claiming multilingual support in production.

5. **Pilot the barrier assessment pattern with SBDC/OMBD.** The "what's in your way?" intake pattern has value beyond trades. Present it to Capital Region SBDC and OMBD as a potential intake tool for first-time entrepreneurs. If they adopt it, TradePath's design contribution extends into the PS2 ecosystem even if TradePath itself remains focused on workforce.
