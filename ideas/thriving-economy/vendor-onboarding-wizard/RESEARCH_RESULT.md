# From Friction to Flow: How Cities Are Guiding Vendors Into Government Markets Faster (and Fairer)

## Executive Summary
Guided onboarding is reducing time-to-contract while widening vendor participation across U.S. municipalities. Historically, government procurement has been plagued by fragmented, paper-based processes that disproportionately burden small and minority-owned businesses (M/WBEs). Today, a multilayered simplification approach—combining step-by-step digital portals, clear checklists, and human assistance—is compressing onboarding from weeks to days. 

Key insights include:
* **Guided portals cut onboarding time from weeks to days:** New York City’s PASSPort moved vendor integrity disclosures online, allowing filings to be completed "within a day" compared to a month or more previously [1].
* **Centralized state marketplaces scale supplier bases substantially:** Virginia’s eVA reports a 200% increase in supplier commodity registrations since its launch, growing to nearly 100,000 businesses competing in the marketplace [2] [3].
* **Federal layers set the pace, but human help makes it stick:** SAM.gov provides a detailed Entity Registration Checklist, while APEX Accelerators delivered 6,284 training events to help vendors navigate complex federal validations [4] [5].
* **Certification portals measurably speed equity-program intake:** Austin’s Small and Minority Business Resources (SMBR) shifted to an online portal and improved its average certification review timeline to just 7 days [6].

## The Federal On-Ramp: SAM.gov + APEX
Standardized checklists and human help drive throughput at scale for federal contracting, which often serves as a prerequisite for local procurement involving federal funds.

### What SAM.gov Simplifies (and Where It Stalls)
To obtain federal money through a contract or grant, an entity must register in SAM.gov [7]. The system provides a comprehensive "Entity Registration Checklist" that guides users through obtaining a Unique Entity ID (UEI), entering Core Data, Assertions, and Reps & Certs [4]. While obtaining a UEI is automated and typically takes 48 hours, full registration requires extensive documentation and validation against IRS and USPS databases [8] [7]. Common failure points include TIN/EIN mismatches and CAGE code assignments, which are handled by the Defense Logistics Agency and typically take 10 business days [8].

### APEX Accelerators' Role
To mitigate these hurdles, APEX Accelerators (formerly PTACs) act as human-guided wizards. They are dedicated to helping businesses find, win, and perform on government contracts [9]. Recent data highlights their impact: APEX Accelerators conducted 6,284 training events (an average of 24 per day), resulting in 549,519 contracts and subcontracts [5]. 

## State Marketplaces: Virginia eVA
Centralization at the state level has proven highly effective at scaling supplier participation. Virginia's eProcurement Marketplace, eVA, serves 245 state agencies and over 900 local governments [3] [10].

### eVA Supplier Onboarding
eVA utilizes a streamlined "Register Now" flow supported by a New Supplier Registration Guide [11] [12]. The guided process prompts vendors to select commodity codes to be matched to business opportunities and requires the upload of a Commonwealth Substitute W-9 Form [11] [12]. 

### Marketplace Metrics & Savings
Since its inception in 2001, eVA has transformed Virginia's procurement from a decentralized, paper-based process to a centralized platform [3]. This centralization correlates with a 200% increase in supplier commodity registrations [2]. The marketplace now includes nearly 100,000 businesses and generates more than $25 million in annual savings for the Commonwealth [3] [10].

## City Portals: Where Guided UX Pays Off
Cities that have adopted guided wizards, status trackers, and Single Sign-On (SSO) show faster onboarding and broader vendor visibility.

### NYC PASSPort
New York City’s Procurement and Sourcing Solutions Portal (PASSPort) replaced antiquated, paper-based processes with a centralized digital platform [13] [1]. The portal uses visual progress cues (e.g., Draft, Ready to Submit, Filed) and blocking/non-blocking alerts to guide vendors through Basic Information, Contacts, Disclosures, and Documentation tabs [14] [13]. To date, the portal has enrolled more than 13,000 vendors, reducing the time to complete filings from a month or more to within a single day [1].

### LA RAMPLA
The City of Los Angeles replaced its legacy BAVN system with the Regional Alliance Marketplace for Procurement (RAMPLA) [15] [16]. RAMPLA simplifies access by utilizing the "Angeleno Account," the City’s single sign-on system [17] [16]. The portal serves as a central hub where companies can apply for business certifications (MBE/WBE/SBE/DBE) administered by the Bureau of Contract Administration [15] [18].

### Chicago iSupplier & MWBE Certification
Chicago modernized its procurement via the iSupplier portal and the Certification & Compliance (C2) system [19] [20]. In 2016, the City stopped accepting paper submissions for certification applications, moving to a paperless process [20]. They also implemented a fast-track processing system for "No Change Affidavits" to expedite reviews [20].

### Seattle OpenGov Launch
In August 2024, Seattle adopted OpenGov to create a more seamless, modernized user experience [21]. The city allows firms to self-identify as WMBE through the Procurement Portal or pursue state certification through the Washington State Office of Minority and Women Business Enterprises (OMWBE) [22]. Seattle also contracts with technical assistance partners like Tabor 100 and Business Impact Northwest to help vendors navigate the process [22].

## Certification Pipelines for Equity
Dedicated certification portals with document checklists and recurring workshops improve timeliness and predictability for small and minority vendors.

### Austin SMBR
Austin’s Small and Minority Business Resources (SMBR) Certification Division uses an online Certification and Compliance System (CCS) powered by B2Gnow [6] [23]. The division hosts monthly pre-certification workshops to guide business owners through eligibility requirements and step-by-step instructions [6]. Through these efforts, Austin SMBR has improved its average reviewing timeline to just 7 days [6].

## Playbook: Designing a "No-Wrong-Door" Vendor Onboarding Experience
To minimize drop-offs, cities must design a unified intake process that routes vendors seamlessly through local registration, state enrollment, and federal SAM.gov requirements.

### 10 Must-Have UX Elements
Effective portals utilize pre-validation checks, role-based guidance, and commodity code pickers (NAICS/NIGP) [16] [6]. Systems like NYC PASSPort succeed by offering clear status trackers (Draft, In Review, Filed), blocking/non-blocking alerts, and save/resume functionality [14] [13]. Single Sign-On (SSO), as seen with LA's Angeleno Account, reduces password fatigue [16].

### Human Help Where It Matters
Digital tools must be paired with human assistance. Integrating APEX Accelerator clinics, multilingual hotlines, and monthly certification workshops (like Austin's pre-certification events) helps vendors navigate complex federal and local requirements [5] [6].

## KPI & Governance: What to Measure
To ensure equity and efficiency, procurement offices should track and publish:
* New vendor registrations (MoM, YoY)
* Time-to-Filed (e.g., NYC's 1-day metric)
* Certification review timelines (e.g., Austin's 7-day average)
* Share of new vendors self-identified or certified as M/WBE/DBE/SBE

## 90-Day Implementation Roadmap
* **Weeks 1-2:** Map the current process; identify quick-win validations (e.g., IRS/USPS matchers); establish KPI baselines.
* **Weeks 3-6:** Configure the intake wizard and status tracker; embed APEX booking links; launch weekly clinics.
* **Weeks 7-10:** A/B test the new guided flow against the legacy process; fix top drop-off points; go-live.
* **Weeks 11-13:** Publish the first vendor onboarding dashboard (similar to PASSPort Public) to build accountability and drive continuous improvement.
