# From Fragmented Forms to a Unified Vendor-Onboarding Wizard: Scaling Richmond's Procurement Pathway for Minority-Owned Businesses

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Vendor Onboarding Wizard challenges the core assumption that procurement friction is purely a technology problem; rather, it is a multi-jurisdictional process fragmentation issue spanning federal, state, and local layers. By reframing this from a Richmond-only IT project to a regional and state-aligned data-sharing initiative, the idea's universe expands significantly. Guided portals have been shown to compress onboarding times from weeks to days, while centralized state marketplaces like Virginia's eVA have scaled supplier bases substantially [1] [2]. However, digital tools alone are insufficient; human assistance, such as that provided by MBDA Business Centers and APEX Accelerators, multiplies these digital gains by guiding vendors through complex validations [3]. 

To maximize impact, this initiative should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Instead, it requires a hybrid governance model where the City controls the policy and data layers, while a mission-driven nonprofit operates the resident-facing SaaS platform. This structure unlocks diverse funding streams—including EDA Economic Adjustment Assistance [4] [5] and Virginia CDBG technical assistance grants [6] [7] —and allows the platform to scale regionally through entities like PlanRVA [8] [9]. By embedding a "Readiness Score" and pairing the digital roadmap with human concierge support, Richmond can transform a fragmented gauntlet into a unified, equitable pathway for minority-owned businesses.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended model:** **Hybrid City / Non-profit partnership**

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond should own the *policy layer* (procurement rules, data standards, SWaM eligibility logic) and provide the *core datasets* (vendor master, contract history) [10] [11]. The City retains stewardship of the "Readiness Score" and integrates it into the City's Procurement Transparency Dashboard [10].
* **What partners should own:** A Richmond-area nonprofit (e.g., Richmond Business Alliance or a similar economic mobility organization) should own and operate the *software platform* (wizard UI, SSO, progress tracker) under a long-term Master Services Agreement. State (eVA) and Federal (SAM) entities serve as *technical data partners* through API agreements.
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The full-stack SaaS development, rapid-iteration UI/UX, and ongoing user-support operations should not be city-owned. These functions demand agile talent and grant-funded staffing that sit better with a mission-driven nonprofit capable of mobilizing philanthropic capital and avoiding the bottlenecks of municipal IT procurement.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Virginia eVA New Supplier Guide:** 
 * *What was attempted:* Streamlined "Register Now" flow and Substitute W-9 uploads [1] [2].
 * *What worked:* Centralization correlated with a 200% increase in supplier commodity registrations.
 * *What failed/Lesson:* Still requires manual document uploads and lacks a unified cross-jurisdictional status tracker. Lesson: Richmond's wizard must pull from eVA rather than duplicate it.
* **City of Pasadena Automated Onboarding (OpenGov):**
 * *What was attempted:* Automated solicitation and vendor onboarding software [23].
 * *What worked:* Sped up the onboarding process significantly.
 * *What failed/Lesson:* Often requires expensive proprietary SaaS licenses. Lesson: A nonprofit-owned, open-architecture model may be more sustainable for Richmond.
* **Richmond Procurement Transparency Dashboard:**
 * *What was attempted:* Launch of a dashboard by OMBD to improve transparency of procurement functions and MBE directories [10] [11].
 * *What worked:* Increased visibility of existing data.
 * *What failed/Lesson:* Transparency alone doesn't fix the onboarding friction. Lesson: The wizard must feed actionable "Readiness Scores" into this existing dashboard.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why might this idea be too small framed only as a Richmond project?**
Framed solely as a Richmond project, the wizard serves a limited supplier pool and duplicates efforts already required by state (eVA) and federal (SAM.gov) systems. Vendors would still face a fragmented gauntlet, limiting the tool's effectiveness.

**What changes if the frame becomes regional or statewide?**
Expanding to a regional frame via PlanRVA [20] [8] adds thousands of firms across the Richmond metro and creates a common data-layer, reducing per-vendor onboarding costs. A statewide frame aligns the tool with Virginia's eVA roadmap, potentially positioning it as the official "Gateway" for all VA agencies and unlocking larger EDA EAA funding pools [4].

**What if a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data?**
This hybrid model enables rapid software iteration, leverages philanthropic grant cycles (e.g., Robins Foundation, Community Foundation) [15] [16], and maintains public accountability without burdening municipal IT departments.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data-Sharing Legal Barriers:** Federal and state agencies restrict third-party access to vendor data. Without secure API agreements with eVA and SAM.gov, the "pre-population" feature will fail.
* **Technical Integration Complexity:** Building Single Sign-On (SSO) across three distinct government platforms is highly complex and prone to budget overruns.
* **Grant Dependence & Maintenance Burden:** If the nonprofit operator relies solely on one-off grants (like CDBG), the platform may become abandonware when funding dries up [6].
* **Duplication of Effort:** Overlapping with existing human-support services like the MBDA Business Center could dilute resources and confuse vendors [3].
