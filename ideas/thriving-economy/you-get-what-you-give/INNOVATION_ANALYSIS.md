# Innovation Analysis: You Get What You Give

## 1. Blue Ocean Reframing (ERRC Grid)

**Core assumption challenged:** Buy-local campaigns need more promotion. They don't — the research shows that the intervention design (closed-loop cards, BOGO subsidy structure, corporate bulk pipeline) determines outcomes far more than awareness. And community currencies (BerkShares, Ithaca HOURS) produce zero measurable macroeconomic impact per a peer-reviewed synthetic-control study spanning 15 years.

| Action | Element | Rationale |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Eliminate** | Community currency / token complexity | BerkShares: 300-400 merchants, no discernible impact on business counts, unemployment, or per capita income. Ithaca HOURS: effectively defunct. Eliminate the idealistic currency model. Use standard payment rails (Mastercard/Visa network) like Yiftee and Factor4 do in 700+ communities. |
| **Reduce** | Subsidy dependence | Baldwin Park, CA saw organic card sales halt entirely when BOGO matching funds expired. Reduce exposure to the "subsidy cliff" by building corporate bulk sales (employer rewards, tourism packages) from day one. Mason, OH generated $1.43M through P&G employee rewards without municipal subsidy. |
| **Raise** | Spend measurement rigor | Most buy-local programs report "cards sold" and "redemption rates" — vanity metrics that don't prove economic impact. NYC used Mastercard's Test & Learn platform to prove Open Streets drove $3M in *incremental* spending via difference-in-differences methodology. Raise the bar from "money moved" to "net-new economic activity." |
| **Create** | A civic engagement reward layer tied to local spending | Colu's City Coins reward civic actions (recycling, volunteering, local shopping) with tokens redeemable at local businesses. No US city has successfully combined civic engagement rewards with a proven closed-loop gift card program. Create this hybrid. |

## 3. Rapid Design Sprint

**Problem sentence:** Richmond residents who want to support local businesses lack a frictionless, measurable way to direct spending to independents, and the City lacks data to prove whether buy-local initiatives produce net-new economic activity or merely displace spending.

**User journey (4 steps):**
1. Resident buys a Richmond community gift card (online or at participating merchants) — optionally with a BOGO match during launch
2. Resident uses the card at any participating Richmond independent business (works on existing POS via Mastercard network)
3. Resident tracks their local spending impact on a personal dashboard: total spent local, merchants supported, civic credits earned
4. City publishes aggregate spend dashboard: total local injection, merchant distribution, corridor trends, and (via Mastercard/Visa analytics) incremental lift vs. control areas

**Prototype shape:** Landing page with: merchant directory + map, BOGO card purchase flow (simulated), personal spending tracker, and aggregate community dashboard. For the weekend, simulate the card infrastructure and focus on the merchant discovery + community dashboard experience.

**First test:** Recruit 10 Richmond residents. Show them the landing page and BOGO offer. Ask: (a) "Would you buy a $25 card that gets you $50 at local Richmond businesses?" (b) "Which merchants would you use it at?" (c) "Would you buy more without the subsidy?" The third question tests Baldwin Park's subsidy cliff.

## 4. Opportunity Mapper

### Opportunity 1: Direct Local Spending Injection
- **User:** Richmond residents who prefer to shop local but default to chains/online out of convenience
- **Outcome:** Redirect $500K+ in consumer spending to independent Richmond businesses in the first 90 days
- **Workflow:** Buy card (with BOGO incentive) → spend at local merchants → track impact → repeat
- **Dependencies:** Gift card platform (Yiftee, Factor4), merchant recruitment (50-100 anchors), BOGO subsidy funding (ARPA, BID, corporate sponsor)
- **Richmond relevance:** Richmond has active BIDs (Carytown, Scott's Addition, Shockoe Bottom) and a strong independent business culture. The infrastructure for merchant recruitment exists.

### Opportunity 2: Corporate Employer Engagement
- **User:** Richmond employers seeking employee rewards and engagement tools
- **Outcome:** Sustainable revenue stream that replaces municipal subsidy after BOGO phase ends
- **Workflow:** Employer buys bulk community cards → distributes to employees as rewards/gifts → employees spend at local merchants
- **Dependencies:** Corporate sales outreach to VCU Health, Dominion Energy, Capital One, CarMax (all Richmond-based)
- **Richmond relevance:** Mason, OH generated $1.43M through P&G alone. Richmond has larger and more numerous major employers.

### Opportunity 3: Economic Impact Measurement
- **User:** City economic development staff, BID managers, funders
- **Outcome:** First rigorous measurement of whether buy-local spending in Richmond represents net-new economic activity
- **Workflow:** Deploy gift cards in targeted corridors → measure spending via card analytics → compare against control corridors using aggregated card-spend data (Mastercard/Visa)
- **Dependencies:** Analytics partnership (Mastercard City Insights or Affinity Solutions), control corridor selection
- **Richmond relevance:** No Richmond buy-local initiative has ever been evaluated with difference-in-differences methodology. This would be a first.

## 6. Assumption Mapping

| # | Assumption | Risk (1-5) | Uncertainty (1-5) | R × U | Validation Method |
|---|-----------|:----------:|:-----------------:|:-----:|-------------------|
| 1 | Richmond residents will buy community gift cards without a BOGO subsidy (post-launch sustainability) | 5 | 4 | **20** | Baldwin Park data shows organic sales halted when subsidy ended. Survey 20 Richmond residents: "Would you buy a $50 local gift card at face value?" |
| 2 | 50-100 Richmond merchants will enroll in the program | 3 | 3 | **9** | Yiftee onboards merchants without new POS hardware. Gauge interest through 10 outreach calls to Carytown/Scott's Addition businesses. |
| 3 | The program generates net-new local spending, not just displacement from purchases residents would have made anyway | 5 | 4 | **20** | This is the fundamental question. Only answerable with DiD methodology using Mastercard/Visa analytics — not possible in a weekend. |
| 4 | Corporate bulk buyers (VCU Health, Dominion, etc.) will purchase Richmond community cards for employee rewards | 4 | 4 | **16** | Contact 3 Richmond-based corporate HR departments: "Would you buy local gift cards as employee rewards?" |
| 5 | The civic engagement reward layer adds meaningful value over a simple gift card program | 3 | 4 | **12** | Colu (Akron, Boston) data is vendor-reported only. No independent evidence that civic rewards drive sustained local spending behavior change. |
| 6 | Prepaid card limitations (no post-authorization tips) won't create merchant friction | 3 | 2 | **6** | Hayward, CA documented this issue. Mitigate with upfront authorization guidance for tipped businesses. |

**Top 3 to validate first:**
1. **Assumption 1 (R×U=20):** Will people buy without subsidies? The entire model's sustainability depends on this. Baldwin Park's experience is a red flag. Mason's corporate pipeline is the proven workaround.
2. **Assumption 3 (R×U=20):** Is the spending net-new? If residents use gift cards for purchases they'd make anyway (coffee shop they already visit), the economic impact is zero. This is the hardest assumption to validate and the most important.
3. **Assumption 4 (R×U=16):** Will corporations buy in? This is the bridge between subsidy-dependent launch and sustainable operation. Without corporate bulk purchases, the program dies when ARPA money runs out.

## Key Takeaway

"You Get What You Give" taps into a proven playbook — Yiftee powers 700+ communities, BOGO programs achieve 80-95% redemption, and Mastercard's Test & Learn methodology can prove incremental impact. But the research delivers two uncomfortable truths. First, community currencies (BerkShares, Ithaca HOURS) produce zero measurable macroeconomic impact per a 15-year synthetic-control study. Second, subsidy-dependent programs die when the subsidy ends (Baldwin Park). The winning weekend build should not try to build a payments platform — that's Yiftee's job. Instead, focus on the two things no existing platform provides: (1) a Richmond civic engagement reward layer that connects community participation to local spending, and (2) a measurement dashboard that articulates *how* you'd prove net-new economic activity using DiD methodology. Show judges you understand the problem deeply enough to know that "money moved" isn't the same as "impact created."
