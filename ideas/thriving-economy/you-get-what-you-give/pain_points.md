# Executive Summary

Minority-owned businesses (MBEs) in Richmond face significant systemic barriers in securing city contracts, primarily stemming from a fragmented and complex procurement ecosystem. A key pain point is the city's transitional state, using two separate platforms, OpenGov and the state's eVA/VBO portal, with inconsistent submission requirements (some electronic, some paper). This creates confusion and increases the administrative burden on small businesses. Furthermore, the city places the onus on vendors to track and acknowledge all solicitation addenda, with failure to do so resulting in bid rejection. This, combined with short response windows and the persistence of paper-based processes, disadvantages firms with limited administrative capacity. Additional barriers include cumbersome insurance and bonding requirements, which can be prohibitive for smaller enterprises. The overall complexity and lack of a single, trusted source of information erodes confidence and leads to missed opportunities, disproportionately affecting newer and underrepresented businesses that lack established networks or the resources to navigate the convoluted system.

# Owner Operator Persona Analysis

## Persona Name

I owner-operator

## Job Type

Functional

## Pain Point Description

Owner-operators face significant functional hurdles due to a fragmented and complex procurement landscape. They must navigate two different systems, Richmond's new OpenGov portal and the state's eVA/VBO platform, which have different submission processes (some electronic, some paper). This creates confusion and increases the administrative burden. A critical pain point is that the responsibility for tracking and acknowledging all solicitation addenda falls entirely on the vendor, and failure to do so can result in an automatic bid rejection. Furthermore, these businesses often struggle with cumbersome, fast-turnaround solicitation processes and readiness barriers, such as meeting bonding and insurance requirements, which can be significant hurdles for smaller firms. The overall platform learning curve and the need to constantly monitor multiple channels for opportunities and updates consume valuable time and resources.

## Evidence

The City of Richmond's procurement website explicitly states its transition to OpenGov, noting that some solicitations require electronic submission there while others require paper submission via eVA/VBO. The site also warns vendors that they are responsible for obtaining any addenda and that failure to acknowledge them may lead to rejection. National research from the Urban Institute (2024) confirms that cumbersome, fast-turnaround, and sometimes paper-based processes are common barriers for entrepreneurs of color. This research also flags bonding and insurance requirements as significant hurdles. Virginia's eVA portal details the multiple steps required for registration, managing commodity codes, and tracking notifications, illustrating the complexity vendors must manage.

## Workaround

To cope with the fragmented system, owner-operators must manually monitor both the OpenGov and eVA/VBO portals. They subscribe to various notifications, meticulously manage their NIGP/commodity codes to receive relevant alerts, and often resort to using personal spreadsheets and setting ad hoc reminders to track deadlines and requirements. For clarification, they may need to directly contact the assigned Contract Specialist for a given solicitation.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The current system imposes significant costs. Businesses lose valuable time cross-checking multiple portals and deciphering complex requirements. There is a constant financial and reputational risk of having a bid rejected due to a missed addendum. The short response windows lead to lost opportunities, as firms may not have the capacity to prepare a quality bid in time. Finally, significant capacity is diverted from core business activities like project scoping and teaming towards administrative compliance tasks like understanding insurance and bonding rules.

The eVA supplier training materials illustrate the complexity involved in registration and responding to different bid types (IFB/RFP/QQ). The Urban Institute report also highlights how fast turnarounds and burdensome processes disadvantage newer and smaller firms, eroding trust and feasibility.

## Equity Dimension

These pain points disproportionately affect Minority Business Enterprises (MBEs), Emerging Small Businesses (ESBs), Disadvantaged Business Enterprises (DBEs), and newer or smaller local firms. These businesses often lack the established procurement networks, administrative capacity, and institutional knowledge to navigate the complex, fragmented system effectively. The lack of clear, consistent digital workflows and the high cognitive load for first-time bidders can create a perception of an 'insider advantage,' discouraging participation and reinforcing systemic inequities in public contracting.


# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

### Who is most affected/excluded
MBEs, ESBs, DBEs, and newer/smaller local firms without established procurement networks or administrative capacity face higher risks of missing addenda, mis-submitting (paper vs electronic), and failing to meet bonding/insurance hurdles. Richmond’s OMBD explicitly targets these groups for support, indicating recognized need.

### System-level contributors
Split systems (OpenGov and eVA/VBO), persistence of paper-based requirements, and vendor-borne responsibility for addenda compound barriers. National research recommends fully digitizing processes, simplifying steps, and addressing bonding/insurance burdens to improve MBE participation.

### Implications for design
A single, unified feed with NAICS/NIGP mapping and readiness filters; explicit addenda tracking/alerts; countdowns for deadlines; plain-language checklists for submission method; bonding/insurance readiness guidance; and OMBD-integrated coaching can reduce inequities and increase fair access.

# Richmond Procurement Systems Overview

## System Name

Fragmented Multi-Portal System (OpenGov, eVA/VBO, and Paper Process)

## Description

The City of Richmond's procurement process is a complex, hybrid system characterized by fragmentation and a mix of digital and analog methods. The city is in the process of transitioning to a new electronic procurement portal called OpenGov for some bids and RFPs. Simultaneously, it continues to use Virginia's statewide e-procurement portal, eVA (and its public-facing component, Virginia Business Opportunities or VBO), to post other solicitations. Critically, some opportunities posted on eVA still require a traditional paper submission to be physically delivered. This creates a confusing environment where the submission method is not uniform. Furthermore, the city's policy states that in the event of a discrepancy, the official hard copy of a document prevails over information on the website, and the vendor bears the full responsibility for finding and acknowledging all addenda.

## Challenge For Mbe

This fragmented system poses significant challenges for Minority-Owned Businesses (MBEs) and other small firms. The primary challenge is the high administrative burden required to navigate the system; businesses must actively monitor at least two separate digital portals (OpenGov and eVA/VBO) and be prepared for paper-based processes. This creates confusion about where to look for opportunities and which submission rules apply. The onus of tracking addenda across these disparate systems places smaller firms, which often have limited administrative capacity, at a high risk of having their bids rejected as non-responsive. This complexity can create a perception of an insider's game, eroding trust and discouraging participation from newer or underrepresented suppliers who are not already familiar with the convoluted process.


# Virginia State Level Context

The broader Commonwealth of Virginia context significantly shapes the procurement environment for Richmond businesses. The central platform is eVA, the state's electronic procurement portal, which includes the Virginia Business Opportunities (VBO) page for public postings. To participate effectively, vendors must register on eVA, a process that is free but requires them to manage specific commodity codes to receive automated email or fax notifications about relevant opportunities. The state also manages a formal certification process for Small, Women-owned, and Minority-owned (SWaM) businesses, a key designation for firms seeking to participate in diversity programs. Virginia's purchasing rules are tiered: purchases under $10,000 may only require a single small business quote, while those from $10,000 to $100,000 involve unsealed bidding, and contracts over $100,000 mandate a formal sealed solicitation. Navigating these different thresholds and the eVA platform's various functions (e.g., responding to IFBs, RFPs, Quick Quotes) presents a learning curve. Within Richmond, the Office of Minority Business Development (OMBD) acts as a crucial local resource, aiming to support MBEs, ESBs, and DBEs in navigating this state-level system and accessing city-specific contracts, thereby working to ensure parity in how taxpayer dollars are allocated.

# Legal And Historical Context

## Croson Case Summary

The legal landscape for minority business enterprise (MBE) programs in the United States was fundamentally shaped by a landmark 1989 Supreme Court case that originated in Richmond itself: *City of Richmond v. J.A. Croson Co.* In this case, the Court struck down the city's then-existing MBE set-aside program, which reserved 30% of construction subcontracts for minority-owned firms. The ruling established the 'strict scrutiny' standard for all state and local government race-based remedial programs. This means that a municipality cannot simply implement such a program based on general societal discrimination; it must prove a 'compelling government interest' by presenting specific, identified evidence of past or present discrimination within its own jurisdiction.

## Purpose Of Disparity Studies

As a direct result of the *Croson* ruling, disparity studies became the primary legal tool for municipalities to establish the 'firm basis in evidence' required to justify and defend MBE programs. These studies analyze a city's contracting and procurement data over a specific period to determine if there are significant disparities between the percentage of contract dollars awarded to minority- and women-owned businesses and the percentage of 'ready, willing, and able' firms available in the local market. If a statistically significant disparity is found, it provides the legal justification for the city to implement narrowly tailored remedial measures—such as those supported by Richmond's OMBD—to address the identified discrimination. Therefore, disparity studies are not just research projects; they are a legal necessity for any city, including Richmond, that seeks to run a constitutionally sound program to promote equity in public contracting.


# Addenda And Updates Risk

A significant pain point and risk for vendors is the City of Richmond's policy regarding solicitation addenda and updates. The city explicitly states that vendors are solely responsible for obtaining any addenda that may be issued for a contract they are pursuing. The policy warns that the 'failure to acknowledge receipt of all addenda may result in rejection of your bid or submission as non-responsive.' This places the full burden of tracking changes on the business, creating a high-stakes environment where a simple oversight can invalidate a significant amount of work. This risk is magnified by the fragmented information landscape, as vendors must diligently monitor multiple potential sources for these critical updates without a unified, reliable notification system.

# Capital And Bonding Challenges

A critical financial barrier for Minority-Owned Businesses (MBEs) is navigating the readiness requirements related to capital and bonding for public contracts. National procurement research, applicable to Richmond's context, identifies 'cumbersome insurance or bonding requirements' as a significant hurdle that can prevent smaller firms and entrepreneurs of color from competing for government work. These requirements often demand a level of financial standing, credit history, or available capital that newer or smaller businesses may not possess. This effectively filters out many MBEs before they can even submit a bid, as they lack the resources to meet these prerequisites. The difficulty in accessing capital and meeting these often-stringent financial stipulations represents a systemic barrier that limits the pool of eligible bidders and hinders efforts to increase diversity in city contracting.

# Project Implications Summary

The identified pain points directly inform the design and goals of the 'you-get-what-you-give' civic tech project, highlighting the urgent need for a centralized, transparent, and user-friendly solution. The fragmentation between OpenGov, eVA/VBO, and paper-based systems necessitates the project's core feature: a single, unified feed of all City of Richmond solicitations. To combat the risk of bid rejection from missed updates, the tool must incorporate explicit addenda tracking and push alerts. The challenge of short deadlines can be mitigated with clear deadline countdowns. To address the complexity and readiness barriers, the project should provide plain-language checklists for submission methods, readiness filters (for bonding, insurance, etc.), and integrated guidance or coaching resources, potentially linking directly to the OMBD. By consolidating information, simplifying processes, and increasing transparency, the project aims to lower the administrative burden, reduce inequities, and provide fair access for MBEs and other small businesses, helping them to pre-qualify, plan, and bid on city contracts more effectively.
