# Executive Summary

The Richmond, Virginia maternal health ecosystem is a collaborative network of public health bodies, community-based organizations (CBOs), hospital systems, and funding entities. Key players include the Richmond and Henrico Health Districts (RHHD) for public health coordination and case management; Urban Baby Beginnings (UBB), a community-based maternal health hub providing doula services and coordinating care; Birth in Color RVA for advocacy and culturally centered support; and major hospital systems like VCU Health and Bon Secours Richmond for clinical, high-risk, and maternity care. Data coordination is largely decentralized. While there is no formal Richmond-specific health data stewardship council, coordination occurs de facto through mechanisms like the Richmond & Henrico Public Health Foundation's (RHPHF) Health Equity Fund (HEF), which uses multiple data sources to guide funding and priorities. Primary data sources for analysis include the Virginia PRAMS survey, for which the Richmond City Health District is an oversampled stratum, making district-level analysis possible. PRAMS microdata is accessible via the CDC's ARF portal or through requests to the Virginia Department of Health (VDH). Additionally, Virginia Health Information (VHI) provides hospital discharge data under license agreements. Public data portals such as the City Health Dashboard, County Health Rankings, and VDH MCH Indicators are also used, but often lack the necessary granularity for detailed local analysis. The primary challenges in the ecosystem revolve around data fragmentation, barriers to accessing restricted microdata for smaller CBOs, and the absence of a centralized data repository or formal local governance body, leading organizations to rely on a patchwork of public dashboards, formal data requests, and partnerships.

# Prams Oversampling In Richmond

## Is Oversampled

True

## Details

The Richmond City Health District (RCHD) is one of only two local health districts in Virginia that are oversampled for the Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS), the other being the Blue Ridge Health District (BRHD). This targeted oversampling allows for more robust local analysis. For example, in 2022, out of 977 total mothers who completed the survey in Virginia, 348 were residents of the Richmond City Health District.

## Data Stratification Level

Due to the oversampling, the Virginia PRAMS data can be stratified at the local health district level specifically for the Richmond City Health District and the Blue Ridge Health District. This allows for district-level analysis, which is not possible for other health districts in the state.


# Prams Microdata Accessibility

## Accessibility For Local Orgs

Local organizations can access PRAMS microdata, but the level of detail varies. State-level microdata is generally accessible to researchers and organizations through the CDC's research file portal. However, access to Richmond-specific district-level microdata is more restricted and is not generally available for public download beyond the aggregated stratifications presented in official dashboards. To get more detailed local data, an organization would likely need to submit a specific request to the Virginia Department of Health.

## Request Process

There are two formal processes for requesting PRAMS data. The first is through the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), where researchers can apply for a SAMS account to access and download multi-jurisdictional datasets from the PRAMS Automated Research File (ARF) web portal. The second process is to go directly through the Virginia Department of Health (VDH) by using their 'Submit a Data Request' pathway to ask for specific PRAMS-related outputs or custom tabulations.

## Known Limitations

A significant limitation is that while state-level microdata is available, the more granular district-level microdata for Richmond is not generally public. Access is typically limited to the aggregated data presented in the VDH PRAMS dashboards. Obtaining more detailed, non-public tabulations or microdata for the Richmond City Health District requires a formal request to the VDH PRAMS program, and access is subject to their data privacy and confidentiality policies.


# Vhi Hospital Discharge Data Access

## Is Accessible

True

## Request Process

To access Virginia's hospital discharge data, an interested party must submit a formal request to Virginia Health Information (VHI). VHI provides specific request forms and data dictionaries to guide applicants through the process of obtaining the data for research, analysis, or planning purposes.

## Requirements

Access to the data is contingent upon completing and signing a license agreement. This agreement outlines the terms of data use and functions as a Data Use Agreement (DUA), ensuring that the data is used responsibly and in accordance with VHI's policies.

## Available Data Types

The data available for request includes inpatient hospital discharge data, which can be ordered as a Public Use File (PUF). The documentation also refers to the availability of supplemental files, suggesting that different cuts or types of discharge data (such as inpatient and potentially outpatient) can be requested through VHI's formal process.


# Vhha Analytics Data Accessibility

## Member Access Level

Full access to the detailed VHHA Analytics platform is restricted to member hospitals of the Virginia Hospital & Healthcare Association (VHHA). Members can use the platform's interactive data analysis tools for hospital-specific analysis and planning.

## Public Access Level

For the public and non-member organizations, access to VHHA Analytics data is limited. These groups cannot access the detailed, hospital-specific analytics environment. Instead, they must rely on the publicly available dashboards provided by VHHA or attempt to arrange negotiated data-sharing agreements.

## Available Public Dashboards

VHHA Analytics provides several public-facing dashboards that offer aggregated data on various topics. These include a dashboard on hospital financial assistance policies and price transparency, a Hospital Workforce Dashboard, a Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) Dashboard, and a behavioral health inpatient data dashboard.

# Data Governance Body Existence

## Local Governance Body Found

False

## Local Governance Details

There is no evidence of a formal, Richmond-specific Data Stewardship Council for health data. However, there are two relevant local entities. First, the City of Richmond has a general Open Data Portal, which is not specific to health data but serves as a platform for citizens to access city data. Second, the Richmond and Henrico Health Districts (RHHD) and the Richmond & Henrico Public Health Foundation's (RHPHF) Health Equity Fund (HEF) functions as a de facto data coordination mechanism. It uses multi-source data to guide investments and set priorities, thereby coordinating data-driven efforts among partners, but it is not a formal data stewardship council.

## State Level Governance Body

The relevant state-level body is the Commonwealth of Virginia’s Office of Data Governance and Analytics (ODGA) Data Governance Council (DGC). Its role is to administer data governance policies, standards, and best practices, as well as to oversee data sharing and analytics projects at the state level.


# Cbo And Doula Collective Data Access Methods

Smaller community-based organizations (CBOs) and doula collectives in Richmond access maternal health data through several common pathways, often relying on publicly available or aggregated information rather than direct access to raw datasets. The primary methods include:

1.  **Public Dashboards and Profiles:** These organizations frequently utilize publicly accessible data portals to understand broad trends and community context. Key resources include the City Health Dashboard, County Health Rankings, and the Virginia Department of Health's (VDH) Maternal & Child Health (MCH) and PRAMS dashboards. These tools provide high-level indicators on population health and some maternal health outcomes.

2.  **Data Dissemination via Coordinating Bodies:** CBOs engage with coordinating entities like the Richmond and Henrico Health Districts (RHHD) and the Richmond & Henrico Public Health Foundation (RHPHF). For instance, the Health Equity Fund (HEF), administered by RHPHF, compiles and analyzes data from multiple sources (including the VDH Health Opportunity Index, CDC's Social Vulnerability Index, PLACES/BRFSS, YRBS, and ACS) to establish funding priorities. This process serves as a mechanism to communicate data-informed needs to community partners. CBOs that are grantees, such as Urban Baby Beginnings, participate in these structures and may also track specific outcomes within their own programs, like mental health trends or rehospitalization rates.

3.  **Requests for Restricted or Partner Data:** For more detailed clinical or utilization data, organizations must navigate more formal channels. They can submit requests to Virginia Health Information (VHI) to access hospital discharge data, which requires a data use license agreement. Alternatively, they may rely on summaries provided by hospital system partners (like VCU Health or Bon Secours) or utilize the public-facing dashboards offered by the Virginia Hospital & Healthcare Association (VHHA). Access to the detailed, hospital-specific VHHA Analytics platform is generally restricted to member hospitals.

# Data Sharing And Coordination Mechanisms

Maternal health data sharing and coordination in Richmond occur through a mix of formal and informal mechanisms. Smaller community-based organizations (CBOs) and doula collectives primarily access data through publicly available dashboards and profiles, such as the City Health Dashboard, County Health Rankings, and the VDH Maternal & Child Health (MCH) Indicators dashboard. For more specific data, formal request processes exist; for example, researchers can access PRAMS microdata via the CDC's ARF portal or submit a data request to the Virginia Department of Health (VDH). Similarly, Virginia Health Information (VHI) provides access to hospital discharge data through a formal request and licensing/data use agreement process. Access to the detailed VHHA Analytics platform is generally restricted to member hospitals, though some public-facing dashboards are available. A key informal coordination mechanism is the Health Equity Fund (HEF), administered by the RHPHF and RHHD. The HEF compiles and analyzes multiple data sources (e.g., HOI, SVI, BRFSS) to inform funding priorities, effectively communicating data-driven needs to partners and grantees. Finally, direct partnerships, such as those between Urban Baby Beginnings and hospital systems like VCU Health and Bon Secours, facilitate data sharing for care coordination.

# Identified Data Gaps And Challenges

The Richmond maternal health data landscape faces several key gaps and challenges that impact analysis and program planning. A primary issue is the lack of a centralized data repository or a formal, Richmond-specific Data Stewardship Council to govern health data sharing and coordination; governance is handled at the state level or through ad-hoc project-based mechanisms like the Health Equity Fund. Publicly available data portals, while useful, have significant limitations. For instance, the City Health Dashboard provides only city-level metrics with limited perinatal detail, County Health Rankings offers high-level annual snapshots, and the March of Dimes PeriStats is primarily state- or metro-focused. This results in a lack of granular, intra-city data. Access to more detailed microdata presents another major challenge, especially for smaller community-based organizations (CBOs). Accessing PRAMS microdata requires navigating the CDC's ARF portal or submitting formal requests to VDH. Similarly, obtaining VHI hospital discharge data necessitates a formal request and license agreement. Furthermore, while VHHA provides some public dashboards, its detailed analytics platform is restricted to member hospitals, creating a barrier for non-members seeking in-depth utilization data. These fragmented systems and access barriers mean that CBOs and doula collectives often must rely on high-level public dashboards or data communicated through coalition meetings and funding processes, rather than having direct access to the rich data needed for targeted interventions.

# Role Of Community Based Hubs

Community-based maternal health hubs, exemplified by Urban Baby Beginnings (UBB) in Richmond, play a critical intermediary role in the local maternal health ecosystem. UBB functions as the leader of Virginia’s Maternal Health Hub system, designed to connect families, hospitals, providers, and other CBOs into a 'seamless local care network.' As an intermediary, UBB bridges gaps between clinical settings and community-based support. It achieves this through direct partnerships with major health systems; for example, UBB collaborates with VCU Health to embed community doulas directly into the clinical setting, making them an accessible resource for patients and providers to overcome care barriers. Similarly, its partnership with Bon Secours established the region's first mobile maternity clinic via the Care-A-Van, providing free perinatal and postpartum care with wraparound social supports to uninsured mothers. In this capacity, the hub serves as a conduit for resources and service coordination, offering families a wide range of services including doula support, prenatal education, lactation consulting, and early childhood programming. By operating at the intersection of community needs and institutional healthcare, hubs like UBB facilitate a more integrated and culturally relevant system of care, channeling resources (like diaper distributions funded by the Health Equity Fund) and data between large systems and the families they serve.

# State Level Data Initiatives

The Virginia Department of Health (VDH) manages several key state-level data initiatives that are highly relevant for local maternal health analysis in Richmond. These initiatives provide crucial data that can be stratified or contextualized for the city.

One of the most significant is the **Virginia Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System (PRAMS)**. VDH's PRAMS program is a state-based survey that specifically oversamples in the Richmond City Health District (RCHD). This oversampling is critical because it allows for the data to be reliably stratified at the local health district level, providing insights specific to Richmond that are not possible in other parts of the state. VDH makes this data accessible through public-facing PRAMS dashboards and also offers a formal "Submit a Data Request" pathway for researchers and organizations seeking custom tabulations or further analysis.

Another key initiative is the **VDH Maternal & Child Health (MCH) Indicators Dashboard**. This dashboard centralizes data from the Virginia Vital Event Statistics Program, offering visualizations on essential MCH measures like infant mortality, preterm birth rates, and the percentage of pregnant people utilizing WIC. While the data is primarily presented at the county level, it serves as a vital source for tracking core outcomes for Richmond City. The dashboard also includes a 'Request Data' link, providing a channel for more specific inquiries.

Finally, the **VDH Health Opportunity Index (HOI)** is a state-level tool that provides data on social and economic factors that influence health outcomes. While not a direct measure of maternal health, it is used extensively by local Richmond partners, such as the Richmond & Henrico Public Health Foundation's Health Equity Fund, to contextualize health disparities and guide investments. By pairing HOI data with health outcome data, analysts in Richmond can better understand the root causes of maternal health inequities.
