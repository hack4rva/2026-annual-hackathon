# Factors In Portal Failure Or Discontinuation

Public health data portals can fail or be discontinued for several key reasons. A primary factor is financial unsustainability, which is often linked to a reliance on single, proprietary technology vendors. The high costs of maintaining, licensing, and upgrading these proprietary systems can become prohibitive over time. Another significant risk factor is the lack of a broad, collaborative ecosystem and clear institutional ownership. Portals that are not embedded within a strong partnership structure (like the academic-public model in Chicago) or a dedicated government agency (like in NYC) may struggle to secure ongoing support and resources. This can lead to the portal becoming outdated or being abandoned when initial project funding runs out or key personnel depart.

# Cdc Wonder Suppression Policy

## Suppression Threshold

10.0

## Data Types Affected

The suppression policy affects not only the raw counts but also any corresponding calculations derived from them. This includes rates and population figures associated with the suppressed count.

## Rationale

The primary reasons for suppressing data with small numbers are to protect the privacy of individuals who might otherwise be identifiable and to ensure the statistical reliability and stability of the presented data.


# Guidance For Local Data Suppression

Local health data portals should implement a clear and transparent small-number suppression policy, drawing from the CDC WONDER model as a best practice. Key recommendations include: 1) Adopt a clear suppression threshold, with counts fewer than 10 being a widely accepted standard, mirroring CDC WONDER. 2) Consistently suppress related calculations, meaning if a raw count is suppressed, any rates, percentages, or other figures derived from that count must also be suppressed to prevent deductive disclosure. 3) Be transparent by creating a dedicated, easily accessible page or section ('suppression disclosure') that explains the policy, the specific threshold used, and the rationale (privacy protection and statistical reliability). 4) Provide context to users, explaining that suppression is a standard and necessary practice in public health data reporting. 5) Where possible, offer alternatives for users needing data on smaller populations, such as suggesting the aggregation of data across multiple years or larger geographic areas to produce statistically stable numbers that meet the publication threshold.

# Portal Guidance For Surfacing Data Caveats

To ensure users correctly interpret data with known limitations, such as the underestimation of American Indian/Alaska Native (AI/AN) mortality, a public health data portal should implement several explicit credibility mechanisms. Drawing from best practices seen in trusted dashboards, the following actions are recommended:

1.  **Methodology Cards or Pop-ups:** For every chart, map, or table that displays data by race/ethnicity, include an easily accessible link or icon (e.g., an 'i' for information) that opens a 'methodology card'. This card should clearly explain the data source and explicitly state the known limitations, such as: "Note: Mortality rates for the American Indian/Alaska Native population are likely underestimated by approximately 34% nationally due to racial misclassification on death certificates, according to NCHS studies. This may affect the data presented here."

2.  **Per-Query Data Flags:** When a user's query or filter selection results in the display of AI/AN data, a visual flag or asterisk should appear directly next to the data point or category label. A tooltip or mouse-over text for this flag should provide a concise warning about the potential underestimation.

3.  **Prominent General Disclosure Section:** The portal should feature a dedicated, clearly labeled section (e.g., "Data Limitations" or "Understanding the Data") that is accessible from the main navigation. This section should provide a detailed explanation of significant data quality issues, including a comprehensive summary of the AI/AN misclassification problem, its causes, and its implications for data interpretation.

4.  **Clear Source and Vintage Labeling:** As a foundational practice, every data visualization must be clearly labeled with its source (e.g., "NCHS Vital Statistics") and the time period it covers (e.g., "2020-2022"). This provides essential context and allows advanced users to seek out original source documentation.

# Key Recommendations For Portal Development

Based on an analysis of existing public health data portals, the following recommendations are provided for organizations seeking to build or maintain a successful platform:

1.  **Establish a Collaborative Governance and Sustainable Funding Model:** Emulate the success of the Chicago Health Atlas by forming a multi-stakeholder partnership that includes public health departments, academic institutions, and technology partners. This structure distributes responsibility for data refresh, discrepancy resolution, and indicator integrity. Secure diverse and long-term funding from sources such as foundations and public-private partnerships to ensure sustainability.

2.  **Adopt a Flexible and Open Technology Strategy:** Consider an open-source platform, following the example of the NYC Environment & Health Data Portal. This approach mitigates the risks of high-cost vendor lock-in, promotes transparency, and fosters a collaborative ecosystem for development and maintenance. Employ agile, user-centered design principles to ensure the portal meets the evolving needs of its audience.

3.  **Prioritize Credibility and User Trust:** Build trust by integrating mechanisms for transparency directly into the portal. This includes providing per-query citations for data sources, offering detailed 'methodology cards' that explain how indicators are calculated, clearly labeling the 'vintage' (date) of all data presented, and publishing a clear policy on data suppression.

4.  **Implement and Disclose Robust Data Handling Policies:** For sensitive data, particularly concerning small populations, adopt and clearly communicate a small-number suppression policy. This protects individual privacy while maximizing the utility of the data for public health analysis.

5.  **Surface Data Limitations and Caveats Proactively:** Do not present data as perfect or infallible. It is critical to transparently communicate known limitations directly within the user interface. For instance, when presenting mortality data, a portal should include a prominent note explaining the significant underestimation of mortality rates for American Indian/Alaska Native populations (~34%) due to racial misclassification on death certificates. This context is essential for accurate interpretation, especially for local-level data.

6.  **Pursue an Incremental Approach to Complex Data Integration:** For ambitious goals like a unified maternal health data portal, begin with a pragmatic approach. Develop dashboards that visualize currently available, though separate, data sources (e.g., PRAMS, vital statistics, local surveys). This provides immediate value to the community and stakeholders while laying the groundwork for more complex, future integration of data from multiple sources like VDH, Census, and the CDC.

# Common Data Sources And Indicators

## Source Name

VDH (Vital Statistics)

## Description

Refers to data from a Department of Health's Vital Statistics office, which records vital events such as births and deaths. This is a fundamental source for calculating mortality and natality rates.

## Example Indicators

Maternal mortality rates, infant mortality rates, number of live births, birth outcomes, all often disaggregated by race and geography.

## Source Name

US Census

## Description

Data from the United States Census Bureau, providing population-level demographic information. This is essential for creating denominators to calculate health rates and for understanding the characteristics of a population.

## Example Indicators

Population counts by race, ethnicity, age, and geographic area (e.g., census tract) used to calculate race-disaggregated health indicators.

## Source Name

CDC (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention)

## Description

A major source of national public health data, tools, and surveillance systems. The context specifically mentions CDC WONDER (Wide-ranging Online Data for Epidemiologic Research), an online database that provides a wealth of public health information.

## Example Indicators

National mortality data, data on various diseases and conditions, and information used for small-number suppression guidelines.

## Source Name

PRAMS (Pregnancy Risk Assessment Monitoring System)

## Description

A surveillance project of the CDC and state health departments. It collects state-specific, population-based data on maternal attitudes and experiences before, during, and shortly after pregnancy. The Philadelphia Department of Public Health's Philly PRAMS dashboard is cited as an example.

## Example Indicators

Data on maternal attitudes, maternal experiences during pregnancy and delivery, prenatal care access, and postpartum experiences.

## Source Name

Local Health Department Data

## Description

Data collected, managed, and reported by municipal or county-level public health departments. Examples include dashboards and reports from Los Angeles County, Boston, and Washington, D.C., which provide localized health data.

## Example Indicators

City-specific perinatal health data, local infant mortality reports, and other community health indicators tailored to the local context.
