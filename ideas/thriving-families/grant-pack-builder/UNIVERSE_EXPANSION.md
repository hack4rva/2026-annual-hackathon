# From Data-Pain to Grant-Power: Scaling the Grant-Pack-Builder for Thriving Families Across Richmond, Virginia and Beyond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Grant Pack Builder addresses a critical bottleneck for Maternal and Child Health (MCH) Community-Based Organizations (CBOs): the manual compilation of neighborhood-level data that consumes 80 to 120 hours per year, per organization. While originally conceived as a Richmond-specific civic tech solution under Mayor Danny Avula's Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) "Thriving Families" pillar, framing this solely as a city-owned project limits its potential and sustainability. 

Expanding the universe of this idea reveals a robust ecosystem of regional, state, and national funding and partnership opportunities. By transitioning to a hybrid city-nonprofit ownership model, the initiative can tap into federal HRSA Title V funds, national foundation grants from the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (RWJF) and Annie E. Casey Foundation, and civic tech accelerators like the Beeck Center. This expanded frame not only ensures long-term maintenance funding but also allows the tool to scale across the Greater Richmond region and eventually statewide, transforming a local hackathon prototype into a replicable data-equity infrastructure.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Partnership**

The Grant Pack Builder should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. A hybrid model leverages the strengths of both sectors while mitigating the risks of municipal budget cuts and procurement delays.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond (via the Mayor's Office or Health District) should own the foundational data-sharing agreements (MOUs) with state agencies like the Virginia Department of Health (VDH) and the Virginia Department of Education (VDOE). The city should also sponsor the initial pilot as a MAP deliverable to provide institutional credibility.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A regional nonprofit, such as the Richmond & Henrico Public Health Foundation (RHPHF) or a local National Neighborhood Indicators Partnership (NNIP) affiliate, should own the software repository, user interface, and community training programs. They are better positioned to apply for philanthropic grants and iterate the software based on CBO feedback.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The resident-facing application layer and the long-term software maintenance should not sit on city servers, as municipal IT departments often lack the agile capacity to maintain open-source, code-first pipelines (like tidycensus + Quarto) over time.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing the Grant Pack Builder solely as a Richmond project makes it too small to attract major national civic tech funding and ignores the reality of regional service delivery. 

* **Regional Shift:** MCH CBOs rarely serve only the City of Richmond; their service areas often bleed into Henrico and Chesterfield counties. By expanding the frame regionally, the tool multiplies its user base and becomes eligible for regional funding pools (like PlanRVA or broader Community Foundation grants).
* **Statewide Shift:** Virginia already provides standardized data via VDH MCH dashboards and VDOE School Health dashboards [20]. If a nonprofit owns the tool, it can easily scale to any Virginia ZIP code by tapping into these existing state APIs, transforming a local tool into a statewide asset eligible for HRSA Title V capacity funds.
* **Ownership Shift:** If the city only pilots the tool and a partner (like RHPHF) scales it, the city avoids long-term IT maintenance burdens while still claiming a policy victory under the MAP Thriving Families pillar.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Quality and Trust:** The biggest technical risk is misrepresenting unstable rates. ACS tract-level estimates carry Margins of Error, and small-number suppression rules must be enforced to prevent unstable rate reporting. The tool must programmatically handle these rules to maintain credibility.
* **Maintenance Burden:** As seen with other civic tech projects, if the tool is built during a hackathon but lacks a dedicated owner for API updates and server costs, it will break within a year.
* **Grant Dependence:** Relying solely on one-time grants (like a $50K RWJF award) creates a fiscal cliff. The project needs a sustainable revenue model, potentially through micro-subscriptions for larger institutional users (e.g., hospitals) while remaining free for small CBOs.
* **Technical Integration:** Upstream schema changes from the Census API or VDH CKAN exports could break the automated pipeline, requiring ongoing developer attention.
