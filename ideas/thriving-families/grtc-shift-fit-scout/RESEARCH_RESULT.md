# Transit Schedules vs. Teen Shifts — Closing the “Last‑Bus Gap” to Improve Youth Job Retention

## Executive Summary

The misalignment between public transit schedules and employer shift times represents a critical, yet solvable, barrier to youth employment retention in US cities. For teens and young adults who rely on public buses, the "last-bus gap"—where evening shifts end after transit service ceases, or morning shifts begin before it starts—drives predictable lateness, absenteeism, and eventual job loss. 

**Key Strategic Insights:**
1. **Late‑shift service gaps drive turnover and absenteeism:** Employers report that a lack of late‑night transit increases lateness, absenteeism, and turnover. Fixed-route transit has been shown to reduce retail turnover by 900–1,000 workers per year in studied regions, saving employers $1.7M–$1.9M annually [1].
2. **Youth are disproportionately exposed to the “schedule misfit”:** In Greater New Haven, 69% of jobs are near an active bus stop during the morning rush, but this drops to 42% in late evenings (10 p.m.–12 a.m.) and Sunday mornings, with retail and restaurant jobs being even less accessible [1] [2].
3. **Program evidence flags transportation as a top youth retention risk:** In Washington D.C.'s 2021 Mayor Marion S. Barry Summer Youth Employment Program (MBSYEP) evaluation, employers reported a 14% year-over-year increase in "youth transportation" challenges, alongside an 11% increase in "frequent absences" [3]. Similarly, 32% of youth cited transportation issues as a reason for not participating in the national YouthBuild program [4].
4. **Shift times commonly fall outside bus spans:** Employer surveys by the Memphis MPO reveal that 32% of shifts start between 3 a.m. and 7 a.m., and 11% run from 9 p.m. to 2 a.m. With 63% of employers requiring exact start and end times, rigid timeliness rules make transportation a vital component of job retention [5].
5. **Youth unemployment falls where transit is stronger:** A 95‑city longitudinal study found that higher public transit density reduces youth unemployment, with the benefits being significantly larger in cities that are less dependent on private automobiles [6] [7].
6. **Safety after dark suppresses viable trips for teens:** On LA Metro, 60% of female riders feel safe during the day, but this plummets to just 20% at night [8]. 
7. **Existing tools enable planning but don’t “hard‑match” shifts to last buses:** Consumer apps like Transit and Moovit offer "arrive by" features [9] [10], and platforms like RideAmigos CommuteHub manage employer benefits [11], but no mainstream tool automatically ingests employer rosters to flag shift-to-transit misfits.
8. **On‑demand and employer shuttles can close the gap:** Agencies are piloting night demand-responsive transit (DRT), such as Belleville, Canada's night DRT which replaced fixed routes to provide flexible, cost-saving night service [12].
9. **Predictable scheduling laws help planning but need transit hooks:** Fair Workweek laws in NYC, Seattle, and San Francisco mandate 14-day advance scheduling and premium pay for changes [13] [14] [15], but currently lack explicit transit-compatibility requirements.
10. **Measurable business case for span and alignment:** Late‑shift transit commuters earn $28 billion annually and facilitate $84 billion in sales, yet their average commute is 48 minutes—twice as long as auto commutes [1]. 

## 1. The Cost of the “Last‑Bus Gap” — Why Transit Schedule Mismatch Erodes Youth Retention

Night and early‑morning service gaps, combined with rigid employer shift requirements, create predictable lateness and absences for bus‑dependent teens. This friction directly increases turnover costs for employers and limits economic mobility for youth.

### APTA Evidence: Lateness, Absenteeism, and Turnover
A lack of late-night transportation options increases turnover and absenteeism [1]. Employee turnover is lower in counties where public transportation is available; fixed-route public transit reduced manufacturing employee turnover by 1,100 to 1,200 workers per year (saving $5.3 million to $6.1 million) and retail turnover by 900 to 1,000 workers (saving $1.7 million to $1.9 million) [1]. Furthermore, absenteeism costs companies an estimated 15 to 20 percent of all direct and indirect payroll expenses [1].

### Youth Program Signals: Spikes in Transportation Challenges
Transportation is consistently cited as a primary barrier in youth workforce programs. In the 2021 MBSYEP evaluation, employers cited "dealing with youths' transportation methods" (25.3%) and "frequent absences" (34.7%) as significant challenges, representing a 14% and 11% increase from the previous year, respectively [3]. In the national YouthBuild evaluation, 32% of young people reported transportation issues as the reason for not participating [4].

### Memphis Shift Profile: Rigid Timeliness Rules
Surveys of employers in the Memphis region highlight the severe mismatch between transit and shift work. Approximately 32% of shifts start in the early morning (3 a.m. to 7 a.m.) and 11% are late night (9 p.m. to 2 a.m.) [5]. Crucially, 63% of employers stated that their employees' start and end times are not flexible [5]. Specific shift times coupled with rigid timeliness rules make transportation a vital component of job retention [5].

| Source | Population | Metric | Finding | Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| APTA / Faulk & Hicks [1] | Retail Workers | Turnover Reduction | Fixed-route transit reduced retail turnover by 900-1,000 workers/year. | Transit access directly saves employers $1.7M-$1.9M annually in retail turnover costs. |

*Key Takeaway: The financial burden of transit-schedule mismatch falls heavily on employers via turnover and absenteeism, creating a clear business case for employer-funded transit mitigations.*

## 2. Where and When Teens Lose Service — Schedule‑Sensitive Accessibility

Schedule‑sensitive accessibility sharply declines after 10 p.m., disproportionately impacting the retail and hospitality sectors where youth are heavily employed.

### Evening Job Access Collapse
In Greater New Haven, 69% of all jobs are physically located within a quarter-mile of an active bus stop during the morning rush hour (7-9 a.m.) [1]. However, in the late evenings (10 p.m.-12 a.m.) and on Sunday mornings, only 42% of jobs are located near an active bus stop [1]. Retail and restaurant jobs, which are crucial sources of employment, are even less likely to be located near active bus stops late at night [1]. 

### UMN Accessibility Observatory Trends
The University of Minnesota's Accessibility Observatory tracks job access via transit. In their 2023 evaluation, job access via public transit mostly decreased across large U.S. urban areas [16]. These declines in transit accessibility are influenced by the frequency and speed of an urban area’s transit service, driven by challenges such as hiring enough drivers and mechanics [16]. 

| Time Period | Job Accessibility (New Haven) | Sectoral Impact | Strategic Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Morning Rush (7-9 a.m.) | 69% of jobs near active stops | High access for traditional 9-to-5 office workers. | Standard transit schedules serve adult professionals well. |
| Late Evening (10 p.m.-12 a.m.) | 42% of jobs near active stops | Severe drop in access for retail and restaurant workers. | Teens working closing shifts are stranded or forced to use expensive rideshare. |
| Sunday Morning (7-9 a.m.) | 42% of jobs near active stops | Limits weekend shift availability for youth. | Weekend service cuts directly harm youth employment opportunities. |

*Key Takeaway: Transit agencies must prioritize evening and Sunday span improvements specifically targeting food service and retail clusters to support the youth workforce.*

## 3. Youth Outcomes and Transit Access — What We Know (and Don’t)

Strong evidence links transit density to youth employment rates, though direct causal evidence on specific retention duration is often buried in program administrative data.

### 95‑City Longitudinal Model
A comprehensive study of the 95 largest US cities between 2000 and 2010 found that higher density public transit systems lead to lower metropolitan unemployment rates for young people [7]. The study revealed "two worlds" of public transit: improvements in public transit are mostly beneficial in cities that are already less dependent on private automobiles [7]. 

### Employer‑Level Economics and Evidence Gaps
While studies confirm that transit access reduces turnover [1], there is a gap in tools that explicitly measure the exact day-to-day retention impact of a missed bus on a teen worker. However, proxy measures—such as the 15 to 20 percent of payroll lost to absenteeism [1] —indicate that stabilizing the commute stabilizes the job.

## 4. Tooling & Practice Landscape — What Exists to Match Shifts and Buses

Consumer trip planners and Transportation Management Association (TMA) platforms exist, but no mainstream tool automatically matches employer shift rosters with first/last bus times and reliability buffers.

### The Current Tooling Gap
Consumer apps like Transit and Moovit offer "arrive by" and "depart at" trip planning [9] [10]. Platforms like RideAmigos CommuteHub provide digital transportation wallets, carpool matching, and automated incentive programs [11]. Shift ATL helps employers with workforce travel surveys and commute program recommendations [17]. Swiftly provides transit agencies with in-depth punctuality analyses and schedule optimization based on historical GPS records [18]. However, none of these tools offer a B2B integration where a retail manager can upload a weekly schedule and instantly see which employees will miss the last bus.

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Transit App [19] | Rider | Yes | No | No | No |
| Moovit [10] | Rider | Yes | No | No | No |

*Key Takeaway: There is a massive market opportunity to build a lightweight "Shift-to-Transit Fit" API that bridges employer scheduling software with GTFS-RT data to flag transit misalignments before the schedule is published.*

### Night Service Models and Fair Workweek Laws
To fill the gap, some regions use substitute models. Belleville, Canada operates a night DRT (Demand Responsive Transit) system with no fixed routes or schedules, substituting for conventional night bus lines to allow for greater savings and flexibility [12]. 

Simultaneously, Fair Workweek laws in cities like New York City require employers to provide fast-food workers with their schedules 14 days in advance and mandate premium pay for schedule changes (e.g., $100 premium for "clopenings" with less than 11 hours between shifts) [15]. These laws improve predictability but currently lack clauses requiring shifts to align with local transit spans.

## 5. Action Blueprint — Pilot to Close the Last‑Bus Gap for Teens

To measurably cut youth lateness and attrition, cities should launch a 12-month, corridor-based pilot combining targeted span extensions, microtransit overlays, and Guaranteed Ride Home (GRH) programs.

### Pilot Components
1. **Span Extension & Microtransit:** Add fixed-route trips to 12:30 a.m. on key corridors. For lower-density areas, implement a night microtransit overlay (10 p.m.–1 a.m.) requiring pooled rides and employer co-funding, similar to the Belleville model [12].
2. **Shift-to-Transit Fit (S2TF) Tool:** Develop a lightweight tool that ingests employer CSV rosters, checks them against GTFS schedules, and flags shifts that end less than 15 minutes before the last bus.
3. **Guaranteed Ride Home (GRH):** Provide subsidized rides (via Uber/Lyft/Taxi) for emergencies or missed last buses. GRH programs remove the fear of being "stranded" and have been shown to cost very little while being abused very little [20] [21].

| Actor | Role | Cost Share | Deliverables |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Transit Agency | Extend fixed-route span; provide GTFS data. | 40% (Operations) | Later bus runs; API access. |
| Employers | Adjust shifts to fit transit; co-fund GRH. | 30% (GRH/Microtransit) | Shift rosters; flexible checkout policies. |
| Workforce Board | Subsidize youth transit passes; manage pilot. | 20% (Passes/Admin) | Youth enrollment; retention tracking. |
| TMA (e.g., RideAmigos) | Provide software platform for GRH and carpooling. | 10% (Software licensing) | CommuteHub deployment; data reporting. |

*Key Takeaway: A successful pilot requires shared financial responsibility between transit agencies (who provide the base network) and employers (who reap the financial benefits of reduced turnover).*

## 6. KPIs, Data, and Evaluation Plan

Funding must be tied to hard outcomes. The evaluation should utilize a before/after methodology with matched controls, leveraging administrative HR data from participating employers.

**Primary KPIs:**
* **Youth Retention:** 30/90-day retention rates (+5–10 percentage point target).
* **Absenteeism & Lateness:** Reduction in absentee days (-20%) and lateness incidents (-25%).
* **GRH Utilization:** Number of rides requested per 100 workers (to monitor costs and prevent abuse).
* **Safety Perceptions:** Pre- and post-pilot surveys on perceived night safety (target ≥+15 pp improvement).

## 7. Risk, Equity, and Governance

### Safety and Perception
Safety concerns disproportionately affect women and marginalized groups, forcing them to modify travel routes or forgo public transit entirely [8]. On LA Metro, 60% of female riders feel safe during the day, but only 20% at night [8]. To mitigate this, the pilot must include environmental improvements (brighter lighting) and the deployment of Transit Ambassadors. LA Metro survey results demonstrate that passengers agree that ambassadors make them feel safer [22].

### Equity Guardrails
The pilot must prioritize corridors with high concentrations of youth of color and low-income workers. Furthermore, data-sharing MOUs must strictly protect the privacy of youth workers when employers share shift rosters for transit-matching analysis.

## 8. Policy & Funding Levers to Scale

To scale these solutions, municipalities must blend transit funds, workforce dollars, and employer contributions.

### Funding Models
States can utilize models like Michigan's "Reliable Rides" initiative, which launched a $260,000 pilot incentive grant program to support employers in addressing workforce transportation challenges [23]. 

### Policy Hooks: Upgrading Fair Workweek Laws
Cities with existing predictable scheduling laws should amend them to include "transit-compatible scheduling" clauses. 

| City | Law Highlights | Potential Transit-Compatibility Add-ons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| New York City | 14-day notice; $100 premium for "clopenings" [15]. | Mandate that closing shifts must end 30 mins before the last scheduled bus, or employer pays GRH. |
| Seattle | Secure Scheduling Ordinance requires 1 hour of pay for added shifts [13]. | Require employers to verify transit availability before scheduling youth for shifts ending after 10 p.m. |
| San Francisco | Formula Retail Employee Rights Ordinance requires 2 weeks' notice [14]. | Integrate transit-matching API into mandatory scheduling software used by formula retail. |

*Key Takeaway: By embedding transit-compatibility into existing labor laws, cities can force a structural alignment between employer demands and public infrastructure realities, permanently closing the last-bus gap for youth workers.*
