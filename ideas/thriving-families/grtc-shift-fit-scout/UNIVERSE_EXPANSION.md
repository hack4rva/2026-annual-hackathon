# Putting the “Last‑Bus Gap” Back in Check: Scaling Richmond’s Shift‑Fit Scout for Thriving Families

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The GRTC Shift-Fit Scout addresses a critical, yet solvable, barrier to youth employment retention: the misalignment between public transit schedules and employer shift times. For teens relying on public buses, the "last-bus gap" drives predictable lateness, absenteeism, and eventual job loss. By framing this idea purely as a Richmond city government project, we limit its potential. Expanding its universe reveals that the Shift-Fit Scout is best positioned as a regional, hybrid-owned infrastructure tool. 

By leveraging federal grants like the FTA Areas of Persistent Poverty program and state-level WIOA youth funding, the City of Richmond can build the core data infrastructure while empowering local nonprofits (like NextUp RVA and United Way) to manage the resident-facing application. This approach transforms the Scout from a localized hackathon concept into a scalable, pre-hire screening API that reduces youth job attrition, saves employers money, and creates a blueprint for statewide transit-workforce integration.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit Governance**

The Shift-Fit Scout should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. A hybrid model ensures technical sustainability while maximizing community trust and adoption. 

* **What the City/GRTC Should Own:** The backend data infrastructure. The city should manage the GTFS-to-nearest-stop API, data licensing, integration with GRTC real-time feeds, and policy-level compliance. This ensures the data remains secure, accurate, and legally compliant.
* **What Partners Should Own:** The frontend user experience and employer outreach. A coalition of nonprofits (e.g., NextUp RVA, Goodwill of Central and Coastal Virginia) should own the resident-facing application, user testing, and wrap-around support services. Business groups like ChamberRVA should own the "employer roster scanner" module, driving adoption among local retailers.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The direct management of employer shift rosters and the day-to-day troubleshooting of individual teen transit plans. The city lacks the agile staffing required for high-touch youth casework, which is already the core competency of existing workforce nonprofits.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Privacy and Trust:** While GRTC GTFS data is public, employer shift rosters are proprietary and sensitive. *Mitigation:* Establish strict Data Use Agreements (MoUs) and ensure the employer-facing module processes data locally or anonymizes outputs.
* **Procurement Blockers:** City-led software development often faces slow procurement cycles. *Mitigation:* The hybrid model allows a nimble nonprofit partner to build the frontend rapidly using philanthropic grant dollars, bypassing municipal procurement delays.
* **Maintenance Burden:** APIs require ongoing maintenance as GTFS feeds update. *Mitigation:* House the core library under a regional body (RRTPO) with dedicated, multi-jurisdictional funding rather than relying on a single city department's budget.
* **Safety After Dark:** A shift might technically fit the bus schedule, but the walk to the stop may be unsafe at night. *Mitigation:* Integrate a "safe-route" overlay using lighting data, partnering with RVA Rapid Transit. [18]
