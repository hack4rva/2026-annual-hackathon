# Scaling Maternal-Metric-Lock-In: Funding, Partnerships & Governance Pathways for Richmond and Beyond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The "Maternal Metric Lock-In" idea addresses a critical systemic flaw in public health: denominator drift. Small definitional slips in residency rules, data vintages, and suppression thresholds produce false trends that misguide public health interventions. For example, inconsistent data reporting can artificially inflate or deflate mortality rates, as seen when misclassification of deliveries under 500 grams artificially altered infant mortality rates. By anchoring metrics to authoritative federal standards like the NVSS Linked Birth/Infant Death data set [1] [2] and CDC Severe Maternal Morbidity indicators, Richmond can eliminate conflicting statistics across organizations. 

Framing this solely as a City of Richmond IT project severely limits its potential and exposes it to municipal budget constraints. Expanding the universe of this idea reveals a robust ecosystem of federal data-modernization grants, state-level perinatal quality collaboratives, and local health-equity philanthropies eager to fund data governance. By transitioning from a city-owned tool to a hybrid stewardship model—where the city provides the initial legal and API framework, a nonprofit manages the open-source codebase, and state agencies like the Virginia Department of Health (VDH) adopt it for statewide scaling—the project can secure sustainable funding and achieve regional interoperability.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended model:** Hybrid city ↔ non-profit ↔ state stewardship.

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond should host the initial API gateway, provide the legal authority for the metric definition registry via the Office of the Chief Data Officer, and mandate its use for city-funded maternal health grants.
* **What Partners should own:** A non-profit entity (e.g., a newly formed "Richmond Maternal Data Commons" or an existing partner like Urban Baby Beginnings) should own and maintain the open-source code, curate the versioned data dictionary, and manage community-partner onboarding. State partners like the Virginia Neonatal Perinatal Collaborative (VNPC) [3] should own the policy scaling to integrate this into a Statewide Maternal Health Data Board [4].
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** Ongoing software-maintenance contracts, the user-support desk, and long-term hosting should not be city-owned. Municipal IT budgets and staff turnover make long-term maintenance of standalone civic-tech tools highly risky.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

| Risk | Description | Mitigation Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data-sharing trust** | Agencies fear loss of control over their numbers. | Emphasize the tool is validation-only, not a data-warehouse; keep raw agency data private. |
| **Technical integration** | Diverse data formats could cause integration delays. | Use an adapter layer and pilot with just two public APIs (e.g., VDH vital stats, CDC WONDER) first. |
| **Maintenance burden** | Municipal IT budgets fluctuate, risking project abandonment. | Create a nonprofit-owned maintenance contract that outlasts municipal staff cycles. |
| **Grant dependence** | Over-reliance on time-bound federal grants (SMHI, ERASE-MM). | Build a diversified revenue mix including local philanthropy (HEF, RMHF). |

*Takeaway:* The biggest blocker is not technical, but organizational trust and long-term maintenance funding, which must be solved via the hybrid ownership model.
