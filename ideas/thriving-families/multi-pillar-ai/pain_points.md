# Executive Summary

Youth and parents in Richmond face significant barriers when seeking employment opportunities due to a highly fragmented and complex ecosystem. The primary pain point is information fragmentation, with job and internship listings scattered across multiple disconnected platforms, including the city's Youth Works RVA (YES) program, the NextUp program locator, and individual employer sites. This forces users to manually track varying eligibility requirements, application deadlines, and program details, leading to confusion and missed opportunities. Logistical hurdles, such as the necessity of obtaining a state ID—a barrier significant enough to require dedicated DMV pop-up events—and uncertainty around transportation to worksites, further complicate the process. Parents express a strong need for a trusted, centralized hub that vets employers for safety and mentorship and provides clear, standardized information on compensation. These challenges create significant equity gaps, disproportionately affecting youth from low-income households, those dependent on public transit, and families with limited digital access or literacy, ultimately hindering their ability to secure valuable work experience.

# Student Persona Profile

The primary user persona is a high school student, aged 16 to 18, enrolled in Richmond Public Schools. This individual is actively looking for a summer job or internship opportunity during the key search period between March and May. Their goal is to find a single, clear source of information for all youth opportunities in Richmond—including various Youth Works RVA tracks, city agency placements, and employer internships—to efficiently apply to suitable roles before the deadlines pass.

# Student Pain Points

## Category

Information Fragmentation

## Description

Students face a scattered and disjointed information landscape when searching for summer jobs. Opportunities are spread across multiple websites and portals, such as the city's Youth Works RVA (YES) page, the separate NextUp program locator, and individual employer websites. This lack of a central hub forces students to manually check and synthesize information from various sources, increasing the complexity of their search.

## Evidence

Youth Works RVA (YES) has multiple tracks with details on a city page, while other opportunities are on NextUp’s separate locator, which requires program-by-program registration and has a desktop-only map feature. Students must check RVA.gov, employer pages, and social feeds.

## Category

Unclear Deadlines & Eligibility

## Description

The deadlines for applications and the specific eligibility requirements (such as age ranges or foster care status) vary significantly between different programs and even between different tracks within the same program. This creates confusion and requires students to meticulously track multiple timelines and rule sets to avoid missing opportunities.

## Evidence

YES/YWRVA publishes annual deadlines (e.g., March 31, 2026) and specific age/eligibility windows that youth must track alongside other providers. The RRHA post for Youth Works shows a specific closing date and separate sign-ups for information sessions.

## Category

Documentation & ID Barriers

## Description

A significant hurdle for many young applicants is the requirement to provide standard work documents, particularly a Virginia state ID. The fact that city partners needed to organize special DMV pop-up events specifically to help youth obtain these IDs is a clear indicator that this is a common and critical barrier that can block an application before it even starts.

## Evidence

An announcement from the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) explicitly mentions that DMV Pop-Up Events were organized to assist youth in securing their Virginia Identification Card, which is noted as an 'important requirement for program participation'.

## Category

Transportation Uncertainty

## Description

Many students in Richmond do not have a driver's license or access to a personal vehicle, making them dependent on public transit. While the GRTC's zero-fare policy is a major benefit, students still face uncertainty as they must independently discover and use separate tools like the GRTC app or Track-by-Text service to plan routes and figure out commute times to potential worksites.

## Evidence

Data shows 85% of local bus riders lack a personal vehicle and 53% do not have a driver’s license. While GRTC provides tools like a mobile app and Track-by-Text, they are separate systems that students must discover and learn to use for trip planning.

## Category

Pay Competitiveness and Clarity

## Description

While programs are advertised as offering 'paid experiences,' there is a lack of clear, centralized information on specific wage rates. This makes it difficult for students to compare opportunities. Evidence from a comparable program in Raleigh shows that noncompetitive pay rates caused participants to leave for better-paying private sector jobs, suggesting Richmond youth face similar pressures and need transparent pay information.

## Evidence

Raleigh’s Summer Youth Employment Program (RSYEP) annual review identified noncompetitive pay as a key challenge, noting that outside businesses were offering teens higher starting rates. This implies a need for clear, competitive pay information on Richmond's job postings.


# Student Workarounds And Status Quo Cost

Currently, students employ several workarounds to navigate the fragmented youth employment system. They are forced to check multiple websites (including RVA.gov's YES page, the NextUp locator, and individual employer pages), monitor social media feeds, and rely on information from school counselors or word-of-mouth. They also attend separate info sessions for different programs and have had to utilize special DMV pop-ups to secure the required state IDs. For transportation, they must manually plan their commutes using the GRTC app or its Track-by-Text service. A common tactic is applying to numerous programs 'just in case' to mitigate the uncertainty. The cost of this status quo is substantial. It leads to missed deadlines and lost job placements, and the fragmented information can result in students applying for roles that are a poor fit. This system places a high time and technology burden on students, creating application fatigue and disproportionately disadvantaging youth who lack stable internet access, government IDs, or strong support networks. Ultimately, this results in foregone wages and missed opportunities for valuable work experience during the short summer window.

# Parent Guardian Persona Profile

The secondary user persona is a parent or guardian residing in Richmond's East End or Southside whose teenage child is seeking their first job. This persona is primarily concerned with the safety, trustworthiness, and logistical aspects of their teen's employment search, as outlined in the 'Emotional Trust Job To Be Done' scenario.

# Parent Guardian Pain Points

## Category

Employer Vetting and Safety

## Description

Parents want assurance that job opportunities are safe and provide constructive mentorship. While programs like YES (Youth Engagement Services) mention 'safe and constructive summer opportunities' and NextUp has a network of 'vetted/approved partners', the specific details about how each individual employer is vetted are not centralized. This fragmentation forces parents to seek information from multiple sources, creating uncertainty about the quality and safety of a given placement.

## Desired Outcome

A trusted, city-backed hub that clearly displays safety and mentorship details for each employer, including information on their vetting process and supervision model.

## Category

Stipend and Wage Clarity

## Description

There is confusion regarding compensation across different job placements. The city's program emphasizes 'paid work-based learning', but pay rates, stipend amounts, and benefits are not standardized or presented consistently in a single location. The complexity is highlighted by comparable cities like Baltimore, which have extensive and potentially confusing payroll and onboarding document requirements (W-4, I-9, etc.), suggesting Richmond families face similar navigational challenges.

## Desired Outcome

A centralized platform where all job listings clearly and consistently state the pay rate or stipend amount, payment schedule, and any required payroll documentation.

## Category

Transportation and Logistics

## Description

Parents, particularly in the transit-dependent East End and Southside, have concerns about the reliability of transportation for their teens. While the zero-fare GRTC bus system is vital for access (as many residents lack personal vehicles or driver's licenses), parents still worry about route reliability and the logistics of early or late shifts. They need context on commute times and supervision to feel secure about their teen's travel to and from work.

## Desired Outcome

An integrated system that provides clear transportation context, including GRTC route reliability, commute time estimates from their neighborhoods, and information about shift timings to help plan logistics and ensure their teen's safety.

## Category

Digital Access and Usability

## Description

Parents encounter 'digital access friction' when researching opportunities, especially on mobile devices. For example, NextUp’s interactive program locator map is noted as being 'desktop/tablet-only'. This forces parents relying on smartphones to scroll through lists of tiles, making it difficult to compare nearby options and logistical details effectively. This creates an equity gap for households with limited access to desktop computers.

## Desired Outcome

A mobile-first platform where all features, including interactive maps, filters, and application portals, are fully functional and easy to use on a smartphone.


# Parent Guardian Workarounds And Status Quo Cost

Currently, the system is broken for parents because critical information on safety, mentorship, and wages is not centralized, forcing them to cross-reference multiple program pages and transit websites. The user experience on mobile devices is often poor, adding another layer of friction. To compensate, parents resort to workarounds such as directly calling program staff, attending various information sessions, relying on word-of-mouth information from school counselors, manually planning bus trips using separate GRTC tools, and physically accompanying their teens to DMV pop-up events to secure required IDs. The cost of this status quo is significant: it leads to fewer and less timely applications due to the high effort involved. Parental hesitation increases when safety and supervision details are unclear. It can also lead to a reliance on less sustainable car-based transportation for off-hour shifts and widens equity gaps for households that have limited data plans, mobile-only internet access, or less time for extensive research.

# Richmond Youth Employment Ecosystem Overview

## Managing Organization

City of Richmond, Office of Community Wealth Building

## Target Age Group

14-24

## Key Offerings

The program offers five distinct tracks: Teens in Richmond & Parks (TRIP), Counselors in Training (CIT), Virtual Earn and Learn, an In-Person Program, and the Founders Mark Program. These tracks provide a mix of virtual learning and hands-on experience through internships and job placements.


# Information Fragmentation Analysis

The information landscape for youth employment in Richmond is highly fragmented, creating significant barriers for students and their families. Details about opportunities are scattered across numerous disconnected platforms. For example, the city's main program, Youth Works RVA (YES), has five distinct tracks (TRIP, CIT, Virtual Earn & Learn, In-Person Internships, Founders Mark), with information residing on a city government page. Meanwhile, other after-school and summer opportunities are listed on the NextUp Youth Program Locator, which is a separate system requiring program-by-program registration and features a map that is only fully interactive on desktop or tablet devices. This forces a potential applicant to navigate at least two primary portals (RVA.gov and NextUpRVA.org), in addition to individual employer websites and social media announcements, to get a complete picture. Key details like eligibility requirements, pay rates, specific worksite locations, application deadlines, and transportation options are not standardized or consolidated, leading to confusion and a high time burden for applicants trying to compare roles and manage applications.

# Transportation Challenges Analysis

## Primary Issue

A core transportation problem is the limited access to personal vehicles for many youth, making them highly dependent on the public transit system to get to and from worksites. Data shows 85% of local bus riders in Richmond lack access to a personal vehicle and 53% do not have a driver's license, creating a significant barrier to employment opportunities that are not easily accessible via public transport.

## Grtc Program Details

The GRTC's zero-fare transit program has been a crucial initiative, enabling many new riders to access jobs and other essential services by removing the cost barrier. A report from 2025 highlights that this program is vital for ensuring residents can access employment. While the program addresses affordability, youth must still navigate trip planning using separate tools provided by GRTC, such as its official mobile application or the 'Track by Text' service, which are not integrated into the job application portals.

## Affected Areas

The transportation barriers disproportionately impact youth residing in transit-dependent neighborhoods, with the report specifically identifying Richmond's 'East End and Southside' as areas where these challenges are most acute. Parents and guardians from these areas express particular concern about transportation reliability and supervision for commutes.

## Impact On Youth Access

Transportation challenges directly limit youth from accessing job opportunities. The high dependency on GRTC means that any disruption to service, lack of awareness of routing tools, or jobs located in areas with poor transit coverage can prevent a student from applying for or keeping a job. The zero-fare program has expanded access, but the logistical challenge of planning commutes remains a significant hurdle that can deter participation.


# Cross Cutting Equity Dimensions

The current youth employment system in Richmond presents significant equity challenges that disproportionately affect specific groups. Youth from low-income households are heavily impacted, particularly those who are transit-dependent and may lack the social capital to easily navigate the fragmented system. The requirement for government-issued IDs is a major barrier, as evidenced by the city's need to organize 'DMV pop-up' events to help youth obtain the necessary documentation. This indicates that without proactive support, many potential applicants are excluded before they can even complete an application. Furthermore, families with limited digital access or literacy face hurdles. The reliance on multiple websites, some of which have features like interactive maps that are only fully functional on desktops, creates a higher burden for those who primarily access the internet via mobile phones with limited data plans. This can lead to missed information, confusion over deadlines, and ultimately, lost opportunities. The system also specifically calls out eligibility for youth in foster care and has a separate program for older, out-of-school youth, signaling that these populations have unique needs that a complex, decentralized system may fail to adequately serve.

# Geographic And Demographic Disparities

The pain points within Richmond's youth employment ecosystem disproportionately affect youth based on their geographic location and demographic background, particularly those residing in the East End and Southside neighborhoods. These areas are highlighted as having a high concentration of transit-dependent families. According to a report on Richmond's transit system, 85% of local bus riders lack access to a personal vehicle, and 53% do not have a driver's license. This high dependency on the GRTC bus system means that any lack of clarity around bus routes, schedules, or the reliability of service creates a significant barrier to accessing jobs located across the city. For parents and guardians in the East End and Southside, concerns about transportation reliability and supervision for their teens commuting to and from work are paramount. The fragmented nature of information forces these families to manually piece together program details with GRTC's separate trip-planning tools, adding a layer of complexity and uncertainty that can deter applications. This reliance on a public transit system that may not perfectly align with work-shift hours or locations creates a geographic disparity, limiting the effective range of opportunities for youth from these specific neighborhoods compared to peers with access to private transportation.
