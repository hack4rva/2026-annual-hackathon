# Beyond the City Limits: Scaling Richmond's Youth-Hub into a Regional Workforce Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Richmond Youth Hub idea possesses transformative potential, but framing it solely as a municipal IT project severely limits its scale and sustainability. Currently, the City of Richmond's Youth Works RVA program aims to serve 750 youth [1], which represents a fraction of the eligible population. Meanwhile, massive federal and state funding streams, such as the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) Youth Formula program, allocate substantial resources to states and local areas based on a formula, requiring a minimum of 75% of funds to be spent on out-of-school youth [2]. To capture these resources, the Hub must expand its universe from a city-owned directory to a regionally integrated, hybrid-governed workforce engine.

By shifting to a hybrid ownership model, Richmond can leverage the agility of local nonprofits like NextUp RVA [3] and the regional reach of the Capital Region Workforce Development Board [4]. This expansion allows the platform to integrate with state-level data systems like the Virginia Longitudinal Data System (VLDS) [5] and tap into federal grants like the Department of Labor's YouthBuild program, which provides pre-apprenticeship occupational skills training and education [6]. Ultimately, the Hub's success relies on treating it as permanent civic infrastructure funded through layered municipal, state, and philanthropic streams.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Joint Authority (City + Nonprofit/Regional Intermediary)**

A pure city-owned model risks becoming bogged down by municipal procurement cycles and staffing constraints, while a purely nonprofit-led model may lack the authority to mandate data-sharing across city agencies and schools. A hybrid joint authority balances these risks by distributing responsibilities based on institutional strengths.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond (via the Office of Community Wealth Building) should own the core data-sharing agreements, compliance frameworks, and baseline funding commitments. The city must also mandate that its own programs, such as Youth Engagement Services (YES) [7], exclusively use the Hub for intake.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A nonprofit intermediary (e.g., NextUp RVA or United Way) should own the agile frontend development, user experience, and day-to-day service delivery. A regional body like the Capital Region Workforce Development Board [4] should own employer outreach and alignment with WIOA funding requirements.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The employer payroll engine and the resident-facing application layer should not be city-owned. Centralized payroll is critical for small business participation, but municipal finance departments are rarely equipped to act as agile HR intermediaries for private employers.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Richmond Youth Engagement Services (YES) / Mayor's Youth Academy:** The City of Richmond currently operates YES, which includes the Youth Works RVA summer program aiming to serve 750 youth [7] [1]. While successful in providing work-based learning and leadership development, it operates as a standalone program rather than a unified navigator for all city opportunities. *Lesson:* Richmond has the programmatic foundation but lacks the centralized digital infrastructure to scale beyond its current capacity.
* **New York City SYEP:** NYC consolidated its youth employment efforts into a single portal managed by DYCD, serving over 100,000 youth annually. *Lesson:* Baselined municipal funding and centralized intake are critical for massive scale.
* **Detroit GDYT:** Detroit implemented centralized payroll, removing the HR burden from small businesses. *Lesson:* Employer tooling is just as important as the youth-facing application; without it, small business participation plummets.
* **Philadelphia WorkReady:** Early iterations functioned merely as a directory, leading to high user drop-off. *Lesson:* A directory-only model fails; the platform must handle centralized intake and application routing.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing the Richmond Youth Hub solely as a municipal project artificially caps its potential. The City of Richmond's internal programs, while valuable, only serve a fraction of the region's youth. 

If the frame shifts to a **regional model**, the Hub can tap into the broader Richmond-Petersburg corridor, aligning with the Capital Region Workforce Development Board's mandate to consolidate workforce efforts and expand the pool of qualified workers [4]. This regional approach pools employer demand, making the platform significantly more attractive to private businesses who recruit across county lines.

If the frame shifts to a **statewide integration model**, the Hub can connect with the Virginia Longitudinal Data System (VLDS), which provides policymakers and researchers access to educational and workforce data [5]. This integration is crucial for tracking long-term outcomes, a requirement for securing federal WIOA and YouthBuild funding [8] [2]. By allowing a nonprofit to own the agile, resident-facing layer while the city and state provide the data backbone, the Hub can scale rapidly without being hindered by municipal IT bottlenecks.

| **Procurement Bottlenecks** | City IT procurement cycles are notoriously slow and may stifle the agile development needed for a modern web portal. | Utilize the hybrid model: have the nonprofit partner (e.g., NextUp RVA) handle tech procurement and development. |
| **Grant Dependence** | Relying solely on competitive federal grants (like YouthBuild) creates sustainability risks if funding cycles end. | Secure baselined municipal funding for core operations while using grants for expansion and wrap-around services. |
