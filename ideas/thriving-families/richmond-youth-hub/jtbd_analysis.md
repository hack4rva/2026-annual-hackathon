# Functional Job To Be Done

## Situation

When it’s spring and I need a paid summer job or internship

## Persona

I, a 16-year-old RPS student in the East End

## Motivation

want one place to filter openings by my age, ZIP, and eligibility

## Expected Outcome

so I can quickly apply to programs I qualify for (like YES Youth Works tracks or local employer internships) without missing deadlines

## Current Workaround

Ask counselors, search city pages, Instagram posts, and individual org sites; DM friends; call around.

## Core Pain Point

Info is scattered with varying eligibility and timelines; uncertainty about required documents (e.g., ID), and how to get to worksites.


# Emotional Trust Job To Be Done

## Situation

When my teen is applying for summer work

## Persona

I, a Richmond caregiver

## Motivation

want a trusted city-backed hub that explains program safety, pay, mentoring, and next steps in clear language

## Expected Outcome

so I can feel confident they’ll be supported, paid on time, and can travel safely to and from placements

## Current Workaround

Rely on school robocalls, flyers, and word-of-mouth; try calling city offices.

## Core Pain Point

Confidence gap due to inconsistent, outdated, or siloed information; hard to judge program quality and transportation feasibility.


# Systems Coordination Job To Be Done

## Situation

When recruiting and placing youth

## Persona

I, an RPS WBL coordinator or community program lead

## Motivation

want a shared intake and referral hub connected to city programs

## Expected Outcome

so I can match eligible students, avoid duplicate applications, coordinate slots and documents (e.g., IDs), and report outcomes

## Current Workaround

Separate Google Forms/Cityspan portals, spreadsheets, emails.

## Core Pain Point

Duplicated data entry, mismatched eligibility, missed referrals, limited visibility into capacity and transportation constraints.


# Data Questions

- Which eligibility fields are authoritative for each partner (age bands, residency, school enrollment, foster care status, GPA, documents like VA ID/Social Security)?
- Can YES provide API or data drops of program tracks, application windows, and slot availability to power real-time matching?
- What data-sharing agreements (MOUs) are acceptable across YES, RPS, RRHA, and nonprofits to support HSDS-based referrals and outcomes tracking?

# User Experience Questions

- What are top search intents by Richmond teens (money, transportation time, skills) and preferred channels (mobile web vs SMS)?
- What barriers most frequently block completion (ID acquisition, banking, resumes) and where should the hub insert supports (e.g., DMV pop-up dates, direct bank account setup)?
- For caregivers, what signals of program quality/safety matter most (mentor ratio, site, hours, pay rate, background checks)?

# Integration Questions

- How should the hub integrate with YES’s Youth Works application form and Youth L.E.A.D.S. cohorts—deep link, SSO, or embedded forms?
- Can RPS Work-Based Learning publish standardized opportunities with eligibility tags and sync counselor referrals back to the hub?
- Can the hub surface GRTC trip planning and Zero Fare guidance, plus site-level transit access info and last-mile notes?

# Equity Questions

- How will the hub prioritize outreach to neighborhoods with lower car access and higher poverty, and present transit-friendly placements first by ZIP and route frequency?
- How will we support teens without IDs/bank accounts—integrating DMV pop-up scheduling, cost info, and stipend alternatives?
- How do we ensure inclusivity for youth in foster care, out-of-school youth (YES Forward), multilingual families, and Black girls-centered programs like Girls For A Change?

# Prior Art Questions

- Which features from Chicago Youth Works/One Summer Chicago (single portal, employer onboarding, capacity display) are feasible in Richmond?
- What from DC’s MBSYEP timeline and certification flow can inform Richmond’s deadline reminders and checklist UX?
- What metrics (applications started/completed, placements, retention, on-time pay, transit use) should be standardized for quarterly reporting across partners?

# Context Transportation

## Provider

GRTC (Greater Richmond Transit Company)

## Program Name

Zero Fare Program

## Status And Funding

The Zero Fare service was launched in March 2020 and is currently active. It has been supported by an $8 million grant from the Virginia Department of Rail & Public Transportation (DRPT) for study and sustainability. GRTC is noted as a national leader in providing this service.

## Relevance To Youth

The Zero Fare program is highly relevant as it removes a significant financial barrier for youth accessing jobs, internships, and school. The context states that the program ensures access to work and that the majority of GRTC riders (74%) are low-income (households earning less than $40,000 a year), a demographic that includes many young people and their families who are transit-dependent. This makes free transit a critical enabler for the success of a youth employment initiative.


# Context Digital Equity

## Summary

The provided information suggests a digital divide in Richmond by highlighting systemic barriers that prevent equitable access to online opportunities. While the project assumes mobile-first access is prevalent, it acknowledges that digital engagement is hampered by non-digital issues like lack of identification and banking access, particularly for vulnerable youth populations.

## Key Plans And Initiatives

The text does not mention specific government plans like the Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan. However, it does reference a city-level initiative to address a related barrier: DMV Pop-Up Events organized to help youth secure Virginia Identification Cards, which are often required for online job applications and onboarding.

## Identified Barriers

The primary barriers identified are not purely digital but are critical for digital participation. These include: 1) Lack of official identification (the cost of a Virginia ID is noted as $10-$26), which is a prerequisite for many online forms and employment verification. 2) Lack of bank accounts, which complicates receiving payment from jobs secured through a digital platform. 3) The need for inclusivity for multilingual families, suggesting language can be a barrier to using English-only digital services.

## Implications For Project

The digital equity context dictates that the mobile-first app cannot be a simple directory. It must be designed as a support system. Key implications include: 1) Integrating information and scheduling for services that address access barriers, such as DMV pop-up dates and locations. 2) Providing resources or direct integrations for setting up bank accounts or exploring stipend alternatives. 3) Ensuring the app is inclusive and accessible for diverse groups, including multilingual families, youth in foster care, and out-of-school youth, which may require multi-language support and targeted outreach.
