# From Dashboard to Data Utility: Scaling RVA People Data into a Regional Health Equity Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

RVA People Data is currently a high-functioning prototype, but its "universe" extends far beyond a single city department. To survive the "civic tech graveyard," it must transition from a Richmond-only dashboard to a Regional Health Data Utility (HDU). The research indicates a massive alignment between this tool and the Virginia Department of Health’s (VDH) Data Modernization Initiative [1], as well as the state's recent maternal health mandates [2]. 

The strategic "north star" for this project is a hybrid regional model hosted by PlanRVA or VCU, funded by a mix of federal CDC infrastructure pass-throughs and local philanthropic capacity grants. By framing the tool as the connective tissue for the Richmond-Henrico Health District (RHHD) and the 173+ indicators as a shared denominator for the region’s health-related grant applications, the project moves from a "nice-to-have" visualization tool to essential civic infrastructure.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Regional/Academic (PlanRVA + VCU + City of Richmond)**

* **What the City Should Own**: The **Narrative and Policy Layer**. The Mayor’s Office should own the "Equity Snapshot" goals and use the data to justify budget allocations within the Thriving Families pillar. They should *not* own the technical backend.
* **What Partners Should Own**: **PlanRVA** should own the **Data Hosting and Regional Expansion**. As a regional planning agency serving nine localities [3], they can bridge the gap between Richmond, Henrico, and Chesterfield. **VCU Center on Society and Health** should own the **Indicator Validation**, ensuring the metrics remain scientifically rigorous [4].
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned**: The **API Maintenance and Pipeline**. Procurement cycles for city software are too slow for a GitHub-based automated stack. A nonprofit or regional body can iterate on the code much faster than a municipal IT department.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **VDH Maternal Health Dashboards**: In April 2025, the Youngkin administration launched statewide dashboards tracking maternal mortality and pregnancy-associated deaths [2]. *Lesson*: RVA People Data must avoid duplicating state-level stats and focus strictly on census-tract granularity and local administrative data.
* **Chicago Health Atlas**: Operated by DataMade, this is a visual health data explorer for every neighborhood and zip code in Chicago [27]. *Lesson*: Successful models rely on strong data transformation pipelines and clear neighborhood-level visualizations.
* **NYC Environment and Health Data Portal**: Provides data on how place shapes health in NYC, utilizing an explicitly open-source strategy [28] [29]. *Lesson*: Open-source strategies serve public health departments well by allowing continuous community contribution and transparency.
* **LA County Equity Explorer**: Used to direct ARPA funding based on equity metrics. *Lesson*: Data must be tied directly to funding formulas and capital improvement plans to drive actual policy outcomes.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **Why Richmond-only is too small**: Richmond’s health outcomes are inextricably linked to the broader Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA). Residents frequently move between Richmond, Henrico, and Chesterfield, but utilize the same regional provider networks. A regional frame captures the true patient journey and aligns with the Richmond and Henrico Health Districts' joint Community Health Assessment [25].
* **From Dashboard to Data Utility**: If framed merely as a website, it becomes a maintenance burden. By framing it as an API-first Health Data Utility (HDU), it becomes the foundational "operating system" that other regional applications (housing finders, clinic locators) can pull from.
* **From "Vibes" to Formulaic Funding**: A shared data denominator allows the city and regional partners to move toward formula-based budgeting, where specific equity disparities automatically trigger priority funding in the Capital Improvement Plan.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Procurement and Maintenance**: The #1 failure mode of civic tech is manual data rot. While the GitHub Actions pipeline mitigates this, city IT departments often struggle to procure and maintain open-source, automated stacks.
* **Data Privacy and Granularity**: Displaying health data at the census-tract level risks violating HIPAA or exposing Personally Identifiable Information (PII) if populations are too small. Strict suppression rules must be enforced.
* **Political Feasibility**: Highlighting stark racial disparities can be politically sensitive. Housing the data within an academic institution (VCU) provides a neutrality buffer against changing mayoral administrations.
* **Trust and Legitimacy**: If the data is not validated by recognized Subject Matter Experts (like RHHD or VCU), local CBOs will not trust it enough to use it in their grant applications.
