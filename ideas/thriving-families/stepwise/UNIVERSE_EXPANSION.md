# Scaling Stepwise: A Regional Blueprint for Richmond's Youth Workforce Equity

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Stepwise is not merely a job board; it is a regulatory navigator designed to solve the "last-mile" friction of youth employment in Virginia. While the Richmond Civic Hackathon birthed the prototype, the idea's true potential lies in its ability to bridge the gap between fragmented state labor laws, regional transit realities, and federal funding streams. To succeed, Stepwise must evolve from a city-specific tool into a regional infrastructure play that automates the three-party signature process for work permits and uses transit data to solve the "transportation desert" problem for 14–24-year-olds.

The optimal path forward is a hybrid ownership model: the City of Richmond provides the policy mandate and data integrations, while a regional nonprofit or the Capital Region Workforce Partnership (CRWP) manages the platform to ensure continuity across jurisdictional lines. By positioning Stepwise as the "front door" for the $98 million federal YouthBuild [1] and WIOA ecosystems, Richmond can transform a hackathon demo into a statewide standard for youth economic mobility.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (Regional Nonprofit-Led with City Data Support)**

* **What the City of Richmond should own:** The "Policy & Data" layer. The City should provide API access to business licenses (for employer verification) and mandate that all City-funded summer youth programs, such as the Youth Engagement Services (YES) program [2], use Stepwise as their application portal.
* **What a Regional Partner (e.g., CRWP) should own:** The "Product & Operations" layer. The Capital Region Workforce Development Board provides oversight for workforce services [3]. A regional body can bridge the gap between Richmond, Henrico, and Chesterfield, ensuring the platform isn't limited by municipal borders.
* **What should NOT be City-owned:** The software maintenance and candidate support. City IT departments are rarely equipped to manage a high-engagement consumer-facing app. A nonprofit partner or a dedicated workforce organization is better suited for rapid UI/UX iterations and maintaining the high-churn job board environment.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Detroit Grow Detroit's Young Talent (GDYT):** A citywide summer jobs program that trains and employs young adults ages 14-24 for up to 120 hours [28]. The 2026 application portal is open from March 13 to May 15 [29]. *Lesson:* Centralized application portals work, but they require massive employer coordination and strict application windows.
* **Richmond Youth Engagement Services (YES):** For youth ages 14–15, this program focuses on career training and workforce readiness [2]. *Lesson:* Richmond already has programmatic infrastructure for the 14-15 age group; Stepwise should serve as the digital front door for YES rather than competing with it.
* **Capital Region WIOA Youth Program:** Assists young adults between 16 and 24 to develop and reach career goals [4]. *Lesson:* The 16-24 demographic is already funded regionally; Stepwise's "Candidate Flow" must align with WIOA intake requirements.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **The "Richmond-Only" Trap:** If framed only as a Richmond project, Stepwise ignores the fact that a teen in the East End might find their best job opportunity in Henrico. A city-only frame limits the employer pool and makes the tool less attractive to regional employers.
* **The Regional Advantage:** By framing this as a PlanRVA or CRWP Regional Initiative, Stepwise can pool funding from multiple jurisdictions. It allows for "Transit-Aware Matching" using GRTC GTFS data [21] that works across county lines, which is critical for a workforce that relies on regional transit.
* **The Statewide Vision:** If Stepwise successfully automates the VAeECS work permit flow, it becomes a product the Virginia Board of Workforce Development could license for every city in the Commonwealth. The "Richmond Pilot" becomes the "Virginia Standard."

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Legal/Regulatory (High):** COPPA compliance for 14-15 year olds is non-trivial. Any data breach involving minors would be catastrophic.
* **Technical Integration (High):** Integrating with legacy state systems for work permits requires significant political capital and technical interoperability.
* **Maintenance Burden (Medium):** Job boards are "high-churn" products. If listings aren't scrubbed weekly, the platform loses trust. This requires dedicated staff.
* **Equity and Access (Medium):** Foster youth and unhoused youth lack the "Parent Flow" required by the current Stepwise architecture. A proxy flow utilizing the Chafee Transition Plan framework [30] is required to prevent exclusion.
