# From Internships to Micro-Gigs: Scaling Unboxed RVA as a Regional Youth Economic Engine

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Unboxed RVA represents a fundamental shift from static youth employment (such as 8-week summer placements) to a dynamic project economy that mirrors the 2026 workforce. While the City of Richmond’s "Youth Works RVA" (formerly Mayor’s Youth Academy) provides a strong foundation by serving youth aged 14-24 [1] [2], the platform’s true potential lies in its ability to act as a "translation layer" between Gen Z skills and Small-to-Medium Business (SMB) needs. To succeed, Unboxed RVA must move beyond a city-only tool and become a regional utility.

The expansion strategy moves the idea from a "Richmond-only pilot" to a Hybrid Regional Model. By leveraging federal Department of Labor (DOL) "Workforce Pathways for Youth" grants [3] and state-level "Virginia Works" apprenticeship funding [4], Unboxed RVA can bridge the gap between Richmond’s 750-student summer program [2] and the thousands of "opportunity youth" currently disconnected from the economy [5]. The core value is not just the marketplace, but the RIA AI Assistant, which mitigates the high "management tax" that prevents small businesses from hiring young people.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (Nonprofit-Led / City-Backed / Regional-Scale)**

* **What the City of Richmond Should Own:** Policy, "Youth Works RVA" brand integration, and the Payment/Liability Shield. The City acts as the "Employer of Record" or fiscal agent to handle the legal complexities of paying minors and ensuring compliance with Virginia Department of Labor and Industry (DOLI) regulations, which strictly govern the hours and types of jobs minors aged 14-15 can perform [6].
* **What a Nonprofit Partner Should Own:** The Technology Stack (RIA) and Employer Relations. Nonprofits move faster than city procurement and can better manage the "sales" aspect of recruiting small businesses. Organizations like Partnership for the Future, which has a dedicated network of local high schools and businesses [7], are ideal for this.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The marketplace software itself. To scale regionally to Henrico, Chesterfield, and beyond, the platform must be "jurisdiction-neutral" so businesses across the county line don't feel it's a "Richmond-only" initiative.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Youth Works RVA (Richmond):** Formerly the Mayor's Youth Academy, this program aims to serve 750 Richmond youth (ages 14-24) through paid summer work experiences [2]. *Lesson:* It is highly seasonal. Unboxed RVA must solve the "10-month gap" where youth have no access to paid work.
* **Boston SuccessLink (Boston, MA):** Redesigned their youth portal using tech- and data-enabled processes to replace ad hoc application management [33]. *Lesson:* Modern, centralized hubs (like futureBOS) are necessary for youth engagement [34]. Unboxed should prioritize low-friction, mobile-first engagement.
* **Virginia Career Works Youth Programs:** Provides services to disconnected youth facing barriers to employment [26]. *Lesson:* Traditional workforce systems can be rigid; Unboxed's project-based approach offers a more flexible entry point.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **Why Richmond-Only is Too Small:** A marketplace limited to Richmond City limits lacks the "liquidity" (enough jobs for enough kids) to survive. It risks becoming a boutique tool that struggles to attract major federal funding.
* **Regional Frame:** By expanding to the Capital Region, the platform taps into massive retail, logistics, and creative hubs in surrounding counties, providing youth with significantly more project variety and aligning with regional workforce goals [35].
* **Nonprofit Ownership of Resident-Facing Layer:** If a nonprofit owns the UX/Marketplace while the city provides the Data API and Payment Rail, the idea becomes a "Public-Private Utility." This allows it to accept private philanthropic dollars (like Community Foundation grants [12]) that are often restricted from going directly to government coffers.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Labor Law Compliance (High Risk):** Virginia DOLI has strict rules on hours and types of work for 14-15 year olds, requiring Employment Certificates and prohibiting hazardous jobs [6]. The platform must hard-filter projects based on age.
* **Payment Processing (Medium Risk):** Paying minors via 1099 is a tax burden for SMBs. The platform must use a 3rd-party "Employer of Record" service or a city-backed fiscal agent.
* **The "Digital Divide" (Medium Risk):** Disconnected youth may lack reliable data plans. Partnering with GRTC, which already provides free transit passes that double as student IDs [32], could offer integrated access solutions.
* **Trust & Legitimacy (High Risk):** Youth may view a "City App" with skepticism. Keeping the "Unboxed" branding separate from the City seal and leading with peer-to-peer marketing is essential.
