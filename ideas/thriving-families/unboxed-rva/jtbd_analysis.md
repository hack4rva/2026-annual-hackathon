# Project Analysis Summary

The Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) analysis for the 'unboxed-rva' project reveals a significant opportunity to address fragmentation in Richmond's youth employment ecosystem. The core insight is that multiple stakeholders—youth, parents/guardians, and program coordinators—struggle with a disjointed and confusing landscape of job opportunities, applications, and data management. For youth, the primary functional job is to find all relevant opportunities in one place to avoid missing deadlines and the pain of navigating multiple websites. Emotionally, both youth and their families need a trustworthy, secure, and supportive platform to feel confident sharing personal information and committing to a job. For program administrators and employers, the key systems job is to simplify the process of publishing roles, screening candidates, and tracking outcomes to reduce administrative duplication and improve placement equity. The strategic direction for 'unboxed-rva' is to function as a central discovery and translation hub. It should not replace but rather aggregate and clarify opportunities from various sources like Youth Works RVA and ChamberRVA partners. The project's focus should be on building trust, providing clarity on eligibility and processes, and creating efficient workflows for employers and coordinators, thereby directly supporting the 'Thriving Families' goal of connecting youth to career development opportunities.

unboxed-rva

Youth employment discovery helping young people explore career pathways and find opportunities.

## Problem Statement

Youth Employment Pathways - Give Richmond youth one clear place to explore summer jobs, internships, and first-job guidance.

## Strategic Pillar

Thriving Families

## Location

Richmond, VA


# Functional Job To Be Done

## Situation

When summer job and internship applications open across multiple Richmond programs (Youth Works RVA, RPS work-based learning, local nonprofits).

## Persona

A Richmond public high school student, 16–18, seeking a first paid experience.

## Motivation

To see all open and relevant opportunities, deadlines, and eligibility in one searchable place and apply or be routed quickly.

## Outcome

To secure a paid, career-relevant placement before slots fill.

## Current Workaround

Manually checking multiple sites (RVA.gov YES/Youth Works, school counselor emails, social media posts, flyers), direct messaging organizations, and relying on word-of-mouth.

## Core Pain

Fragmented information, inconsistent timelines, unclear eligibility and pay, missed deadlines, and duplicated application forms.


# Emotional Trust Job To Be Done

## Situation

When sharing personal information to apply for a first job (SSN, address, income, immigration status concerns) and asking questions about pay, schedules, and transportation.

## Persona

A Richmond teen and/or their parent/guardian.

## Motivation

To have a trustworthy, youth-friendly hub that clearly explains how data is used, confirms program legitimacy, and offers real human support.

## Outcome

To feel safe, respected, and confident committing to an opportunity.

## Current Workaround

Asking a trusted teacher, counselor, or older sibling; texting peers; or avoiding applying altogether due to uncertainty.

## Core Pain

Low trust in unknown portals, fear of scams, unclear data privacy practices, and stigma or confusion around documentation requirements (work permits, I-9 forms, banking for direct deposit).


# Systems Coordination Job To Be Done

## Situation

When coordinating placements across city programs (YES/Youth Works RVA, YES Forward), employer partners (ChamberRVA network), and school-based supports (RPS).

## Persona

A program coordinator or employer.

## Motivation

To have a simple way to publish roles, screen candidates, match them by interests and logistics (age, eligibility, transit), and track outcomes.

## Outcome

To fill positions equitably and report on impact without duplicative data entry.

## Current Workaround

Using separate spreadsheets, forms, email chains, social media posts, and performing manual cross-checking with city and school staff.

## Core Pain

Duplicated outreach efforts, mismatched expectations between parties, slow onboarding for host sites, limited visibility into applicant readiness and logistical needs (like transportation), and burdensome reporting requirements.


# Summary Of Key Pain Points

## Pain Point

Fragmented Information and Process Inefficiency

## Description

Job opportunities, deadlines, and eligibility requirements are scattered across multiple platforms like government websites, school emails, and social media. This leads to confusion, missed deadlines, and requires applicants to fill out duplicated forms for different programs.

## Pain Point

Low Trust and Security Concerns

## Description

Youth and their guardians experience a lack of trust in unfamiliar application portals. There are significant fears about potential scams, unclear data privacy practices, and how sensitive personal information (like SSN, income, or immigration status) will be used and protected.

## Pain Point

Confusion Around Documentation and Logistics

## Description

There is considerable stigma and confusion surrounding the necessary documentation for employment, such as work permits, I-9 forms, and setting up bank accounts for direct deposit. There is also a lack of clarity regarding logistical support like transportation.

## Pain Point

Systemic Coordination Failures

## Description

For program coordinators and employers, the lack of a central system results in duplicated outreach efforts, mismatched expectations between employers and youth, slow onboarding for host organizations, and burdensome, manual reporting processes.

## Pain Point

Limited Visibility and Mismatched Placements

## Description

Program coordinators have limited visibility into an applicant's readiness, logistical needs (like transportation), and interests, which can lead to inequitable or mismatched placements and a failure to connect youth with the most suitable opportunities.


# Summary Of Current Workarounds

## Workaround

Manual Multi-Site Checking

## Description

Youth manually check various websites (e.g., RVA.gov), school counselor emails, social media posts, and physical flyers to find job information. They also directly message organizations to inquire about opportunities.

## Workaround

Reliance on Word-of-Mouth and Personal Networks

## Description

To find opportunities and vet their legitimacy, youth rely on information from trusted sources like teachers, counselors, older siblings, and peers rather than official but unknown portals.

## Workaround

Application Avoidance

## Description

Due to uncertainty about the application process, fear of scams, or confusion about requirements, some youth and their families choose to avoid applying for opportunities altogether.

## Workaround

Ad-Hoc Coordination Tools

## Description

Program coordinators and employers use a patchwork of separate spreadsheets, disparate forms, email chains, and social media posts to manage the application and placement process. They also perform manual cross-checking with city and school staff.

This entity operates the City of Richmond's primary summer workforce program, Youth Works RVA. Its role is to engage Richmond's youth in career exploration through work-based learning, connecting them with internships and job placements in city government, non-profits, and local businesses. It manages the application process, provides job readiness training, and facilitates wage payment, serving as the central operational hub for city-sponsored youth employment.

# Strategic Recommendations For Unboxed Rva

## Recommendation

Position 'unboxed-rva' as a centralized discovery hub that aggregates opportunities and clarifies the youth employment ecosystem, complementing rather than duplicating the official Youth Works RVA portal and other application systems.

## Justification

The primary user pain point is the fragmentation of information, leading to confusion and missed opportunities. A discovery hub would solve this by providing one clear place to explore all opportunities (from Youth Works, ChamberRVA partners, etc.), understand eligibility, pay, and deadlines, and then route users to the correct official application. This approach leverages existing infrastructure, reduces development complexity, and directly addresses the core 'functional job' for youth. It also provides value to coordinators by simplifying outreach and candidate routing.

## Priority

High
