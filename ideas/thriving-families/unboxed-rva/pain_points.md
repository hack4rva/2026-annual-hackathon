# Executive Summary

Richmond's youth employment ecosystem presents significant pain points for its key users, creating a strategic opportunity for the unboxed-rva project. For youth (ages 16-18), the landscape is confusing and fragmented, characterized by multiple programs like Youth Works RVA with staggered, hard deadlines. They face substantial documentation hurdles, including needing an original Social Security card and a state-issued ID, which can cost between $10 and $26 to obtain. Younger teens (14-15) face an additional layer of complexity with the state-mandated Employment Certificate process. Transportation to job sites also remains a significant uncertainty. For parents and guardians, these challenges translate into emotional and financial burdens, including anxiety over sharing sensitive personal data like SSNs, the stress of navigating disparate application windows, and the responsibility for transportation if public transit is not a viable option. Program coordinators and employers are also strained, managing complex coordination between government, non-profits, and businesses. They are burdened by administrative compliance, such as the multi-party electronic process for youth work permits, and are under pressure to reduce systemic barriers like transportation and childcare while tracking outcomes and minimizing overhead. The strategic opportunity for unboxed-rva is to act as a central discovery and translation hub. By aggregating opportunities, normalizing timelines, and clarifying eligibility and documentation requirements in a privacy-forward manner, the project can reduce fragmentation and build trust. It can simplify the journey for youth and families while creating efficient workflows for administrators, ultimately addressing systemic inequities and better connecting Richmond's youth to career development opportunities.

# Project Context And Mission

The unboxed-rva project is a civic tech initiative in Richmond, VA, designed as a youth employment discovery tool to help young people explore career pathways and find opportunities. It operates under the city's 'Thriving Families' strategic pillar. The core problem it aims to solve is the fragmentation and confusion within the local youth employment ecosystem by giving Richmond youth one clear, centralized place to explore summer jobs, internships, and first-job guidance. The project's mission is not to replace existing programs like Youth Works RVA or offerings from ChamberRVA partners, but rather to function as a central discovery and translation hub. It will aggregate and clarify opportunities from these various sources, making it easier for users to navigate. The strategic focus is on building trust with youth and families, providing clarity on complex eligibility and application processes, and creating more efficient workflows for program coordinators and employers, thereby directly supporting the goal of connecting youth to meaningful career development opportunities.

# Youth Persona Pain Points

## Pain Point

Confusing, deadline-driven landscape across programs

## Description

Youth in Richmond seeking their first paid work experience are confronted with a confusing and fragmented ecosystem of programs. Various initiatives, such as Youth Works RVA, operate with distinct and often staggered timelines, fixed seasonal application windows, and multiple program tracks for different age groups. This forces young people to constantly chase information across different portals, attend various info sessions, and navigate a time-sensitive landscape, increasing the risk of missing deadlines or opportunities.

## Evidence

The provided research shows that Youth Works RVA has a fixed seasonal window with an application deadline of March 31st, 2026. It also features multiple tracks, including a 'Virtual Earn and Learn Program' for ages 14-15, an 'In-Person Program' for ages 16-24, and a 'Founders Mark Program' for entrepreneurship. This structure implies a complex and time-sensitive navigation process for potential applicants.


# Parents Guardians Persona Pain Points

## Pain Point

Trust and data-security concerns around sharing SSNs and personal documents

## Description

Parents and guardians of youth applicants experience significant concern and anxiety over the requirement to share highly sensitive personal information, such as Social Security numbers and state-issued IDs. The process of applying for youth employment programs involves submitting original documents, which raises valid questions about data security, privacy, and how this information is stored and protected by various city and nonprofit entities.

## Evidence

The Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) FAQ for the Mayor's Youth Academy explicitly requires 'Original, signed social security card; State issued Photo ID' for Work Experience participants. This formal requirement to hand over sensitive, original documents is a major source of the trust and security pain point for families.


# Program Coordinators Employers Pain Points

## Pain Point

Administrative/documentation burden and compliance steps

## Description

Program coordinators and employers are burdened by significant administrative tasks and complex compliance requirements. This includes managing documentation across multiple partners and pathways and adhering to specific legal steps, especially for younger participants. For example, hiring 14- and 15-year-olds involves a multi-party registration process to obtain a required Employment Certificate, and employers must maintain detailed records for inspection, which adds to their overhead and operational complexity.

## Evidence

According to the Virginia Department of Labor and Industry (DOLI), all 14- and 15-year-old workers must have an Employment Certificate obtained via an electronic system that requires separate registrations from the youth, the employer, and a parent/guardian. This process can only begin after a firm job offer is made, and employers must maintain these signed certificates and time records, which are subject to inspection.


# Current Workarounds And Costs

The current system for finding youth employment in Richmond is highly fragmented, forcing users to employ a variety of inefficient workarounds. Youth manually check multiple websites like RVA.gov's YES/Youth Works page, rely on school counselor emails, social media, and word-of-mouth to discover opportunities. They attend virtual information sessions and use ad-hoc resources like DMV pop-up events to acquire necessary identification. Parents also attend these virtual sessions to navigate the complex landscape on behalf of their children. Program coordinators attempt to mitigate this fragmentation through centralized info sessions, partnerships with organizations like the DMV, and by providing bus passes. However, the cost of this status quo is substantial. The fragmented information and staggered timelines cause youth to miss deadlines and lose out on placements. They face out-of-pocket costs for state IDs, which range from $10 to $26, and lose valuable time navigating the multi-party employment certificate process required for 14-15 year olds. For parents, the costs include anxiety over sharing sensitive personal data, the real cash outlay for IDs, and time taken away from their own work to manage logistics. For the system as a whole, program coordinators spend excessive staff time on duplicative outreach, eligibility verification, and troubleshooting documentation issues, leading to lost matches between youth and employers and perpetuating inequitable access to opportunities.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

The fragmented youth employment ecosystem in Richmond creates significant equity challenges, disproportionately affecting and excluding specific groups. Youth from low-income households are heavily impacted by upfront costs for government-issued IDs and the time scarcity their families face in navigating complex application processes. Newly arrived and immigrant youth, along with their families, face particular uncertainty and anxiety around requirements for Social Security Numbers (SSNs), especially when program requirements conflict with broader state guidance (e.g., WIOA). The system also places a higher burden on 14-15-year-olds, who must navigate a complex, multi-party electronic Employment Certificate process after receiving a job offer, a hurdle not faced by older teens. Furthermore, access to opportunities is limited for youth in 'transit deserts' or those whose families cannot provide daily transportation, even with the availability of GRTC bus passes. The increasing reliance on digital platforms for applications, document uploads, and communication excludes youth without reliable internet access, personal devices, or digital literacy support. Finally, smaller employers who lack dedicated HR capacity may struggle with the compliance and administrative burdens of hiring youth, particularly younger teens, which can limit the overall number of available opportunities.

# Documentation And Privacy Barriers

## Barrier Summary

Obtaining and submitting required documentation is a major barrier in Richmond's youth employment system. The process creates hurdles related to cost, access, and privacy. Youth are required to produce original, physical documents, which can be difficult to access. There is a specific, burdensome administrative process for 14-15 year olds involving an Employment Certificate. Furthermore, families express significant privacy and data security concerns about sharing sensitive information like Social Security Numbers, especially given the fragmented nature of the application ecosystem.

## Required Documents

Commonly required documents for youth work experience programs include an 'Original, signed social security card,' a 'State issued Photo ID,' and a 'Report card or verification of school enrollment.' Additionally, all 14 and 15-year-old workers in Virginia are required to obtain an Employment Certificate through a multi-party electronic system involving the youth, employer, and a parent/guardian after a firm job offer has been made.

## Privacy Concerns

Parents and guardians have significant trust and data-security concerns around sharing sensitive personal documents to multiple programs. The explicit requirement for an 'Original, signed social security card' is a primary source of anxiety. This is compounded for families with immigration-related concerns about how SSN information will be used and stored.

## Impacted Groups

These documentation barriers disproportionately affect several groups. Youth without readily available IDs or Social Security cards, such as newly arrived or immigrant youth, face significant hurdles. Low-income families are particularly burdened by the out-of-pocket costs ($10-$26) required to obtain a state ID. The complex Employment Certificate process specifically impacts 14-15-year-olds, creating a higher administrative barrier for the youngest members of the workforce.


# Transportation And Digital Divide Barriers

## Transportation Summary

Transportation is a critical and persistent barrier to youth employment in Richmond. While programs like the Mayor's Youth Academy attempt to mitigate this by utilizing the GRTC bus system and issuing Go Cards, this solution is incomplete. A significant gap exists for youth who cannot or will not use public transportation, for whom the responsibility and cost of daily transport shifts entirely to their families. This creates a major inequity for youth living in areas poorly served by public transit or whose families lack the resources to provide rides, effectively limiting their access to opportunities.

## Digital Divide Summary

A significant digital divide impacts access to youth employment opportunities. The application process is heavily reliant on digital access and literacy. Programs in Richmond and comparable cities like Durham and Baltimore require youth to complete online applications, upload documents, and maintain a work-appropriate email that they check regularly. Some programs also involve virtual or hybrid participation, which presumes access to reliable internet and devices. Youth lacking these resources or the support to navigate these digital systems are at a high risk of being excluded from the application process entirely.

## Key Evidence

Evidence for these barriers is clear. The City of Richmond's OCWB FAQ explicitly states that while the Mayor's Youth Academy provides GRTC Go Cards, families are responsible for transport if the youth does not ride the bus. The value of this transit support is highlighted by GRTC's data, which shows 74% of its riders live in households earning less than $40,000 a year, and its zero-fare program depends on ongoing funding. The digital barrier is evidenced by the Durham YouthWorks program, which requires an active email address, and the Baltimore YouthWorks program, which requires online document uploads. These requirements create hurdles for those on the wrong side of the digital divide.
