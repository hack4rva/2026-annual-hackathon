# Output Report

## High Schooler Persona Analysis

### Persona: Richmond Public Schools High Schooler (14–19)
**Job-to-be-Done:** Find one clear place to discover and apply for city-backed summer jobs, internships, and work-based learning tracks.

#### 1. Concrete Pain Points with Evidence
*   **Fragmented Information and Eligibility Variations:** Program details are scattered across multiple city web pages and partner sites. The main Youth Engagement Services (YES) page provides a high-level overview of tracks, while a separate legacy FAQ page contains specific age, grade, and residency requirements, and the application is on yet another page. Partner organizations like the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) also post their own announcements. This forces youth to piece together information from various sources. **Evidence:** The YES overview lists tracks for ages 14-24, but sub-programs have narrower age bands, such as Counselors in Training (CIT) for ages 14–16 and Work Experience for ages 16–19, creating confusion about who qualifies for what [1, 2].
*   **Confusing Timelines and Deadlines:** Application windows are tight and not centrally communicated. Youth must actively monitor multiple sources to catch key dates. **Evidence:** Applications typically open in March and, according to an RRHA post for a future cohort, close on March 31. Information sessions are held on fixed dates in March, and selection notifications are sent in April, creating a high risk of missing the opportunity if not vigilant [2, 4].
*   **Heavy Documentation Burden:** The application process requires numerous documents that can be difficult to obtain. This includes a report card, proof of residency, a photo ID, and for the Work Experience track, an original Social Security card and a state-issued ID. **Evidence:** The FAQ page lists these requirements, and the fact that partners like RRHA host special "DMV Pop-Up Events" to help youth get a Virginia ID card indicates this is a known and significant barrier [2, 4].
*   **Transportation Uncertainty:** While the city provides support, it's not a complete solution, leaving logistical gaps. The program provides weekly GRTC Go Cards, but if a worksite is not accessible via transit, families are responsible for transportation. **Evidence:** The FAQ explicitly states this policy. While GRTC's zero-fare system helps, route coverage, frequency, and last-mile connections remain potential obstacles for participants [2, 7].
*   **Lack of Clarity Across Program Tracks:** The YES program offers multiple tracks (e.g., TRIP, CIT, Founders Mark, in-person vs. virtual) with different goals and eligibility criteria. Youth must independently research and map their interests and qualifications to the correct track. **Evidence:** The YES overview page lists these distinct programs, but the nuances of each are not consolidated in one easy-to-compare format [1].

#### 2. What Breaks Today, Workarounds, and Cost of the Status Quo
*   **What Breaks:** There is no centralized, user-friendly hub for discovery and application. Information is fragmented across the main YES page, a legacy FAQ, the application form itself, and partner announcements. Eligibility rules and deadlines are inconsistent or distributed across these sources.
*   **Workarounds:** Students resort to asking school counselors or teachers, monitoring scattered social media posts (like RRHA's), repeatedly checking multiple RVA.gov pages, and relying on word-of-mouth from peers.
*   **Cost of the Status Quo:** The direct costs include missed deadlines, leading to lost summer wages and valuable work experience. The indirect costs are significant: stress and time spent hunting for documents (SSN card, state ID), confusion about placement details, hours, and pay, and a high barrier to entry that creates opportunity loss, especially for first-time applicants.

#### 3. Equity Dimensions
*   **Who is Most Affected:** The current system disproportionately burdens first-generation applicants who lack familial experience with such processes. It also disadvantages youth without easy access to government IDs or Social Security cards, those with limited internet or device access for online applications, students living in areas with infrequent transit service, and families who are non-native English speakers.
*   **Who is Excluded:** The system can exclude out-of-school 16–17-year-olds if program tracks are tied to school enrollment. Non-Richmond residents are ineligible. Most critically, youth unable to secure the required identification documents by the strict deadlines are shut out. While the YES Forward program serves out-of-school youth aged 18–24, a gap may exist for younger individuals in similar situations [1].

## Parent Guardian Persona Analysis

### Persona: Parent/Guardian in Richmond
**Job-to-be-Done:** Help my teen secure a safe, constructive summer opportunity.

#### 1. Concrete Pain Points with Evidence
*   **Documentation and Eligibility Burden on Families:** Parents are primarily responsible for gathering the necessary paperwork to prove their child's eligibility. This includes providing proof of residency and school enrollment, and for certain tracks, ensuring the teen has an original Social Security card and a state-issued ID. **Evidence:** The FAQ and Work Experience requirements explicitly list these documents, with the FAQ noting that a parent's documents are often needed to establish residency [2].
*   **Difficult Timeline and Communication Management:** Parents must track application windows, deadlines, and communications across various channels to ensure their teen doesn't miss a step. **Evidence:** With applications opening in March, acceptances emailed in April, and different timelines for various tracks, parents must monitor RVA.gov, partner sites, and their email to coordinate orientations and training sessions [2, 4].
*   **Transportation Planning and Safety Concerns:** The program's transportation support has limits, requiring parental involvement. The city provides weekly GRTC Go Cards, but families must arrange transport if the worksite isn't on a bus route. **Evidence:** The FAQ states this policy. Furthermore, some worksites may have late hours (e.g., Kings Dominion), forcing parents to coordinate late-night pickups and manage safety concerns, even with the city's zero-fare transit system [2, 7].
*   **Unclear Expectations for Placements and Hours:** There is a lack of clear, consolidated information about the day-to-day realities of the jobs. **Evidence:** The FAQ notes that work hours can vary, sometimes up to or exceeding 40 hours per week, and that lunch breaks are unpaid. This variability makes it difficult for parents to gauge the suitability and safety of a placement and plan for family schedules [2].

#### 2. What Breaks Today, Workarounds, and Cost of the Status Quo
*   **What Breaks:** A single, parent-friendly resource consolidating all critical information—eligibility, documents, timelines, placement details, transportation, and pay norms—does not exist. Key announcements are dispersed across different RVA.gov pages, PDFs, and partner social media posts.
*   **Workarounds:** Parents resort to calling program offices directly, attending virtual information sessions, taking their children to DMV pop-up events to secure IDs, relying on school counselors for reminders, and using social media to stay informed.
*   **Cost of the Status Quo:** The costs include a significant time burden on parents to secure documents and navigate the fragmented system. There's a high risk of missing deadlines, leading to their teen losing out on a structured summer activity. It also creates additional logistical stress related to transportation and childcare coordination, and anxiety about their child's safety during late shifts.

#### 3. Equity Dimensions
*   **Digital Divide:** Households without reliable internet access or digital devices face significant hurdles in completing online applications and uploading required documents.
*   **Navigational Complexity:** While targeted mobility programs exist to address inequities—such as the bikeshare pilot in Fairfield Court for RRHA residents and the OETM's Free Rides to Work van service for TANF-eligible families—they require parents to discover and navigate yet another program, adding to their cognitive load [5, 6].
*   **Last-Mile Barriers:** Even with zero-fare transit, families in historically underserved neighborhoods may face persistent last-mile transportation gaps and scheduling conflicts that make participation difficult.

## Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

### Cross-Cutting Equity Considerations
Several systemic issues create barriers for all users, disproportionately affecting Richmond's most vulnerable residents.

*   **Transportation Barriers:** While the city-wide GRTC zero-fare policy is a major step toward equity, it doesn't solve all transportation challenges. Research from the Richmond Fed and VCU confirms that transportation remains a primary barrier to employment, especially for low-income individuals [8, 9]. Specific worksite locations or non-standard hours can render public transit impractical. Targeted initiatives like the OETM's bikeshare pilot in Fairfield Court and the "Free Rides to Work" van service for TANF-eligible families acknowledge these gaps but add another layer of programmatic complexity for families to discover and enroll in [5, 6].

*   **The Digital and Document Divide:** The requirement for online applications and the submission of specific government-issued documents creates a significant hurdle. Youth and families lacking stable internet, scanners, or access to essential documents like an original Social Security card or a state-issued ID are at a distinct disadvantage. The fact that community partners like RRHA organize "DMV Pop-Up Events" is direct evidence that obtaining a state ID is a prevalent barrier preventing program participation [4]. This aligns with VCU research identifying lack of internet access and computer skills as key employment barriers in Richmond's East End [8].

*   **Program Fragmentation and Branding:** The youth employment ecosystem in Richmond is complex. The transition from the Mayor's Youth Academy (MYA) to Youth Engagement Services (YES), coupled with multiple program tracks (Youth Works RVA, TRIP, CIT, etc.) with varying age bands and eligibility rules, can be confusing. This fragmentation disproportionately affects first-time applicants and those without the social capital or guidance to navigate the system effectively [1, 2].

*   **Alignment with City-Wide Equity Goals:** The Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) includes a specific metric aimed at decreasing the number of 16–19-year-olds who are not enrolled in school and not employed (NEET) [10]. The friction in the current application and discovery process directly undermines this stated equity goal. Streamlining access and centralizing information would be a direct and impactful way to support this key performance indicator.

## Comparable Cities Summary

### Findings from Comparable Cities
Analysis of youth employment programs in similar U.S. cities validates that the challenges faced by Richmond's youth and families are not unique but are common systemic issues. These examples highlight recurring themes of documentation burdens, strict deadlines, and eligibility complexities.

*   **Baltimore, MD (YouthWorks):** This program demonstrates a similarly stringent documentation process, requiring applicants to navigate I-9 forms, work permits, and proof of work authorization. Their use of detailed checklists underscores the heavy administrative burden placed on participants [11].

*   **Pittsburgh, PA (Learn & Earn):** Pittsburgh's program requires applicants to upload multiple forms of proof for address, household income, age, Social Security number, and citizenship. This reliance on online document uploads mirrors the digital divide challenges present in Richmond [12, 13].

*   **Raleigh and Durham, NC (Summer Youth Employment):** Both cities enforce strict application windows and city-residency rules. Raleigh also requires mandatory orientations, background checks, and fingerprinting, adding more logistical steps. Durham's use of a lottery system for participation further illustrates how demand can outstrip supply, making timely application critical [14, 15].

*   **Louisville, KY (SummerWorks):** This program relies on seasonal registration pushes, reinforcing the need for applicants to be vigilant and monitor announcements to avoid missing the enrollment period [16].

Collectively, these programs show that managing application windows, proving eligibility with multiple documents, and navigating complex requirements are standard friction points in municipal youth employment initiatives.
