# Executive Summary

Limited-English and privacy-conscious immigrants and refugees in Richmond, VA, face significant barriers when accessing community services, primarily driven by three interconnected issues. First, information is highly fragmented, with no centralized, trusted source for available resources, forcing residents to rely on informal networks or navigate multiple disconnected agency websites. A Virginia statewide needs assessment explicitly identified this as a major barrier. Second, pervasive language barriers, including inconsistent availability and quality of interpretation and translation services, prevent meaningful access to critical information and support. Third, a profound fear of data sharing and negative immigration consequences (the 'public charge' chilling effect) deters many, including those who are eligible, from seeking help. This fear is exacerbated by a lack of clear, explicit signals on service provider websites and intake forms regarding privacy, confidentiality, and eligibility requirements (e.g., whether ID is required), leading residents to delay or completely forgo essential services.

# Functional Persona Analysis

## Persona Description

This persona represents a limited-English, privacy-conscious immigrant or refugee who has recently arrived in Richmond or is experiencing a change in their family's needs related to health, legal matters, schooling, or housing. Their primary motivation is to find trustworthy, nearby community services in their native language without the friction of creating an account or the fear of sharing personal details that could compromise their privacy or safety. They are seeking a direct path to assistance to resolve their needs promptly and safely.

## Pain Points

This persona faces several concrete pain points. Firstly, there is a significant issue with fragmented information and the lack of a central hub for resources. A Virginia statewide needs assessment explicitly identified the "Lack of a centralized source of information on statewide resources and services" as a key barrier, a finding reiterated in the Richmond regional plan which calls for a centralized hub and database. Secondly, language barriers are a primary obstacle. The ONA report lists this as a top barrier, with interviews citing inconsistent interpreter quality, an over-reliance on poor machine translation, and a need for more bilingual staff. Thirdly, the implementation of language access services is inconsistent. While Richmond has a language access policy (AR 5.24), individual departments are still in the process of operationalizing it, meaning gaps in service persist. Fourthly, digital access barriers are prevalent; research from VCU RISE highlighted how LEP residents faced difficulties with online portals like vaccine preregistration sites, underscoring the need for low-tech and multilingual channels. Lastly, there is significant uncertainty around eligibility and documentation requirements, with the ONA report documenting confusion about immigration status and a scarcity of legal help, where translation errors can have severe consequences.

## System Failures

The current system fails this persona in several critical ways. Service listings are disconnected and spread across multiple platforms like 211, FindHelp, and individual agency websites, with no single source of truth. The language support offered is highly variable, with no guarantee of quality or availability. There is a lack of clear, standardized signals regarding documentation requirements, ID needs, costs, and eligibility criteria, forcing users to guess or make multiple inquiries. Furthermore, the intake process is often duplicated across different organizations, forcing individuals to retell their stories and fill out similar forms repeatedly, which is inefficient and can be re-traumatizing.

## Workarounds And Costs

To navigate the broken system, individuals resort to a variety of workarounds. They often rely on informal networks, asking friends or using WhatsApp groups for recommendations. They may try to contact the city's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) or specific well-known nonprofits directly. In some cases, they physically visit clinics or offices in person to get information. They also depend on ad hoc support from bilingual staff they happen to encounter or use family members, including children, as interpreters. These workarounds come at a high cost. They lead to significant delays in receiving care and benefits, and sometimes result in forgoing help altogether. The need to repeatedly share personal stories causes intake fatigue and erodes trust. Errors from poor translation or unqualified interpreters can lead to serious consequences. The process involves wasted time and travel, creates a dependency on informal networks that may have incomplete or inaccurate information, and increases the risk of exposure to misinformation. This also places a significant administrative burden on the frontline staff of service organizations.

## Equity Implications

The failures of the current system disproportionately affect and exclude specific vulnerable groups. Limited English Proficiency (LEP) residents, including speakers of Spanish and other top languages in the region, are the most directly impacted. Undocumented individuals, who are often hesitant to interact with official systems, are also severely affected. The digital divide means that those without reliable broadband or digital devices are left behind by online-only systems. New arrivals, who are unfamiliar with U.S. systems of care and governance, face a steep learning curve. Furthermore, low-literacy users, even if they are proficient in their native language, can be excluded by low-quality machine translations or complex jargon. The lack of consistent, high-quality interpreter access beyond Spanish further marginalizes speakers of other languages. Digital-only intake processes inherently exclude those with limited digital access or literacy, compounding existing inequities.


# Emotional Trust Persona Analysis

## Persona Description

This persona represents a resident who is deeply wary of sharing Personally Identifiable Information (PII) due to their personal circumstances. This includes individuals with undocumented immigration status, those in mixed-status families, or survivors of domestic violence (DV) or other situations where anonymity is crucial for safety. Their primary motivation is to explore available service options anonymously, in their own language, and to be able to identify clear, trustworthy signals about safety and eligibility before they ever have to interact with a person or a system that requires their data.

## Pain Points

The primary pain point for this persona is a profound fear of negative consequences from data sharing. This is heavily evidenced by the "public charge" chilling effect; national data from the Urban Institute (2022) shows one in six adults in immigrant families avoided public programs due to green card concerns, and research in Health Affairs (2020) documents reduced enrollment in safety-net programs following the public charge announcement. A second pain point is the lack of clear, trusted privacy cues and confidentiality policies in service directories. Fears are often driven by misinformation and uncertainty, and without explicit signals like "no ID required" or "your data will not be stored," eligible users may disengage before even attempting to access a service. Thirdly, there are specific safety concerns for survivors of domestic violence, who often do not disclose their situation due to fears around privacy and repercussions, highlighting a critical need for anonymous service exploration. Finally, the scarcity and confusion around legal services, as cited in the ONA report, cause individuals to delay seeking help to avoid exposing their status.

## System Failures

The current system breaks down for this persona because it is not designed for trust and anonymity. Service directories and referral portals rarely standardize or display critical safety information, such as whether a service is "safe for undocumented" individuals, requires "no ID," or has a strict confidentiality policy. Many modern referral portals demand the creation of an account and the submission of PII as a first step, creating an immediate barrier. When a handoff to an external system is required, there are often no privacy warnings or clear explanations of what data will be shared. The pervasive atmosphere of fear, whether founded or not, leads to the non-use of services even when a person is fully eligible, representing a fundamental failure of the system to reach those it is intended to serve.

## Workarounds And Costs

To mitigate their risks, these individuals employ workarounds that prioritize anonymity over efficiency. They may engage in anonymous browsing using general search engines, which lack the curated and verified information of a dedicated directory. They often rely on trusted community organizations to make inquiries on their behalf, using the organization as a privacy shield. They might use a friend's phone or a public computer to avoid creating a digital trail. A common workaround is to completely avoid digital or otherwise auditable channels for seeking help. The costs of these workarounds are severe: they result in missed benefits and delayed care, which can exacerbate health problems. For survivors of domestic violence, it means continued exposure to harm. For those with legal issues, it can lead to worsening situations. This pattern often results in increased utilization of expensive emergency services as a last resort and contributes to a long-term erosion of trust between vulnerable communities and public institutions.

## Equity Implications

The equity implications are significant, as these system failures disproportionately exclude the most vulnerable. Undocumented immigrants and mixed-status families are directly targeted by the chilling effects of immigration policy rhetoric. Survivors of domestic violence (DV) and sexual assault (SA), LGBTQ+ immigrants, and anyone with prior negative experiences with authorities are also deeply affected. The fear of surveillance or repercussions is a powerful deterrent. As research from Health Affairs notes, these chilling effects extend beyond the specific programs being targeted to shape "perceptions of all public services," creating a systemic barrier that disproportionately excludes these marginalized groups from the social safety net.


# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Most Affected Populations

The populations most affected and excluded by the current barriers to accessing services include: residents with limited English proficiency (LEP), particularly speakers of Spanish and other top languages in the region; undocumented individuals and mixed-status families; individuals with low digital literacy or without reliable access to broadband and devices; new arrivals who are unfamiliar with U.S. systems; survivors of domestic violence (DV) or sexual assault (SA); LGBTQ+ immigrants; and individuals who have had prior negative experiences with authorities.

## Key Exclusionary Factors

Systemic factors leading to exclusion are numerous and include: significant language barriers, such as inconsistent quality of interpretation, over-reliance on poor machine translation, and a lack of bilingual staff; fear of data sharing and its consequences, particularly the 'public charge' chilling effect which causes immigrant families to avoid public programs for fear of jeopardizing their green card status; concerns about privacy and confidentiality, especially for survivors of domestic violence; uncertainty and confusion regarding eligibility rules and documentation requirements; the scarcity of affordable legal assistance, especially for immigration issues; and a fragmented information landscape with no centralized, trusted source for service discovery.

## Intersectionality Summary

Intersecting vulnerabilities significantly compound the barriers to accessing essential services. For example, a resident with limited English proficiency who is also undocumented faces a dual barrier of not understanding available information and fearing that any interaction with official services could lead to immigration enforcement. The 'chilling effects' of policies like the public charge rule extend beyond specific programs to create a general distrust of all public services, disproportionately excluding groups like mixed-status families and DV survivors who may also be immigrants. Furthermore, the digital divide intersects with language barriers; as noted in a VCU RISE study, the shift to online-only portals for services like vaccine preregistration effectively excluded many LEP residents who lacked the digital access or literacy to navigate these systems. Similarly, low-quality machine translations can exclude even literate LEP users, demonstrating how a single vulnerability is exacerbated by systemic failures in accessibility.


# Pain Point Information Fragmentation

## Description

Service information in the Richmond area is highly fragmented, with disconnected listings scattered across various platforms like 211, FindHelp, and individual agency websites. There is no single, reliable central hub for residents to find comprehensive information. This fragmentation also leads to a lack of standardization in how information is presented, with variable language coverage and unclear signals regarding documentation requirements, cost, and eligibility, which creates significant duplication of intake processes across different organizations.

## Evidence

A Virginia statewide needs assessment conducted by the Office of New Americans (ONA) explicitly identifies the top barrier as a 'Lack of a centralized source of information on statewide resources and services available to immigrants in Virginia.' This finding is reiterated for the Richmond region specifically. Furthermore, the Richmond regional immigrant/refugee strategy plan calls for the development of a 'centralized hub of available resources,' a 'regional database of services,' and the exploration of 'newcomer navigation services (i.e. regional welcome center),' confirming the recognized need to solve this fragmentation at a systemic level.

## User Impact

The direct consequences for users are significant and multifaceted. They are forced to rely on informal and potentially unreliable workarounds, such as asking friends, using WhatsApp groups, or physically visiting clinics to get information. This leads to delays in accessing care and benefits, with some individuals forgoing help altogether. Users experience 'intake fatigue' from having to repeatedly tell their story and provide information. The status quo results in wasted trips, dependency on ad-hoc networks that may contain misinformation, and a significant administrative burden on the frontline staff of service organizations.


# Pain Point Language Barriers

## Description

There are systemic failures in providing consistent and meaningful multilingual access to services and information. Although the City of Richmond has a formal language access policy (AR 5.24) and a Language Access Plan (LAP), implementation is inconsistent across different city departments. This creates gaps in service delivery, as departments must individually operationalize the plan by designating liaisons, translating vital documents, and establishing interpretation pathways. The result is an overreliance on low-quality machine translation and a lack of sufficient bilingual staff, leading to ineffective communication.

## Evidence

The Virginia Office of New Americans (ONA) report lists 'Language barriers to accessing services and education' as a top barrier for immigrants statewide, with interviewees citing 'inconsistent interpreter quality' and the fact that 'Translation and interpretation errors may have serious consequences.' Research from VCU RISE highlights how digital-only or English-first systems, such as Virginia's COVID-19 vaccine preregistration portal, created significant barriers for residents with Limited English Proficiency (LEP). Additionally, the organization ReEstablish Richmond has specifically addressed the lack of language access at the Virginia DMV. The widespread nature of this issue is corroborated by actions in comparable cities like Baltimore, Durham, and Raleigh, which have all implemented formal plans or ordinances to mandate and systematize language access.

## User Impact

The consequences for LEP individuals are severe, directly denying them meaningful access to critical services. Poor-quality machine translations and inconsistent availability of human interpreters lead to the misunderstanding of vital information, which can have serious consequences for eligibility and compliance. Users are often unable to complete necessary processes, such as applying for benefits or registering for services. This results in wasted trips, frustration, and exclusion from essential public resources, effectively making services inaccessible even when they theoretically exist.


# Pain Point Data Privacy And Fear

## Description

A pervasive climate of fear and mistrust surrounding data privacy acts as a significant barrier that prevents vulnerable residents from seeking help. This is particularly true for undocumented individuals, mixed-status families, and survivors of domestic violence. In the absence of clear, explicit, and trusted signals about confidentiality and data safety (e.g., 'no ID required,' 'your data will not be shared'), many eligible individuals will pre-emptively disengage and avoid seeking services for fear of negative consequences.

## Causes

The root causes of this fear are complex. A primary driver is the 'chilling effect' of federal immigration policies, particularly the 'public charge' rule, which has created widespread fear that accessing public benefits could jeopardize future green card applications or immigration status. This is compounded by a general fear of data sharing between service agencies and immigration enforcement, as well as a broader distrust of government. For specific populations like domestic violence survivors, fear of disclosure and privacy breaches in service settings prevents them from seeking safety and support.

## Evidence

National data quantifies the impact of these fears. A 2022 Urban Institute report found that 'One in six adults in immigrant families with children avoided public programs in 2022 because of green card concerns.' A 2020 study in Health Affairs corroborated this, finding that after the public charge rule announcement, over 20% of adults in low-income immigrant families avoided noncash government programs due to immigration fears. The study noted that these chilling effects extend beyond targeted programs to affect 'perceptions of all public services.' For domestic violence survivors, research published in PMC shows that fears of disclosure and misconceptions about privacy limit identification and support in healthcare settings.

## User Impact

The impact on users is severe, forcing them to forgo critical health, safety, and economic support. This leads to missed benefits, delayed medical care, worsening legal situations, and increased reliance on costly emergency services. For survivors of domestic violence, it can mean continued exposure to harm. This fear disproportionately excludes the most vulnerable populations—undocumented immigrants, mixed-status families, and DV/SA survivors—and erodes long-term community trust in public and nonprofit institutions.


# Pain Point Eligibility And Documentation Uncertainty

## Description

Widespread confusion caused by unclear, unstated, or inconsistent eligibility and documentation requirements acts as a major deterrent for service seekers. Information presented on websites and in directories often fails to clearly state who qualifies for a service, what specific documents are needed to apply, whether immigration status is a factor, and what the potential costs are. This lack of upfront, standardized information creates a high degree of uncertainty and anxiety for users.

## Examples

Specific examples of this uncertainty include service providers having inconsistent or unstated ID requirements, making it impossible for a user to know what to bring. There is significant ambiguity about whether an individual's immigration status affects their eligibility for various local and state programs. Furthermore, information about costs is often hidden or unclear, preventing users from determining if a service is affordable for them. The Virginia ONA report specifically documents how confusion related to immigration status and a scarcity of affordable legal help act as barriers to accessing necessary services.

## User Impact

The consequences for users are wasted time, money, and emotional energy. Individuals make wasted trips to service providers only to be turned away for not being eligible or not having the correct paperwork. This leads to 'intake fatigue' and frustration from having to repeat their story multiple times. The uncertainty also causes users to self-disqualify from services for which they might actually be eligible, leading them to forgo critical support and benefits out of confusion and fear of rejection.


# Current Workarounds And Limitations

## Method

Informal Community Networks (Friends/WhatsApp)

## Description

Residents rely on asking friends, family, or community members, often through informal channels like WhatsApp groups, to find and vet information about available services.

## Limitations

This method is highly dependent on the strength and knowledge of one's personal network and carries a significant risk of spreading outdated information or misinformation. It is not a scalable or reliable solution for accessing critical services.

## Method

Direct Outreach to Specific Organizations

## Description

Individuals directly call known entities like the City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) or specific nonprofit organizations they are already aware of to ask for help or referrals.

## Limitations

This approach contributes to the fragmentation of information, as no single entity has a complete view. It also places a significant administrative burden on the frontline staff of these organizations, who must repeatedly answer similar inquiries.

## Method

Online Search and Digital Directories (211, FindHelp, Google)

## Description

Residents use general search engines like Google or dedicated resource directories such as 211 Virginia and FindHelp to search for services online.

## Limitations

These platforms often have disconnected listings with variable language support. They lack standardized, clear signals on crucial details like documentation requirements, cost, and eligibility. Critically for privacy-conscious users, they rarely provide explicit privacy indicators (e.g., 'no ID required,' 'safe for undocumented individuals') and referral portals frequently require creating an account and sharing personally identifiable information (PII).

## Method

In-Person Visits

## Description

Lacking clear or trusted information, residents physically travel to clinics, community centers, or agency offices to inquire about services directly.

## Limitations

This workaround is highly inefficient, leading to wasted time, money, and travel for residents. It can result in multiple failed attempts to find the correct service provider, causing frustration and delays in receiving care.

## Method

Ad Hoc Interpretation

## Description

Residents rely on bilingual staff who may not be trained interpreters, or on family members (including children), to provide interpretation during service interactions.

## Limitations

The quality of ad hoc interpretation is often inconsistent and can be poor. As noted in the Virginia statewide needs assessment, 'translation and interpretation errors may have serious consequences,' leading to mistakes in eligibility determination, compliance, and the delivery of care.

## Method

Anonymous Browsing and Evasive Digital Tactics

## Description

Privacy-wary residents, such as those with undocumented status, engage in anonymous browsing, use friends' phones, or avoid digital and auditable channels altogether when seeking information to prevent data tracking.

## Limitations

This workaround is driven by fear and a lack of trust in existing systems. It prevents users from accessing services that may require digital intake and demonstrates that current platforms fail to provide the necessary safety and privacy assurances to engage vulnerable populations.


# Cost Of Status Quo

## Cost For Residents

The human cost for individuals and families is substantial. It includes delays in receiving or completely forgoing critical care and benefits, which can lead to negative health outcomes and worsening legal situations. Residents experience significant stress, wasted time, and financial costs from making wasted trips to incorrect service providers. The process creates 'intake fatigue' from having to repeatedly tell their personal stories. For vulnerable individuals, such as survivors of domestic violence, the status quo can mean continued exposure to harm. Furthermore, reliance on informal networks exposes them to the risk of misinformation, and fear of data sharing (the 'chilling effect') causes many eligible families to avoid public programs altogether.

## Cost For Providers

Service providers face systemic inefficiencies and high operational costs. The fragmented system leads to duplicated intake processes across different organizations, wasting valuable staff time and resources. Frontline staff bear a significant 'administrative burden' from handling repetitive inquiries that could be addressed by a centralized information hub. This inefficiency contributes to staff burnout and detracts from their ability to deliver core services. The lack of a coordinated system means that limited resources are not allocated as efficiently as they could be.

## Cost For Community

The broader societal costs are significant. The 'chilling effects' of policies like the public charge rule and general fear of data sharing lead to a 'long-term community trust erosion,' as residents' 'perceptions of all public services' are negatively affected. This disengagement from public programs by eligible families, particularly immigrants, creates public health and safety risks, as issues like healthcare needs go unaddressed, leading to increased reliance on more costly emergency services. This systemic exclusion also reduces the community's overall economic productivity and weakens social cohesion by creating barriers for new arrivals and vulnerable populations.


# Digital Divide Challenges

## Access And Affordability

Barriers to digital access and affordability are significant for many residents. The research identifies 'those without broadband/devices' as a key excluded population. The explicit recommendation to create 'SMS/offline options' for the 804me project underscores the reality that many target users cannot reliably access or afford home internet or the data plans required for online-only services. This dependence on mobile-only or non-existent internet access prevents them from utilizing online service portals and information hubs.

## Digital Literacy

Challenges with digital literacy present a major hurdle, particularly for 'low-literacy users' and residents with limited English proficiency. Navigating complex government websites, online service portals, and digital intake forms requires a level of technical skill that many do not possess. A VCU RISE study highlighted how LEP residents faced significant barriers with Virginia's online vaccine preregistration portal, indicating that even when translation is available, the complexity of the digital interface itself can be exclusionary.

## Compounding Impact

The digital divide significantly exacerbates other systemic barriers, particularly those related to language access. The COVID-19 pandemic provided a stark example of this compounding effect. A press release from ReEstablish Richmond noted that the pandemic compounded barriers for English language learners by prohibiting walk-in services and 'imposing digital' requirements. When services move to online-only platforms without robust, low-tech alternatives, they effectively shut out individuals who may already be struggling with language barriers, lack of transportation, or unfamiliarity with U.S. systems, thus deepening their exclusion from critical support.


# Richmond Va Specific Context

## Entity Or Plan Name

Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE)

## Type

Government Office

## Role And Services

A division of the City of Richmond's Department of Human Services that oversees the city's language access initiatives. OIRE is responsible for establishing and coordinating the citywide implementation of the Language Access Plan (LAP). It also serves as a point of contact for residents, providing information and handling complaints related to language access.

## Entity Or Plan Name

Administrative Regulation 5.24 (Language Access Policy)

## Type

City Policy

## Role And Services

This is the official policy committing the City of Richmond to making its services and programs accessible to all residents, including those with limited English proficiency (LEP). It mandates that city departments must offer translation of vital documents and provide methods for LEP individuals to request interpretation services.

## Entity Or Plan Name

City of Richmond Language Access Plan (LAP)

## Type

City Plan

## Role And Services

Coordinated by OIRE, this plan operationalizes the city's Language Access Policy. It provides the framework for city departments to implement language access, including appointing liaisons, translating vital documents, and establishing pathways for interpretation. The plan and related resources are available to the public.

## Entity Or Plan Name

Richmond Regional Immigrant/Refugee Strategy

## Type

Regional Plan

## Role And Services

A strategic plan based on local insights that corroborates the findings of a statewide needs assessment. A key focus area is improving access to services by developing a centralized hub of available resources, creating a regional database of services, and exploring the creation of a newcomer navigation service or regional welcome center.

## Entity Or Plan Name

ReEstablish Richmond

## Type

Non-Profit Organization

## Role And Services

A community organization that works with refugees and immigrants. The provided context highlights their work in addressing the lack of language access at the Virginia DMV, noting that the COVID-19 pandemic compounded these barriers for English language learners by imposing digital-only processes and prohibiting walk-ins.

## Entity Or Plan Name

University Research Group

The report notes inconsistent quality of interpretation and the need for more bilingual staff at state agencies.


# Comparable City Approaches

## City

Baltimore, MD

## Initiative

2024 Language Access Ordinance

## Key Features

The ordinance mandates that city agencies must provide free translation and interpretation services for residents with limited English proficiency. These services are explicitly free for anyone living in or visiting Baltimore.

## Relevance To Richmond

This provides a model of a strong, legally binding mandate for language access, going beyond a policy or plan. It reflects a widespread recognition of language barriers and the need for standardization, which is a key issue in Richmond.

## City

Durham, NC

## Initiative

Language Access Plan (LAP)

## Key Features

The plan serves as a management tool to ensure compliance with Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. It is designed to ensure all LEP individuals have reasonable and equitable access to City services and resources, equivalent to English-speaking individuals.

## Relevance To Richmond

Durham's LAP is a formal, policy-driven approach that Richmond can look to as a model for structuring and implementing its own language access commitments to ensure meaningful access for LEP individuals.

## City

Raleigh, NC

## Initiative

Language Access Plan

## Key Features

Raleigh's plan extends beyond basic translation and interpretation services. It also includes the development of accessible tools and provides specific guidance for city departments on implementation.

## Relevance To Richmond

This approach highlights the importance of a comprehensive strategy that includes not just services but also tools and departmental support, offering a more holistic model for improving language access.

## City

Louisville, KY

## Initiative

LMG Language Access Plan

## Key Features

This plan is explicitly framed around Title VI protections against national origin-based discrimination for LEP persons. Its goal is to ensure access to services and resources by reducing organizational barriers.

## Relevance To Richmond

Louisville's plan reinforces the legal and civil rights basis for language access, providing another example of a formal municipal plan designed to systematically remove barriers for immigrant communities.

## City

Pittsburgh, PA

## Initiative

Welcoming Pittsburgh Roadmap

## Key Features

This plan emphasizes making 'better language integration and access part of the culture.' A key goal is to foster safer and more trusting relationships between the immigrant community and first responders/city services.

## Relevance To Richmond

Pittsburgh's focus on building trust directly addresses the 'privacy-conscious' persona's pain points. It shows that a successful strategy must include not only technical access but also cultural change and relationship-building to overcome fear and hesitation.
