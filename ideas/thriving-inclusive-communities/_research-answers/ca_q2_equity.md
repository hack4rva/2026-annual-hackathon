# Executive Summary

In Richmond, Virginia, significant barriers to equitable and coordinated cross-agency services persist, particularly for vulnerable populations. While racial disparities in the justice system are well-documented, there is a critical lack of publicly available data on referral completion rates by demographic for reentry residents, making it impossible to confirm if they are systematically underserved by the referral ecosystem. For residents with limited English proficiency, Virginia Career Works (VCW) operates under a state mandate to provide meaningful language access, and it has adopted federal flexibility allowing for self-attestation of WIOA eligibility. Regarding immigrant families, although the City of Richmond has a policy of not entering into 287(g) agreements with ICE, the prevalence of other state and local ICE contracts across Virginia likely creates 'chilling effects' that deter mixed-status families from seeking services. The consent management landscape is fragmented; while the homeless services sector utilizes a shared HMIS system with a common consent framework, other major agencies like the Department of Social Services (DSS), Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA), and VCW maintain their own separate release of information (ROI) forms. A 'Core Eligibility Pack' to streamline intake across these agencies is feasible for baseline information, but would not eliminate the need for agency-specific forms and verification processes without formal data-sharing agreements. Finally, reentry residents are likely to experience inconsistent smartphone and internet access, a 'supercharged digital exclusion' that undermines the effectiveness of digital coordination tools without dedicated support for devices and digital literacy.

# Key Findings By Topic

State-level reports mention referrals but do not publish outcomes by race or gender, so it cannot be confirmed if the ecosystem systematically underserves this group.
• **Language Access & WIOA Self-Attestation:** Virginia's WIOA State Plan mandates that local career centers provide meaningful language access for Limited English Proficient (LEP) individuals. Furthermore, Virginia Workforce Letter VWL-24-03 explicitly authorizes the use of self-attestation for WIOA Title I eligibility, aligning with federal flexibilities.
• **Immigrant Families & Chilling Effects:** The City of Richmond states its police department will not sign a 287(g) agreement with ICE. However, the Legal Aid Justice Center identifies 32 active state and local government contracts with ICE across Virginia, which likely contributes to 'chilling effects' and fear of data sharing among mixed-status families statewide, including in the Richmond area.
• **Consent Management Workflow:** Consent management in Richmond is largely siloed. The Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (CoC) uses a shared consent framework within its Homeless Management Information System (HMIS). Outside of this, major agencies like RRHA, DSS, and VCW each maintain their own separate and specific Release of Information (ROI) forms and consent processes.
• **Feasibility of a Core Eligibility Pack:** A standardized 'Core Eligibility Pack' containing documents like photo ID, proof of residence, SSN cards, and income proof could satisfy many of the minimum intake requirements for DSS, VCW, and RRHA. However, it would not be a complete solution, as agency-specific forms (e.g., RRHA's HUD-9886) and unique verification procedures would still be required.
• **Smartphone Access for Reentry Residents:** National research indicates that returning citizens experience 'supercharged digital exclusion.' They rely heavily on smartphones but face significant challenges with affordability, device stability, and consistent service. This instability can undermine digital coordination efforts unless tools are designed for low-bandwidth and offline use, and support for devices and digital navigation is provided.

# Reentry Service Equity Analysis

There is well-documented evidence of significant racial disparities in Virginia's justice system, leading to a disproportionate representation of Black residents, including men, among the justice-involved and reentry populations. For instance, an Urban Institute snapshot highlights that Black youth, who constitute 20% of Virginia's youth population, account for 71% of commitments. This pattern of overrepresentation is understood to extend to the adult population in Richmond. However, based on available public information, it is not possible to definitively conclude that this population is systematically underserved by the referral ecosystem itself through differential outcomes. Publicly available reports, such as the VADOC Status Report on Transitional and Reentry Services, describe referral processes and partnerships with local entities like the Richmond Behavioral Health Authority (RBHA), but they do not publish data on referral acceptance or completion rates broken down by demographic characteristics like race and gender for the Richmond area. Consequently, while the premise of disproportionate justice involvement is correct, there is a lack of specific data in the identified sources to prove that the referral ecosystem systematically fails Black men at a higher rate than other groups.

# Reentry Referral Data Analysis

An examination of available data reveals a significant gap in information regarding referral acceptance and completion rates for reentry services when broken down by demographic groups in Virginia and specifically in Richmond. Publicly available, cross-agency data on 'referral completion rates by demographic' for Richmond's reentry population was not found in the provided sources. The most relevant data located is from the Virginia Recovery Courts 2024 Annual Report, which indicates that statewide, there were 1,063 referrals made to adult recovery court programs, with 553 of those being accepted, resulting in a 52% acceptance rate. However, this report does not provide any further breakdown of these numbers by race, gender, or locality, making it impossible to analyze outcomes for specific populations or for the Richmond area. Other state-level documents, like the VADOC Status Report on Transitional and Reentry Services, mention referral partnerships but do not publish quantitative data on referral outcomes by demographic. Therefore, there is no available data in the reviewed sources to perform a demographic analysis of referral completion rates.

# Language Access Services

Language access for residents with limited English proficiency (LEP) is mandated and supported through various state and local policies. According to Virginia’s WIOA Combined State Plan for 2024–2027, each Local Workforce Development Board (LWDB) is required to develop an access strategy and local policy to ensure that LEP individuals have meaningful access to all programs and services, enabling them to participate effectively regardless of their ability to speak, read, write, or understand English. A statewide Accessibility Taskforce also focuses on removing language barriers to support these efforts. The Virginia Employment Commission (VEC), a core partner that provides services within Virginia Career Works (VCW) centers, explicitly states that language services, auxiliary aids, and accessibility services are available upon request. The VEC prioritizes the translation of vital documents into electronic, printed, or oral forms as needed and provides links to "Help in Any Language" resources. Similarly, the Richmond-Harrisburg Housing Authority (RRHA) has a Limited English Proficiency Language Policy that ensures the translation of vital documents into common languages and provides access to sight translation through telephonic or internet-based interpreter systems. While a specific Richmond VCW center page detailing on-site interpreters was not found, the governing state plan and core partner policies confirm a commitment to providing interpreter and translation services to ensure meaningful access.

# Wioa Self Attestation Flexibility

The state of Virginia has officially adopted and authorized the use of Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) self-attestation flexibility. According to Virginia Workforce Letter VWL-24-03, titled 'WIOA Title I Eligibility Guidance (Change 1),' staff at American Job Centers (AJCs) are directed to use state-developed self-attestation, telephone, and virtual verification methods for determining eligibility. This state-level policy is explicitly aligned with the federal Training and Employment Guidance Letter (TEGL) 23-19 Change 2, which provides guidance on these flexible verification methods. Therefore, the use of self-attestation for eligibility criteria is not only permitted but is an adopted policy available to front-line staff at Virginia Career Works centers.

# Immigrant Service Navigation And Fears

Undocumented residents and mixed-status families in Richmond navigate a complex environment where local assurances of safety contrast with a broader state context that can generate fear. The City of Richmond has taken an official stance of non-cooperation with certain federal immigration enforcement actions, stating that the Richmond Police Department has not and will not sign a 287(g) agreement with ICE, which would delegate federal immigration enforcement powers to local police. The city also promotes resources such as legal aid for immigrant residents. However, this local policy exists within a statewide reality that likely creates 'chilling effects.' The Legal Aid Justice Center (LAJC) documents 32 active state and local government immigration enforcement agreements and contracts with ICE across Virginia as of 2026. The LAJC notes that the existence of these collaborations can create a perceived risk and contribute to chilling effects among immigrant communities, making them hesitant to access services for fear that their data could be shared. This fear is compounded by confirmed ongoing ICE activity in Central Virginia. While individual agencies like the Richmond Department of Social Services state that client information is confidential and will only be shared for program administration, the overarching fear of data sharing and ICE access likely remains a significant barrier for mixed-status families navigating cross-agency services.

# Consent Management Ecosystem

The consent-management workflow in Richmond's nonprofit ecosystem is fragmented rather than unified. A sector-specific shared consent framework exists within the homeless services sector, managed through the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (CoC) and its Homeless Management Information System (HMIS). This system utilizes coordinated entry policies and standard HMIS privacy notices and Release of Information (ROI) forms for data sharing among participating CoC partner agencies. However, outside of this specific network, agencies maintain their own distinct and separate consent and information release protocols. For example, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) requires applicants to sign the federal HUD-9886 Authorization for Release of Information as well as its own specific consent forms as detailed in its Administrative Plan. Similarly, the Department of Social Services (DSS) has its own confidentiality and information-sharing rules limited to the administration of its specific programs like SNAP and TANF. Virginia Career Works (VCW) also maintains its own documentation and management system for WIOA program eligibility. Therefore, a centralized, cross-ecosystem consent management system does not exist; instead, a hybrid model is in place where one sector (homeless services) is coordinated while others operate independently.

# Standardized Intake Feasibility Analysis

An evaluation of the feasibility of creating a 'Core Eligibility Pack' to satisfy the minimum requirements for the Department of Social Services (DSS), Virginia Career Works (VCW), and the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) reveals that it is partially feasible but with significant limitations. A standardized pack could streamline the initial intake process by gathering commonly required documents. This core pack could feasibly include: a government-issued photo ID, proof of residence, Social Security cards (or acceptable alternatives), birth certificates, proof of income (such as pay stubs or benefit letters), a recent tax return, details on household composition, and relevant immigration or citizenship documents. This collection would cover the baseline identity, residency, and income verification needs for DSS programs like SNAP and TANF, and many of the eligibility verifications for VCW's WIOA services. However, this pack would not simultaneously satisfy all minimum requirements due to agency-specific forms and differing verification standards. The primary challenges are: 1) RRHA requires the specific HUD-9886 'Authorization for Release of Information' form, which would not be part of a generic pack. 2) RRHA has a stricter verification hierarchy, prioritizing third-party verification and only allowing self-certification (often requiring notarization) when other methods are unavailable. This contrasts with Virginia Career Works, which is authorized to use more flexible methods like self-attestation and virtual verification for WIOA eligibility. Therefore, while a Core Eligibility Pack could significantly reduce redundant paperwork for clients, it would not eliminate the need for agency-specific forms and processes. Full cross-agency acceptance would necessitate formal Memorandums of Understanding (MOUs) and policy alignment on verification standards and data sharing.

# Digital Divide For Reentry Population

While specific data on smartphone access and device stability for Richmond's reentry population was not found, broader research indicates that individuals returning from incarceration face significant challenges, often termed 'supercharged digital exclusion.' This population relies heavily on cell phones for connectivity, but this access is frequently inconsistent and unstable. Research highlights that returning citizens struggle with the affordability of devices and service plans, a lack of digital skills, and interruptions in service, which are common among low-income users. This reliance on 'mobile-only' access can also be limiting, as it leads to less diverse use of the internet and can restrict the ability to complete complex tasks required for accessing services or employment. The instability caused by device loss, high costs, and limited data plans undermines the effectiveness of digital coordination tools that presume consistent access. Therefore, it should be expected that the reentry population in Richmond experiences inconsistent device and service stability. Any digital coordination strategy should be designed to be mobile-first, low-bandwidth, and offline-capable, and should ideally be paired with supports such as device/plan assistance and digital navigation services to be effective.

# Agency Data Sharing Policies

Data-sharing agreements and privacy policies among Richmond and Virginia agencies are fragmented and context-dependent. For homeless services, the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (CoC) utilizes a centralized Homeless Management Information System (HMIS) with a coordinated entry policy and standard privacy notices and Release of Information (ROI) forms for data sharing among its partner agencies. Outside of this specific sector, a more siloed approach prevails, where each agency maintains its own consent and privacy protocols. For instance, the Richmond Department of Social Services (DSS) states that applicant information is confidential and will only be shared with persons directly connected to program administration. Similarly, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) requires applicants to sign specific federal (HUD-9886) and internal authorization forms. A formal data-sharing agreement exists between the Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC) and the Department of Medical Assistance Services (DMAS) to coordinate Medicaid coverage for individuals during and after incarceration, ensuring continuity of care. Regarding immigrant communities, while the Richmond Police Department has a policy of not entering into 287(g) agreements with ICE, the Legal Aid Justice Center notes that 32 active state and local government contracts with ICE exist across Virginia. This broader landscape of state-level collaboration can create significant 'chilling effects,' where fear of data being shared with immigration enforcement may deter undocumented residents and mixed-status families from seeking necessary services, even from agencies with protective policies.

# Identified Data And Service Gaps

The research identified several significant data and service coordination gaps in Richmond's social safety net:

**Data Gaps:**
*   **Referral Completion Demographics:** The most prominent data gap is the absence of publicly available, Richmond-specific data on service referral completion rates broken down by demographic characteristics such as race and gender, particularly for the reentry population. While state reports show high-level numbers (e.g., a 52% acceptance rate for Recovery Courts statewide), they lack the granularity needed to assess equity in the referral ecosystem.
*   **'Chilling Effect' Metrics:** There is no quantitative data available to measure the specific impact of 'chilling effects' on mixed-status families in Richmond, making it difficult to gauge the extent to which fear of ICE and data sharing deters them from accessing needed services.
*   **Reentry Digital Access Data:** No Richmond-specific data was found on smartphone access, device stability, or digital literacy among returning citizens. Service planning relies on national studies and qualitative evidence rather than local metrics.

**Service & Coordination Gaps:**
*   **Fragmented Consent Management:** A major coordination gap exists in consent management. Outside of the homeless services sector's integrated HMIS, there is no centralized or shared Release of Information (ROI) system. Clients must navigate separate, agency-specific consent forms for DSS, RRHA, and VCW, creating redundancy and administrative burden.
*   **Siloed Eligibility Verification:** While a 'Core Eligibility Pack' could streamline initial document collection, its utility is limited by the lack of formal inter-agency agreements (MOUs) to accept shared documentation and align verification processes. Agencies like RRHA still require their own specific forms (HUD-9886) and have stricter verification standards, preventing a true 'tell us once' system.
*   **Digital Equity Support:** The reliance on digital tools for service coordination is undermined by the 'supercharged digital exclusion' faced by reentry residents. There is a service gap in providing dedicated support, such as access to affordable devices and data plans, and digital navigation assistance to ensure these tools are effective and equitable.
