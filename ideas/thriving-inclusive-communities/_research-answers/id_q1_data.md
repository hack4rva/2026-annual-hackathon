# Executive Summary

In Richmond, Virginia, the immigrant and refugee service landscape is supported by several platforms, with Help1RVA (powered by findhelp) being a key resource listing over 1,800 local programs. While findhelp aims to review program data every six months and processes updates daily, the specific percentage of listings that are actively 'claimed' and verified by providers in Richmond is not publicly available. Programmatic access to these listings is not available via a free, public API; instead, it is offered through findhelp's suite of REST APIs for paying customers and partners. There is a demonstrable qualitative overlap of major immigrant-serving organizations, such as Commonwealth Catholic Charities, across Help1RVA, 211 Virginia, and the Unite Virginia network, but no quantitative analysis of this overlap is published. Demographically, Richmond's population is approximately 9-10% foreign-born, and 12.1% of residents speak a language other than English at home. Regionally, immigrants in Central Virginia are predominantly from Asia and Latin America, with India being the most common country of birth. A significant digital divide impacts immigrant-heavy neighborhoods, where residents likely have a high dependency on smartphones for internet access, though granular neighborhood-level data on this is scarce. Finally, the research found no evidence of sustained, city-built anonymous immigrant service directories in other U.S. cities, suggesting that most municipalities partner with established platforms rather than creating and maintaining bespoke systems.

# Digital Divide In Richmond

The City of Richmond acknowledges a significant digital divide among its residents and is actively taking steps to address it. In September 2024, the Richmond City Council unanimously approved a resolution to advance a Digital Equity Assessment and Implementation Plan. The primary goal of this plan is to bridge the connectivity gap and provide high-speed gigabit internet access to all residents across the city. This initiative underscores the city's recognition that reliable internet is a crucial utility. Broader research from the Richmond Fed highlights a key aspect of this divide, noting that individuals who depend solely on smartphones for internet access are often from low-income households or households of color. This trend is a critical consideration for the city's equity plan, as it aims to move residents from limited, mobile-only access to more robust, high-speed home connections.

## Affordability Issues

The city's focus on bridging the digital divide implies that affordability is a significant barrier for many residents, including those in immigrant communities. Reliance on smartphone-only access is often linked to the use of prepaid data plans, which can be more expensive per gigabyte and less reliable than contract-based home broadband. Dependency on free public Wi-Fi at locations like libraries or community centers is also a common strategy for those facing affordability challenges, though it offers inconsistent and often insecure connectivity.

## Related City Initiatives

The City of Richmond is actively working to address these issues through its Digital Equity Assessment and Implementation Plan. A resolution unanimously approved by the City Council aims to develop a strategy to provide high-speed gigabit internet access to all residents. This initiative directly targets the connectivity gaps experienced in neighborhoods like Southwood and the East End, with the goal of improving affordability, reliability, and access for underserved populations.


# Case Studies Of Immigrant Service Directories

Based on a review of available information, no authoritative, evaluative literature or official city documentation was found that catalogs specific U.S. city-run, anonymous immigrant-service directories and analyzes which have been sustained beyond a pilot phase versus which have failed. The research did not identify any specific case studies to compare.

However, the findings suggest a broader trend: many cities opt to maintain general service directories by partnering with established platforms like Findhelp (formerly Aunt Bertha), 211, or Unite Us, rather than building a bespoke, anonymous, immigrant-only directory. This approach leverages existing infrastructure and networks.

While no specific comparative analysis of anonymous immigrant directories was found, the research provides general factors that influence the persistence of service directories. Initiatives that are integrated with established platforms and benefit from strong community partnerships and a clear, sustained funding and governance model tend to persist. Conversely, bespoke pilot projects that lack sustained funding or a long-term governance structure frequently cease to operate after the initial phase.

# Virginia 211 Overview

211 Virginia is a comprehensive information and referral service that provides listings for immigrant and refugee services across the state. The directory includes major providers such as Commonwealth Catholic Charities (CCC), which offers Refugee Resettlement Services in the Richmond/Henrico area. Through 211 Virginia, individuals can find detailed information on services including orientation, employment assistance, case management, English as a Second Language (ESL) classes, school liaison services, and housing support for refugees. This demonstrates a clear overlap with other service directories, as major immigrant-serving organizations are present across multiple platforms.

# City Of Richmond Immigrant Support

## Office Name

City of Richmond Department of Social Services (DSS) – ESI Refugee Assistance

## Key Services

The City of Richmond's DSS provides refugee assistance, making refugees eligible to apply for the same assistance programs as United States citizens. This is supplemented by a network of community partners accessible through platforms like Help1RVA, which is led locally by the YMCA of Greater Richmond. The YMCA provides Social Needs Navigators to help individuals connect with over 1,800 local programs. Key nonprofit providers like Commonwealth Catholic Charities (CCC) offer specialized services including legal assistance for immigrants and refugees, as well as refugee resettlement services covering orientation, employment, and case management.

## Language Access Policy

The city's support ecosystem includes language access provisions. According to U.S. Census data (2020-2024), 12.1% of Richmond residents speak a language other than English at home. Regional analysis by The Commonwealth Institute indicates that Central Virginia's immigrant population is predominantly from Asia and Latin America, with India being the most common country of birth. To meet these needs, the YMCA’s Social Need Navigators for the Help1RVA platform speak multiple languages and can connect users to a translation service to ensure non-English speakers can access resources.

## Contact Information

To apply for refugee assistance benefits through the City of Richmond DSS, individuals can call: (855) 635-4370. For assistance navigating social services through the Help1RVA platform, individuals can visit the YMCA of Greater Richmond's website: https://www.ymcarichmond.org/resources/help1rva/.


# Key Challenges And Gaps

A primary challenge in understanding and improving service delivery for immigrants in Richmond is the prevalence of significant data gaps. Publicly available metrics are lacking for the percentage of 'claimed' versus 'unclaimed' service listings on Help1RVA, which makes it difficult to assess the accuracy and currency of the information provided. Similarly, there are no published quantitative counts of the overlap between the Help1RVA, 211 Virginia, and Unite Virginia platforms, obscuring the true level of redundancy or fragmentation in the service ecosystem. Detailed demographic data, such as the top non-English/Spanish languages spoken city-wide, requires direct analysis of complex census tables and is not readily available. Another major gap is the lack of granular, neighborhood-level data on the digital connectivity profiles of immigrant-heavy areas like Southwood and the East End, which is critical for designing accessible digital services. The digital divide is a recognized issue, with evidence suggesting a high reliance on smartphone-only internet access among low-income and minority households, which can limit access to services not optimized for mobile. Furthermore, the lack of a free, public API for Help1RVA's listings prevents community organizations and developers from creating tailored, potentially anonymous, tools for immigrants. Access is restricted to findhelp's paying partners, creating a barrier to innovation. Finally, the research identified a lack of documented case studies or best practices for successful, sustained, city-run anonymous immigrant service directories, meaning any such initiative in Richmond would face challenges in funding and governance without a proven model to follow.
