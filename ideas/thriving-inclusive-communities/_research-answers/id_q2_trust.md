# Executive Summary

Immigrant communities in Richmond, Virginia, primarily rely on trusted, informal, and community-based channels for communication and service discovery. WhatsApp is noted as 'incredibly popular,' and Spanish-language radio, such as Ultra Radio's 'RVA Latinas Show,' serves as a vital bridge to resources. Service navigation heavily depends on word-of-mouth, faith networks like the Catholic Church, and community navigators, a need highlighted by a statewide study. Key barriers persist, including a significant 'trust deficit' with government entities, particularly among those with uncertain legal status, and the lack of a centralized, multilingual resource directory. The City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant & Refugee Engagement (OIRE) functions as a crucial one-stop liaison, providing direct navigation services, legal clinics, and language access support, and facilitating a network of partner organizations to improve cross-referrals. However, the system remains fragmented, with specific recommendations from a VCU/ONA study pointing to the need for a statewide multilingual hotline and other accessible information tools to better serve all residents, including those with limited literacy.

# Barriers For Vulnerable Communities

Immigrant communities in the Richmond area, particularly those with precarious legal situations such as undocumented individuals and asylum seekers, often avoid using tools or systems that have a direct connection to government infrastructure. A comprehensive 2022 study by Virginia Commonwealth University (VCU) and the Virginia Department of Social Services' Office of New Americans (ONA) identified a significant 'trust deficit' between these communities and government entities. The report explicitly states that 'uncertain legal status' acts as a major barrier to civic integration and service access. This fear is a primary driver, as 'Many immigrants… fear contact with the government because of uncertain legal status'. This apprehension, which can be compounded by misinformation or negative prior experiences with authorities in their home countries, leads to a deliberate avoidance of official channels and government-linked digital tools, even if those tools are designed to provide essential aid. Therefore, while not a categorical rule for every individual, there is substantial evidence supporting the conclusion that a portion of Richmond's immigrant population with uncertain legal status will actively avoid services that appear to be operated by or affiliated with any level of government.

# Service Navigation For Limited Literacy

Residents in Richmond with limited literacy, regardless of their native language, primarily navigate service discovery through verbal and interpersonal channels rather than text-based or digital ones. The 2022 VCU/ONA study on barriers to immigrant integration explicitly recommends the establishment of a 'toll-free multilingual hotline' for the very reason that 'some immigrants do not have access to technology and/or a sufficient literacy level to navigate an online repository of services.' This highlights a fundamental reliance on spoken communication. The report further emphasizes that information about crucial programs, such as ESL classes, is 'often hear about... through word of mouth.' This informal, community-based information sharing is a primary pathway. Another critical component is the role of 'community navigators.' Service providers in the Richmond metro area noted that 'system navigation... can be a barrier... if you don't have someone to share that knowledge with you,' underscoring the need for hands-on, person-to-person guidance. Locally, institutions have created resources to address this. The City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant & Refugee Engagement (OIRE) provides free interpretation for accessing city services, and Richmond Public Schools (RPS) offers a 'Family Language Line' and employs a bilingual ombudsman and social worker to help families navigate resources and get referrals verbally.

# Findhello App Discontinuation Analysis

## Status

## Reasoning

USAHello has confirmed on its website that the 'FindHello app is no longer available.' The organization did not provide a detailed technical or financial post-mortem for the decision. The official statement simply notes that the choice to sunset the app 'was not an easy decision, but we believe it is the responsible one,' suggesting that continuing to maintain the app was no longer sustainable or the best use of resources.

## Recommended Alternatives

In place of the FindHello app, USAHello now directs users to several alternative pathways for finding local support. These include calling or visiting 211.org, using the FindHelp.org platform, checking local government websites (specifically mentioning Offices of New Americans), and visiting local public libraries for assistance.

## Lessons Learned

The primary lesson inferred from the discontinuation of FindHello is the significant challenge and resource-intensive nature of creating and maintaining a standalone, accurate service directory. By recommending established, general-purpose platforms like 211.org and FindHelp.org, USAHello implicitly suggests that leveraging these larger, existing infrastructures is a more sustainable and responsible approach than attempting to build and support a parallel, specialized system.

# Government Trust Deficit Details

A detailed analysis of the relationship between immigrant communities and government entities in Virginia, including the Richmond area, reveals a significant 'trust deficit,' as documented in the 2022 study conducted by VCU and the Office of New Americans (ONA). This deficit is a major barrier that 'government officials must overcome' to effectively serve these populations. The primary root of this mistrust is fear tied directly to legal status. The report states, 'Many immigrants… fear contact with the government because of uncertain legal status.' This fear is not just a vague anxiety; it is a powerful motivator that leads individuals and families to avoid any interaction with official bodies, which can be perceived as a risk of detention or deportation. This apprehension can be exacerbated by misinformation circulating within communities and by traumatic prior experiences with government authorities in their countries of origin. The impact of this trust deficit is profound, creating a substantial barrier to civic integration and access to essential services. Even when beneficial programs like healthcare, legal aid, or housing assistance are available through government or government-linked organizations, the fear of providing personal information to an official system prevents many from applying. This avoidance forces them to rely solely on a smaller, often over-burdened, network of non-profit organizations, faith-based groups, and informal community support, limiting their access to the full spectrum of available resources.

# Challenges In Service Information Access

A primary systemic challenge immigrants face in Virginia, including the Richmond metro area, is the 'lack of a central place/resource to learn about services available,' as identified in a 2022 study by VCU and the Virginia Office of New Americans (ONA). This fragmentation means that service discovery is often ad-hoc, relying on word-of-mouth or the assistance of navigators. The study highlights a significant 'need for navigators' because system navigation itself is a barrier without someone to share knowledge. This is compounded by a 'trust deficit' with government, which makes some immigrants, particularly those with uncertain legal status, fearful of contact with official or government-linked systems. Furthermore, the study explicitly notes that an online-only repository is insufficient, as 'some immigrants do not have access to technology and/or a sufficient literacy level to navigate an online repository,' underscoring the need for a trusted, centralized, and multilingual directory that includes non-digital access points.

# Statewide Integration Study Recommendations

## Study Title

Barriers to Integration of Virginia’s Immigrants

## Conducting Organizations

VCU (Virginia Commonwealth University) and the Virginia Department of Social Services (VDSS) Office of New Americans (ONA)

## Recommendation Summary

The study's key recommendation to address information access barriers is to establish a centralized, multilingual information system that is accessible to immigrants with varying levels of technological access and literacy.

## Recommendation Details

To implement its central recommendation, the study provides specific suggestions. It proposes the establishment of a 'toll-free multilingual hotline' specifically because 'some immigrants do not have access to technology and/or a sufficient literacy level to navigate an online repository of services.' Additionally, to counter misinformation and reach communities through existing channels, the report recommends creating short, multilingual orientation videos. It suggests that 'short excerpts of such videos could be used as public service announcements on Spanish and other language television and radio and be shared through social media.'
