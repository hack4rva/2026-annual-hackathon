# Project Analysis Summary

A Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) analysis for the 'circle-trust' project in Richmond, VA, identifies a critical need to streamline cross-agency service navigation for residents seeking housing, workforce, and reentry support. The analysis reveals significant functional, emotional, and systemic challenges. Functionally, residents face duplicative intakes, confusing eligibility criteria, and logistical burdens when interacting with multiple agencies like the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA), the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB), and the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC). Emotionally, residents, especially vulnerable populations like returning citizens, experience stigma, fear of data misuse, and the trauma of repeatedly recounting their stories. From a systems perspective, front-line staff at these agencies struggle with fragmented systems, a lack of feedback on referrals, and inefficient communication, leading to poor outcomes. The 'circle-trust' project is designed to address these interconnected problems by creating a trust-first, single-story intake process facilitated by vetted intermediaries, aiming to improve referral outcomes and align with the city's equity goals.

# Functional Job To Be Done

## Situation

Trying to secure housing and job training after a recent eviction risk or job loss.

## Persona

A Richmond resident

## Motivation

To have a trusted person capture my information once and coordinate referrals across Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) resident services, the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) career services, and the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) homeless system.

## Expected Outcome

To quickly get stable housing, income, and benefits without the need to repeatedly tell my story to different agencies.

## Current Workaround

Calling the Homeless Connection Line, applying separately through RRHA's online portals, physically visiting OCWB career stations, and searching through online directories like findhelp.org, which involves retelling personal history at each intake point.

## Core Pain Point

Experiencing duplicative intake processes, confusion over eligibility requirements for different programs, referrals that lead to dead ends or drop-offs, and significant time and transportation burdens associated with interacting with multiple, separate agencies.


# Emotional Trust Job To Be Done

## Situation

Reentering the community under supervision after a period of incarceration.

## Persona

A returning citizen in Richmond

## Motivation

To work with a credible navigator, potentially embedded with an agency like OCWB, RRHA, or a reentry provider, who will protect my privacy and advocate for me across different agencies.

## Expected Outcome

To feel respected and safe while sharing sensitive personal details only once, and to be confident that following through with the process will lead to tangible, real help.

## Current Workaround

Relying on probation or parole officers, ad hoc case managers, or community organizations such as OAR of Richmond, each of whom possesses only partial information and with whom the level of trust may vary.

## Core Pain Point

Facing stigma, fearing the misuse of personal data, experiencing the trauma of repeatedly recounting past events, and feeling uncertain that referrals will actually result in receiving services.


# Systems Coordination Job To Be Done

## Situation

Attempting to connect a resident with services from other agencies while working at a social service organization.

## Persona

A front-line staff member at OCWB, RRHA, or a GRCoC partner organization

## Motivation

To have a trusted, closed-loop cross-agency referral process that includes clear client consent protocols and visibility into the status of referrals.

## Expected Outcome

To reduce the number of client no-shows, avoid conducting redundant assessments, and be able to coordinate care effectively to achieve measurable outcomes for residents.

## Current Workaround

Using a combination of email and phone calls for 'warm handoffs', tracking information in spreadsheets, navigating separate agency portals (for RRHA, OCWB, workforce centers), and using disparate platforms like Unite Virginia and findhelp that do not share a common client context.

## Core Pain Point

Dealing with fragmented systems, unclear eligibility criteria and referral routing pathways, and having limited or no feedback loops to confirm the results of a referral.


# Data Questions

## Question

What minimum data set and consent language satisfy Virginia social services confidentiality requirements while enabling cross-agency sharing for navigation?

## Relevance

This is critical for legal compliance, as the project must adhere to Virginia's specific confidentiality statutes for social services data (e.g., § 63.2-105). Getting this right is fundamental to establishing the data-sharing Memorandums of Understanding (MOUs) between agencies like OCWB, RRHA, and non-profits in the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC).

## Question

Can circle-trust integrate with existing networks (e.g., Unite Virginia) while maintaining local consent standards and avoiding duplicate records?

## Relevance

Unite Virginia is an established statewide platform. The project needs to determine if it can leverage this existing infrastructure without compromising its core principle of a trust-first, locally-managed consent process. Avoiding record duplication is essential to fulfilling the 'tell your story once' promise and preventing further fragmentation of resident data.

## Question

What are the rules of engagement and audit trails for sharing intake notes between city units (OCWB, OHS), RRHA, VADOC community supervision, and non-profits in the GRCoC?

## Relevance

This question addresses the practical governance needed to build trust among diverse partners. Establishing clear protocols and auditable records for data access is essential for agencies like the Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC), which handles sensitive reentry information, to participate alongside city departments (OCWB), the housing authority (RRHA), and community non-profits.


# User Questions

## Question

Which navigator roles in Richmond are best positioned as ‘trusted intermediaries’ (OCWB career coaches, OHS Resource Center navigators, RRHA Resident Services Coordinators, or reentry case managers)?

## Relevance

Identifying the correct intermediaries is the foundational element of the 'circle-trust' model. The project's success is contingent on residents perceiving these navigators as trustworthy advocates. This is critical for user adoption, especially within a community that may have historical reasons to distrust institutional systems.

## Question

What are residents’ pain points at each entry point (Homeless Connection Line, RRHA portals, OCWB stations), and where is a single-intake model most impactful?

## Relevance

A detailed understanding of the specific frustrations and bottlenecks residents currently experience is essential for designing a solution that offers tangible value. Pinpointing where a single-intake process can alleviate the most significant burdens will be key to demonstrating the project's effectiveness and encouraging its use.

## Question

What languages, accessibility needs, and trauma-informed practices are required for consent and storytelling in Richmond neighborhoods most affected by housing instability and justice involvement?

## Relevance

For the system to be truly inclusive and effective, it must be accessible to all residents, including those with disabilities, limited English proficiency, or past trauma. This question is critical for ensuring the process of sharing one's story is safe, respectful, and empowering, which is essential for building and maintaining trust.

## Question

What minimum data set and consent language satisfy Virginia social services confidentiality requirements while enabling cross-agency sharing for navigation?

## Relevance

The project's legal and functional viability depends on a clear, user-friendly consent process that complies with Virginia's strict confidentiality laws (§ 63.2-105). An improper or confusing consent process could lead to legal challenges and, more importantly, destroy resident trust, undermining the entire 'tell your story once' premise.

## Question

How do OCWB’s cohort-based workforce pathways and the City’s Community Resource & Training Center “one-stop” inform navigator placement and operating hours?

## Relevance

To be useful, the service must be physically and temporally accessible to the residents who need it. This question is critical for ensuring that navigators are available at the times and locations that align with residents' existing patterns of seeking help, thereby lowering barriers to access and increasing adoption.


# Integration Questions

## Question

How will circle-trust align with the GRCoC coordinated entry (Homeward/HCL) without duplicating diversion/assessment?

## Relevance

Homeward's Homeless Connection Line (HCL) is the established front door for homeless services in the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC). To be effective and not create confusion, circle-trust must integrate its workflow with the HCL's existing diversion and assessment processes, ensuring it complements rather than duplicates this critical community resource.

## Question

What is the lightest-weight interface to RRHA’s applicant/resident portals and OCWB’s workflows for closed-loop status updates?

## Relevance

Both the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) and the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) have their own digital systems for residents and clients. For circle-trust to enable true closed-loop referrals, it needs a practical, low-burden method to connect with these systems to send referrals and receive status updates, ensuring navigators know the outcome of their efforts without requiring a costly overhaul of existing agency technology.

## Question

How can navigators view referral outcomes from workforce partners (CRWDB centers, OCWB cohorts) and reentry supports (VADOC, Sheriff programs) in one place?

## Relevance

The project's value proposition is a unified view for navigators. This requires technical and procedural integration with a wide array of partners, including the Capital Region Workforce Development Board (CRWDB), OCWB's training programs, and reentry services from the Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC) and Richmond Sheriff. A single interface is essential for a navigator to effectively coordinate care across the housing, workforce, and reentry sectors.


# Equity Questions

## Question

How will circle-trust advance Richmond’s Equity Agenda (reentry support, workforce pathways) and measure reductions in disparate outcomes by race and neighborhood?

## Relevance

This question is critical for ensuring the project serves as a tool for social justice, not just a technical fix. Aligning with and measuring contributions to the city's Equity Agenda is essential for securing community and political support and proving that the project actively reduces systemic inequities in service access and outcomes.

## Question

What safeguards ensure residents control data visibility across agencies and can revoke consent without losing access to help?

## Relevance

For communities that have been historically marginalized or over-surveilled, empowering residents with granular control over their personal data is a fundamental equity issue. Providing the ability to revoke consent at any time without penalty is crucial for building trust and ensuring the solution does not become another tool of disempowerment.

## Question

How will the project resource community-based org participation (time, training, language services) so smaller, culturally specific providers are not excluded?

## Relevance

True equity requires ensuring that the organizations best positioned to serve specific communities—often smaller, grassroots, and culturally specific non-profits—can participate. This question addresses the need to create an inclusive ecosystem, preventing the project from inadvertently favoring larger organizations and ensuring it reaches the most marginalized residents.


# Prior Art Questions

## Question

What lessons from Unite Virginia’s closed-loop referrals and from findhelp usage in Richmond should shape circle-trust’s data model and handoff protocols?

## Relevance

Unite Virginia and findhelp.org are platforms already active in the Richmond service ecosystem. Analyzing their adoption, successes (like Unite Virginia's closed-loop model), and limitations is critical for circle-trust to design a data model and referral protocol that provides unique value and avoids the pitfalls that may have limited the impact of these existing tools.

## Question

How do OCWB’s cohort-based workforce pathways and the City’s Community Resource & Training Center “one-stop” inform navigator placement and operating hours?

## Relevance

The Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) and the city's new 'one-stop' center represent successful, on-the-ground service delivery models in Richmond. Understanding their strategies for navigator placement, client engagement, and physical accessibility is crucial for designing the circle-trust operational model to meet residents where they are and integrate with proven local approaches.

## Question

Where have similar “tell your story once” pilots succeeded or failed in Virginia localities (data-sharing MOUs, navigator training, consent UX), and who should we consult locally (Homeward, RRHA, OCWB, CRWDB, OAR, VADOC, Sheriff)?

## Relevance

This question is vital for risk mitigation. By studying analogous projects in other Virginia localities, the project can learn about common obstacles, such as executing data-sharing MOUs or designing effective consent processes. Consulting with the listed key Richmond stakeholders (Homeward, RRHA, OCWB, etc.) is essential to ground these lessons in the specific political and operational context of the city.
