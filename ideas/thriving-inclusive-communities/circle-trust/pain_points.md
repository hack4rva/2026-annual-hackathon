# Executive Summary

Richmond, VA residents navigating cross-agency services for housing, workforce, and reentry support face critical pain points that create significant barriers to stability. These include fragmented, siloed intake processes that require individuals to repeatedly tell their stories to different agencies; long wait times and access chokepoints, such as the Homeless Connection Line (HCL); and referral pathways that lead to dead ends or drop-offs. For vulnerable populations like returning citizens, these functional hurdles are compounded by emotional burdens, including fear of data misuse, stigma, and the trauma of recounting past events. Systemically, front-line staff are hampered by capacity shortages, a lack of feedback on referrals, and administrative friction from navigating bespoke eligibility rules for each program. The financial and time costs of transportation to multiple service providers further exacerbates these challenges for low-income residents. The circle-trust project aims to address these interconnected problems by creating a trust-first, single-story intake process. This system, facilitated by vetted intermediaries, is designed to streamline service navigation, reduce duplicative data collection, provide transparent referral tracking, and build trust with residents, ultimately improving service delivery outcomes and aligning with the city's equity goals.

# Project Context Circle Trust

The circle-trust project is designed as a systemic intervention to address the fragmented and burdensome process of accessing social services in Richmond, VA. Its primary goal is to create a trust-first, single-story intake process that streamlines navigation for residents and improves referral outcomes across housing, workforce, and reentry support agencies. The project's design is a direct response to identified pain points: functionally, it seeks to eliminate duplicative intakes and confusion over siloed eligibility criteria; emotionally, it aims to build trust and safety for vulnerable residents, like returning citizens, by protecting their privacy and reducing the trauma of repeatedly sharing sensitive information. The proposed solution involves using vetted intermediaries to capture a resident's information once, which can then be used for consented, privacy-preserving referrals to multiple agencies. Key features include building referral feedback loops and status tracking visible to both clients and navigators to prevent drop-offs, integrating transportation supports to mitigate logistical barriers, and focusing on high-friction chokepoints in the current system, such as access to the Homeless Connection Line and after-hours shelter intakes. By doing so, the project aims to reduce administrative burdens on agency staff, increase system throughput, and ultimately provide residents with a more dignified and effective path to stability.

# Functional Job Persona Analysis

The 'Functional Job' persona represents a Richmond resident who is in a precarious situation, trying to secure stable housing and job training following a recent job loss or the threat of eviction. Their primary motivation is to find a streamlined and trustworthy process where their personal information is captured only once by a trusted individual who can then coordinate referrals across various key agencies, including the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) for resident services, the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB) for career services, and the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) for homeless services. The ultimate expected outcome for this individual is to quickly and efficiently obtain stable housing, a steady income, and necessary benefits without the emotionally draining and time-consuming burden of repeatedly telling their personal story to different agencies and navigating multiple, disconnected systems.

# Functional Job Pain Points

## Pain Point

Long waits and limited access via the Homeless Connection Line (HCL)

## Evidence

The City of Richmond's CAPER report notes that due to bed shortages, funding limits, and staffing challenges, there are longer wait times to access emergency beds through the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care’s (GRCoC) Homeless Connection Line. This results in families facing turn-aways and being placed on waitlists for shelter and other resources.

## Impact On User

Inability to access immediate emergency shelter, leading to prolonged instability, being placed on long waitlists, or being turned away entirely from critical services.

## Pain Point

Fragmented intakes and siloed eligibility pathways

## Evidence

The Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority’s (RRHA) Admissions & Continued Occupancy Policy shows that a homelessness preference can only be accessed through a specific referral agreement with the Richmond Behavioral Health Authority (RBHA). This exemplifies a program-specific, agency-gated intake process rather than a single, unified system.

## Impact On User

Causes significant confusion and administrative burden as residents must navigate multiple, non-unified intake processes. This increases the risk of failing to access available benefits due to not knowing the specific, complex referral pathways required by each agency.

## Pain Point

Referral drop-offs and operational gaps

## Evidence

Community insights from a 2023 report on homelessness in Greater Richmond highlight 'gaps in shelter intakes,' where beds remain empty overnight if staff are not available to process intakes. There are also tradeoffs between staffing for health/safety versus case management, which negatively affects client throughput and successful exits from the system.

## Impact On User

Receiving referrals that lead to dead ends or closed doors. Available resources like shelter beds go unused due to logistical or staffing gaps, which delays access to critical services and prolongs the user's state of housing instability.

## Pain Point

Insufficient resources and prioritization constraints

## Evidence

Materials from the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) explicitly state there are 'not enough resources to serve everyone who is eligible.' The coordinated entry system is consequently forced to 'say no a lot' and prioritize the most severe cases for its limited slots.

## Impact On User

Even if a resident is eligible for a program, they may be denied services due to resource scarcity and strict prioritization rules. This leads to being turned away and left without support, fostering a sense of hopelessness.

## Pain Point

Significant transportation burdens to reach multiple service providers

## Evidence

A regional micro-transit study documents first/last-mile barriers and high per-trip costs (e.g., $6+ fares for some paratransit options). Furthermore, a zero-fare transit report shows 85% of local bus riders lack a car and 53% lack a driver’s license, highlighting their vulnerability to transportation challenges when trying to navigate services spread across the city.

## Impact On User

Incurring significant financial costs and losing valuable time traveling between multiple, geographically dispersed service providers. This is a major barrier for low-income individuals who often lack personal vehicles and must rely on costly or inconvenient public transit.


# Functional Job Workarounds And Costs

For a Richmond resident seeking housing and employment support, the current system breaks down at several key points. These include chokepoints at centralized phone intakes like the Homeless Connection Line (HCL), which experiences long wait times; overall capacity shortages for services; and agency-specific eligibility and referral gates, such as the exclusive RRHA-RBHA pathway for a homelessness preference, which are not transparent to the public. Furthermore, inconsistent intake staffing can lead to available shelter beds remaining unused. To navigate this broken system, residents resort to various workarounds. They call multiple hotlines (HCL, Eviction Help Line, 2-1-1), physically visit walk-in centers like the Community Resource & Training Center for 'warm referrals,' and search online directories. While city navigators attempt to help, the underlying fragmented, multi-agency intake processes persist. The cost of this status quo is immense. It leads to prolonged homelessness and housing instability, forces residents to repeatedly recount traumatic stories to different agencies, and imposes significant time and financial costs for multiple trips. In a city with a high eviction rate, this inefficient system increases the risk of eviction and further economic harm for its most vulnerable residents.

# Emotional Trust Job Persona Analysis

The 'Emotional Trust Job' persona is a returning citizen in Richmond who is reentering the community under supervision after a period of incarceration. Their primary motivation is to work with a credible navigator, who might be embedded with an agency like the Office of Community Wealth Building (OCWB), Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA), or a specific reentry provider. This navigator is seen as essential to protect their privacy and advocate for them across different service agencies. The expected outcome for this individual is to feel respected and safe while sharing sensitive personal details, with the assurance that this information will be shared only once. Ultimately, they need to be confident that engaging with the process will lead to tangible, real help, rather than bureaucratic dead ends.

# Emotional Trust Job Pain Points

## Pain Point

Fear of dead-ends and slow connections across systems.

## Evidence

A state homeless program report flags a lack of cross-system referrals when healthcare providers identify housing as a top need, which is indicative of broader cross-system gaps that also affect justice-involved individuals. [VA Homeless Report]

## Emotional Impact

Uncertainty that referrals will actually result in receiving services, leading to a feeling of being lost in the system and that the effort will not lead to tangible help.

## Pain Point

Significant housing access constraints for justice-involved populations.

## Evidence

State reentry and corrections materials emphasize ongoing housing barriers for subgroups (e.g., veterans with complex needs) and the limited availability of long-term options. [VA Reentry Status; VADOC Veterans Roadmap]

## Emotional Impact

Facing stigma and systemic barriers to the fundamental need for stable housing, which compounds the stress and difficulty of successful reentry.

## Pain Point

Employment instability and fragile outcomes post-release.

## Evidence

JLARC reports show that only about one-third of people released between April 2022 and December 2023 maintained employment across all four post-release quarters, underscoring the fragile outcomes when navigation and support systems are fragmented. [JLARC]

## Emotional Impact

Experiencing deep uncertainty about future stability and feeling that the system is not designed to support long-term success, increasing the risk of failure.

## Pain Point

Trust and privacy concerns amid data sharing initiatives.

## Evidence

The Commonwealth's creation of a 'Data Trust' with a stated emphasis on confidentiality reflects the high sensitivity around information use and the need for credible intermediaries to build trust with this population. [VA Reentry Status]

## Emotional Impact

Fearing the misuse of personal data and experiencing the trauma of repeatedly recounting past events and sensitive information to different agencies.


# Emotional Trust Job Workarounds And Costs

For returning citizens, the current system breaks down due to significant cross-agency referral and eligibility silos across housing, workforce, and benefits programs. This is compounded by a limited supply of supportive housing and uneven, often non-existent, feedback loops on referrals, leaving individuals uncertain about the status of their applications. As a workaround, they rely on a patchwork of partial supports, including probation or parole officers, the Richmond City Sheriff's Office (RCSO), and various community programs like OAR and faith-based groups. These entities often possess only partial information, and the level of trust can vary significantly. City navigators may provide 'warm referrals,' but these often lack shared-status visibility across agencies. The cost of this status quo is severe: it forces individuals to repeatedly retell traumatic histories to different agencies, causes delays in accessing stable housing and jobs, and ultimately contributes to elevated risks of recidivism and unemployment.

# Systems Coordination Challenges

From the perspective of front-line staff and agencies in Richmond, significant systems coordination challenges severely hamper service delivery. A primary pain point is capacity and staffing constraints; the City of Richmond's CAPER report cites provider staffing shortages, such as for case managers, which directly affects their ability to meet client outcomes. Community insights further reveal that gaps in intake staffing can lead to shelter beds remaining empty overnight, a critical failure in a system with high demand. Another major issue is the existence of fragmented referral processes and siloed eligibility rules. For example, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority’s (RRHA) homelessness preference is only accessible via a specific referral from the Richmond Behavioral Health Authority (RBHA), forcing staff to navigate bespoke, agency-gated pathways that increase administrative friction and require duplicative data collection from clients. This is compounded by a limited supply of affordable housing and insufficient client assistance funding, which prolongs shelter stays and clogs the entire system. Furthermore, agencies recognize that logistical barriers for clients, such as first/last-mile transportation issues and per-trip costs, impede attendance at appointments and worsen referral drop-offs. The status quo is characterized by the absence of a unified, single-story intake system, bottlenecks in phone-based access points like the Homeless Connection Line, and a lack of feedback loops on referrals. The cost of these inefficiencies is high: staff spend valuable time re-collecting client histories, placements are missed, and the overall system throughput is reduced, leading to client churn and staff burnout.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Most Affected Populations

The failures of Richmond's fragmented service system most negatively impact several specific demographic groups. These include justice-involved residents, who are disproportionately Black in Richmond and face compounded barriers upon reentry. Low-income and carless residents are also heavily affected, as they bear the highest coordination and travel costs. Households with children are particularly vulnerable, facing shelter turn-aways and waitlists. Other at-risk groups include marginalized renters who are disproportionately affected by the high local eviction burden, veteran subgroups with complex medical and mental health needs, older adults, and parenting youth who may be excluded when resource scarcity forces prioritization.

## Identified Disparities

The evidence points to several specific disparities within the system. Research from the RVA Eviction Lab highlights a high local eviction burden that disproportionately affects marginalized renters, a finding consistent with national data showing persistent racial and gender disparities in eviction threats. Justice-involved residents, who are disproportionately Black in Richmond, face compounded stigma, significant housing screening barriers, and employment hurdles that impede successful reentry. Furthermore, families with children experience distinct access issues, including being turned away from emergency shelters or placed on waiting lists, as documented in the city's CAPER report. Staffing gaps can even result in family shelter beds remaining unused, exacerbating the disparity in access for this group.

## Systemic Exclusion Factors

Underlying systemic factors contribute directly to these inequities. Transportation access is a major structural barrier; a high dependency on public transit among low-income residents, combined with costs and first/last-mile service gaps, can directly lead to missed appointments and referrals. The Zero-Fare transit report notes that 85% of local bus riders lack vehicle access. Another key factor is systemic resource scarcity, which forces the Continuum of Care to prioritize limited resources. This prioritization can inadvertently exclude vulnerable groups like families, older adults, or parenting youth who may require more robust cross-system links, childcare, or other tailored supports to successfully navigate the system. For returning citizens, systemic stigma manifests as concrete housing screening barriers and employment hurdles that are difficult to overcome without coordinated support.

# Homeless Services Navigation Barriers

## Connection Line Issues

The Homeless Connection Line (HCL), the primary access point for the GRCoC, experiences significant issues. The city's CAPER report notes 'longer wait times to gain access to emergency beds' through the HCL, attributed to bed shortages, funding limits, and staffing challenges. Furthermore, the HCL has limited operating hours (Monday–Friday 8:00 AM–9:00 PM; Saturday and Sunday 1:00 PM–9:00 PM), creating a chokepoint for individuals seeking help outside these times.

## Shelter Access Barriers

Accessing emergency shelter is a major challenge. The GRCoC reports a 'high number of turn-aways for emergency shelter and a waiting list for shelter and other resources for homeless families with minor children.' This problem is compounded by a shortage of affordable housing, which causes longer shelter stays, thus reducing bed availability. Additionally, operational issues, such as intake staff not being available, can lead to beds remaining empty overnight even when there is high demand.

## Coordinated Entry Flaws

The Coordinated Entry system, designed to streamline access, has systemic flaws. It is fragmented, as evidenced by the RRHA's homelessness preference which requires a separate, specific referral from RBHA, bypassing the main system. Due to 'not enough resources to serve everyone who is eligible,' the system is forced to prioritize and deny services frequently. This leads to referral drop-offs and dead ends. A state-level report also flags a 'lack of cross-system referrals,' indicating that even when other systems (like healthcare) identify a housing need, a connection to the homeless services system is not guaranteed.


# Logistical And Access Barriers

## Transportation Barriers

Transportation presents a significant challenge for residents accessing services in Richmond. A regional micro-transit study highlights the burden of 'first/last-mile' barriers, which can prevent individuals from using transit effectively. Furthermore, the cost of transportation is a major hurdle, with user fares for services like GRTC CARE On-Demand starting at $6 per trip and Access Chesterfield trips costing as much as $33. These costs create a substantial financial burden for low-income residents. The issue is compounded by a high dependency on public transit; a report on zero-fare transit found that 85% of local bus riders lack access to a personal vehicle and 53% do not have a driver's license, underscoring their vulnerability to any frictions in transit access.

## Digital Divide Issues

While not explicitly labeled a 'digital divide,' the system's structure creates significant problems for those with limited digital access or literacy. The current workaround for residents involves navigating a fragmented digital landscape, including 'applying separately through RRHA’s online portals' and 'searching through online directories like findhelp.org'. This reliance on multiple, non-integrated online systems requires users to have consistent internet access, digital skills to navigate different websites, and the ability to manage information across various platforms. This de facto requirement for online navigation can act as a barrier for individuals who lack the necessary resources or skills, effectively excluding them from accessing services.

## Time And Cost Burden

The overall burden of time and out-of-pocket costs required to navigate Richmond's service system is substantial. Residents face the direct financial cost of transportation for multiple trips to different agencies, with documented per-trip fares for paratransit options adding up quickly. Beyond monetary costs, there is a significant time burden associated with making these multiple trips and with the need for 'repeated storytelling' at each new agency or intake point. This process is inefficient and emotionally taxing for clients and contributes to referral drop-offs. The cumulative effect is a high cost of the status quo, measured in prolonged instability, lost time, and direct expenses for transportation and potentially childcare, which are borne by the residents least able to afford them.


# Insights From Comparable Cities

## City Name

Louisville

## Observed Challenge

A 2022 analysis of homelessness in Louisville identified several key barriers for individuals attempting to use the shelter system. These included strict entry deadlines, the inability for individuals to stay with their partners, and rules preventing them from keeping their pets.

## Relevance To Richmond

The challenges observed in Louisville provide corroboration for the issues in Richmond. It demonstrates that access rules and operational limitations are common drivers of service drop-offs and barriers in other Continuums of Care (CoCs) as well. This validates that the pain points experienced by Richmond residents are part of a wider pattern of systemic challenges in homeless service delivery across different cities.
