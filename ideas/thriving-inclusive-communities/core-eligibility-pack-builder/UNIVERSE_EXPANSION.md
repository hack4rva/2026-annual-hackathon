# Privacy-First Eligibility Pack Builder: Funding, Partnerships, and Ownership Roadmap for Richmond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Core Eligibility Pack Builder represents a paradigm shift in benefits intake, challenging the assumption that multi-agency navigation requires heavy, server-side infrastructure. However, framing this solely as a Richmond city government project limits its potential and exposes it to severe compliance bottlenecks. Nationwide scans reveal zero production-grade, client-only, multi-program intake tools in U.S. social services, primarily due to strict state public records laws and HIPAA Business Associate Agreement (BAA) requirements. 

To succeed, this idea's universe must expand into a hybrid ownership model funded by federal and state modernization grants. By leveraging up to $5 million in USDA SNAP Process and Technology Improvement Grants [1] or tapping into the Virginia Department of Social Services' FY26-28 Eligibility Modernization budget [2], the project can secure the runway needed for compliance and development. Partnering with national civic tech leaders like Code for America and the Beeck Center will provide the necessary technical assistance and credibility to scale this privacy-first, client-side architecture from a local pilot to a statewide or national standard.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (Nonprofit-Led Resident Layer + State/City Data Enablement)**

The City of Richmond should not own the resident-facing application. A pure "store-nothing" client-side tool directly conflicts with government public records laws and FOIA practices that expect agencies to retrieve and retain records. Instead, ownership should be split:

* **What Partners/Nonprofits Should Own:** A civic tech nonprofit (e.g., Code for America or a local equivalent) should own and maintain the open-source PWA, the client-side PDF generation logic, and the user interface. This shields the city from direct liability regarding ephemeral data processing and allows for agile, cross-jurisdictional scaling.
* **What the City/State Should Own:** The City of Richmond and Virginia state agencies (like DSS) should own the publication of versioned, machine-readable eligibility rules (via CDN) and the standardized intake endpoints (if a consented, ephemeral upload hybrid model is adopted). 
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The hosting of the resident-facing application and the direct generation of the client-side PDFs. City ownership of the app triggers heavy procurement, BAA, and records retention mandates that will crush the MVP.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Server-Based Portals (BenefitsCal / GetCalFresh):** 
 * *What was attempted:* End-to-end server-hosted intake portals.
 * *What worked:* Massive scale and high conversion rates for assisted workflows (95.6% completion with CBO help vs. 60.6% self-service).
 * *What failed/Limitations:* Heavy procurement, strict BAA requirements, and massive server infrastructure costs.
 * *Lesson:* A hybrid model is needed to close the assisted vs. self-service gap without building full back-ends.
* **Code for America Safety Net Innovation Lab:**
 * *What was attempted:* Multi-year initiative partnering with 15 states to transform safety net access [13].
 * *What worked:* Launching mobile-friendly integrated benefits applications (e.g., in Maryland) [14].
 * *Lesson:* State-level partnerships are more effective for eligibility tools than city-level silos.
* **Benefits Data Trust (BDT) Virginia Pilot:**
 * *What was attempted:* Data coordination and matching between WIC, Medicaid, and SNAP in Virginia [17].
 * *What worked:* Establishing Data Sharing Agreements (DSAs) for targeted outreach [23].
 * *Lesson:* Virginia agencies are open to cross-program data coordination if the legal frameworks (DSAs) are properly established.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond project is too small because eligibility rules for SNAP, Medicaid, and major housing vouchers are determined at the state and federal levels, not by the city. 
* **If the frame becomes regional (PlanRVA/Capital Region):** It better aligns with workforce and transit realities, allowing CBOs across county lines to use the tool.
* **If the frame becomes statewide:** The tool can tap into the Virginia DSS Eligibility Modernization budget [2] and serve as a universal front-end for all Virginia residents, drastically increasing the ROI on the rule-bundle maintenance.
* **If a nonprofit owns the resident layer:** The city avoids FOIA and HIPAA liabilities, acting only as a data publisher and pilot site, while the nonprofit scales the open-source tool nationally.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Compliance & Records Retention (Biggest Blocker):** State public records laws expect agencies to retain intake data. A strict "we store nothing" approach collides with these duties. *Mitigation:* Design a hybrid consent-based upload that auto-deletes after verified receipt.
* **HIPAA & BAAs:** Client-side tracking and PHI disclosures are subject to BAAs. *Mitigation:* Ensure zero third-party trackers and keep all PHI strictly local to the device until explicit, consented handoff.
* **Accessibility (WCAG 2.1 AA):** Generating tagged, accessible PDFs client-side is notoriously difficult. *Mitigation:* Allocate specific grant funding (e.g., from SNAP PTIG) to specialized frontend accessibility engineering.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Eligibility rules change frequently. *Mitigation:* Treat eligibility rules as signed, versioned code artifacts delivered via CDN, maintained by a state-level or national civic tech partner, not city IT.
