# Executive Summary

Richmond residents applying for services across the Department of Social Services (DSS), the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA), and Virginia Career Works face a fragmented, repetitive, and burdensome documentation process. The core problem is the lack of a unified system, forcing applicants to navigate three distinct online portals (CommonHelp, RRHA Applicant Portal, Virginia Workforce Connection), each with its own registration process and inconsistent requirements for essential documents like proof of identity, income, and residency. This inefficiency creates a frustrating and time-consuming experience, leading to repetitive data entry, stalled applications due to verification delays or funding issues, and significant administrative burdens for both residents and agency staff. The current system creates notable equity challenges, disproportionately affecting individuals with limited digital literacy, language barriers, or lack of access to technology.

# Resident Applicant Pain Points

## Pain Point

Repetitive Data Entry and Inconsistent Requirements

## Description

Residents are forced to submit similar personal information and documentation to multiple agencies through separate, non-integrated application portals. They must use CommonHelp for DSS benefits, the RRHA Applicant Portal for housing, and the Virginia Workforce Connection (VAWC) for WIOA services. These systems have varying requirements for acceptable documents and verification. For example, WIOA allows self-attestation for many criteria and does not strictly require a Social Security Number (SSN) for services, whereas the RRHA requires original SSN cards for all household members and prefers third-party verification over self-certification. This inconsistency creates confusion and a significant, frustrating burden of repetitive work.

## Current Workaround

Applicants resort to carrying physical 'paper folders' with their documents or keeping photos of documents on their phones to have them ready for different appointments. They also heavily rely on public resources, such as using the free computers, scanners, and notaries at the Richmond Public Library to prepare and submit their applications.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This creates a significant and unnecessary administrative burden for both residents and agency staff. It leads to lost time, increased frustration, and a higher likelihood of errors or incomplete submissions, which can delay access to critical services.

## Evidence

The existence of three distinct portals (CommonHelp, RRHA Applicant Portal, VAWC). The differing policies on SSN collection between WIOA and RRHA. The contrast between the Virginia Poverty Law Center's simplified SNAP checklist and the more complex official Virginia DSS SNAP Manual. The DSS's own acceptance of faxed applications and use of drop boxes points to the reliance on physical documents.

## Pain Point

Stalled Applications and Delays

## Description

Residents frequently encounter significant delays and stalled applications, which introduces uncertainty and can lead to a loss of essential benefits. These delays can stem from internal agency processes, such as the DSS's reliance on computer matching systems for verification which can be slow if discrepancies arise. They can also be caused by external factors, such as a lack of government funding, which can abruptly halt the process for applicants who were on the verge of receiving aid.

## Current Workaround

When an application is stalled due to verification issues, the applicant has a limited time (e.g., 10 days for SNAP) to provide the required documents. When it is stalled due to systemic issues like funding, the primary recourse is to be placed back on a waitlist, leaving residents in a state of uncertainty with no immediate action to take.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The costs are severe, including prolonged periods of uncertainty, acute stress, and significant instability, particularly regarding housing. For example, the suspension of RRHA vouchers left 395 families in a state of housing precarity. Delays in receiving benefits like SNAP can lead to food insecurity.

## Evidence

The RRHA suspended the issuance of Housing Choice Vouchers in May 2025 due to a lack of federal funding, affecting an estimated 395 families. The DSS's SNAP verification process relies on computer matching that can cause delays. Broader systemic issues in Virginia, such as 'paperwork barriers' and long call center wait times during the Medicaid unwinding, indicate this is a widespread problem.

## Pain Point

Systemic Inequity and Accessibility Barriers

## Description

The current application system disproportionately harms residents who face specific barriers, including those with limited English proficiency, low digital literacy, or lack of access to technology. While some resources like a Spanish version of CommonHelp and a City Language Access Plan exist, their effectiveness for all non-English speakers (e.g., Arabic speakers) is a potential data gap. The system's reliance on online portals creates a 'digital divide,' disadvantaging those without reliable internet or computer access.

## Current Workaround

Residents must rely on public infrastructure to bridge the accessibility gap. This includes using computers and scanners at public libraries or visiting DSS computer kiosks. For those who struggle with online applications or have complex cases, seeking in-person support at DSS service locations is a crucial, though potentially time-consuming, workaround.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This system creates extra hurdles for the most vulnerable residents, potentially excluding them from accessing necessary services. It places the burden of overcoming systemic barriers on the individual, requiring them to spend additional time and resources (e.g., transportation) to complete tasks that are straightforward for more privileged applicants.

## Evidence

The DSS website acknowledges the digital divide by directing residents to public libraries and DSS computer kiosks. The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan highlights the statewide need to improve broadband affordability and digital literacy. The availability of in-person support is noted as critical, but its quality and accessibility can vary, and long wait times are a known barrier.

# Dss Commonhelp Challenges

Applying for benefits such as SNAP through the Virginia Department of Social Services (DSS) via the CommonHelp portal presents several significant challenges for Richmond residents. A primary issue is the complex and potentially slow verification process. According to the official Virginia DSS SNAP Manual, the system relies heavily on computer matching with multiple databases to verify applicant information. If any discrepancies are found, the application can be stalled, leading to delays in receiving essential benefits. Applicants are given a tight deadline of 10 days to provide any required verification, which can be a substantial hurdle for individuals who need to obtain documents from other sources. The system's handling of questionable information, by flagging it for manual review, contributes to these processing delays. Furthermore, there are mandatory verification requirements for identity, income, and residency. The process also highlights a digital divide; the DSS website itself acknowledges this by directing residents without computer access to public libraries or DSS computer kiosks. The acceptance of faxed applications and the use of physical drop boxes, while intended as workarounds, underscore the reliance on physical documents and the challenges some residents face with purely digital submissions. Broader systemic issues, such as reports of 'paperwork barriers' and long call center wait times during the recent Medicaid unwinding process in Virginia, suggest that these administrative burdens are widespread within the state's benefits administration and likely impact DSS applicants as well.

# Rrha Application Challenges

The application process for the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) is characterized by long waitlists, abrupt systemic failures, and stringent documentation requirements. A critical issue highlighted is the unreliability of the process due to funding uncertainties. In May 2025, the RRHA suddenly suspended the issuance of Housing Choice Vouchers because of a lack of federal funding. This action directly impacted an estimated 395 families who were scheduled to receive vouchers, placing them back on the waitlist and creating a state of housing instability and uncertainty. Beyond these systemic failures, the application process itself is demanding. The RRHA's Admissions and Continued Occupancy Policy (ACOP) mandates strict documentation. Notably, it requires original Social Security Number cards for all household members, a stricter requirement compared to other agencies. Furthermore, the RRHA has a rigid hierarchy of acceptable identification documents and explicitly prefers third-party verification over self-certification, adding another layer of difficulty and potential delay for applicants trying to assemble their packets. These strict requirements, combined with the need to use a separate 'RRHA Applicant Portal,' contribute to the overall administrative burden and frustration for residents seeking housing assistance.

# Virginia Career Works Wioa Challenges

The eligibility and documentation process for the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) programs, accessed through Virginia Career Works and the Virginia Workforce Connection (VAWC) portal, is notably more flexible than that of other local agencies. This flexibility is a core feature designed to reduce barriers and avoid delays in service delivery. According to the WIOA Title I Eligibility Guidance, the program allows for self-attestation for many of the eligibility criteria, reducing the burden on applicants to produce extensive third-party documentation upfront. A crucial distinction is the policy on Social Security Numbers (SSNs). The guidance explicitly states that providing an SSN is not required to receive WIOA services, although it is requested for reporting purposes. This makes the program more accessible to individuals who may be hesitant or unable to provide an SSN. While this flexibility is beneficial, the primary 'challenge' it presents to a resident is navigating the inconsistency between these rules and the much stricter requirements of other entities like the RRHA, which mandates original SSN cards. This difference in policy can create confusion for individuals applying to multiple programs simultaneously.

# Verification Process Breakdowns

Verification processes create significant bottlenecks for residents due to differing standards and systems across agencies. The Department of Social Services (DSS) relies on computer matching systems for SNAP verification, which can lead to delays and stalled applications if data discrepancies are found. When information is flagged, households are given only 10 days to provide additional verification, a challenging timeframe for those needing to obtain documents from external sources. This contrasts sharply with the standards of other agencies. For instance, Virginia Career Works (WIOA) allows self-attestation for many eligibility criteria specifically to avoid such delays. Conversely, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) has the strictest standard, preferring third-party verification over self-certification and requiring original documents like SSN cards. This patchwork of verification standards—ranging from lenient self-attestation to rigid third-party validation and reliance on potentially slow data-matching systems—creates a confusing and inefficient system where an application can be stalled at one agency while being acceptable at another.

# Minimal Data Set Analysis

To create a minimal data set and document list that satisfies the DSS, WIOA, and RRHA, a 'Core Eligibility Pack' would need to consolidate information across three primary categories: identity, income, and residency, while adhering to the most stringent requirements of any single agency. The provided research indicates significant variation, particularly with the RRHA having the strictest policies. A comprehensive minimal pack would therefore need to include:

1.  **Proof of Identity:** While all agencies require this, the RRHA's Admissions and Continued Occupancy Policy (ACOP) has a strict hierarchy of acceptable documents and requires original Social Security cards for all household members. Therefore, the pack must include high-quality digital copies of original SSN cards and primary identification like a driver's license or state ID that would meet RRHA standards.

2.  **Proof of Income:** All three agencies verify income. The DSS utilizes computer matching systems and gives applicants 10 days to provide verification if needed, as outlined in the Virginia DSS SNAP Manual. The WIOA allows for self-attestation in some cases to expedite services. The RRHA prefers third-party verification. To satisfy all, the pack should contain recent pay stubs, benefit award letters (e.g., for Social Security), and any other official income documentation. It should be prepared to meet the third-party verification standard preferred by the RRHA.

3.  **Household Information:** Data on household size and composition is critical for both DSS (for benefits like SNAP) and RRHA (for housing unit size and eligibility). This would include names, dates of birth, and relationship to the head of household for all members.

4.  **Residency:** Proof of residency in Richmond is a common requirement. This can typically be satisfied with a utility bill, lease agreement, or an official letter addressed to the applicant at their Richmond address.

5.  **Status Information:** This may include disability status, veteran status, or other specific criteria relevant to program eligibility for WIOA or priority status for housing.

The key challenge is that WIOA's allowance for self-attestation and its optional SSN policy conflict directly with the verification-heavy processes of DSS and the strict documentation rules of RRHA. Therefore, a truly minimal pack must be built to the highest common denominator of proof, which is largely dictated by the RRHA's ACOP and the DSS's verification needs.

# Ssn Requirement Variability

A significant point of inconsistency for a unified eligibility pack is the variability in the requirement for a Social Security Number (SSN) across the agencies. The 'WIOA Title I Eligibility Guidance' explicitly states that providing an SSN is optional and cannot be a barrier to receiving services, although it is requested for reporting purposes. This policy is designed to reduce barriers and avoid delays in service delivery.

In stark contrast, the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA), as per its 'Admissions and Continued Occupancy Policy (ACOP),' mandates the provision of original SSN cards for all household members. This is a non-negotiable requirement for housing assistance. Similarly, the Department of Social Services (DSS) relies on computer matching systems for verification for programs like SNAP, a process that typically uses the SSN as a primary identifier. This creates a fundamental conflict: a resident applying for WIOA services is empowered to withhold their SSN, but if they use the same eligibility pack to apply for housing through RRHA or benefits through DSS, the application would be considered incomplete without it. This discrepancy complicates the creation of a single 'minimal data set' and highlights a key area where user choice (as promoted by WIOA) clashes with administrative requirements (from RRHA and DSS).

# Privacy And Consent Model Considerations

The project's goal of a client-controlled, offline consent model without a central server addresses the core user pains of repetitive data submission and lack of control over sensitive information. While the provided research does not detail a specific technical implementation, it highlights the user needs that such a model must satisfy. An exploration of the proposed model reveals several key considerations and challenges:

1.  **Granular vs. Bundle-Level Consent:** The inconsistency in data requirements (e.g., SSN being optional for WIOA but mandatory for RRHA) makes granular, field-level consent crucial. A user should be able to generate a QR code for a WIOA application that excludes their SSN, and a separate one for an RRHA application that includes it. A simple 'bundle-level' consent would force users to over-share data, defeating the purpose of a privacy-centric design.

2.  **Data Sharing via QR Code:** In an offline model, the QR code would likely contain the user's data itself, encrypted and digitally signed. The caseworker's device would scan the code, decrypt the data using a public key, and verify the signature to ensure authenticity and integrity. The consent parameters (e.g., which fields are shared, the receiving agency, a timestamp) would be embedded within this signed data payload.

3.  **Expiration Windows:** Implementing time-limited consent is feasible. The signed data within the QR code can include an expiration date. An agency's scanning application would be programmed to reject any data payload where the current date is past the embedded expiration date. This gives the client control over the temporal validity of their shared information.

4.  **Revocation in an Offline Architecture:** This is the most significant challenge. Without a central server to maintain a 'revocation list,' it is impossible to remotely invalidate a previously shared QR code. Once an agency has scanned the data, they possess it. Revocation becomes a procedural and trust-based issue rather than a technical one. The user could issue a new QR code with a more recent timestamp and request the agency to disregard the previous one, but there is no technical enforcement mechanism. This limitation is a critical trade-off for achieving a serverless, client-controlled architecture and must be clearly communicated to users to build trust.

# Caseworker And Agency Pain Points

- A significant pain point for agency staff is the immense administrative burden created by the current fragmented system. Caseworkers must process applications that often contain the same information residents have submitted to other agencies, leading to redundant data entry and verification.
- Staff must contend with incomplete applications and inconsistent documentation. Because each agency has slightly different rules (e.g., WIOA's allowance for self-attestation vs. RRHA's strict document hierarchy), caseworkers spend valuable time chasing down missing information or trying to validate documents that don't meet their specific criteria.
- Agency staff bear the brunt of applicant frustration caused by systemic delays. When applications stall due to verification issues or external factors like the RRHA's suspension of housing vouchers, it is the frontline staff who must manage the high volume of inquiries from anxious and frustrated residents, diverting time from processing applications.
- The reliance on non-digital and outdated methods of submission, such as faxed applications and physical drop boxes mentioned in the context of DSS, creates logistical challenges for staff in managing, scanning, and processing paper documents in an increasingly digital workflow.
- Caseworkers must also provide direct support to residents impacted by the digital divide. This involves guiding them through online portals or using agency computer kiosks, which is essential work but adds to the staff's workload and can contribute to longer wait times for all clients seeking in-person assistance.
