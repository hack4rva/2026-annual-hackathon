# Scaling Client-Carried Service Passports: Funding, Partnerships, and Governance for Richmond's Cross-Agency Handoff Memo

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The *Cross-Agency Handoff Memo* is a zero-server, browser-based PDF generator that allows residents or caseworkers to create a portable "service passport." Because it stores no data, the tool bypasses HIPAA and FERPA barriers and can be deployed rapidly. However, the true strategic question is how to own, fund, and scale it beyond a hackathon prototype. Evidence shows that similar client-carried artifacts, such as the Veterans Affairs (VA) wallet cards and hospital discharge summaries, achieve measurable reductions in repeat intake and emergency department use when paired with a hybrid paper-and-digital format [1] [2]. 

Funding streams at the city, regional, state, and federal levels already earmark money for client-owned coordination tools. Programs like the Virginia Housing Trust Fund Homeless Reduction Grant [3], the Virginia Homeless Solutions Program (VHSP) [4], and the DCJS Pre-Release and Post Incarceration Services (PAPIS) grant [5] are prime targets. Private foundations, including the Richmond Memorial Health Foundation (RMHF), also prioritize trauma-informed, low-tech pilots [6]. The optimal governance model is a **city-nonprofit hybrid**: the City of Richmond supplies branding, policy alignment, and a modest hosting budget, while a nonprofit partner (e.g., United Way Richmond 211) manages day-to-day operations, outreach, and evaluation [7]. This structure balances political feasibility, community legitimacy, and scalability, leaving the door open for future regional integration through bodies like PlanRVA [8].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City-Nonprofit (with regional scaling pathways)**

To maximize adoption and minimize liability, the memo should not be exclusively city-owned or exclusively nonprofit-led. A hybrid model leverages the strengths of both sectors while mitigating their respective weaknesses.

* **What the City of Richmond Should Own:** The City should provide official branding and policy alignment, adopting the memo as an official "City of Richmond Service Passport" under the Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) Thriving & Inclusive Communities pillar. The City's IT department should provide a sub-domain (e.g., passport.rva.gov) and a modest annual budget for SSL and static site hosting.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A lead nonprofit (such as United Way 211 Virginia) should own the user experience, agency outreach, and community liaison efforts [7]. They should design UI testing, conduct caseworker training, and manage the data-free feedback loop. The nonprofit should also lead grant applications and manage reporting for philanthropic and state funds.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The City must avoid owning any long-term data strategy or attempting full HMIS integration through this tool until a state-wide HRSN waiver is active. The City should not manage day-to-day caseworker training or direct client outreach, as nonprofits already possess the necessary community trust and established service networks.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **VA Homeless Wallet Card (National):** The VA created printable wallet cards to spread the word about resources for veterans who are homeless or at risk [1]. *What worked:* Simple, physical artifact that clients can easily carry. *What failed:* Low digital integration and reliance on physical retention. *Lesson:* Add a QR-code link to a printable/online backup to prevent loss.
* **Hospital One-Page Discharge Summaries:** Recommended by the USICH to help identify people exiting institutions who are at risk of housing instability [2]. *What worked:* Immediate readability for downstream providers. *What failed:* Lack of standardized formats across different facilities. *Lesson:* Standardize fields (the "red-box" approach) and make the PDF printable on demand.
* **Richmond 211 Service Directory:** A free, confidential, 24/7 service connecting residents with local resources [17]. *What worked:* Centralized, highly accessible resource list. *What failed:* It is a directory, not a client-held artifact for hand-offs, meaning agencies still repeat stories. *Lesson:* Pair the memo with 211 for a "quick-share" of the PDF via text or email.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this idea solely as a Richmond project limits its potential, as residents frequently cross county lines for services, employment, and housing. 

* **City-Only:** If the project stays under the City's Innovation Office, decision-making is quick, and branding is controlled. However, it risks having a limited budget and lacking the outreach capacity needed for wide adoption.
* **Hybrid City-Nonprofit (Recommended):** The City provides branding and hosting, while a nonprofit runs day-to-day operations. This unlocks grant-writing expertise and community trust, though it requires a clear Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) to manage coordination overhead.
* **Regional (PlanRVA) Adoption:** If PlanRVA adopts the tool, it becomes a standard across the Richmond metro area [8]. This provides economies of scale and aligns with federal "regional tools" funding, but requires aligning agency workflows across multiple jurisdictions.
* **State-Level Integration:** Reframing the memo as a feeder into Virginia's broader health and housing initiatives (like the 1115 Demonstration Waiver) [26] positions Virginia as a national leader. The risk is legal complexity around consent across counties, which can be mitigated by embedding standardized consent language.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Legal / Privacy:** There is a risk that agencies misinterpret the memo as Protected Health Information (PHI), leading to HIPAA breach concerns. *Mitigation:* Clearly label the memo as a *client-owned communication artifact* and include a consent disclaimer.
* **Procurement:** City procurement rules may require a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) if any data is perceived as stored. *Mitigation:* Emphasize the zero-server architecture and use existing cloud-agnostic static site contracts.
* **Community Trust:** A "paper only" solution might be perceived as stigmatizing or low-tech, leading to low uptake among digitally marginalized groups. *Mitigation:* Offer both printed and QR-linked digital versions, co-designed with a community advisory board.
* **Agency Buy-In:** Caseworkers may view generating the memo as extra paperwork, stalling adoption. *Mitigation:* Pilot the tool with "caseworker champions" and integrate memo generation into existing intake workflows.
* **Funding Dependence:** Over-reliance on a single grant (e.g., VHSP) makes the project unsustainable if not awarded. *Mitigation:* Pursue parallel funding streams across state, foundation, and federal levels.
