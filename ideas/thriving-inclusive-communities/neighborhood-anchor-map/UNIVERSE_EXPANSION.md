# Mapping Trust, Transit, and Transfer: Scaling Richmond's Neighborhood-Anchor Map for Immigrant Inclusion

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Neighborhood Anchor Map's true potential extends far beyond a standalone City of Richmond IT project. By fusing trusted community anchor locations with dynamic transit accessibility, this tool addresses a critical gap in immigrant service discovery: the reality of physical access. While the city possesses the foundational open data (GIS facility locations, GRTC GTFS feeds), it lacks the inherent community trust and agile development capacity required to maximize adoption among vulnerable populations. 

Expanding this idea's universe reveals a robust ecosystem of federal transportation equity grants, state-level immigrant inclusion initiatives, and local philanthropic support ready to fund transit-integrated civic tech. By transitioning from a city-owned concept to a hybrid governance model—where the city provides the data infrastructure and a trusted nonprofit coalition manages the resident-facing platform—Richmond can unlock over $2 million in potential funding. This approach not only ensures long-term sustainability but also positions the map as a scalable template for regional and national adoption.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: City-Nonprofit Hybrid**

Pure city ownership risks long-term sustainability due to IT staffing constraints and potential trust barriers among undocumented or mixed-status families. Conversely, a purely nonprofit-led effort would struggle to maintain real-time integration with municipal GIS and transit data. 

* **What the City Should Own:** The underlying data infrastructure. The City of Richmond's GIS department and GRTC should own the APIs, the static and real-time GTFS feeds [1] [2], and the baseline public facility datasets (libraries, community centers) [3]. The city should also own the legal framework (Data-Sharing MOUs) and the pursuit of federal infrastructure grants.
* **What Partners Should Own:** The resident-facing application, community validation, and user experience. A trusted local nonprofit (e.g., ReEstablish Richmond or Sacred Heart Center) should own the front-end interface, the curation of "trust signals" (languages spoken, ID requirements), and the community outreach strategy.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The user data and the curation of non-municipal anchor institutions. To protect vulnerable populations, the city must not collect or store user analytics or PII from this tool.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **NLC Civic Mapping Initiative (Memphis, TN & Alexandria, VA):** CMI partnered with the National Head Start Association to map transit proximity to Head Start centers [28] [34]. 
 * *What worked:* The data directly spurred the Memphis Area Transit Authority (MATA) to relocate bus stops closer to three centers [35] [29]. 
 * *Lesson:* Transit-overlay maps shouldn't just be discovery tools for residents; they are diagnostic tools for city planners to fix infrastructure gaps.
* **Seattle AccessMap (Taskar Center, UW):** A multimodal routing map that accounts for pedestrian paths, elevation, and curb ramps [31] [36].
 * *What worked:* Treating sidewalks and micro-barriers as a routable transportation network [30].
 * *Lesson:* Open-source routing engines can handle complex, localized accessibility data. Richmond can leverage similar open-source frameworks (like OpenTripPlanner) rather than building routing from scratch.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond city project limits its potential. 
* **Regional Shift:** Immigrants and refugees do not live their lives strictly within city limits; housing affordability often pushes them into Henrico or Chesterfield counties, while jobs and anchors remain in the city. Scaling to a regional frame (via PlanRVA) aligns with regional transit (GRTC) realities and opens up larger federal planning grants.
* **Statewide Shift:** If the hybrid model (City API + Nonprofit UI) succeeds, the underlying codebase can be adopted by the Virginia Office of New Americans to scale across other transit-dense Virginia regions (e.g., Hampton Roads, Northern Virginia).
* **Ownership Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the tool can iterate faster, bypass slow municipal procurement cycles for UI updates, and maintain higher trust levels among undocumented users who may fear government-owned tracking.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Maintenance Burden:** Static GTFS feeds become outdated quickly. *Mitigation:* Design the system to automatically ingest GRTC's GTFS updates [37] [2] rather than relying on manual uploads.
* **Trust and Privacy:** Users may fear the map tracks their location. *Mitigation:* Zero-login requirement; no location data stored; nonprofit branding on the front-end.
* **Transit Realism:** Scheduled GTFS overestimates real access. *Mitigation:* Clearly communicate that travel times are estimates; plan a Phase 2 integration of GTFS-Realtime data.
* **Information Overload:** Mapping too many services creates noise. *Mitigation:* Strictly limit the map to 50-100 highly vetted "anchor" institutions based on community feedback.
