# Re‑Entry 72‑Hour Navigator: Expanding Funding, Partnerships, and Governance for a City‑Scale, Rapid‑Response System

## Executive Summary

The first 72 hours after release from incarceration represent a critical window where returning citizens are highly vulnerable. While the Reentry 72-Hour Navigator idea originated as a Richmond-specific civic innovation, its true potential requires expanding beyond city government ownership. The data indicates that no single tool works in isolation; hybridizing pre-release ID issuance, verified navigator referrals, immediate transportation, and printed guidance produces the fastest early stability gains. 

To scale this effectively, Richmond must adopt a hybrid governance model. The city should provide the technological infrastructure and data-sharing agreements, while community-based nonprofits like OAR of Richmond and REAL LIFE manage the front-line peer navigation. Funding can be aggressively sourced from state-level grants like the Virginia DCJS PAPIS program and federal SAMHSA reentry grants, matched by local philanthropic dollars from the Community Foundation for a greater Richmond. By shifting from a city-owned silo to a regionally funded, nonprofit-operated, and city-enabled ecosystem, the 72-Hour Navigator can transform from a hackathon concept into a sustainable, multi-jurisdictional reentry standard.

## 3. Governance & Ownership Models

Determining who owns the 72-Hour Navigator dictates its agility, trustworthiness, and long-term survival.

### 3.1. The Hybrid Model Maximizes Trust and Capability

A purely city-owned model risks slow procurement cycles and low trust among returning citizens. A purely nonprofit-led model lacks the authority to mandate data sharing from the jail or DMV. 

**Expanded Ownership Recommendation:** The project should adopt a **Hybrid Model**. 
* **The City Should Own:** The data-sharing agreements (MOUs with the Sheriff's Office and DMV), the technical infrastructure/API integrations, and the procurement of the digital dashboard.
* **Nonprofit Partners Should Own:** The resident-facing peer navigation, the distribution of the printed checklists, and the weekly updating of the Verified Provider List (VPL).
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The direct case management and peer support relationships, which are better received when facilitated by organizations like OAR or REAL LIFE.

## 5. Scale-Shift & Systems-Level Implications

While designed for Richmond, the 72-Hour Navigator addresses a systemic failure that extends across Central Virginia.

### 5.1. From City Project to Regional Standard

Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential. Returning citizens frequently cross county lines for housing, employment, and parole reporting. If the frame becomes regional (e.g., Central Virginia / PlanRVA), the Navigator can integrate regional transit routes and cross-county shelter capacities. If a nonprofit like OAR (which already serves the broader area) owns the resident-facing layer, the tool can easily scale to adjacent jurisdictions without requiring new municipal legislation.

## 6. Risks, Constraints & Mitigation

Several blockers could derail the implementation of the Navigator if not proactively managed.

* **Data-Sharing & Privacy:** Integrating release dates from the Sheriff's Office with DMV ID status requires strict MOUs. *Mitigation:* Limit data sharing to a "need-to-know" basis (release date, ID status) rather than full criminal histories.
* **Trust & Community Legitimacy:** Returning citizens may distrust a city-branded tracking tool. *Mitigation:* Brand the printed checklist under the umbrella of a trusted nonprofit partner like Nolef Turns or OAR.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Verified Provider Lists decay quickly if not updated. *Mitigation:* Allocate specific grant funding (via PAPIS) to fund a part-time role dedicated solely to calling and verifying shelter/service capacity weekly.
