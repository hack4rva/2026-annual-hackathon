# Executive Summary

In the critical first 72 hours post-release, returning residents in Richmond, VA, face a series of significant pain points rooted in a fragmented and uncoordinated system of essential services. The most pressing challenge is navigating multiple agencies—such as Probation and Parole, the DMV, OAR of Richmond, the Department of Social Services (DSS), and Virginia Career Works—each with its own disparate location, shifting operating hours, and unique procedural requirements. This lack of synchronization frequently leads to wasted trips, timing conflicts, and missed appointments. A major roadblock is obtaining a state ID from the DMV, which has stringent and complex documentation requirements for identity, legal presence, residency, and Social Security number, often using original documents that newly released individuals do not possess. Accessing emergency shelter is another hurdle, as it is gated by a centralized phone intake system, the Homeless Connection Line, which faces rising demand and potential backlogs. Furthermore, the geographical dispersion of these key service sites across the city, combined with a reliance on multiple and sometimes confusing transit tools, places a heavy navigational burden on the individual. The cumulative effect of these obstacles elevates the risk of technical probation violations for missed check-ins or uncompleted tasks, potentially leading to re-incarceration and undermining the goal of successful reintegration.

# Returning Resident Persona Profile

The 'returning resident' persona is an individual in the immediate 72 hours following release from a correctional facility like the Richmond City Justice Center. Their situation is characterized by extreme time pressure and a critical need to complete a series of mandatory and essential tasks to begin their reintegration. This includes checking in with their probation officer, securing a state-issued ID, applying for social service benefits (like SNAP or Medicaid), finding workforce support, and arranging for emergency shelter. They often lack stable housing, personal transportation, a mobile device, and the necessary original documents required by various agencies. Emotionally, they are in a vulnerable state, facing the anxiety of navigating a complex and unforgiving bureaucracy while carrying the stigma of their past. Their core motivation is to successfully reintegrate and avoid recidivism. They want to follow a clear, simple, step-by-step process that tells them exactly where to go, when to go, what to bring, and how to get there. A key emotional driver is the desire to be treated with dignity and not have to repeatedly recount their sensitive personal history to every new service provider, which can be retraumatizing and fatiguing. The desired outcome is to efficiently and successfully complete all required check-ins and applications within the 72-hour window, securing an ID, benefits, and shelter access without wasted trips, missed deadlines, or the added stress of conflicting information. Ultimately, they seek to establish a foundation for stability that enables them to find employment and housing, thereby reducing their risk of re-incarceration.

# Overarching Systemic Failures

The challenges faced by returning residents in Richmond are symptoms of fundamental, system-level failures. A primary failure is the profound lack of inter-agency coordination and data sharing. Essential service providers, including the Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC), Department of Social Services (DSS), DMV, OAR, and local shelters, operate in silos with separate, non-communicating systems. This forces individuals to repeatedly provide the same sensitive information and documentation at each stop, increasing emotional fatigue and the potential for errors. Secondly, the system provides conflicting and decentralized information. There is no single source of truth for operating hours, locations, or required documents, which vary significantly between agencies and can change without notice. For example, OAR has limited intake hours and is closed on Fridays, while the DMV has Saturday hours, creating a complex scheduling puzzle for someone with no transportation and a 72-hour deadline. This fragmentation places the entire burden of navigation and synthesis on the individual, who is least equipped to handle it immediately post-release. They are expected to be their own case manager, piecing together a viable itinerary from disparate and often unreliable information. This is exacerbated by a growing digital divide; while agencies push towards online applications (e.g., DSS's CommonHelp) and phone-based intake (Homeless Connection Line), returning residents often lack the devices, digital skills, or even the phone data necessary to engage with these channels, as highlighted by the Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan. These systemic flaws create a process that is inherently inequitable, disproportionately penalizing those without stable housing, digital literacy, or strong support networks.

# Task Specific Pain Points

## Task

Probation Check-In

## Pain Points

Returning residents face the challenge of reporting to Probation & Parole District 1, which has specific operating hours (Mon–Fri 9am–5pm) at a distinct location (6866 Everglades Dr). These hours can conflict with other essential appointments, and failure to complete this court-ordered task within the compressed 72-hour window post-release carries significant risk.

## Current Workaround

Individuals rely on paper lists provided upon release from jail, word-of-mouth information, or making ad-hoc calls to confirm hours and location details.

## Cost Of Failure

Missing a required check-in can lead to a technical probation violation, which can result in sanctions or re-incarceration, completely derailing the reentry process.

## Task

Obtaining a DMV ID

## Pain Points

The Virginia DMV has stringent and complex documentation requirements, demanding original documents for one proof of identity, one proof of legal presence, two proofs of Virginia residency, and proof of a Social Security Number. This is a major barrier for individuals who may not have these documents readily available upon release.

## Current Workaround

Current methods involve making multiple, often unsuccessful, trips to the DMV, attempting to gather the necessary original documents from various sources, and relying on case managers for guidance when available.

## Cost Of Failure

Failure to obtain a state-issued ID prevents access to numerous other critical services, including employment, housing applications, and opening bank accounts, effectively halting progress in reentry.

## Task

Applying for DSS Benefits

## Pain Points

The process to apply for benefits like SNAP and Medicaid is fragmented. Individuals are directed to apply via phone, an online portal (CommonHelp), or a physical drop box at the Southside location on Hull St. This creates confusion and requires separate verification steps, which are difficult without proper documentation or a stable address.

## Current Workaround

Applicants must navigate the confusing system by calling the designated phone number, using the online portal if they have digital access, or physically traveling to a DSS location to use a drop box.

## Cost Of Failure

Delays in the application process result in a lack of access to essential support like food (SNAP) and healthcare (Medicaid), leading to immediate food and health insecurity.

## Task

Securing Shelter

## Pain Points

Accessing emergency shelter is not a walk-in process. It requires a mandatory phone call to the centralized Homeless Connection Line for coordinated entry. With a reported 10% increase in homelessness in Richmond, this system creates a significant bottleneck, leading to long wait times and uncertainty.

## Current Workaround

The only workaround is to call the Homeless Connection Line, the number for which is often found on 'street sheets' or through 211, and wait for an intake assessment and placement.

## Cost Of Failure

Bottlenecks and delays in the intake process lead to prolonged homelessness and instability. This not only increases personal risk but also makes it substantially harder to attend other required appointments and secure employment.

## Task

Finding Job Assistance

## Pain Points

Virginia Career Works, a key resource for employment support, has limited hours, especially on Fridays when it closes at 2:00 PM. This shortened window on a critical day within the 72-hour period makes it difficult to access job assistance services.

## Current Workaround

Individuals must attempt to schedule a visit within the narrow operating hours, often having to prioritize it over other time-sensitive tasks like probation check-ins or DMV visits.

## Cost Of Failure

Missing the opportunity to connect with workforce services leads to delayed employment, prolonging financial instability and dependence on benefits.


# Digital Divide Challenges

The digital divide presents significant barriers for individuals reentering society in Richmond, particularly within the critical first 72 hours post-release. According to the Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan, formerly incarcerated individuals often lack the necessary devices, digital skills, and affordable internet access to navigate essential services. While many are pushed towards mobile-only internet access, this is often insufficient for complex and critical tasks such as completing job applications or applying for benefits through online portals like Virginia's CommonHelp. The plan explicitly notes that inmates typically cannot use tablets or other technologies while incarcerated, which limits their ability to develop the digital skills needed immediately upon release. This lack of digital literacy and access creates a major pain point, as many crucial resources and applications are increasingly online-only, effectively excluding those without the means or knowledge to engage with them. The time and cost associated with using phones for calls or data further exacerbates the problem for individuals with limited financial resources.

# Cost Of The Status Quo

The current, fragmented system for reentry in Richmond imposes significant tangible and intangible costs on returning residents. Tangible costs include wasted time and money on multiple, uncoordinated trips across the city to agencies with conflicting hours, leading to missed appointments and critical deadlines. This inefficiency directly elevates the risk of technical probation violations, as individuals struggle to complete mandatory check-ins within the strict 72-hour window, which can result in sanctions or re-incarceration. Furthermore, delays in securing necessary documents or accessing services lead to prolonged homelessness due to shelter intake backlogs and missed opportunities for stable employment and essential benefits like SNAP and Medicaid. The intangible costs are equally severe, encompassing significant emotional and psychological stress, anxiety, and fatigue from navigating a complex and unforgiving system. This experience of repeating personal stories and facing constant hurdles can lead to feelings of stigma, withdrawal from seeking help, and extended instability, which ultimately increases the risk of recidivism.

# Existing Workarounds And Their Failures

Returning residents currently rely on a variety of ad-hoc and unreliable methods to navigate the reentry process. These workarounds include using paper lists or 'street sheets' provided upon release from jail, which often contain outdated information on agency hours or locations. They also make ad-hoc calls to agencies or general helplines like 211, depend on informal word-of-mouth guidance from peers, or use case managers when available. A primary workaround involves making multiple, uncoordinated trips on the GRTC bus system to physically visit the dispersed service locations. These methods fail because they are inefficient and do not address the root problem of fragmented, conflicting, and shifting information. Paper lists are static and cannot account for last-minute changes. Calling agencies is time-consuming and dependent on limited office hours. Word-of-mouth is inherently unreliable. Making numerous trips across town to locations like the DMV on W Broad St, OAR on W Clay St, and Probation on Everglades Dr is costly in both time and transit fare (even if currently free, it requires planning and time), and significantly increases the chances of arriving at a closed office or missing a critical appointment window, thereby defeating the goal of a swift and successful 72-hour transition.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

The challenges of reentry in Richmond disproportionately affect several marginalized groups, creating significant equity issues. Individuals without stable housing are severely impacted by systemic barriers, such as the Virginia DMV's requirement for two proofs of a physical residency, as PO boxes are not accepted. This makes obtaining a necessary ID nearly impossible for the homeless. Furthermore, access to shelter itself is gated by a centralized phone intake system, the Homeless Connection Line, which can be a bottleneck, especially as CARITAS reports a 10% rise in homelessness and more complex needs in the community. Justice-involved individuals as a group face heightened digital exclusion; they often lack devices and digital skills upon release, yet are expected to navigate online-only applications for benefits and jobs within a critical 24-72 hour window. The Virginia Digital Opportunity Plan notes that mobile-only access is insufficient for these tasks. Additionally, individuals with language barriers face extra friction, a problem acknowledged in the state's plan which calls for simplified language. Finally, the limited and variable hours of essential agencies (e.g., OAR closed on Fridays, Career Works with reduced Friday hours) place a heavy burden on workers, caregivers, and anyone reliant on public transportation, amplifying existing disparities and making it harder to complete time-sensitive tasks.

# Emotional And Psychological Toll

The process of navigating reentry services in the first 72 hours inflicts a heavy emotional and psychological toll on returning residents. A primary source of this burden is the need to repeatedly share their sensitive personal history and situation with each separate agency—from probation and OAR to the DMV, DSS, and shelter intake. Because these agencies' systems do not share data, the individual is forced to retell their story, fill out distinct forms, and provide separate verification at every step. This repetitive and dehumanizing process causes emotional fatigue, reinforces feelings of stigma, and can lead to withdrawal from seeking essential services. Compounding this is the significant anxiety and fear associated with uncertainty. Individuals worry about being turned away from the DMV due to its stringent original-document requirements or from DSS due to complex eligibility rules. The entire experience, occurring immediately after the trauma of incarceration and during a period of high vulnerability, is characterized by stress and frustration rather than support, which can undermine stability and increase the risk of recidivism.

# Probation Violation Risks

A significant and high-stakes risk for returning residents is committing a technical probation violation by failing to complete the mandatory check-in with their probation and parole officer within the first 72 hours of release. According to a Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC) report, technical violations are a significant issue among state-responsible populations. This risk is not necessarily due to willful non-compliance, but is a direct consequence of the logistical chaos of the reentry process. Individuals must navigate a fragmented system of services—securing an ID, applying for benefits, finding shelter—while also ensuring they report to the correct Probation & Parole office, such as District 1 at 6866 Everglades Dr, which operates only on weekdays from 9am to 5pm. The difficulty in coordinating travel and appointments within this narrow window makes missing the check-in a tangible danger, which can escalate to sanctions or immediate re-incarceration, derailing the entire reentry effort before it can even begin.

# Documentation And Id Barriers

## Agency

Virginia Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV)

## Barrier Description

The primary barrier to obtaining a state-issued ID for reentering individuals is the DMV's strict requirement for multiple original documents to prove identity, legal presence, residency, and Social Security number. Many individuals post-incarceration do not have these documents, and the DMV will not accept copies or documents that appear altered. This creates a significant roadblock to accessing other essential services.

## Required Document Types

One proof of identity, one proof of legal presence, two proofs of Virginia residency, and proof of Social Security Number (e.g., Social Security card, W-2 form, payroll stub). All documents must be originals.

## Challenge For Homeless

Individuals experiencing homelessness face a specific and significant hurdle in providing the required two proofs of Virginia residency. The DMV does not accept P.O. boxes or business addresses, and without a stable physical address, it is nearly impossible to produce documents like a lease agreement or utility bill. While a no-cost ID program exists for homeless youth, this does not extend to the general adult population, leaving them disproportionately affected.


# Transportation Navigation Barriers

## Transportation Mode

GRTC bus system

## Challenge Description

The primary transportation challenge is navigating between geographically dispersed service locations across Richmond. A returning resident must coordinate multiple bus trips to different agencies, each with its own unique and often conflicting operating hours, all within a critical 72-hour timeframe. While fares are currently zero, the logistical complexity of planning the journey remains a significant barrier.

## Key Locations

Key service sites are spread out across the city, including the DMV on W Broad St, a DSS drop box on Hull St, the Probation & Parole office on Everglades Dr, and OAR of Richmond on W Clay St.

## Technology Dependency

Effective trip planning relies heavily on technology such as the GRTC website, third-party apps like 'GRTC Today', or Google Maps. This creates a digital divide, as individuals immediately post-release often lack smartphones, reliable data plans, or the digital skills needed to use these tools, making offline navigation extremely difficult and confusing.

# Recommendations For Navigator App

Based on the identified pain points for returning residents, the 'reentry-72-hour-navigator' app should prioritize several key features. First, it must serve as a single source of truth, providing authoritative, up-to-date hours, addresses, and phone numbers for essential services like Probation & Parole District 1, OAR, DMV Richmond Central, Richmond DSS, and Virginia Career Works. Second, the app should offer offline-accessible, step-by-step checklists that detail the exact documents required for each agency, with a particular focus on the stringent ID requirements at the DMV (including proofs for SSN, legal presence, and residency) and guidance on what to do if documents are missing. Third, it should integrate transit wayfinding, including offline maps with key GRTC bus routes like the Pulse on Broad Street and the 1A on the Hull Street corridor, and clearly note the current fare-free status. To address the emotional burden of repeating personal stories, the app could include a secure, reusable user profile or a 'document-minder' feature where users can privately store notes and information to share selectively with case managers or agencies. Finally, it should provide clear guidance on accessing emergency shelter through the Homeless Connection Line, setting realistic expectations about intake timing and procedures.
