# RVA-Helps — Scaling Richmond’s Fresh, Open-Referral Service Hub to Regional & State Impact

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The universe of "RVA Helps" expands significantly when reframed from a localized, city-owned directory into a federated "resource data utility" integrated with state and national infrastructure. By leveraging existing platforms like the 211 National Data Platform and Virginia's No Wrong Door (NWD) system [1] [2], Richmond can avoid the costly trap of building a standalone directory from scratch. 

Instead of bearing the estimated $2.76 billion annual burden of manual data maintenance seen in healthcare, Richmond can utilize AI-assisted deduplication tools like the Open Referral Resource Record-Matcher [3] and tap into federal interoperability grants (such as ONC LEAP) [4]. This shifts the focus from vanity metrics to the Successful Connection Rate (SCR), ensuring residents actually receive the services they seek.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION
**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

* **What the City should own:** The City of Richmond should own the data-use agreements, procurement of the baseline API subscriptions (e.g., 211 Virginia), and the integration of embeddable widgets into municipal intake workflows (e.g., 311, social services).
* **What partners should own:** A designated nonprofit coalition (or an existing entity like Homeward VA or VirginiaNavigator) should own the day-to-day data stewardship, community engagement, and the active 90-day verification cadence. 
* **What should NOT be city-owned:** The resident-facing application layer and the underlying proprietary database should not be city-owned. City-owned directories historically suffer from data decay when political administrations change or budgets tighten.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART
* **Virginia No Wrong Door (NWD) & VirginiaNavigator:** DARS oversees this statewide initiative, partnering with VirginiaNavigator to maintain a directory of over 26,000 resources [2] [28]. *Lesson:* Do not rebuild the database; federate with this existing, funded state infrastructure.
* **Homeward VA Coordinated Entry:** Homeward manages the HMIS and coordinated entry system for the Greater Richmond Continuum of Care [18] [19]. *Lesson:* Workflow integration is critical. RVA Helps must embed into existing intake systems like HMIS rather than forcing caseworkers to use a separate portal.
* **Connect 211 & Open Referral:** A 211 call center teamed up with software developers to create a modern search engine using the HSDS format [29] [30]. *Lesson:* HSDS compliance allows for seamless data portability between 211 systems and local municipal interfaces.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS
Framing this solely as a Richmond project limits its potential and ignores the reality that residents cross county lines for services (e.g., Henrico, Chesterfield). 
* **Regional Shift:** If framed regionally, the project can integrate with Homeward VA's regional CoC [18] and tap into broader Community Foundation grants [9]. 
* **Statewide Shift:** By aligning with DARS' No Wrong Door system [31], the city can leverage federal ACL funding [5] and Medicaid 1115 waiver initiatives [14]. 
* **Ownership Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer while the city provides data, the platform gains community trust, avoids municipal procurement bottlenecks, and ensures continuity across mayoral administrations.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS
* **Data Staleness & Maintenance Burden:** The biggest risk is data decay. Without a funded 90-day verification cadence, the directory will become obsolete.
* **Technical Integration:** Bridging HSDS (social services) with HL7 FHIR (healthcare) requires specialized expertise, potentially necessitating partnerships with entities like CRISP Shared Services [21].
* **Grant Dependence:** Relying solely on short-term grants (like the 9-month RWJF grant [13]) creates a funding cliff. Core stewardship must be baked into city/state operational budgets.
* **Closed-Loop vs. Successful Connection:** Tracking actual service receipt (SCR) requires follow-up mechanisms (e.g., SMS), which introduces privacy and HIPAA compliance challenges.
