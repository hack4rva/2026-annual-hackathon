# Executive Summary

Richmond residents navigating housing, workforce, and reentry services face a deeply fragmented and overburdened system, creating significant pain points that the rva-helps project aims to address. The most critical issue is the lack of a unified entry point, forcing individuals in crisis to repeatedly share sensitive information across multiple, disconnected channels like the Homeless Connection Line (HCL), 2-1-1, and various walk-in centers. This problem is exacerbated by severe systemic failures, including limited emergency shelter capacity leading to high turn-away rates, long waitlists for families, a scarcity of affordable housing, and obsolete public housing stock. For returning citizens, the absence of a multi-agency data-sharing system hinders coordinated support. These failures result in delayed assistance, prolonged instability, increased risk of recidivism, and significant trauma and distrust among residents who must retell their stories to navigate a confusing and inefficient web of services.

# Resident Facing Instability Pain Points

## Pain Point

Limited Shelter Capacity and Long Waits via Coordinated Entry

## Evidence

The Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) experiences a high number of turn-aways for emergency shelter and maintains a waiting list for families with minor children. Community members report extended wait times to access emergency beds through the Homeless Connection Line (HCL).

## System Breakdown

The centralized Coordinated Entry System (CES) intake process, primarily through the HCL, is bottlenecked by insufficient system capacity. Referrals frequently stall due to a scarcity of affordable housing stock and staffing shortages in case management, overwhelming the primary access point.

## Cost Of Status Quo

Residents experience longer shelter stays, delayed housing placements, and increased risk of unsheltered homelessness. The process leads to significant family instability and the trauma of having to repeatedly navigate a system that cannot meet demand.

https://www.rva.gov/sites/default/files/2024-09/Draft%20FY%202023%20CAPER.pdf

## Pain Point

Fragmented Access Points and Repeated Intakes

## Evidence

Residents must navigate a complex web of parallel access points including the Homeless Connection Line (HCL), 2-1-1, the City's 3-1-1, Homeward’s Street Sheet, and walk-in centers. This forces individuals, including sensitive populations like survivors and returning citizens, to re-disclose personal information and retell their stories multiple times.

## System Breakdown

Service providers operate in silos with no unified intake or data-sharing system. There is inconsistent application of consented data sharing and a lack of clarity on how information is used, forcing residents to repeat the intake process with each new agency or program they encounter.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This fragmentation causes significant delays in receiving help, leads to service avoidance, and can retraumatize individuals. It fosters distrust in the system and results in deteriorating stability for those in need.

## Pain Point

Lack of Coordinated Reentry Services Due to Disconnected Data Systems

## Evidence

There is no unified, multi-agency data sharing information system between the Virginia Department of Corrections (VADOC) and partner agencies. This gap hinders coordinated reentry planning and seamless referrals for returning citizens.

## System Breakdown

Disconnected data and intake systems require returning citizens to repeatedly explain their situation. Eligibility screens vary between agencies, and housing options are severely limited for justice-involved individuals due to restrictive landlord policies and background checks.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The lack of coordination leads to unstable housing and employment post-release, administrative churn for service providers, and an increased risk of recidivism for returning citizens.

## Pain Point

Systemic Barriers Impede Access to Employment and Support Services

## Evidence

State reports and local plans highlight that barriers such as lack of childcare and transportation impede access to training and employment for workforce seekers. Furthermore, there is a noted lack of cross-system referrals, particularly when healthcare providers identify housing instability as a primary health-related social need.

## System Breakdown

Intake processes across different systems (workforce, housing, health) do not consistently address these fundamental needs. There are limited 'warm handoffs' between systems, forcing participants to navigate multiple, uncoordinated intakes to piece together the support they require.

## Cost Of Status Quo

Residents experience delayed employment, higher rates of program drop-off, and subsequent income loss, perpetuating cycles of instability.

Continuum of Care providers consistently report that a lack of affordable housing and willing landlords is a primary barrier to resolving homelessness. This is compounded by RRHA waitlist closures, obsolete public housing stock, and landlord policies that include restrictive criminal background checks.

## System Breakdown

The entire system of care is constrained by a severe lack of viable housing options. Even when a resident successfully navigates the intake and referral process, the final step of securing housing often fails due to low rental vacancy rates and policies that screen out vulnerable populations.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This scarcity prolongs homelessness, leads to extended and costly shelter stays, and undermines family stability, trapping residents in a cycle of instability regardless of their engagement with support services.

# Resident Workarounds And Costs

Currently, a Richmond resident facing instability must employ several workarounds to navigate the fragmented service landscape. The primary method is to contact the Homeless Connection Line (HCL), the main access point for the Coordinated Entry System, often requiring repeated calls due to high volume and capacity issues. Other workarounds include calling 2-1-1 Virginia for information and referrals, physically visiting the City's Community Resource and Training Center for a 'warm referral', or using resources like Homeward’s Street Sheet. Some residents secure assistance from navigators, such as those at YMCA's Help1RVA, by completing a separate online intake form. For sensitive populations like returning citizens or survivors, workarounds involve sharing minimal information, relying on a trusted advocate like a probation officer or shelter staff member to make connections, or avoiding services altogether until a crisis point. The costs of this status quo are significant: residents endure the trauma and inefficiency of retelling their story at each new intake, face slow handoffs and long delays for services, and have no visibility into their referral status. This leads to prolonged homelessness, family instability, reduced workforce participation, and a higher risk of recidivism, ultimately fostering deep distrust in the very systems designed to provide help.

# Resident Equity Dimensions

The fragmented and under-resourced service system in Richmond disproportionately affects specific vulnerable populations. Families with minor children are impacted by shelter waitlists and prioritization trade-offs. Black residents are particularly affected by the scarcity of affordable housing, a situation exacerbated by Richmond's historically high eviction rates which have disproportionately impacted Black households. Justice-involved individuals face compounded barriers, as they are often screened out of housing opportunities due to criminal background checks, a significant hurdle in a market with few willing landlords. Furthermore, the system excludes those with logistical challenges; individuals without government-issued IDs or reliable transportation, as well as parenting youth who lack access to childcare, face significant barriers at various intake points, effectively shutting them out from needed services. These equity dimensions highlight how a system with fragmented access, unclear eligibility, and insufficient resources deepens instability for the communities who need support the most.

## Pain Point

Forced Re-disclosure of Sensitive Information Across Multiple Systems

## Evidence

The existence of multiple, parallel access points for services in Richmond (Homeless Connection Line, 2-1-1, 3-1-1, Street Sheet, walk-in centers) forces individuals to re-disclose their personal and often traumatic stories repeatedly to different organizations.

## System Breakdown

The current system lacks a unified intake process and consistent, consented data-sharing protocols. There is variable clarity on who can see an individual's data, leading to repeated intakes which can retraumatize sensitive populations like survivors and returning citizens.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This failure leads to individuals avoiding or delaying seeking help, which can cause their situations to deteriorate into a crisis. It also causes retraumatization from having to retell their story and fosters deep distrust in the service system.

## Pain Point

Lack of a Unified, Secure Data-Sharing System

## Evidence

A 2025 Virginia state report on reentry services explicitly states, 'There is no unified multi-agency data sharing information system between VADOC and other agencies that assist individuals in VADOC custody or in community supervision.' This gap complicates the ability to share data in a way that is both privacy-aware and based on client consent.

## System Breakdown

Data and intake systems are disconnected between key agencies (like the Virginia Department of Corrections and community partners). This requires returning citizens to repeatedly provide their information and story, while eligibility screens vary by agency, creating confusion and barriers.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The absence of a unified system contributes to an increased risk of recidivism, unstable housing and employment, significant administrative churn for providers, and erodes the trust of returning citizens.

## Pain Point

Fear of Data Misuse and Stigma Leading to Service Denial

## Evidence

The Virginia reentry report highlights advocacy efforts aimed at 'encouraging landlords to remove barriers to permanent housing such as criminal background checks.' This indicates that a returning citizen's sensitive data (their criminal record) is actively used to deny them essential services like housing, creating a justified fear of data misuse.

## System Breakdown

The system allows for screening policies that directly use a person's past against them, particularly in the housing sector. This creates a significant barrier for justice-involved individuals, limiting their options and reinforcing stigma.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The cost is unstable housing, exclusion from critical services, and a general avoidance of systems that are perceived as punitive rather than helpful. This perpetuates cycles of poverty and instability.

## Pain Point

Critical Barriers to Accessing Basic Needs Post-Release

## Evidence

The Virginia reentry report notes that returning citizens face critical barriers including housing access, transportation, and obtaining essential documents like birth certificates, which are often destroyed by jails after 30 days. These barriers are compounded by the lack of coordinated services.

## System Breakdown

The reentry process is fragmented. There is no seamless handoff that ensures a person has their essential documents, transportation, and a clear path to housing and healthcare upon release. This systemic failure places the burden of navigation entirely on the individual at their most vulnerable point.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This leads to immediate instability, making it difficult to secure employment or housing and increasing the risk of recidivism. It creates a cycle of crisis management rather than proactive support.

# Returning Citizen Survivor Workarounds And Costs

To cope with a fragmented and untrustworthy system, returning citizens and survivors adopt several workarounds. They often share only the minimal amount of information necessary, withholding sensitive details out of fear of misuse or stigma. Another common strategy is to rely on a known, trusted navigator—such as a staff member at Help1RVA, a shelter worker, or a probation officer—to advocate on their behalf and navigate the complex web of services. The most extreme workaround is to avoid seeking services altogether until a situation becomes a full-blown crisis. The costs of these actions are severe: they result in delayed help, which allows problems to worsen, and can lead to a complete avoidance of the support systems designed to help them, ultimately causing deteriorating personal stability.

# Returning Citizen Survivor Equity Dimensions

The equity dimensions for returning citizens and survivors are stark, as they are a group systematically excluded by the current service landscape. Stigma is a primary driver of this exclusion; justice-involved individuals face 'compounded screening barriers,' particularly for housing, where their criminal background is used to deny them access. This fear of data misuse is well-founded and creates a significant barrier to seeking help. The lack of trusted, private, and secure pathways for sharing information forces them to choose between retraumatizing themselves by retelling their story and avoiding services altogether. This dynamic means they are often unable to receive timely help, only engaging with systems at a point of crisis. Furthermore, as noted in the Virginia reentry report, returning citizens often lack essential documents and transportation, which can exclude them at the very first step of intake, compounding their marginalization.

# Systemic Issue Fragmented Access

The service delivery system in Richmond for housing, workforce, and reentry support is fundamentally fragmented, creating significant navigation challenges for residents in need. Individuals seeking help are confronted with multiple, parallel access points—including the Homeless Connection Line (HCL), 2-1-1 Virginia, the city's 3-1-1 service, and walk-in centers—without a clear, unified pathway. This fragmentation forces residents to retell their stories and submit to separate intake processes for each agency or service, a process that is both inefficient and traumatizing. For instance, a person facing housing instability who also needs employment support will find limited 'warm handoffs' between the housing system (GRCoC) and the workforce system (Virginia Career Works/OCWB). Similarly, for returning citizens, a Virginia state report explicitly notes there is 'no unified multi-agency data sharing information system' between the Department of Corrections and partner agencies, which severely hinders coordinated reentry planning and service delivery. This systemic fragmentation results in delayed help, administrative churn, and a significant burden placed on the individual to navigate a complex and disjointed web of services.

# Systemic Issue Resource Scarcity

A critical underlying issue that exacerbates navigation difficulties in Richmond is the severe scarcity of essential resources, particularly affordable housing and emergency shelter. The Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) experiences a 'high number of turn-aways for emergency shelter' and maintains a waiting list, especially for homeless families with children. This bottleneck is a direct result of limited shelter capacity. Beyond immediate shelter, the primary barrier to resolving homelessness is the systemic 'lack of affordable housing' and a very low rental vacancy rate, which makes it difficult for providers to place individuals and families into permanent housing. The Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) faces its own constraints, including periodically closing its housing waitlists to new applicants and managing an 'obsolete public housing stock,' further limiting the options for low-income residents. This pervasive resource scarcity means that even when a resident successfully navigates an intake process, they often face long waits for actual services, leading to prolonged instability and straining the entire support system.

# Systemic Issue Data And Trust

Systemic challenges related to data management and trust are a major barrier for Richmond residents, particularly for vulnerable populations like returning citizens and survivors of trauma. The core of the problem is the absence of a 'unified multi-agency data sharing information system' that would allow for secure, consented information transfer between agencies like VADOC, GRCoC, and OCWB. In its place is a fragmented system where individuals are forced to repeatedly disclose sensitive personal information at multiple intake points. This process of 'repeated storytelling' is not only inefficient but can be retraumatizing and significantly erodes user trust. Residents are left with uncertainty about who has access to their data and how it is being used, leading to a justifiable fear of stigma or misuse. This lack of a trusted, privacy-protecting data infrastructure creates a high-stakes environment where individuals may choose to share minimal information, rely on informal advocacy from known navigators, or avoid seeking services altogether until they are in a state of crisis, ultimately delaying help and exacerbating their instability.

# Analysis Of Current Navigation Tools

## Tool Name

Homeless Connection Line (HCL)

## Managing Organization

Greater Richmond Continuum of Care (GRCoC) / Homeward

## Effectiveness

The Homeless Connection Line (HCL) serves as the primary and 'crucial' access point for the region's Coordinated Entry System (CES) for individuals and families experiencing homelessness. It provides a centralized phone number for people seeking shelter and routes them to available shelter and diversion resources. It operates on extended hours (8 AM-9 PM on weekdays, 1 PM-9 PM on weekends), offering more accessibility than standard business hours.

## Limitations

Despite its central role, the HCL is hampered by systemic issues. Users report 'longer wait times' to access emergency beds due to 'capacity bottlenecks' in the system. The line is not a 24/7 service. Most significantly, it is just one of several fragmented access points (alongside 2-1-1, 3-1-1, and walk-in centers) and is not fully integrated with other service systems like workforce or reentry. This means that while it is the entry point for homeless services, it does not prevent a user from having to repeat their information when seeking other types of support. It functions as an intake and referral line, but these referrals can stall due to the severe scarcity of shelter and housing resources, leading to delays and frustration for users.

This provides a valuable external benchmark and underscores the difficulty and importance of the problem rva-helps aims to solve.

# Implications For Rva Helps

## Recommendation Area

User Trust

## Specific Recommendation

Design the system with explicit consent and role-based data sharing capabilities. The tool should provide clarity to the user on who will see their information and for what purpose, building trust and encouraging safe disclosure.

## Pain Point Addressed

This recommendation directly addresses the pain points of privacy-hesitant users, such as returning citizens and survivors, who fear their information will be misused. It mitigates the low trust, stigma, and uncertainty about data use that stems from repeated, uncontrolled storytelling across different agencies.
