# Executive Summary

The City of Richmond, Virginia, demonstrates a commitment to improving resident engagement and planning equity through initiatives like the comprehensive Richmond 300 master plan process and city-wide language access services. The Richmond 300 plan involved extensive outreach, including targeted events in historically impacted neighborhoods and a Spanish-language session, successfully engaging a high percentage of first-time participants in a city planning process. However, a significant transparency gap exists regarding the routine, quasi-judicial planning process. There is no publicly available data detailing the demographic or geographic background of individuals who comment at Planning Commission hearings, nor is there tracking of repeat versus first-time commenters. This makes it impossible to assess whether engagement in these crucial decision-making meetings reflects the city's diversity. Furthermore, while the city acknowledges the digital divide and has language access protocols, there is a lack of specific data mapping these challenges onto high-development areas. Community organizations serve as vital information bridges, but official channels lack specific data on renter participation and language needs tied to active rezoning cases, indicating a disconnect between broad policy goals and the granular data needed to ensure equitable outcomes in specific development projects.

# Key Findings

*   **Planning Commission Demographics:** There is no publicly available dataset from the City of Richmond that provides a demographic or geographic breakdown of public commenters at Planning Commission hearings.
*   **Awareness Channels:** For the Richmond 300 plan, residents learned about proposals through a mix of official city channels (press releases, a 3,490-subscriber email list, social media, meeting announcements) and in-person events. There is no equivalent survey data for routine development proposals.
*   **First-Time vs. Repeat Commenters:** No statistics are published for Planning Commission hearings. However, for the broader Richmond 300 planning process, first-time participants were a significant majority in the first phase of consultation (62%) and a large minority in the second phase (42%).
*   **Engagement in Historically Impacted Neighborhoods:** The city made deliberate outreach efforts for the Richmond 300 plan in areas like the East End, Jackson Ward, and Church Hill. However, there is no official quantitative data to determine if engagement rates in these neighborhoods are lower than the citywide average.
*   **Digital Divide and Development:** While Richmond's City Council has formally recognized the need to close the digital divide, the city has not published any maps or data that overlay census tracts with low broadband penetration onto areas of high development.
*   **Language Access:** The city's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) provides free interpretation and translation services, with in-office Spanish support and other languages available via a third-party service. The Richmond 300 process included at least one Spanish-language sharing session. There is no data available on the most spoken languages specifically within high-development corridors.
*   **Renter vs. Owner Data:** The city's planning documents do not provide a breakdown of renter versus owner households specifically for neighborhoods with active rezoning cases.
*   **Community Information Bridges:** Community organizations play a key role. The Richmond 300 process partnered with numerous civic associations (e.g., Union Hill, Church Hill), faith-based groups, and community events. The Richmond Tenants Union also serves as an active, informal information source for development issues.

# Digital Divide And Development Overlap

## Broadband Data Source

The provided research references the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) via a Richmond Fed publication for general context on urban versus rural broadband access. However, it explicitly notes that Richmond-specific, tract-level broadband adoption maps are not published by the City of Richmond in the planning materials that were reviewed.

## Key Findings

The primary finding is that specific data identifying which census tracts or neighborhoods in Richmond have the lowest broadband penetration is not available in the city's published planning documents. While there is no specific neighborhood breakdown, the Richmond City Council has acknowledged the issue by adopting a resolution to declare high-speed internet a public necessity, signaling a policy intent to close the city's digital divide. A Richmond Fed feature provides broader context, noting that urban households generally have much higher access rates than rural ones, but does not offer a granular analysis within the city itself.

## Overlap Analysis

An evidence-based overlap analysis between low-broadband access areas and high-development corridors or growth nodes is not available in the reviewed City of Richmond documents. The source material explicitly states that this analysis cannot be performed because the City has not published a tract-level data overlay map that would allow for such a comparison.


# Language Access In Planning

## City Policy

The city's policy is its "Language Access Plan (LAP)." Compliance with this plan, as well as a "HUD-COR Settlement Agreement," is part of the "iSpeak Richmond Orientation" for city staff.

## Responsible Office

The City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) is the office responsible for providing and coordinating language access services.

## Services Offered

The City of Richmond provides free interpretation and translation services for residents accessing city services. Specific services include in-office Spanish/English interpretation provided by OIRE staff during office hours, and interpretation for other languages (as well as after-hours Spanish) through a contracted service, United Language Group. The city also conducts an "iSpeak Richmond Orientation" to train staff on compliance, protocols, and tools for interacting with Limited English Proficient (LEP) residents.

## Languages Noted

Spanish is explicitly mentioned as a language for which in-office interpretation is available. The Richmond 300 planning process also included at least one Spanish-language sharing session held on the Southside. While other languages are available via the United Language Group, they are not individually specified in the provided text.

# Richmond 300 Engagement Overview

The public engagement process for the 'Richmond 300' master plan provides the most detailed insight into resident participation in city planning. While specific data for Planning Commission commenters is not published, the Richmond 300 effort details outreach strategies, communication channels, and participant profiles.

**Outreach and Awareness Channels:** The city utilized a multi-channel approach to inform residents. Official city channels include posting Planning Commission agendas, minutes, and material packets on the Legistar portal, with the City Clerk providing general guidance on public comment. For the Richmond 300 plan, outreach was broader, including Mayor's press releases, a substantial email list of over 3,490 subscribers (with an average open rate of 30.5%), social media campaigns on Facebook and Instagram, and announcements during City Planning Commission and City Council meetings. In-person engagement was also key, with forums and sharing sessions held across various neighborhoods.

**Engagement in Historically Impacted Neighborhoods:** There was a deliberate effort to engage residents in areas like Jackson Ward, Church Hill, and the East End. Events were strategically located in these communities, such as visioning sessions at MLK Middle School (East End) and outreach at the 2nd Street Festival celebrating Jackson Ward. The city also engaged with local civic associations like the Union Hill and Church Hill Civic Associations. However, the engagement reports do not publish comparative data to quantify whether these neighborhoods participate at different rates than the city-wide average.

**Language Access:** The City of Richmond's Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) offers free interpretation and translation services for residents accessing city services. This includes in-office Spanish/English interpretation and access to other languages through the United Language Group. As part of the Richmond 300 process, at least one Spanish-language sharing session was held at the Broad Rock Library on the Southside to ensure accessibility for Spanish-speaking residents.

**Community Partnerships:** The city leveraged existing community organizations as informal information bridges. The Richmond 300 team partnered with civic associations, faith-based groups, and ethnic organizations. Examples include the Cedar Street Baptist School Giveaway, the Latino Parade and Festival, Obama Elementary Back-to-School Night, and Innovate Fulton. Separately, the Richmond Tenants Union operates as an active advocacy group, sharing information related to development and tenant rights.

**Identified Data Gaps:** The reviewed materials highlight several areas where data is not publicly available. There is no published demographic or geographic breakdown of commenters at regular Planning Commission hearings. Similarly, the city does not publish tract-level maps of broadband penetration overlaid with high-development areas, making a precise analysis of the digital divide's impact on development awareness difficult. Finally, a consolidated breakdown of renter vs. owner households specifically tied to current, active rezoning cases by neighborhood is not available in the city's published planning documents.

# Data Gaps And Limitations

The available data has significant limitations for assessing planning equity in Richmond's routine development process. The most critical gaps include:

*   **Absence of Planning Commission Data:** There is a complete lack of routinely published data on the demographics (race, ethnicity, age, income) or geographic origin (neighborhood, census tract) of public commenters at Planning Commission meetings. Data on the frequency of participation (repeat vs. first-time commenters) is also unavailable.
*   **Reliance on Proxy Data:** The most detailed engagement metrics come from the one-time Richmond 300 master plan process (2018-2020). While this data is extensive, it cannot be assumed to represent participation in current, individual rezoning and development cases before the Planning Commission.
*   **Lack of Comparative Engagement Rates:** While the city conducted targeted outreach in historically impacted neighborhoods for Richmond 300, the resulting reports do not provide quantitative analysis comparing participation rates from these areas against a citywide average.
*   **No Overlap Analysis for Digital Divide:** The city has not published any analysis or mapping that overlays areas of low broadband penetration with designated growth corridors or areas with high concentrations of development proposals.
*   **Missing Granular Language and Housing Data:** There is no available data specifying the most common non-English languages spoken within high-development corridors, nor is there a published breakdown of renter vs. owner households tied specifically to an up-to-date list of active rezoning cases.

# Overall Analysis Of Planning Equity

An overall analysis of planning equity in Richmond reveals a city with progressive policies and stated intentions that are not yet fully supported by transparent, ongoing data collection. The city's efforts during the Richmond 300 master plan demonstrate a capacity for broad and inclusive outreach, successfully engaging new voices and targeting historically marginalized communities. The existence of the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement and its language access plan is a crucial piece of city-wide infrastructure for equity. 

However, these positive frameworks are undermined by a critical lack of transparency in the regular, legally-binding development review process. Without knowing who is participating in Planning Commission hearings, the city cannot know whose voices are missing. This data gap makes it impossible to assess whether the process is equitable or if it continues to favor those with the time, resources, and knowledge to navigate the system. The acknowledged digital divide, which the City Council is seeking to address, remains a structural barrier to participation, especially as meetings and information move online. Furthermore, the lack of specific data on renters—who may be most vulnerable to displacement from new development—and the language needs within rapidly changing neighborhoods means that outreach may not be effectively reaching those most impacted. While community organizations provide an essential bridge, reliance on them is not a substitute for a systematic, data-driven approach to ensuring all residents can meaningfully participate in shaping their city's future.
