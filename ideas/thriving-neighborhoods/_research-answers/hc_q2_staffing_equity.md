# Executive Summary

The City of Richmond's affordable housing compliance and monitoring framework faces significant challenges related to transparency, staffing, and process efficiency. Key operational details, such as the number of staff dedicated to compliance monitoring, internal processing times for certifications, and data on the geographic concentration of affordable units, are not publicly available. The city relies on what the mayor's office has termed 'labor-intensive manual processes' and a self-reporting model for compliance, which an Office of the City Auditor review suggests requires more formalized policies, clearer roles, and better monitoring. While a defined workflow exists between the Housing and Community Development (HCD) department and the City Assessor's office for the AHTEP program, facilitated by an online portal, the extent of system integration remains unclear. This operational environment raises concerns about potential equity gaps, as cumbersome manual processes may disproportionately burden smaller, community-based developers. Furthermore, tenants in units where affordability restrictions lapse, such as with the AHTEP tax exemption, are left with only standard Virginia landlord-tenant protections, making them vulnerable to significant rent increases.

# Key Findings

## Category

Staffing & Workflow

## Finding Summary

The City of Richmond does not publicly disclose the number of HCD staff assigned to affordable housing compliance monitoring. The Mayor's office has acknowledged that current workflows depend on 'labor-intensive manual processes,' and the city has not published details on the end-to-end workflow or average processing time for AHTF annual certifications.

## Implication

The lack of transparency regarding staffing and workflows suggests potential operational inefficiencies and resource strain. It prevents a clear assessment of the city's capacity to effectively monitor its growing portfolio of affordable housing and makes it difficult for stakeholders to identify and address process bottlenecks.

## Category

Inter-departmental Coordination

## Finding Summary

HCD and the Assessor's Office follow a defined process for the AHTEP program: HCD reviews eligibility and notifies the Assessor to conduct inspections. Applicants use the city's EnerGov Self-Service portal. However, public documents do not confirm if this portal functions as a fully integrated, shared case-management system for tracking handoffs between the departments.

## Implication

While a formal process exists, the potential lack of a deeply integrated shared system could lead to communication gaps, data silos, and delays between departments, complicating the administration of the tax exemption program for both city staff and applicants.

## Category

Data & Transparency

## Finding Summary

Richmond has not published consolidated data or maps showing the neighborhood concentration of AHTF, AHTEP, and EAHP-funded housing. Similarly, there is no public data or analysis on whether compliance failures are concentrated among specific developers or in certain neighborhoods.

## Implication

This absence of data makes it impossible for policymakers, researchers, and the public to evaluate the equitable distribution of affordable housing across the city, identify geographic or developer-specific patterns of non-compliance, and hold repeat offenders accountable.

## Category

Tenant Impact

## Finding Summary

When an AHTEP tax exemption lapses or is terminated, the affordability requirements cease to apply. Landlords can then raise rents subject to standard Virginia law (VRLTA), which requires a 60-day written notice for rent increases but does not include local rent control measures.

## Implication

Tenants in units losing their AHTEP status are vulnerable to sudden and significant rent hikes, which can lead to displacement. Their protections are limited to the statewide minimum, which is not designed to preserve long-term affordability.

## Category

Equity

## Finding Summary

The city has not published a formal study quantifying an equity gap resulting from its self-reporting compliance model. However, official acknowledgements of 'labor-intensive manual processes' and an audit recommendation for more formalized procedures suggest systemic weaknesses exist.

## Implication

The reliance on cumbersome manual processes and self-reporting likely creates a disproportionate administrative burden on smaller, community-based developers who may lack the dedicated staff and resources of larger firms, potentially creating a systemic disadvantage and hindering their participation in affordable housing development.

# Ahtf Annual Certification Workflow

## Workflow Stages

The City of Richmond has not published a detailed, step-by-step internal workflow for processing AHTF annual certifications, such as the reminder cadence, internal review queue structure, or specific sign-off roles. The process is based on a self-reporting model where AHTF fund recipients are required to provide annual certifications of their compliance with all applicable affordability requirements. The certification must be submitted in a form that is acceptable to the HCD during the 15-year affordability compliance period.

## Key Deadlines

The primary deadline for the annual certification process is January 15th. AHTF recipients must submit their certifications by this date each year throughout the 15-year affordability compliance period, which begins upon the completion of the construction.

## Processing Time Estimate

Not published. The City of Richmond has not released information regarding the average or typical time it takes for HCD to process a single property's annual certification submission.

## Reporting Requirements

The core reporting requirement is the submission of 'annual certifications of compliance' with all affordability rules. This certification must be provided in a format deemed acceptable by HCD. This is a mandatory annual requirement for the duration of the 15-year AHTF affordability period.


# Assessor And Hcd Coordination On Ahtep

## Coordination Steps

The coordination process for the Affordable Housing Tax Exemption Program (AHTEP) follows a specific sequence: 
1. An applicant submits an application to the Housing and Community Development (HCD) department.
2. HCD reviews the application for completeness and eligibility.
3. If the application is deemed eligible, HCD notifies the Assessor’s Office that a Base Value inspection is required.
4. The Assessor’s Office schedules and conducts the Base Value inspection.
5. Within 10 business days of the inspection, the Assessor's Office sends a 'Base Value Report' to the applicant with its findings.
6. After the property's construction is complete and a Certificate of Occupancy is issued, the Assessor’s Office conducts a final inspection.
7. Once the final inspection is concluded, the Director of HCD officially grants the tax exemption, which becomes effective on January 1st of the following year.

## Involved Departments

The primary city departments involved in the coordination and administration of the AHTEP are the Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) and the Office of the City Assessor.

## Tracking Method

Applicants are required to create an account and submit their application through the city's online 'EnerGov Self-Service portal'. However, the provided public materials do not explicitly state whether HCD and the Assessor's Office use this portal or another single shared case-management system to track the handoff and subsequent steps of the process between the two departments.

## Key Documents

Several key documents are part of the AHTEP process. These include the initial AHTEP application, the 'Base Value Report' issued by the Assessor's Office after the initial inspection, the property's 'Certificate of Occupancy' which is a prerequisite for the final inspection, and the final grant of exemption from the Director of HCD. Property owners must also file an annual renewal application.

# Tenant Impact Of Ahtep Exemption Lapse

## Rent Increase Rules

If an AHTEP tax exemption lapses or is terminated, the affordability conditions tied to the program no longer apply. Consequently, landlords are permitted to raise rents. The increases are subject to the terms of the existing lease agreement and the overarching Virginia state law. Virginia does not have a local rent-control regime.

## Tenant Notification Requirements

Under the Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA), if a landlord owns more than four rental units (or more than a 10% interest in more than four units), they must provide tenants with written notice of a rent increase at least 60 days before the end of the lease term.

## Governing Legislation

Tenant protections are governed by the statewide Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA). Key provisions include §55.1-1204(K) regarding the notification period for rent increases and §§55.1-1245 and 1252, which stipulate that a landlord must follow the formal court eviction process and cannot unlawfully evict a tenant.


# Equity Analysis Of Self Reporting Model

## Potential Barriers

The primary barrier identified is the reliance on 'labor-intensive manual processes' for compliance. Furthermore, a special review by the Office of the City Auditor (OCA) recommended establishing 'formal policies and procedures that define roles and responsibilities, process steps, timing, definitions, data sources, and monitoring.' The current lack of these formal structures, combined with the self-reporting model where applicants bear the burden of proof, creates a high administrative and documentation burden that can be a significant barrier.

## Affected Developer Types

The source material suggests that the identified barriers and process gaps can 'disproportionately burden smaller, community-based developers.' These developers may lack the administrative staff, legal counsel, and other resources that larger, well-resourced for-profit developers have to navigate complex and labor-intensive reporting requirements.

## Evidence From Guidelines

While there is no quantified study of an equity gap, evidence is found in official city acknowledgements. The Mayor's office has announced measures to 'update workflows and identify necessary technology improvements to reduce reliance on the labor-intensive manual processes.' Additionally, the OCA's findings of inconsistent implementation and its recommendation for formalizing the entire process indicate significant systemic gaps. These process gaps, rather than measured inequitable outcomes, serve as evidence that the current self-reporting model is likely to create an equity gap.

# Ahtep Program Process And Requirements

## Property Eligibility

The provided context does not specify detailed property eligibility criteria such as age or number of units. However, it states that applications are reviewed for completeness and eligibility by the Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD).

## Affordability Requirements

To receive and maintain the tax exemption, properties must remain affordable according to program guidelines. The exemption can be terminated for violations. The specific percentage of affordable units or required Area Median Income (AMI) levels are not detailed in the provided text, but applicants bear the burden of proof to demonstrate that their units are meeting these guidelines during the annual renewal process.

## Application Steps

The application process involves several key steps: 1) Applicants must create an account and submit an application through the city's online EnerGov Self-Service portal. 2) The Department of Housing and Community Development (HCD) reviews the application for completeness and eligibility. 3) If eligible, HCD notifies the Assessor’s Office to conduct a Base Value inspection. 4) The Assessor’s Office schedules the inspection and sends a Base Value Report to the applicant within 10 business days. 5) After construction is complete and a Certificate of Occupancy is issued, the Assessor's Office conducts a final inspection. 6) The Director of HCD then grants the tax exemption, which becomes effective on January 1st of the following year.

## Fees

The provided context does not contain information regarding application fees for single-family or multi-family properties for the AHTEP program.

## Renewal Process

The partial tax exemption is granted for 15 years but requires annual renewal. Property owners must file a renewal application with the Director of Housing and Community Development before the deadline of November 1st each year. During renewal, applicants have the burden of proof to demonstrate that their units continue to meet the program's affordability guidelines.


# Virginia Tenant Protection Laws

## Act Name

Virginia Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (VRLTA)

## Rent Increase Notice

Under the VRLTA, if a landlord owns more than four rental units (or has a greater than 10% interest in more than four units), they must provide a tenant with written notice of a rent increase at least 60 days before the end of the lease term.

## Eviction Procedure

A landlord is prohibited from evicting a tenant without following the legal court eviction process as stipulated by Virginia law. This ensures that tenants are granted due process before being removed from a property.

## Key Code References

Key sections of the Virginia Code mentioned are §55.1-1204(K) for the notification of a rent increase or nonrenewal, and §§55.1-1245 and 55.1-1252, which govern the court eviction process.
