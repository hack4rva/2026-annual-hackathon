# Executive Summary

Nationally, several municipalities have developed tools for affordable housing compliance monitoring, offering potential models for the City of Richmond. These include Denver's Affordable Housing Portal for annual rental compliance reporting, San Francisco's public MOHCD dataset and portfolio dashboard, Chicago's visual ARO Dashboard for public transparency, Los Angeles's LAHD Land Use Covenants registry, and Washington D.C.'s Housing Insights data tool. These systems emphasize public accessibility, standardized reporting, and centralized data management.

A February 2026 special report by Richmond City Auditor Riad Ali identified significant deficiencies in the city's own affordable housing compliance infrastructure. The audit's high-priority recommendations urged the city to take formal legislative action to clarify ambiguous city code, develop formal policies and procedures for monitoring, establish a transparent reporting process to the City Council, and resolve the disposition of $2.47 million that was credited to a special reserve but not properly appropriated to the Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF). The audit also called for enhanced financial oversight, including maintaining a separate AHTF in the accounting structure (which was subsequently established as Fund 0405) and setting a minimum operating reserve for the Delinquent Tax Sales fund.

For developing its own system, Richmond could look to Virginia Housing’s HDS NextGen/WTCMS, a statewide platform for managing tenant certification and compliance, as a structural model. Additionally, open-source frameworks, such as the codebase for D.C.'s Housing Insights, and general analytics tools (like Apache Superset or Metabase) offer pathways for building a custom compliance dashboard. The National Housing Preservation Database (NHPD) also serves as a key resource for tracking federally assisted properties in Richmond. However, there was no verified evidence of a U.S. city adopting a compliance dashboard built during a hackathon, nor was a specific "March 1" tenant data validation deadline for Virginia Housing publicly documented.

# National Compliance Tool Profiles

## City

Denver

## Tool Name

Affordable Housing Portal (rental compliance)

## Description

Denver's Department of Housing Stability operates a compliance reporting portal for covenant-restricted rental properties. Annually, designated property contacts receive a link to this portal to report and validate property- and unit-level data, ensuring ongoing compliance with affordability agreements. The city also provides a public resource hub with training materials and user guides to support partners.

## Key Features

Annual compliance reporting cycle initiated via email with a unique link to a secure portal. The system requires review and validation of property- and unit-level data, with specific required fields marked. It handles utility allowance calculations. Data submitted represents a snapshot as of a specific date (e.g., September 1st). A public-facing asset management and compliance resource hub is also available.

## Data Tracked

Property-level and individual unit-level data for covenant-restricted rental properties. This includes a snapshot of data as of a specific date each year, with required fields for compliance verification.

## City

San Francisco

## Tool Name

MOHCD/OCII Affordable Housing Portfolio dataset and dashboards

## Description

The Mayor's Office of Housing and Community Development (MOHCD) and the Office of Community Investment and Infrastructure (OCII) publish an open dataset and a public dashboard. These tools provide a transparent view of the city's affordable housing portfolio and development pipeline, including units created through the Inclusionary Affordable Housing program.

## Key Features

A publicly available dataset on the DataSF portal, updated quarterly, containing 34 columns of data at the street-address level. A separate Portfolio & Pipeline Dashboard provides visual snapshots of projects that are completed/occupied and those in development.

## Data Tracked

The dataset tracks affordable rental and ownership housing developments, including project status (completed/occupied, in pipeline), geographic location (street address), and units produced through the Inclusionary Affordable Housing Program.

## City

Chicago

## Tool Name

Affordable Requirements Ordinance (ARO) Dashboard

## Description

The Chicago Department of Housing (DOH) developed this public-facing Tableau dashboard to increase transparency and accessibility of data related to the city's Affordable Requirements Ordinance (ARO).

## Key Features

A publicly accessible, interactive dashboard built on the Tableau platform. It organizes and visualizes ARO data geographically, making it easier for the public to understand the ordinance's impact.

## Data Tracked

The dashboard tracks the status and geographic location of projects subject to the Affordable Requirements Ordinance.

## City

Los Angeles

## Tool Name

LAHD Land Use Covenants and related registries

## Description

The Los Angeles Housing Department (LAHD) maintains information on land use covenants that obligate property owners to maintain a certain number of units as affordable for households at various income levels. To enhance visibility and access, LA also operates public registries for affordable and accessible housing.

## Key Features

Maintenance of covenant information for units with affordability obligations. The city also operates the public 'Affordable & Accessible Housing Registry,' which allows users to search for available housing, thereby supporting compliance visibility and tenant access.

## Data Tracked

Land use covenants, including the specified number and type of dwelling units designated for occupancy by extremely low, very low, low, or moderate-income households.

## City

Washington, DC

## Tool Name

Housing Insights

## Description

Housing Insights is a data tool created for affordable housing advocates and government officials to better understand the inventory and condition of subsidized affordable housing in Washington, DC. It functions as a comprehensive database of the city's subsidized housing stock.

## Key Features

A publicly accessible data tool and database that originated as a civic data project (Code for DC). It provides a comprehensive inventory of subsidized affordable housing. While used by officials, it is operated externally and not as an official DC government system.

## Data Tracked

A complete inventory of subsidized affordable housing available in DC, including data on property conditions.


# Richmond Auditor Report Summary

A February 6, 2026, special report from Richmond City Auditor Riad Ali detailed significant failures in the city's management and funding of the Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF). The audit found that the city had largely failed to implement ordinances designed to fund the AHTF. Specifically, revenue from a tax abatement program, which was supposed to be credited to a special reserve and then appropriated to the trust fund, was not handled correctly. The audit highlighted that only in fiscal year 2022 was the required revenue—amounting to nearly $2.5 million ($2,472,307)—credited to the special reserve, but these funds were never actually appropriated or transferred to the AHTF. The report identified the root causes of this failure, including the lack of any implementation plan for the funding process and unclear, vague language in the relevant City Code Section 12-46. Furthermore, the audit raised concerns about the financial health of the Delinquent Tax Sales special fund, the source of the AHTF revenue, noting it was at risk of having a negative balance. The city administration, under Mayor Danny Avula, concurred with all seven of the auditor's recommendations aimed at rectifying these issues.

# Virginia Housing System Analysis

## System Name

Web Tenant Compliance Management System (WTCMS) on HDS NextGen

## Key Features

The system facilitates compliance monitoring through electronic submissions and updates for tenant certifications. Key functionalities include centralized monitoring of tenant data, owner/agent submission portals, and management of the entire compliance portfolio. The upgrade to the HDS NextGen platform brought improvements to the tenant certification update process.

## Relevance For Richmond

Virginia Housing's statewide compliance infrastructure serves as a clear and relevant reference model that the City of Richmond could adapt for its local affordable housing programs. Richmond could model features such as a system for annual property and unit-level reporting, the use of standardized tenant certification data formats, automated validation workflows, and the implementation of deadline-driven compliance cycles to ensure timely reporting from property owners.

## Key Deadlines

The provided source material did not contain an official Virginia Housing document that publicly states a 'March 1' tenant data validation deadline. The available documents regarding the HDS NextGen/WTCMS system and the implementation of HOTMA do not specify this particular deadline.


# Nhpd Overview

The National Housing Preservation Database (NHPD) is a comprehensive national inventory that tracks federally assisted affordable rental housing. Its primary purpose is to support the preservation of affordable housing by identifying and analyzing properties that may be at risk of being lost from the affordable housing stock. The database includes properties subsidized by various federal programs, such as those from the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), and in many states, it also includes properties financed through the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program. Stakeholders use the NHPD to monitor these properties, understand the landscape of subsidized housing, and strategize preservation efforts.

# Richmond Ahtf Funding Context

The issues surrounding Richmond's Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF) stem from a history of administrative and legislative failures to implement funding ordinances as intended. City code stipulated that revenue from a tax abatement program should be placed into a special reserve fund and subsequently appropriated to the AHTF. However, a February 2026 audit found this process was not followed. For years, funds were not properly transferred, effectively commingling them and preventing the AHTF from receiving its dedicated revenue stream. The only instance of partial compliance was in fiscal year 2022, when nearly $2.5 million was credited to the special reserve, but the crucial final step of appropriating and transferring this money to the AHTF never occurred. These failures were attributed to ambiguous language in the city code and a complete lack of a formal implementation plan, which meant there were no defined roles, procedures, or oversight. This systemic breakdown in financial management and compliance created the urgent need for a robust monitoring infrastructure. The audit's recommendations for clearer legislation, formal policies, enhanced oversight, and dedicated financial tracking—including maintaining a separate fund in the city's accounting system—were direct responses to these long-standing problems. In a proactive step, the city did establish a separate fund ('0405' - Affordable Housing Trust Fund) in its financial system in December 2025, acknowledging the need to segregate these funds properly.

# Key Challenges In Compliance Monitoring

The February 2026 audit of Richmond's Affordable Housing Trust Fund (AHTF) highlights several key challenges common in municipal affordable housing compliance monitoring:

1.  **Legislative Ambiguity:** The audit found that the relevant city code itself was unclear, leading to inconsistent application and a failure to meet objectives. A primary recommendation was for the city to "take formal action to resolve the identified issues in City Code Sections 12-46," demonstrating that a lack of clear, actionable legislation is a fundamental barrier to effective compliance.

2.  **Lack of Formal Documented Procedures:** The city lacked a plan and formal policies for implementation. The auditor recommended the development of "formal policies and procedures that define roles and responsibilities, process steps, timing, definitions, data sources, and monitoring." Without these, compliance becomes ad-hoc, dependent on institutional knowledge that can be lost with staff turnover, and difficult to enforce consistently.

3.  **Inadequate Financial Oversight and Controls:** This was a major finding of the Richmond audit. In fiscal year 2022, nearly $2.5 million in tax abatement revenue was credited to a special reserve but was never appropriated to the AHTF as intended. The audit recommended determining the disposition of these funds, maintaining a separate AHTF within the city's accounting structure, and establishing a minimum operating reserve for the Delinquent Tax Sales fund to prevent it from going negative. These issues point to a critical failure in financial tracking and management.

4.  **Lack of Transparency and Accountability:** The audit recommended developing a process to "enhance oversight, accountability, and transparency" and establishing a formal reporting process to the City Council. The absence of such mechanisms means that failures, like the misallocation of funds, can go unnoticed for extended periods. This challenge is what public-facing dashboards, like those in Chicago and San Francisco, are designed to address by making program data accessible to officials and the public.

# Best Practices For Monitoring Systems

Based on the features of national compliance tools and the recommendations from the Richmond City Auditor's report, several best practices emerge for developing and maintaining an effective affordable housing compliance monitoring system:

1.  **Establish Legislative and Definitional Clarity:** The foundation of any compliance system is clear, unambiguous law. As highlighted by the Richmond audit's top recommendation, cities must ensure their codes and ordinances are precise, with clear definitions for terms, obligations, and processes to avoid confusion and non-performance.

2.  **Implement Centralized, Standardized Reporting:** An effective system requires a single source of truth. Denver's Affordable Housing Portal, which requires property contacts to log in annually and validate property- and unit-level data by a specific date, exemplifies this. Similarly, Virginia Housing's WTCMS provides a statewide platform for electronic submissions. This approach ensures data is consistent, timely, and complete.

3.  **Promote Data Transparency and Public Accessibility:** Making compliance data public enhances accountability. Chicago's ARO Dashboard, San Francisco's MOHCD open dataset and portfolio dashboard, and D.C.'s Housing Insights tool all serve to make information about affordable housing projects accessible to officials, advocates, and the public, fostering trust and oversight.

4.  **Develop Formal Policies and Procedures:** To ensure consistency and continuity, especially through staff changes, a system must be governed by documented procedures. The Richmond audit specifically called for formal policies defining roles, responsibilities, process steps, timing, data sources, and monitoring protocols. This formalization is crucial for operational effectiveness.

5.  **Ensure Robust Financial Controls and Dedicated Funding:** Money must be tracked meticulously. Best practices from the Richmond audit include maintaining a separate, dedicated fund for affordable housing in the official accounting structure (like Richmond's Fund 0405), establishing minimum reserve thresholds for related funds, and creating a documented process to track financial transfers from authorization to completion.

6.  **Leverage Existing and Open-Source Technology:** Cities do not need to build systems from scratch. The existence of open-source projects like D.C.'s Housing Insights provides a reusable framework. Furthermore, general open-source dashboarding tools (Apache Superset, Metabase) and data publishing platforms (like Socrata, used by San Francisco) can be adapted to create powerful, cost-effective compliance monitoring dashboards.
