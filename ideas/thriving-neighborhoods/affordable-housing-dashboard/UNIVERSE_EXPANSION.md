# From Internal Audit to Regional Accountability: Scaling Richmond's Affordable Housing Compliance Universe

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The "Affordable Housing Compliance Dashboard" is currently framed as an internal productivity tool for the City of Richmond's Housing and Community Development (HCD) staff to track performance agreements and affordability deadlines. However, research reveals that its true potential lies in transitioning from a siloed monitoring tool to a regional accountability engine. By connecting the City's internal compliance data with the regional infrastructure of the Partnership for Housing Affordability (PHA) and the data-modeling capabilities of HousingForward Virginia, the project can solve the critical "data provenance" problem that currently stalls it. 

The universe for this idea expands significantly when viewed through the lens of federal administrative funding and state-level Planning District Commission (PDC) grants. The recommendation is a Hybrid Ownership Model: the City maintains the "System of Record" for sensitive compliance data, while a regional nonprofit partner hosts the "System of Engagement" for public transparency and developer coordination. This shift moves the project from a "manual lift" fix to a systemic solution for Richmond's "Thriving Neighborhoods" goals, anchoring the dashboard to specific production targets rather than just visualizing static data.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Owned Data / Nonprofit-Led Interface)**

* **What the City Should Own (The Internal Compliance Engine):** The City must own the sensitive database of performance agreements, developer tax IDs, and specific unit-level audit results. This must remain behind the City firewall for legal and privacy reasons. The City's primary responsibility is establishing a "Compliance Data Export" protocol to extract this data from closed systems like EnerGov.
* **What Partners Should Own (The Public Accountability Layer):** A regional nonprofit (such as PHA or PlanRVA) should host the public-facing dashboard that aggregates anonymized data. This ensures the tool survives city leadership transitions, maintains community legitimacy, and can integrate with broader regional housing frameworks.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned (The Resident-Facing Search Tool):** The City is poorly positioned to maintain a "Zillow-style" interface for residents seeking housing. This should be offloaded to a regional partner who can integrate it with the Richmond Redevelopment and Housing Authority (RRHA) waitlist and other social services.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **HousingForward "Sourcebook" (Virginia):** A statewide Tableau dashboard that collects and summarizes housing trends [11]. *Lesson:* Do not reinvent the wheel; Richmond should leverage their existing data cleaning pipeline for ACS and HUD data rather than building a bespoke platform from scratch.
* **Cambridge "Rental Monitor" (MA):** Uses Power BI to track the rental housing market and affordability [17]. *Lesson:* High-frequency updates are required for the data to be actionable for staff and policymakers.
* **Madison "Housing Tracker" (WI):** Anchored to a specific target of supporting 15,000 new homes by 2030 [18]. *Lesson:* Dashboards without a goal line are just spreadsheets with pictures. Richmond must anchor its dashboard to Mayor Avula's MAP targets.
* **Raleigh "Affordable Housing Dashboard" (NC):** Provides real-time updates on housing development and expansion efforts [19]. *Lesson:* Transparency in the development pipeline reduces political friction and builds public trust.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **The "Symptom" vs. "System":** The manual lift experienced by HCD staff is a symptom of fragmented data architecture. If the dashboard only fixes the manual lift for staff, it remains a utility. If it exposes the compliance gap to the public and tracks progress against city goals, it becomes a powerful policy lever.
* **Regional Framing:** Housing markets in Central Virginia are regional. If Richmond builds this alone, it is a small internal tool. If PlanRVA builds it for the region, it becomes a major infrastructure project capable of attracting state-level "Regional Housing Development" funds (up to $1.5M) [3].
* **Nonprofit Layer Agility:** If the City owns the resident-facing layer, it will likely fail due to procurement hurdles and maintenance decay. If a nonprofit like PHA owns it, they can iterate faster, integrate it with community-based resources, and maintain it outside of political election cycles.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Silos & Provenance (High Risk):** Richmond's EnerGov instance is closed and lacks a public API. Without a middleware solution or export protocol, the dashboard will require manual data entry, defeating its core purpose.
* **Covenant Expiration Liability (Legal Risk):** If the dashboard incorrectly flags a property as "in compliance" when it is not, or misses an expiring affordability covenant, the City faces liability and the permanent loss of affordable units.
* **Maintenance Burden (Technical Risk):** Hackathon projects often die without a dedicated Product Manager. Relying on volunteer maintenance is unsustainable for a critical compliance tool.
* **Political Sensitivity (Political Risk):** Publicly showing that a significant portion of a developer's portfolio is "out of compliance" or at high risk may create friction with the development community.
