# Turning Participation Data into Equity-Driving Action: Expanding the Ownership, Funding & Impact Horizon for Richmond's Participation-Analytics Dashboard

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Moving from Headcounts to Equity Infrastructure
The Participation Analytics Dashboard represents a critical shift in civic technology: moving from merely counting how many people attend a meeting to systematically identifying who is missing from the democratic process. While originally conceived as a City of Richmond tool to support the Mayoral Action Plan's Thriving Neighborhoods pillar, framing this solely as a municipal IT project severely limits its potential. To truly close representation gaps, this dashboard must evolve into a regional equity infrastructure supported by a hybrid governance model. 

By expanding the universe of this idea, Richmond can leverage existing regional data hubs like the PlanRVA GeoHub [1] and tap into massive federal funding pipelines explicitly designed for environmental justice and community connectivity, such as the EPA's Thriving Communities Grantmaking Program [2] and the DOT's Reconnecting Communities Pilot [3]. Furthermore, by partnering with established local advocacy groups like Virginia Community Voice [4], the city can offload the political risk of highlighting its own engagement failures while empowering community organizations to trigger targeted outreach playbooks when demographic gaps exceed acceptable thresholds.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

### The Hybrid City-Nonprofit Data Trust Model
The dashboard should not be solely owned by the City of Richmond. Instead, it requires a **Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership** model to ensure data continuity, political independence, and community trust.

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the data collection mandates, the integration with the Open Data Portal [5], and the legislative hooks (e.g., Legistar integration [6]). The city is responsible for mandating digital sign-ins across departments and providing the raw, anonymized geodata.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A coalition of local nonprofits (such as Virginia Community Voice or the Partnership for Smarter Growth [7]) should own the resident-facing analytics layer, the "gap alerts," and the outreach playbooks. They should act as the data trust, interpreting the gaps and holding the city accountable.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own the evaluation of its own engagement success. If the city controls the narrative layer, there will be immense political pressure to suppress data showing severe demographic skew (e.g., if a rezoning meeting is 90% white and wealthy in a diverse neighborhood).

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

### Lessons from Peer Cities and Local Initiatives

* **Austin, TX - Centralized Engagement (PublicInput):** Austin centralized engagement across 42 departments, collecting data from over 91,000 participants and 1.1 million survey responses [17]. *Lesson:* Centralization works and scales rapidly, but requires a unified software procurement strategy.
* **Seattle, WA - Racial and Social Equity (RSE) Index:** Seattle uses a census tract-based tool to identify planning and investment priorities based on demographics [18] [19]. *Lesson:* Tying participation data directly to an established equity index creates an undeniable mandate for targeted outreach.
* **Richmond, VA - Equitable Development Scorecard:** Developed by neighbors on Richmond's Southside, this tool evaluates development equity [12]. *Lesson:* Richmond residents already have an appetite for scoring and evaluating planning decisions; the dashboard digitizes and scales this existing community desire.
* **GRTC Title VI Public Participation Plan:** GRTC conducts "Four-Factor Analysis" using ACS data to discover Limited English Proficiency populations for transit planning [20]. *Lesson:* Regional transit already uses demographic overlays for compliance; the dashboard can automate this process for the city.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

### Moving Beyond Municipal Boundaries
Framing this idea solely as a Richmond municipal project limits its impact. Housing markets, transit lines (GRTC), and environmental impacts do not stop at the city limits. 

* **Regional Frame:** By integrating with PlanRVA's GeoHub [1], the dashboard can track participation across the entire 9-locality Richmond region. This is crucial because regional transportation and housing decisions often suffer from the most severe representation gaps.
* **Statewide Frame:** If successful, the data governance model could be exported to other Virginia localities using the Virginia Geographic Information Network (VGIN) base mapping standards.
* **Nonprofit Interface:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the tool shifts from being a "government compliance dashboard" to a "community accountability engine," drastically increasing its legitimacy and usage by advocacy groups.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

### Navigating the Blockers

* **Data Privacy and Trust:** Collecting geographic data from residents requires strict privacy-by-design. If residents feel tracked, participation will plummet. *Mitigation:* Use aggregate reporting only; adhere to responsible data use playbooks [13].
* **Departmental Silos:** If the Planning Department uses the tool but Parks and Rec does not, the data is incomplete. *Mitigation:* The Mayor's Office must mandate centralized data collection across all departments, similar to Austin's approach [17].
* **The "Decorative Dashboard" Syndrome:** If the dashboard highlights massive equity gaps but the city does not change its outreach tactics, the tool breeds cynicism. *Mitigation:* Hardwire "gap alerts" to mandatory outreach playbooks (e.g., if a gap exceeds 20%, intercept surveying is automatically funded and deployed).
