## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The "Renter-Aware Notifications" initiative possesses the potential to scale far beyond a localized Richmond city government project. By shifting from a municipal-only framework to a hybrid public-private model, the initiative can leverage regional housing coalitions, statewide legal advocacy networks, and federal environmental justice funding. While Virginia Code § 15.2-2204 currently mandates written notice of land use proposals to property owners [1] [2], municipalities have the flexibility to expand these requirements. 

By treating the technical challenge as a commodity (using USPS Delivery Point Validation) and focusing on the operational and trust barriers, Richmond can pioneer a scalable model. Partnering with established tenant advocacy groups like Housing Opportunities Made Equal of Virginia (HOME VA) and the Virginia Poverty Law Center (VPLC) will provide the necessary community trust and legal backing. Furthermore, aligning the project with federal priorities opens access to significant funding streams, such as HUD's PRO Housing grants [3] and the EPA's Thriving Communities Technical Assistance Centers [4].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

To maximize trust, funding eligibility, and operational efficiency, this initiative should not be solely city-owned. A hybrid model divides responsibilities based on institutional strengths:

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must own the legal ordinance amendments, the GIS buffer generation, the USPS DPV address extraction, and the automated trigger within the municipal permitting system (e.g., EnerGov). The city is the authoritative source of zoning data and must maintain the legal baseline for due process.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A coalition of nonprofits (e.g., HOME VA, VPLC) should own the resident-facing communications, the digital opt-in portals, and the community organizing layer. Nonprofits are better positioned to handle multilingual outreach, navigate tenant trust issues, and manage opt-in data without triggering municipal privacy or surveillance concerns.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own the ongoing SMS/email alert databases or the direct tenant organizing efforts, as municipal ownership of tenant data introduces severe privacy liabilities and reduces participation rates among vulnerable populations.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **Virginia Statutory Baseline:** Virginia Code § 15.2-2204 requires written notice of land use proposals to be given to property owners at least five days before a hearing [1] [2]. This establishes the legal floor, but does not prohibit municipalities from expanding notice to occupants.
* **Richmond Regional Housing Framework:** Launched in 2020 by the Partnership for Housing Affordability, this framework outlines regional housing needs and strategies [12] [13]. *Lesson:* Regional coalitions already exist to support housing equity initiatives; the notification tool should be integrated into this broader framework.
* **RVA Eviction Lab Research:** The lab has highlighted Richmond's historically high eviction rates (over 11%) [19]. *Lesson:* Renters in Richmond are highly vulnerable; notifications must be paired with tenant rights information and legal aid resources (like VPLC and LAJC) to be truly empowering rather than just informative.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why a Richmond-only frame is too small:**
Framing this solely as a Richmond municipal project limits its funding potential and ignores the transient nature of the regional renting population. Housing markets and transit corridors cross municipal lines.

**Regional and Statewide Shifts:**
If framed regionally in partnership with the Partnership for Housing Affordability (PHA) [12], the project can tap into regional planning funds and create a standardized GIS notification layer for neighboring counties (Henrico, Chesterfield). If scaled statewide, the open-source GIS/DPV extraction tool could be adopted by the Virginia Department of Housing and Community Development, transforming Richmond from a single implementer into a statewide testbed for civic innovation.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Legal Authority:** While VA Code § 15.2-2204 mandates owner notice [1], opponents may argue against the cost or legality of expanding this. *Mitigation:* Frame the ordinance as an expansion of due process, supported by VPLC legal review.
* **Data Privacy & TCPA:** Collecting phone numbers for SMS opt-ins introduces strict Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) liabilities. *Mitigation:* The City must not hold this data; a nonprofit partner must manage the opt-in database with explicit consent workflows.
* **Community Trust:** Renters may view municipal mailers with suspicion, especially in high-eviction areas [19]. *Mitigation:* Co-brand all materials with trusted entities like HOME VA [8] and LAJC [16].
* **Procurement & Maintenance:** City IT departments often lack the bandwidth to maintain custom civic tech. *Mitigation:* Utilize commodity APIs (USPS DPV) and open-source frameworks (via Code for America [18]) to minimize technical debt.
