# Report Summary

The research findings indicate a systemic exclusion of renters from Richmond, Virginia's neighborhood development process. The city's official notification system is designed exclusively for property owners, meaning the 59% of Richmond households who rent are not directly informed about proposed developments that could significantly impact their homes and communities. The primary pain point for renters is the difficulty and time-consuming nature of staying informed and participating, as they must proactively monitor complex city portals or rely on inconsistent word-of-mouth. For community organizers, the core challenge is the lack of a systematic method to identify and mobilize renters in multifamily buildings, which hinders their ability to ensure the collective voice of tenants is heard. This status quo contributes to renter displacement, erodes trust in local government, and perpetuates inequities by prioritizing the interests of property owners and developers.

# Project Overview

The 'renter-aware-notifications' project is a civic tech initiative in Richmond, VA, operating under the 'Thriving Neighborhoods' pillar. Its primary goal is to make it easier for residents to find and understand development proposals near them. The project specifically aims to address the fact that 59% of Richmond's population are renters and are systematically excluded from the city's official deed-based notification system for new developments. The proposed solution is to generate printable door-hanger PDFs for multifamily buildings located near these development proposals. These materials are designed to be accessible, provided in both English and Spanish, to inform tenants in a timely manner about changes that could affect their community.

# User Personas Overview

The report analyzes user pain points through the Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) framework, identifying three key user personas. The first is the 'functional' renter in an apartment building, whose primary need is to be notified about new developments in a timely and accessible way to understand the potential impact and have an opportunity to participate. The second is the 'emotional' or long-term renter, who feels invested in their neighborhood and needs to feel their voice matters to the city to foster trust and a sense of belonging, which is currently undermined by a lack of direct communication. The third persona is the community organizer, who represents a 'systemic' need for an efficient way to identify and mobilize renters in multifamily buildings to ensure their collective voice is effectively represented in the development process.

# Functional Renter Pain Points

## Pain Point

Exclusion from Direct Notification System

## Evidence Or Source

Richmond's Zoning Ordinance mandates that written notice for many development proposals is mailed only to 'owners of real property within 150 feet' of the subject property. This is confirmed in city ordinances and application forms.

## Current Workaround

Renters are forced to rely on inconsistent and unreliable methods like word-of-mouth from neighbors or community organizations, as they are not included in the official, direct communication channel.

## Cost Of Status Quo

Renters are denied timely, direct information, which prevents them from organizing, advocating for their interests, or preparing for potential displacement. This is especially critical in a city with one of the highest eviction rates in the U.S.

## Pain Point

Onerous and Inequitable Information Discovery Process

## Evidence Or Source

The city's Planning and Development Review (PDR) online portal and its interactive mapping tool are the primary alternatives for information, but they require a level of digital literacy and consistent effort that creates a barrier.

## Current Workaround

Renters must proactively and continuously monitor the city's PDR online portal, which can be difficult to navigate and time-consuming.

## Cost Of Status Quo

This workaround consumes significant time and disadvantages renters with limited internet access or digital skills, a disparity acknowledged in Richmond's Digital Equity Plan. It creates an unequal burden on renters to stay informed.

## Pain Point

Inaccessible Public Hearings and Participation Opportunities

## Evidence Or Source

Planning Commission meetings, where public hearings on development proposals are held, are typically scheduled on Mondays at 1:30 PM.

## Current Workaround

The only way to participate is to attend these meetings, which often requires taking time off from work.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The meeting schedule is prohibitive for many working individuals, including a large portion of the renting population, who cannot afford to miss work. This effectively silences their voices in the formal decision-making process.

# Community Organizer Pain Points

## Pain Point

Inability to Systematically Identify and Contact Renters

## Challenge Detail

Unlike homeowners, whose information is in public property records, there is no centralized, publicly accessible database of renters. This makes it extremely difficult for organizers like the Richmond Tenants Union to know who to contact in large, multifamily buildings.

## Impact On Organizing

This fundamental lack of information is a major hurdle that prevents organizers from efficiently informing renters about their rights, upcoming projects, and opportunities for collective action. It makes targeted outreach nearly impossible.

## Pain Point

Reliance on Labor-Intensive and Ineffective Outreach Methods

## Challenge Detail

Without direct contact information, organizers must resort to time-consuming tactics like door-to-door canvassing. These efforts are often thwarted by restricted access to apartment buildings.

## Impact On Organizing

This inefficiency drains limited volunteer and staff resources. It results in incomplete mobilization, meaning the collective voice of renters remains fragmented and underrepresented in public hearings.

## Pain Point

Lack of Granular Data for Strategic Outreach

## Challenge Detail

While the city provides GIS parcel data, it is not clear if this data includes the number of units within each multifamily building. This specific data point is crucial for planning.

## Impact On Organizing

Without knowing the number of units, organizers cannot accurately estimate the scale of their outreach efforts, allocate resources effectively, or understand the potential number of affected renters. This data gap leads to ineffective advocacy and a failure to grasp the full scope of a development's impact.

# Analysis Of Current Workarounds

Renters and community organizers in Richmond are forced to use a variety of inefficient and unreliable workarounds to stay informed about neighborhood development due to their exclusion from the official notification system. For individual renters, the primary workaround involves actively and continuously searching the City of Richmond's Planning and Development Review (PDR) online portal. This method is described as onerous, difficult to navigate, and time-consuming, placing a significant burden on the resident. While the city provides an interactive mapping tool for pending zoning cases, this still demands a high degree of digital literacy, consistent internet access, and proactive effort that many renters may not possess. The alternative is to rely on informal channels like word-of-mouth from neighbors or community organizations, which is inherently inconsistent and unreliable. For community organizers, such as the Richmond Tenants Union, the lack of a centralized database of renters necessitates labor-intensive outreach methods like door-to-door canvassing, which is not only time-consuming but can be ineffective in buildings with restricted access. The cost of these workarounds is high: renters miss opportunities to engage, leading to potential displacement and housing insecurity, while organizers expend significant resources on outreach that could be better used for advocacy.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

The owner-centric notification system in Richmond creates significant and intersecting equity issues that disproportionately harm marginalized communities. The core problem is that the system, by design, excludes the 59% of Richmond households who rent. This group is not a monolith; renters in Richmond are disproportionately people of color and individuals with lower incomes. Therefore, a system that excludes renters systematically disenfranchises these specific demographic groups from civic processes that directly affect their housing stability and community character. This perpetuates a power imbalance, prioritizing the interests of property owners and developers over a significant portion of the city's population. The consequences are severe, contributing to housing insecurity and displacement in a city that has one of the highest eviction rates in the United States. By making it difficult for renters to participate, the system exacerbates existing racial and economic inequities and fosters an erosion of trust between residents and local government, leaving many to feel that their voices are deliberately unheard and do not matter in the development of their own neighborhoods.

# Language And Accessibility Barriers

Language access and other accessibility issues present significant barriers for renter participation in Richmond's development process. The 'renter-aware-notifications' project itself acknowledges this by providing materials in both English and Spanish, but the research indicates a need to look beyond this initial step. To be truly equitable, the project and the city must consider the other languages spoken in Richmond, and the text suggests leveraging resources like the City of Richmond's Language Access Plan and its Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement to gather data on the specific language needs of different communities. Beyond language, there is a critical physical and temporal accessibility barrier. Public hearings on development proposals at Planning Commission meetings are typically held on Mondays at 1:30 PM. This schedule is prohibitive for a large segment of the population, particularly working individuals who cannot afford to take time off from their jobs to attend and voice their opinions. This scheduling practice effectively disenfranchises many renters and low-wage workers from participating in crucial decisions about their neighborhoods.

# Digital Divide Challenges

The digital divide significantly exacerbates the information gap for renters in Richmond, making the already flawed system of development notifications even more inequitable. The primary workaround for the lack of direct, mailed notice is to proactively monitor the City of Richmond's online Planning and Development Review (PDR) portal and its associated interactive maps. This reliance on digital tools inherently excludes residents who are on the wrong side of the digital divide. Many renters, particularly those with lower incomes, may lack reliable, high-speed internet access at home or the digital literacy required to navigate complex, time-consuming government websites. The research explicitly states that this system 'disadvantages individuals with limited internet access or digital literacy' and requires a 'level of digital literacy and consistent effort that many renters... may not possess.' The city's own Digital Equity Plan acknowledges these existing disparities. By making the only viable alternative to official notification a digital one, the current system compounds the exclusion of renters, ensuring that those who are already marginalized are the least likely to find out about development projects that could lead to their displacement.

# Data Sourcing And Logistical Hurdles

The project faces several practical data and logistical challenges. A primary hurdle is the reliability and frequency of the development proposal data from the city. There is a significant data challenge in using the City of Richmond's GIS parcel data to accurately identify which properties are multifamily buildings and, crucially, to determine the number of individual units within each building. This information is vital for understanding the scale of outreach needed. Logistically, the physical distribution of door-hangers presents its own set of problems. It is a labor-intensive process that can be inefficient and difficult, particularly in large apartment buildings that may have restricted access, preventing organizers from reaching residents' doors.
