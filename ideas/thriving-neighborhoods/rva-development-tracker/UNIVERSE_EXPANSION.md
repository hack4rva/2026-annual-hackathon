# From Hackathon Prototype to Regional Infrastructure: Scaling the RVA Development Tracker

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The RVA Development Tracker is currently a "weekend-viable" prototype that solves a critical transparency gap by merging spatial data (GeoHub) with legislative records (Legistar). However, to move beyond a fragile hackathon project, it must transition from a standalone "app" to a regional data utility. Research into successful models like Colorado Springs and Seattle reveals that the most sustainable path is not building custom software, but creating a "routing layer" within existing ArcGIS ecosystems. 

By shifting the frame from a Richmond-only tool to a regional planning resource managed by PlanRVA, the project unlocks massive federal funding via the EPA and USDOT "Thriving Communities" programs [1] [2]. This expansion addresses the "homeowner capture" risk by moving from a passive "pull" tool to an active "push" system that serves renters and under-resourced neighborhoods through partnerships with civic associations and community-based organizations.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid-Regional**

To ensure long-term viability, the RVA Development Tracker should adopt a Hybrid-Regional ownership model. This prevents the project from dying in municipal procurement cycles while ensuring data authority.

* **What PlanRVA (Regional) Should Own**: The hosting and maintenance of the regional GIS data layers. PlanRVA is the natural "data steward" for the Richmond Metro area and already manages the regional GeoHub [3]. They have the infrastructure to scale this across jurisdictions.
* **What the City of Richmond Should Own**: The "Source of Truth." The City must commit to data hygiene—specifically ensuring that every permit in EnerGov is linked to a geographic coordinate and a Legistar record. The City owns the API endpoints and the nightly ETL processes.
* **What a Nonprofit (e.g., Code for RVA) Should Own**: The user experience (UX) and community-facing features. Nonprofits can iterate faster than government procurement and can lead the "AI summary" experimentation without exposing the City to legal risk regarding zoning interpretations.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned**: The front-end application. Government-owned apps often suffer from "version rot." By keeping the front-end with a civic tech partner, the tool stays modern, open-source, and community-driven.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

Analyzing how other cities have tackled this problem reveals that custom applications are rarely the answer; leveraging existing enterprise GIS is the standard.

* **Colorado Springs Development Tracker**:
 * *What was attempted*: An interactive mapping application allowing users to see development applications under review [19].
 * *What worked*: They used ArcGIS to create a unified dashboard that tracks project progress and upcoming neighborhood meetings [20].
 * *Lesson for this idea*: Do not build a custom app from scratch. Use ArcGIS Experience Builder to stitch together existing systems.
* **Seattle "Shaping Seattle"**:
 * *What was attempted*: A map showing property and building complaints alongside permits [21] [22].
 * *What worked*: Integrating complaints with development activity gives a holistic view of neighborhood changes.
 * *Lesson for this idea*: Residents care about *problems* (complaints/code violations) as much as *proposals*. The tracker should eventually incorporate this data.
* **Boston BPDA Project Browser**:
 * *What was attempted*: A map and database of all Development Projects and Planned Development Areas [23] [24].
 * *What worked*: Clear routing and project search capabilities.
 * *Lesson for this idea*: Visuals and clear project boundaries matter more than raw tabular data for community buy-in.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

**Why Richmond-only is too small:**
Development in surrounding counties (Henrico, Chesterfield) directly impacts Richmond's traffic, housing prices, and infrastructure. A city-only tool misses the "commutershed" reality. Furthermore, federal grants prioritize regional impact over single-city silos.

**The Regional Frame:**
Moving to a PlanRVA-led model allows the tool to cover the entire metro area, transforming it into a "Central Virginia Regional Development Portal." This scale shift makes the project eligible for massive federal technical assistance grants (like the USDOT Thriving Communities Program) that require regional coalitions [1].

**The Nonprofit Layer:**
If the City provides the API and a nonprofit (Code for RVA) provides the App, the project avoids the "procurement death spiral." The city only needs to pilot the data pipeline, while the partner scales the interface, ensuring the tool survives political administration changes.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Silos (High Risk)**: EnerGov (permits) and Legistar (agendas) do not natively communicate. Without a shared Case ID convention, the tool requires manual matching, which will not scale.
* **Data Staleness (High Risk)**: Richmond’s GeoHub "Plans of Development" layer hasn't been updated since June 2022. A tracker built on stale data is functionally useless and damages community trust.
* **Procurement (High Risk)**: The City's IT procurement process could stall the project indefinitely. Keeping the front-end open-source mitigates this.
* **Trust/Hallucination (Moderate Risk)**: AI summaries might misinterpret complex zoning variances, leading to community outrage or legal confusion. AI should be used to tag/categorize, not write definitive legal summaries.
* **Maintenance Burden (Moderate Risk)**: Hackathon projects often die when the lead developer moves on. The code must be transferred to an organizational repository (e.g., Code for RVA).
