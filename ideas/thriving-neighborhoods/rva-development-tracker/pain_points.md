# Executive Summary

Users attempting to track development in Richmond, VA, face significant, well-documented pain points stemming from a fragmented and non-intuitive digital infrastructure. The city's systems require residents, community members, and civic advocates to navigate multiple, disconnected platforms—including the EnerGov/Online Permit Portal, Legistar for meeting agendas, and various separate GIS maps—to piece together a full picture of a single development proposal. This fragmentation makes it difficult to discover nearby projects, verify facts, and coordinate timely civic engagement. Key challenges include non-standard search functionalities that require specific knowledge, a multi-step process to find agendas and materials, and a lack of integrated, proximity-based alerting. This difficult user experience creates a significant time burden, fosters reliance on rumor, erodes public trust, and creates barriers to the equitable civic participation that the city aims to foster.

# Resident Functional Job Analysis

## Persona

Richmond resident/neighbor

## Job To Be Done

To have a simple, unified way to discover, map, and track new development proposals near their address, enabling them to understand potential impacts and engage in the decision-making process.

## Pain Point Description

Residents face significant hurdles in tracking local development due to a fragmented and non-intuitive digital infrastructure. There is no single, map-based view to see all nearby proposals; instead, information is scattered across separate tools like the Parcel Mapper, Zoning Mapper, and the EnerGov Permit Portal. The primary search portal, EnerGov, has a finicky and non-standard search function that requires users to know specific formatting rules, such as omitting street types. Discovering meeting agendas and related materials in the Legistar system is a multi-step process requiring users to navigate calendars and apply filters. Crucially, there are no built-in, proximity-based alert systems to automatically notify residents about new proposals in their vicinity.

## Evidence

Evidence for these pain points is found across official city documentation. The city's own 'helpful links' page lists four separate mapping and permit tools, demonstrating the fragmented discovery process [1]. The official user guide for the Online Permit Portal (OPP) explicitly instructs users on the non-standard address search format: 'only use the Street number and Street Name... DO NOT use the street type' [2]. The Planning Commission's webpage directs users through a complex, multi-step process to find agendas in Legistar, involving specific view and filter selections [3]. The absence of any mention of proximity-based notifications in the EnerGov or Legistar guides confirms the lack of automated 'near me' alerts [2, 3].

## Current Workaround

To overcome these challenges, residents must engage in a series of inefficient manual tasks. This includes manually searching the EnerGov portal using its specific address rules, bookmarking and checking multiple different city mapping websites, and regularly visiting the Legistar calendar to check for agendas and meeting packets for various commissions and boards. They are forced to track important dates and deadlines themselves, often using personal calendars or notes.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The current system imposes a significant time burden on residents who wish to stay informed. The complexity and fragmentation make it easy to miss hearing dates, especially when agendas are posted across different bodies or when meeting access is limited to specific technologies like Microsoft Teams, which requires advance knowledge. This ultimately results in a lost opportunity for residents to provide public comment and participate in decisions that directly affect their neighborhoods before they are finalized.


# Community Member Emotional Trust Job Analysis

## Persona

Concerned community member

## Job To Be Done

To find a neutral, plain-language source of truth about a local development project, with official links and a clear history, in order to feel confident they have facts rather than relying on rumors from social media or word-of-mouth.

## Pain Point Description

Community members struggle to trust the information they find about development projects due to its complexity and dispersion. Verifying the actual status of a project requires cross-referencing multiple official systems, such as EnerGov for case status and Legistar for hearing materials, which increases confusion. Accessing public meetings is not a simple, one-click process; instructions often point to separate audio streams and Microsoft Teams links that must be located within the correct calendar item. While the city has a language access policy, these services are not integrated into the case discovery workflow, requiring residents with limited English proficiency to proactively contact a separate office (OIRE). Furthermore, statutory public notice minimums, which focus on property owners, often fail to reach all affected stakeholders like renters and nearby neighbors, fueling rumor cycles.

## Evidence

The difficulty in verifying information is evident in the need to consult both the EnerGov portal for case details and Legistar for meeting agendas and materials [1, 2, 3]. Meeting access instructions on Legistar specify that audio is streamed via the website while participation is through a separate Microsoft Teams link, creating a two-step process [4, 5, 6]. The city's 2023 Language Access policy and the OIRE's service page confirm that free interpretation is available, but it is a service to be requested rather than being proactively offered alongside case information [7, 8]. Finally, Virginia's notice statute emphasizes written notice to property owners and agents, which can inadvertently exclude a wider circle of community members [9].

## Current Workaround

To gain confidence in information, community members must manually download and read through dense agenda packets from Legistar. For language assistance, they must know to call or email the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE). Often, they first hear about projects through informal channels like social media and then attempt to backfill their understanding by navigating the difficult official websites to find primary source documents.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The high friction in accessing and verifying official information leads to lower trust and confidence in the public process. It encourages a reliance on hearsay and second-hand information, which can be inaccurate or incomplete. This ultimately results in residents missing the opportunity to participate in public hearings because they discovered the project too late or could not navigate the unclear instructions for engagement.


# Civic Advocate Coordination Job Analysis

## Persona

Civic advocates and neighborhood leaders

## Job To Be Done

To have a consolidated timeline of all official actions related to a development proposal (e.g., application, staff reports, meeting dates, decisions) in order to effectively coordinate community input and engagement.

## Pain Point Description

Civic advocates face systemic inefficiencies when trying to organize community responses to development. There is no consolidated timeline or workflow; case information is split between EnerGov (for permits, plans, inspections) and Legistar (for agendas, packets, action summaries), with contextual data living in yet other GIS viewers. Retrieving materials is record-centric, requiring advocates to know specific case identifiers or arcane search rules to find documents. Monitoring progress is cumbersome, as the main Legistar calendar lists dozens of city boards and committees, forcing leaders to manually filter and search across dates to track a single project's journey, increasing the risk of missing a key step. This stands in stark contrast to peer cities like Pittsburgh, which have launched consolidated public-facing portals.

## Evidence

The fragmented workflow is documented by the city's own web pages, which point to separate systems for permits (EnerGov) and legislative actions (Legistar) [1, 2, 3]. User guides for Richmond's portal and comparable systems emphasize the need for precise address formats or record numbers to find information, and then require users to click through to an 'Attachments' section to find documents [2, 11]. The Legistar calendar itself shows the long list of bodies that must be monitored [5]. The benchmark gap is highlighted by Pittsburgh's launch of 'OneStopPGH,' a public-facing map that consolidates datasets for permits, zoning, and enforcement into a single, user-friendly platform—the exact kind of tool advocates in Richmond lack [10].

## Current Workaround

To manage this complexity, advocates and neighborhood leaders resort to manual, time-consuming methods. They build their own spreadsheets to track case milestones, subscribe to multiple city meeting calendars, and manually share links and updates within their neighborhood networks via email or social media.

## Cost Of Status Quo

The status quo incurs a high cost in coordination time and volunteer effort. The manual tracking process increases the likelihood of missing critical comment windows or hearing dates, weakening the community's voice. It also leads to duplicated effort as multiple groups or individuals independently try to track the same complex process. Neighborhoods with less volunteer capacity or technical expertise are disproportionately affected.

# Systemic Failures And User Workarounds

## System Name

Richmond City's Public Information Systems (EnerGov, Legistar, GIS Mappers)

## Failure Description

Information regarding local development proposals is highly fragmented across multiple, non-integrated city systems, making it difficult for residents to discover and track projects. There is no single, unified map or portal that provides a comprehensive view. The city's own 'RVA 311 helpful links' page directs users to separate, distinct websites for the 'Permit Portal - Energov,' 'Parcel Mapper,' 'Zoning Mapper,' and 'Neighborhood Mapper.' This siloed approach forces residents to know which specific tool to use for a particular piece of information and prevents a simple 'near me' discovery process for all related development activities.

## User Workaround

To build a complete picture of a single development proposal, a user must engage in a cumbersome, manual process of cross-referencing multiple official websites. This involves searching the EnerGov portal (using its specific, non-standard search rules like omitting street types), separately consulting various GIS maps to understand spatial context, and then navigating the Legistar system to find related Planning Commission meeting agendas, staff reports, and hearing dates. This workaround requires users to manually track case IDs and dates across these disparate systems, often using their own spreadsheets or calendar reminders.

## Evidence Source

City of Richmond's 'RVA 311 helpful links' page and the accompanying research synthesis describing the fragmented discovery process across multiple tools.


# Cost Of The Status Quo

## Wasted Resident Time

Residents lose significant time and effort due to the fragmented system. They must perform manual workarounds, such as learning the non-intuitive search rules for the EnerGov portal (e.g., omitting street types), bookmarking and cross-referencing multiple GIS mappers (Parcel, Zoning, Neighborhood), and repeatedly checking the Legistar calendar for different commissions and committees. Finding meeting agendas and materials on Legistar is a multi-step process that requires users to know which specific commission to check and how to filter the calendar. Accessing meetings themselves is not straightforward, often requiring users to find specific Microsoft Teams links or know that audio is streamed separately via Legistar. These hurdles disproportionately affect residents with limited time or digital literacy. Furthermore, Virginia's statutory notice laws, which focus on property owners and formal publication, can fail to reach renters and other nearby community members, effectively excluding them from the process until it's too late.

The status quo undermines Richmond's stated goals for equitable development and inclusive engagement, as outlined in plans like the Richmond 300 Master Plan. The city's 2023 Language Access Policy commits to making services accessible, yet language assistance is not integrated into the development tracking workflow; residents must know to proactively contact the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE). Neighborhoods with less volunteer capacity are more likely to miss key deadlines and lose their voice in decisions, exacerbating existing inequities.


# Evidence From Richmond Sources

Evidence from Richmond-specific sources reveals a fragmented and challenging environment for residents trying to track local development. The city's own resources point users to multiple, disconnected tools, including a Parcel Mapper, Zoning Mapper, Neighborhood Mapper, and a separate Online Permit Portal (EnerGov), with no single, unified view. User complaints and documented procedures highlight specific frustrations:

1.  **Fragmented Discovery and Non-Intuitive Search:** The official user guide for the Online Permit Portal (EnerGov) explicitly instructs users to omit street types (like 'Rd' or 'Ave') when searching for an address, a non-standard requirement that creates a barrier to entry. There is no single "near me" map that consolidates proposals from different systems.

2.  **Complex Agenda and Document Retrieval:** To find information on Planning Commission meetings, residents are directed to the Legistar calendar, where they must use a multi-step process involving the 'List View' and 'Search Calendar' features to locate specific dates and documents. This cumbersome process is required to find agendas, meeting materials, and access links.

3.  **Meeting Access Hurdles:** Accessing meetings is not straightforward. Instructions indicate that audio is streamed via Legistar, but participation often requires a separate Microsoft Teams link found within the specific calendar entry for that meeting. This multi-step process can be confusing and deter participation.

4.  **Lack of Proximity-Based Alerts:** None of the existing city systems (EnerGov, Legistar) offer a feature for residents to receive automatic, proximity-based notifications for new development cases near their homes. Residents must proactively and manually check multiple calendars and case records.

5.  **Siloed Language Access:** While the City of Richmond has a Language Access Policy (2023) and provides free interpretation services through the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE), this service is not integrated into the development tracking workflow. Residents, particularly those with Limited English Proficiency (LEP), must be aware of this separate service and proactively seek it out, rather than being prompted or offered assistance directly on case pages or meeting notices.

6.  **Insufficient Legal Notice:** Virginia's state law sets minimums for public notice that focus on publication and written notice to property owners and agents. This can leave many stakeholders, such as renters and nearby community members not on the direct mailing list, unaware of proposals, leading to a reliance on rumors and social media.

# Data Accessibility And Reliability Issues

An investigation into Richmond's public data sources reveals significant issues with both accessibility and reliability from a user's perspective. While data is technically public, its accessibility is severely hampered by systemic fragmentation. Information is spread across the EnerGov permit portal, the Legistar system for legislative records, and multiple separate GIS mapping applications, with no unified interface. Usability issues create further barriers; for example, the EnerGov portal requires users to follow non-standard address search formats (omitting street types), and the Legistar system requires a complex, multi-step process to filter and find specific meeting agendas and documents. Furthermore, there are no proactive notification features like 'near me' alerts, forcing residents to manually and repeatedly check for updates. While the city has a language access policy and offers free translation services, these are not integrated into the data portals, requiring residents with limited English proficiency to know about and contact a separate office (OIRE) for assistance. From a reliability standpoint, the primary issue is the lack of a single source of truth. Because data is siloed, residents cannot rely on any one system to provide a complete and accurate picture of a development's status, timeline, and associated documents. This forces users to cross-reference multiple official systems, increasing the chance of confusion and reducing confidence in the information, which in turn fuels reliance on rumors and social media.

# Technical Integration Challenges

The primary technical challenge in building the proposed `rva-development-tracker` is the need to integrate data from multiple, disparate, and likely non-API-driven city systems. The project's goal of deep-linking to official records faces hurdles, as systems like Legistar and EnerGov are designed for human navigation, not programmatic access. For instance, finding a specific agenda item or case attachment may require scraping web pages and simulating a user's journey of filtering calendars or clicking through record details, a process that is brittle and prone to breaking when the host sites are updated. The existence of stable URLs for meetings (e.g., `https://go.rva.gov/CPC2026`) is helpful, but linking to the specific document or case file within that meeting's context remains a challenge. Similarly, while the EnerGov portal integrates ESRI map functionality, embedding an official, interactive GIS map that combines layers from the city's various mappers (Parcel, Zoning, Neighborhood) into a single view would be difficult. It would likely require accessing and combining several underlying GIS data services or files, which may not be publicly exposed for this type of third-party use. The fundamental challenge is that the proposed tracker must create the data aggregation and correlation layer that the city has not yet built, effectively reverse-engineering connections between permits in EnerGov, hearings in Legistar, and spatial data in GIS systems.

# Project Differentiation From Prior Art

The proposed `rva-development-tracker` is fundamentally different from existing tools like the Richmond Regional Development Tracker because its primary goal is to support resident engagement with official city workflows, not to serve as a regional marketing tool. While other trackers may focus on showcasing completed projects or attracting investment, this project is designed specifically to solve the functional and coordination pain points residents face when interacting with the city's fragmented systems. It would improve upon the status quo by unifying discovery into a single, map-based interface that merges case status from EnerGov with meeting milestones from Legistar. Its key differentiators would be providing 'near me' alerts, auto-generated plain-language summaries with direct links to official documents, and coordination tools like watchlists for neighborhood groups. Unlike a general information portal, it is conceived as a trust layer and an engagement tool that directly addresses the documented failures in the current public-facing systems, making the development process more transparent, accessible, and navigable for the average resident.
