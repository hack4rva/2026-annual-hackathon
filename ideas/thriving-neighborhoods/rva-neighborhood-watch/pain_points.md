# Output Report

## Summary Of Findings

The core problems for Richmond residents trying to track neighborhood development stem from a fragmented and difficult-to-navigate information ecosystem. Information is scattered across multiple, non-integrated city systems, including the Online Permit Portal for permits, Legistar for legislative documents, and separate GIS mapping tools. These systems feature hard-to-search records with non-intuitive query behaviors that can lead to false negatives, forcing residents to use specific, unforgiving search formats or visit city offices in person. A significant issue is the lack of proactive, geo-targeted alerts for development proposals. The city relies on statutory public notices, which involve newspaper ads and mailed notices to a very limited group of property owners with as little as a five-day lead time, effectively excluding most renters and nearby residents from early engagement. Furthermore, all materials are presented in technical, bureaucratic language without plain-language summaries, creating a high barrier to understanding for the average layperson.

## Resident Persona Analysis

The general 'Richmond resident' persona, whose goal is to get a clear, map-based summary of nearby development proposals to decide on engagement, faces several significant pain points. 

1. **Fragmented Systems and Scattered Information**: Residents cannot find a single, unified source for development information. They must independently navigate the Online Permit Portal for permits and inspections, the Legistar system for Planning Commission agendas and case documents, and separate GIS mapping tools. The city's own instructions highlight this complexity, directing users through a multi-step process to find meeting information on the Legistar calendar, which is described as: "click here to view the Legistar calendar. Within the ‘Legistar Calendar’… click on the ‘Search Calendar’… using preset values… ‘current year’ and department ‘Planning Commission’."

2. **Difficult-to-Search Case Records**: The city's primary portals are not user-friendly. The Online Permit Portal's search function is brittle; the city's FAQ warns that searches can fail unless users avoid punctuation and abbreviations, and it acknowledges a known issue with single-digit addresses, advising residents to "come into the office until this problem is resolved." For rezonings and Special Use Permits (SUPs), residents are directed to Legistar, a legislative tracking tool, rather than a resident-focused, map-based browser.

3. **Lack of Proactive, Geo-Targeted Alerts**: The system is reactive, placing the burden of discovery on the resident. Public notice is governed by Virginia Code §15.2‐2204, which mandates newspaper ads and mailed notices to only the property owner, abutting properties, and properties directly across the street. This notice can be provided with a minimum of only five days before a hearing, which is insufficient time for meaningful review and engagement. This process inherently misses the vast majority of a neighborhood's stakeholders, including renters and those who live nearby but not immediately adjacent to a project.

4. **Technical Language and Process Unfamiliarity**: The available materials are not designed for laypeople. They are housed in technical and legislative portals and lack plain-language summaries or map-based visualizations. The city's own 'Code Refresh' initiative acknowledges the need to correct historic disparities stemming from complex zoning policies, indicating an awareness that the current system is not accessible to all.

**Current Workarounds and Cost of the Status Quo**: Today, residents must manually and repeatedly check multiple websites, rely on word-of-mouth from neighbors, or hope to see information on neighborhood social media groups. This system breaks down because there is no unified, resident-facing map that aggregates all development activity. The cost of this status quo is significant: residents who are not in the narrow abutter radius often learn about projects too late to provide early, constructive input. This pushes engagement to the final, more contentious public hearing stages, increasing mistrust between residents and developers and leading to less collaborative outcomes.

## Renter Persona Analysis

The 'long-time resident/renter' persona, who seeks transparent and trustworthy information about development in their community, faces pain points that erode trust and create barriers to participation.

1. **Lack of Plain-Language, Centralized Summaries**: Trust is undermined when information is hard to find and harder to understand. Case materials are distributed across the Online Permit Portal, Legistar, and GIS, with complex instructions geared towards administrative workflows, not resident comprehension. This opacity makes residents feel that information is being hidden, even when it is technically public.

2. **Language Access Barriers**: For residents with Limited English Proficiency (LEP), the barriers are even higher. Richmond's Language Access Plan identifies public hearing notices as "vital documents" that should be translated. However, the plan also notes significant challenges in serving all communities, such as "finding interpreters… for… Mixteco." While the city is supposed to inform residents of their right to a free interpreter, gaps in implementation mean LEP residents can be effectively excluded from understanding development proposals in a timely manner.

3. **The Digital Divide**: The city's reliance on online portals like the OPP and Legistar presumes residents have reliable internet access and the digital literacy to navigate them. A City Council resolution (RES. 2024‐R026) calling for a "Digital Equity Assessment and Implementation Plan" is a formal acknowledgment that such access is not universal in Richmond, indicating that a significant portion of the population is cut off from the primary channels of information.

4. **Exclusion from Direct Notification**: State law (Va. Code §15.2‐2204(B)) mandates that mailed notices are sent to property "owners." This systematically excludes renters, who may have lived in a neighborhood for decades but have no legal right to direct notification about a project next door. They are left to learn about potentially impactful changes through rumors, yard signs, or second-hand from their landlords.

**Current Workarounds and Cost of the Status Quo**: This system breaks for renters, LEP residents, and those on the wrong side of the digital divide. They are forced to rely on informal networks like neighbors and community associations, or ad hoc translations from community groups. The cost is lower and later participation in the civic process. Decisions feel opaque and are made without the input of a significant part of the community, which breeds resentment, fuels conflict, and can lead to preventable appeals and project delays.

## Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

Several cross-cutting equity dimensions significantly impact who can participate in Richmond's development review process.

*   **Language Access**: Richmond's Language Access Plan designates public hearing notices as "vital documents" requiring translation and affirms the right to free interpretation. However, it also acknowledges practical challenges in providing interpreters for all languages, such as Mixteco. This indicates that non-English speaking residents, particularly those from smaller linguistic communities, face substantial barriers to understanding and participating in decisions affecting their neighborhoods.

*   **Renters and Non-Owner Residents**: The state-mandated public notice process, as defined in Va. Code §15.2‐2204(B), is centered on property owners. Mailed notices are sent to the owners of the subject and adjacent properties, systematically excluding renters from direct, official notification. This disproportionately affects lower-income residents and communities of color, who have higher rates of renting.

*   **Digital Divide**: The city's increasing reliance on online portals for disseminating information and conducting business creates a digital divide. City Council Resolution 2024‐R026, which calls for a Digital Equity Plan, is an official acknowledgment that unequal access to high-speed internet and digital literacy skills are significant problems in Richmond. This prevents residents without reliable access from monitoring development activity, accessing documents, or participating in virtual meetings.

*   **Historically Underrepresented Groups**: The city's 'Code Refresh' initiative explicitly identifies the need for special outreach to "historically underrepresented stakeholders, such as Latino residents, renters, African American residents, youth, low-income residents." This reflects a recognition that the status quo processes for planning and zoning have historically failed to engage these groups, leading to development patterns that have created and reinforced disparities.

## Solutions From Comparable Cities

Analysis of comparable cities reveals several successful models that Richmond could adopt to address its identified pain points.

*   **Durham, NC**: Durham offers an opt-in email "Public Notification Service" for all planning-related hearings. This service explicitly goes "beyond state law requirements" and is not restricted by distance, meaning any interested person can subscribe to receive weekly or monthly updates on all scheduled cases. This provides a strong precedent for proactive, geo-agnostic, and regular notifications that place a low burden on residents.

*   **Raleigh, NC**: Raleigh has implemented an online "Rezoning Engagement Portal." This platform allows residents to read plain-language summaries of active rezoning cases, view questions and comments from others, and submit their own feedback directly to planning staff while cases are still under review. This serves as a model for creating a two-way, case-specific engagement hub that centralizes information and facilitates early input.

*   **Pittsburgh, PA**: Pittsburgh is cited as a reference for consolidating its development review workflow. The city provides central public notices and utilizes a "OneStopPGH" online portal for application submissions, suggesting a more integrated, public-facing hub for development-related information and processes.
