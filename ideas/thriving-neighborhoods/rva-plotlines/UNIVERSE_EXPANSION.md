# From Map Dots to Neighborhood Narratives: A Strategic Roadmap for RVA Plotlines

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

RVA Plotlines represents a critical technical leap in Richmond's civic infrastructure, but it currently exists as a "historical archive" rather than an "actionable tool." To fulfill Mayor Danny Avula's Mayoral Action Plan (MAP) pillar of Thriving Neighborhoods [1] [2], the project must shift from merely showing what happened to informing residents when and how to act. The data exists within the Legistar Events API, but the "narrative gap" remains the primary barrier to equitable participation. 

The universe for this idea extends far beyond a city-hosted map. By positioning RVA Plotlines as a Regional Transparency Engine, the project becomes eligible for high-value federal grants like HUD's PRO Housing program [3] [4] and USDOT SMART grants [5] [6]. The optimal path is a Hybrid Ownership Model: the City of Richmond maintains the data pipeline, while a regional nonprofit or quasi-public body manages the resident experience layer to ensure the tool remains politically neutral, multi-lingual, and accessible to the 59% of Richmonders who rent.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid (City-Provisioned / Nonprofit-Led)**

* **What the City Should Own:** The Back-end Data Pipeline. The City's IT/GIS department should ensure the Legistar and EnerGov APIs are stable, geocoded, and public-facing. This treats civic data as essential infrastructure.
* **What a Nonprofit Partner Should Own:** The Resident Experience (UX/UI). A nonprofit or regional body (like PlanRVA) is better positioned to iterate quickly, support multiple languages, and build trust with marginalized communities who may be skeptical of government-run portals.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The Community Commentary/Chat Layer. To avoid First Amendment complications and the burden of moderating political speech, the discussion or AI Chat features should live on a non-governmental platform.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing RVA Plotlines solely as a Richmond project is too small because zoning and development decisions are inherently regional. A large development in neighboring Chesterfield County directly impacts Richmond's infrastructure, traffic, and housing prices, but would not appear on a city-restricted map. If the frame shifts to a regional model owned by a body like PlanRVA, the tool becomes a "Central Virginia Development Tracker." This unlocks regional transportation grants and makes the platform indispensable for regional planners and developers. Furthermore, if a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the tool can integrate "Advocacy Steppers" (e.g., "Click here to email your council member") that a city government cannot legally or politically host.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Fragility:** The system relies on third-party APIs (like Legistar). If the City changes vendors, the scraper will break.
* **NIMBY Weaponization:** Making it too easy to oppose development could inadvertently stall affordable housing projects. The AI summaries must include city-wide impact context.
* **AI Hallucination:** Using AI to summarize dense legal text carries high risk. A hallucinated hearing date or zoning characterization could lead to legal liability or community distrust.
* **Maintenance Burden:** Civic tech projects often rot after the initial launch if they lack a dedicated, funded maintainer.
