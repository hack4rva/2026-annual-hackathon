# Executive Summary

Research into the user experience of Richmond's development-related civic data portals reveals significant pain points stemming from a fragmented and technically complex information ecosystem. Users must navigate multiple, disparate city systems—including various ArcGIS mapping applications, the EnerGov Online Permit Portal, and the Legistar legislative portal—to piece together a complete picture of any single development project. This fragmentation creates distinct challenges for different stakeholders. Neighborhood association leaders face a time-consuming functional challenge, manually cross-referencing jargon-heavy documents and maps with uncertain data timeliness to brief their members. Long-time renters in gentrifying areas confront an emotional and trust deficit, struggling to find clear, trustworthy narratives about how projects will impact their community, leading to fear and disengagement. Small developers grapple with a systems and coordination problem, lacking a single, chronological view of a project's journey, which introduces schedule risks and administrative burdens. The core opportunity for the rva-plotlines project is to act as a crucial integration and translation layer, unifying these scattered data sources into a single, coherent, and accessible platform that provides plain-language summaries, clear timelines, and actionable information for all community members.

# Neighborhood Association Leader Analysis

The Neighborhood Association Leader persona is driven by a functional job-to-be-done: when a new development like a rezoning or special use permit is filed in their area, they need to quickly understand its tangible impacts—such as changes in building height, land use, parking, and project timelines—in a clear, plain-language format, preferably on a map. The ultimate goal is to effectively brief their association members and make an informed decision on whether to submit formal comments or attend a Planning Commission hearing. However, the current system presents significant obstacles. Key pain points include the fragmentation of information across multiple, separate city tools like various ArcGIS apps (Parcel, Zoning, Development, Land Use) and the EnerGov permit portal. This is compounded by uncertainty about the timeliness and status of the data, as the city itself notes that some maps are only 'updated periodically.' Furthermore, portals like EnerGov are jargon-heavy and process-centric, focusing on internal case types and workflows rather than the real-world impacts of a project, which creates a steep learning curve. This forces leaders into inefficient workarounds, such as manually cross-referencing information between the different mapping tools, then separately searching the EnerGov permit portal and the Legistar legislative portal to find agendas, staff reports, and meeting outcomes. The cost of this status quo is high: it involves time-consuming lookups, carries the risk of missing key hearings or relying on stale data, and makes it difficult to succinctly brief community members without first translating complex technical documents. This creates an 'information architecture inequity,' privileging experienced users with the time and skills to navigate these systems while burdening volunteer neighborhood leaders.

# Long Time Renter Analysis

The 'Long-time Renter' persona in gentrifying areas of Richmond is driven by an emotional and trust-based Job-to-be-Done (JTBD). When they encounter a notice about a new development, their primary motivation is to find a clear, trustworthy, and non-technical story about its potential impact on their community, specifically concerning rents, traffic, and local services. This need is rooted in a desire to feel included in decisions that directly affect their lives and to mitigate fears of displacement. The current system fundamentally fails to meet this need. There is no clear, non-technical narrative that connects a development proposal to its likely community impacts. Instead, information is scattered across technical and inaccessible source documents on platforms like EnerGov and Legistar, which are designed for process management, not for telling a story about community impact. This lack of a coherent narrative reduces trust and comprehension. Furthermore, the data's reliability is questionable; for instance, the city's Development Mapper warns that it is only 'updated periodically,' which undermines residents' confidence that they have the latest information. Faced with these barriers, renters resort to workarounds like asking neighbors or scanning social media for information, which can be unreliable or incomplete. The cost of this status quo is significant: it fosters low confidence in the civic process, creates fear of missing critical opportunities for input, and leads to inequitable participation. The exclusion is particularly acute for residents with Limited English Proficiency (LEP) and those who are digitally disconnected, as materials may not be translated or accessible even when city policies for language and digital access exist.

# Small Developer Analysis

For the 'Small Developer' persona, the primary systems and coordination job-to-be-done is to maintain a single, chronological, and geographically-anchored view of a project's entire journey through various city agencies, including the Planning Commission, Board of Zoning Appeals (BZA), and City Council. This unified view is critical for coordinating with stakeholders and mitigating surprises. However, the current system is broken, lacking a single, chronological, or geospatial 'project journey' view. This fragmentation, combined with a history of significant permitting delays, elevates coordination risks. Small developers are forced to adopt manual and inefficient workarounds, such as maintaining separate spreadsheets to track project progress, constantly checking multiple disparate systems like EnerGov for permit status and Legistar for legislative outcomes, and proactively following up with city staff if promised timelines are not met. The costs associated with this inefficiency and process opacity are substantial, leading to increased carrying costs, significant schedule risks, potential for missed milestones, and duplicated efforts due to the fragmented tracking of information across unlinked platforms.

# Systemic Failures And Workarounds

The core systemic failure across Richmond's civic tech landscape is the fragmentation of critical development information across multiple, non-integrated platforms: EnerGov for permits, Legistar for legislative records, and a suite of separate ArcGIS apps for mapping. This structure breaks the user's ability to get a single, coherent, and plain-language view of a development project. There is no unified "project journey" that combines parcel context, proposal details, review milestones, and legislative outcomes. This forces all user types—from neighborhood leaders to small developers—into inefficient workarounds. They must manually cross-reference information by searching for case numbers or addresses across the different portals, piecing together the story of a project from jargon-heavy, process-centric documents. The costs of this status quo are significant: it consumes a vast amount of time for users, introduces the risk of relying on stale or incomplete data (as some maps are only updated 'periodically'), and creates a high barrier to entry for civic participation. This information architecture inequity privileges experienced users with the time and skills to navigate the complexity, while effectively disengaging or excluding residents, renters, and those with fewer resources, ultimately undermining trust and equitable participation in the development process.

# Energov Portal Issues

## Primary Issue

Process opacity and historic permitting delays/backlogs.

## User Impact

The portal's design, which emphasizes internal case types and application workflows, imposes a steep learning curve on lay users like residents and neighborhood leaders, making it difficult to find and interpret relevant project information. For small developers, the historic backlogs, which peaked at nearly 1,200 applications, created significant project uncertainty, increased carrying costs, and schedule risks. The need for applicants to proactively monitor their own applications and contact city leaders if timelines slip indicates a lack of reliable, automated tracking and accountability within the system.

## Documented Evidence

Evidence includes the EnerGov Online Permit Portal User Guide, which illustrates the multi-step, case-type-driven workflows. Additionally, a November 2022 Richmond BizSense article details the city's efforts to reduce a permit backlog from a peak of nearly 1,200 applications, acknowledging a prior 'poor reputation'. A March 2022 WTVR report notes that a sitting City Councilmember personally experienced these permitting delays, corroborating long-standing complaints from the development community.


# Legistar Portal Issues

## Primary Issue

Inaccessible, technical, and fragmented legislative information.

## User Impact

Because formal agendas, staff reports, and meeting outcomes are housed in Legistar, separate from the mapping and permitting tools, users are forced to jump between systems. The documents themselves are often jargon-heavy and not designed as plain-language narratives, which prevents residents, renters, and neighborhood leaders from easily understanding the real-world impacts of development proposals. This lack of accessible information reduces trust, comprehension, and the ability for community members to participate meaningfully in hearings or provide informed feedback.

## Documented Evidence

The primary evidence is the structure of the system itself, where legislative records for the Planning Commission and City Council are located in Legistar, requiring a separate navigation pattern from EnerGov or ArcGIS. The pain points described for neighborhood leaders and renters highlight that these artifacts are not plain-language narratives about community impacts (like changes to rent, traffic, or services), but rather technical documents focused on procedural steps.


# Arcgis Mapping Tool Issues

## Primary Issue

Data and tool fragmentation, coupled with data currency uncertainty.

## User Impact

Users must consult multiple, separate city ArcGIS applications, each with different data layers and functionalities. This fragmentation causes confusion and makes it difficult to get a comprehensive view of a property or project. Furthermore, the city explicitly notes that some maps, like the Development Mapper, are only 'updated periodically,' which undermines user confidence and creates uncertainty about whether they are viewing the most current and accurate information for nearby development cases.

## List Of Fragmented Tools

The city's Interactive Mapping Tools page and ArcGIS Online portal list several separate, non-integrated applications that users must navigate, including the Parcel Mapper, Zoning Parcel Mapper, Development Mapper, Land Use Project Mapper, Zone Lookup, and the general Richmond Zoning Map.


# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

## Digital Divide Impact

The reliance on a suite of online-only tools for accessing development information systematically excludes residents who lack reliable, affordable, and high-speed internet. As of September 2024, Richmond's City Council was still actively pressing for a concrete plan to close the city's digital divide, a goal first established in a 2021 resolution. This ongoing gap means that residents without adequate digital access are unable to participate in the development process, track projects in their neighborhoods, or access vital documents, creating a significant equity risk where civic engagement is predicated on internet connectivity.

## Language Access Gaps

Despite the City of Richmond having a formal Language Access Plan (2020) and an Administrative Regulation (2023) that designates public hearing notices as 'vital documents' requiring translation, there is a risk of inconsistent execution. These policies commit the city to providing meaningful access for Limited English Proficiency (LEP) residents through translated materials and multilingual web tools. However, the research suggests that if these policies are not consistently implemented and monitored across all departments and platforms (like EnerGov, ArcGIS, and Legistar), LEP communities are effectively excluded from understanding and participating in development decisions that directly impact their lives.

## Displacement Risk Factor

Information asymmetry is a significant contributor to gentrification fears and displacement risk, particularly for long-time renters. The current system, with its fragmented, jargon-heavy, and process-centric portals, fails to provide clear, non-technical narratives about a project's potential impact on community factors like rent, traffic, and local services. This opacity breeds mistrust and anxiety, leaving residents to rely on informal networks like social media. Without accessible and trustworthy information, residents cannot effectively advocate for their interests, making them more vulnerable to the pressures of development-driven displacement.

## Most Affected Populations

The failures of the current system most negatively impact residents who lack the time, resources, or specialized knowledge to navigate its complexity. This includes long-time renters, particularly those in gentrifying neighborhoods who are concerned about displacement. Residents with Limited English Proficiency (LEP) are another key affected group, as they are dependent on the city's consistent execution of its language access policies to participate. Furthermore, residents on the wrong side of the digital divide, who lack reliable and affordable internet access, are systematically excluded from the primarily online civic information ecosystem.

# Recommendations For Rva Plotlines

## Recommendation

Build an integration and translation layer that achieves four key objectives: (a) unifies data from EnerGov, various ArcGIS layers, and Legistar milestones into a single, geocoded project timeline; (b) generates plain-language summaries of project details (e.g., height, uses, parking, timelines) and provides a clear status; (c) surfaces key dates for meetings, public participation windows, and final outcomes; and (d) supports multilingual content and low-bandwidth access to align with the city's language access and digital equity goals.

## Target Persona

Long-time renter in a gentrifying area

## Solves Pain Point

This recommendation directly solves the core pain points of technical and inaccessible source documents and unclear data currency. By providing a 'clear, trustworthy, non-technical story of what’s proposed,' it replaces the need to navigate complex portals like EnerGov and Legistar. This addresses the user's low confidence and fear of missing critical meetings, empowering them to feel included in decisions that affect their community.
