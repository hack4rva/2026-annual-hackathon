# Executive Summary

Richmond residents and neighborhood leaders face significant challenges in tracking local development projects due to the city's fragmented and opaque data systems. There is no single, authoritative source to see what projects are proposed, their current status, the relevant decision-making body, and how to participate. Residents must navigate a complex web of separate systems, including the Legistar calendar for meeting agendas (which are split by department like the Planning Commission and Commission of Architectural Review), the EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP) for submission records (which has its own search quirks), and various GIS mapping tools, one of which is currently being updated. This fragmentation is compounded by technical jargon (SUP, rezonings, site plans) and a lack of clarity on how proposals align with the Richmond 300 Future Land Use plan. The burden of piecing this information together leads to a high cost for civic engagement: residents miss opportunities to comment, neighborhood leaders experience burnout from manually tracking multiple cases, and a general trust gap emerges due to inconsistent information. The lack of a simple, map-based, resident-friendly discovery tool means that access to information is inequitable and dependent on an individual's technical savvy and available time.

# Resident Persona Analysis

A Richmond resident seeking to understand and participate in local development decisions faces a series of significant hurdles due to a fragmented and opaque information ecosystem. Their journey is characterized by confusion and inefficiency, starting with the lack of a single, centralized source for project information. 

Key pain points include:

*   **Fragmented Information Sources:** There is no unified calendar or platform. Residents must navigate between multiple city websites and systems. For instance, meeting agendas for the Planning Commission, Commission of Architectural Review (CAR), and Board of Zoning Appeals (BZA) are all on Legistar, but exist as separate entries, requiring users to manually cross-reference different pages to get a complete picture of activities in their area. 

*   **Opaque Processes and Jargon:** The language and procedures of urban planning are not easily accessible. Residents encounter technical terms like 'SUP' (Special Use Permit), 'rezonings,' and 'site plans' without clear, plain-language explanations. It is difficult to determine which government body is responsible for a specific decision, leading to confusion about which meeting to attend to be effective.

*   **Multiple, Disconnected Systems:** The problem extends to the digital tools themselves. A resident might have to search for project records in the Online Permit Portal (EnerGov), find the related hearing on a Legistar agenda, and then try to locate the property on a separate GIS mapping tool. These systems do not communicate with each other, have inconsistent search functions and identifiers, and create a high-friction experience.

*   **Difficulty Connecting Plans to Reality:** Residents struggle to understand how a specific development proposal aligns with the city's long-term vision, the 'Richmond 300' Future Land Use (FLU) plan, versus the legally binding current zoning. The FLU plan is described as visionary, not a legal entitlement, making it difficult for a layperson to quickly assess if a project is 'consistent' with the plan.

*   **Lack of User-Friendly Discovery Tools:** There is a significant gap in resident-friendly, map-based discovery tools. The city's official 'Land Use Project Mapper' is noted as being under an update, and existing tools are not designed for simple 'what's near me' queries. This void is partially filled by third-party media trackers, but these are not official, comprehensive, or integrated with the city's public comment process, leading to inequitable access for less tech-savvy residents.

# Resident Process Breakdown

The current process for a Richmond resident to find and understand information about a single development project is fundamentally broken by fragmentation. There is no unified, authoritative starting point. A resident's journey is a confusing and manual process of piecing together clues from multiple, disconnected city websites and databases. 

This journey typically involves:
1.  **Searching for Records:** The resident might start at the Online Permit Portal (EnerGov) to search for plans and permits, a system with specific search quirks that can be difficult to navigate (e.g., not using street types in addresses).
2.  **Finding the Hearing:** They must then separately check the Legistar calendar to find the relevant meeting agenda for the Planning Commission, Commission of Architectural Review, or another body. There is no direct link from the project record in EnerGov to the meeting agenda in Legistar.
3.  **Understanding the Location:** To understand the project's context, the resident has to use yet another system, the city's GIS mapping tools, which have their own interfaces and may be out of date (e.g., the 'Land Use Project Mapper is being updated').

What breaks is the absence of a unified, map-based discovery layer that connects a location to its active cases, status, next hearing, and instructions on how to act. Furthermore, the process for interpretation is broken; residents lack a quick, authoritative translation of what a case is, how it relates to city plans versus zoning law, and who the ultimate decision-maker is. This forces residents into a high-friction, time-consuming, and often unsuccessful search for basic information.

# Resident Workarounds And Costs

In the absence of a streamlined and centralized system, Richmond residents are forced to employ a variety of manual and inefficient workarounds to track development projects. These workarounds include manually scanning multiple department calendars and individual meeting agendas on the city's Legistar site, a tedious and error-prone task. Residents also attempt to search for records on the Online Permit Portal (EnerGov) and then separately try to find corresponding meeting information. 

When official channels are too confusing, residents turn to informal ones, such as asking neighbors on social media to gain clarity or information. They may also rely on third-party, non-authoritative sources like the media-powered BizSense Development Tracker to get a map-based view of projects, though this lacks official process integration. To understand complex documents like the Richmond 300 plan, they must read the dense narrative and attempt to infer how a specific case aligns with it.

The cost of this status quo is significant. For the resident, it results in a massive time burden and high cognitive load, effectively creating a barrier to civic participation. This leads to missed items and short notice windows, increasing the chance of late discovery when it's more difficult to influence an outcome. The confusion over processes and jargon can lead residents to attend the wrong meeting or misinterpret a project's status. Ultimately, this friction erodes trust in the public process and creates inequitable access, as only those with the time, technical savvy, and persistence can navigate the system, leaving many others without a voice.

# Systemic Issue Fragmented Data

## Issue Description

Development-related information in Richmond is highly fragmented, with no single source of truth for residents. Decision-making bodies and their schedules are scattered, and records are stored in multiple, disconnected systems. To understand a single project, a user must piece together information from various city web pages and databases, each with its own interface and search quirks. This creates a significant barrier to understanding what is happening in their neighborhood, as there is no consolidated 'case hub' that links a project's location, details, hearing schedule, and public comment instructions.

## Involved Portals

A user must check several separate portals to get a full picture of a development project. These include: the Legistar calendar for agendas from the Planning Commission, Commission of Architectural Review (CAR), Urban Design Committee (UDC), and Board of Zoning Appeals (BZA); the EnerGov Online Permit Portal (OPP) for plan submissions and public records, which has specific search rules; various GIS mapping tools, some of which are noted as being under updates; and separate pages like the PDR Site Plan page.

## Consequence

The direct result of this data fragmentation is a lack of a single, authoritative source for development information. This leads to significant user confusion, a high time investment to manually correlate data across systems, and a greater chance of missing important updates or comment deadlines. The use of inconsistent identifiers between systems creates friction and increases the risk of missing status changes. Ultimately, this fragmented system undermines public trust and leads to missed opportunities for civic participation.


# Systemic Issue Technical Jargon

## Issue Description

The official documents and portals related to development in Richmond are filled with technical jargon, acronyms, and opaque process descriptions that create a significant barrier to public understanding. Residents are confronted with specialized language without clear, plain-language explanations, making it difficult to grasp the nature of a proposal, which governing body is responsible, and how to engage effectively. This issue is compounded by the complex relationship between the visionary Richmond 300 Future Land Use plan and the legal power of current zoning, which is a source of confusion for the public.

## Jargon Examples

Specific examples of jargon and acronyms found in Richmond's development process include: SUP (Special Use Permit), LC&E, BZA (Board of Zoning Appeals), CAR (Commission of Architectural Review), UDC (Urban Design Committee), rezonings, and site plans. Additionally, residents struggle to understand the distinction and relationship between the Richmond 300 Future Land Use (FLU) plan and a property's actual zoning.

## Impact On Accessibility

This specialized language makes critical information inaccessible to the average resident, effectively excluding those without a background in urban planning or real estate. The impact is confusion over which city body is responsible for which decision, difficulty determining the correct meeting to attend, and misinterpretations of proposals. This can lead to disengagement, polarized debates based on misinformation, and an inequitable system where only the most knowledgeable or well-resourced residents can participate meaningfully.

# Cross Cutting Equity Analysis

The current system for tracking development in Richmond creates significant equity barriers, disproportionately excluding specific segments of the population. These barriers are not uniform and impact residents based on language, digital access, and available time and resources.

*   **Language Access Barriers**: The city's planning artifacts, Legistar agendas, and technical portals like EnerGov are predominantly English-first. This effectively excludes Limited English Proficient (LEP) residents from participating, forcing them to rely on intermediaries. While the city has a Language Access Plan and the Office of Immigrant and Refugee Engagement (OIRE) provides some services, these are not integrated into the core digital infrastructure, placing the burden of access on the user.

*   **Digital Navigation Burden**: The requirement to navigate multiple complex systems with non-intuitive interfaces creates a digital divide. The Online Permit Portal (OPP) has specific search rules that can frustrate even tech-savvy users, and the need to cross-reference information across GIS maps, Legistar, and the OPP disadvantages residents with limited digital literacy, older devices, or unreliable internet access. This digital friction means that access to crucial civic information is not equitable.

*   **Time and Capacity Constraints**: The fragmented nature of information and short notification windows for meetings place a heavy time tax on participation. This disproportionately affects renters, shift workers, caregivers, and others who cannot dedicate significant time to manually polling calendars and deciphering agendas. It also creates inequity between neighborhoods; those with well-resourced neighborhood associations and experienced leaders are better able to navigate the system, while others are left behind, leading to volunteer fatigue and unequal advocacy outcomes.

To address these inequities, the analysis suggests several cross-cutting interventions: providing multilingual summaries and meeting guides, offering SMS/email alerts to reduce the calendar-checking burden, creating printable one-pagers for offline sharing, ensuring a mobile-first and low-data UI, and standardizing plain-language templates for case summaries to reduce the reliance on volunteer translation labor.
