# SIMBY — Soon In My Backyard

**Team:** Singlestone
**Pillar:** Thriving Neighborhoods
**Tagline:** Soon, in our backyard.
**Hackathon:** Hack for RVA

---

## The Problem

> "Richmond is changing, block by block — but for most residents, the first time we hear about a change is when a project is already underway, and our only recourse is to shout 'Not in my backyard' to no avail."

The current system for community engagement in neighborhood development is broken:
- Residents find out about changes too late to shape them
- The language used in planning documents is inaccessible to most people
- Engagement is concentrated in one loud public meeting or among the small percentage of residents (estimated at 0.2%) who actively post on platforms like the RVA subreddit
- City staff are burdened with translating, summarizing, and running additional meetings to gather community input

---

## Solution

**SIMBY (Soon In My Backyard)** brings planned development and neighborhood requests into one place, in plain language, so residents can engage early and clearly.

When more residents can understand what's coming and speak up in their own words:
- The city gets a **clearer signal of what neighborhoods actually need**
- Without additional workload on city staff — no need to translate, summarize, or run more meetings

---

## Demo Walkthrough

### Sign-Up
- Takes seconds with minimal information required
- Users set their **preferred language** at sign-up (English, Spanish, Arabic, and others)

> "If you can't read it, you can't shape it."

### Planning View — What's Coming to Your Neighborhood
- Right after sign-up, the user lands on the **planning view of their own neighborhood**
- Shows all planned development projects nearby

#### Tapping on a Project
Each project reveals:
- **Status**
- **Key dates**
- **Photos** of the project or site
- A **plain-language summary** of what's happening — written so anyone can understand it without planning expertise

#### Multilingual
- If Spanish or Arabic was selected at sign-up, all project information appears in that language

### Requests View — Design Your Neighborhood
A shift from reacting to actively shaping:

- Users can **drop a pin** on the map for something they want in their neighborhood (e.g., a grocery store, a coffee shop, a park)
- Residents can write requests and comments **in their native language** — no translation required on their end
- The request system accepts any written language, enabling full participation from every community

### The Neighborhood Signal — Zoomed Out
Zooming out on the map reveals the aggregate picture:
- Every request, upvote, and comment becomes a **neighborhood-level signal**
- Shows: what people want, where they want it, and why
- The map was seeded with **big ideas from the Richmond 300 planning process** — so it starts with real community priorities rather than a blank slate

---

## Notifications

Residents are **notified based on their zip code** when:
- Projects in their neighborhood change status
- Public meetings are scheduled for projects in their area

This means more people show up to meetings **early, prepared, and represented** — rather than hearing about decisions after the fact.

---

## Who Benefits and How

| Stakeholder | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Residents | Understand what's planned before it's locked in; can advocate in their own language |
| City | Clearer, more representative community signal; less staff burden for translation and meetings |
| Developers | Can build with **real community demand** rather than guessing at what neighborhoods want |
| Entrepreneurs | Can **spot opportunity** and invest with confidence based on visible community needs |

---

## The Bigger Vision

> "SIMBY starts with early understanding of what's being planned, but it ends with something bigger. Neighborhoods clearly signaling what they need — the voice of citizens becoming a tailwind for progress."

> "When neighborhood needs become clear, they become actionable."

Instead of one loud meeting or the 0.2% of residents who post online, SIMBY creates a **living, representative pulse of a neighborhood** — a continuous, multi-language signal of what communities actually want.
