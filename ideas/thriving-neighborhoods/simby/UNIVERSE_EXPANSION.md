# Scaling SIMBY: From Richmond Pilot to Regional Equity Infrastructure

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

SIMBY (Soon In My Backyard) represents a critical evolution in civic engagement, moving from reactive "NIMBY" shouting matches to proactive, multilingual neighborhood co-design. While the hackathon prototype demonstrates a "Blue Ocean" shift in resident-led requests and accessibility, its long-term viability is currently threatened by a fragile data foundation. To survive, SIMBY must transition from a standalone Richmond app into a regional civic infrastructure tool that serves as the "engagement layer" for federal infrastructure compliance.

The "universe" for SIMBY expands significantly when framed as a compliance and equity engine for multi-million dollar federal grants from agencies like USDOT, HUD, and the EPA. By shifting to a hybrid ownership model—where the City of Richmond and PlanRVA provide automated data feeds while a regional nonprofit manages the community-facing interface—SIMBY can bypass the "civic tech graveyard" of manual data entry and procurement bottlenecks, unlocking federal planning dollars while giving residents a genuine voice in their neighborhoods.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid Regional/Nonprofit Partnership**

* **What the City/Region (PlanRVA) Should Own:** The **Data Pipeline**. The City must provide stable, machine-readable APIs (Legistar for legislation, GeoHub for permits). PlanRVA should act as the regional convener to ensure cross-jurisdictional data standards and integration into regional transportation studies.
* **What a Nonprofit Partner Should Own:** The **User Experience and Community Trust**. A nonprofit (e.g., a dedicated civic tech organization or a community-based coalition) should manage the app interface, multilingual translations, and resident notifications. This ensures the tool remains "of the people" and can iterate faster than city procurement allows.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The **Resident Request Layer**. To ensure residents feel safe sharing "big ideas" or critiques without fear of government surveillance or immediate dismissal, the "Signal" data should be curated by a third party before being presented to city planners as an aggregate report.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **PublicInput (VDOT Engagement Hub):** The Virginia Department of Transportation uses PublicInput as an online resource for outreach to shape transportation [20]. *Lesson:* State agencies are already paying for digital engagement hubs; SIMBY must differentiate through its multilingual, resident-first "request" layer rather than just top-down information sharing.
* **Grand Junction "Chip Games":** Ran 27 workshops where residents physically placed density chips on maps to co-design scenarios. *Lesson:* Resident co-design builds consensus better than top-down models. SIMBY digitizes this concept.
* **Boulder ArcGIS Urban Pilot:** Proved that co-designed scenarios build consensus, while black-box models generate opposition. *Lesson:* Transparency and participatory modeling are essential for trust.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

* **The "Richmond-Only" Frame (Too Small):** If framed only as a Richmond tool, SIMBY is a "nice-to-have" dashboard that will struggle for budget priority against essential services and will likely die due to manual data entry fatigue.
* **The "Regional Engagement" Frame (Just Right):** By framing SIMBY as the Regional Signal Layer, it becomes a shared utility for Richmond, Henrico, and Chesterfield. This aligns with PlanRVA's regional transportation and resilience funding [23] [18].
* **The "Federal Compliance" Frame (The Power Move):** If SIMBY is the tool that proves "Meaningful Community Engagement" for a USDOT SS4A or RCP grant [4] [1], the tool pays for itself by unlocking federal infrastructure capital.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Data Drift (Critical):** If the City does not commit to an API-first approach (Legistar/GeoHub), SIMBY will require manual updates, which is the #1 failure mode for civic tech.
* **The "Loudest Voice" Bias:** Even with multilingual support, digital tools can be dominated by tech-savvy residents. Mitigation requires partnering with physical community hubs.
* **Procurement Bottlenecks:** City Hall's procurement cycle could kill momentum. A nonprofit fiscal sponsor is needed to accept foundation grants and iterate quickly.
* **Trust and Legitimacy:** If residents feel their "pins" go into a black hole, they will abandon the platform. The feedback loop must be visible.
