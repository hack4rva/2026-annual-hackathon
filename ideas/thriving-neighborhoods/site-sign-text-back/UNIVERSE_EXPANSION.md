# Text-Back Zoning Signs: Scaling Richmond's Neighborhood-Info Service Beyond the City

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

A city-nonprofit hybrid model, funded through a mix of Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) planning funds, state digital-opportunity initiatives, and foundation grants, can launch the nation's first "text-case-number" zoning-info service. While a nationwide sweep found that 0% of U.S. municipalities currently use SMS on zoning signs—favoring QR codes instead—this reliance on QR codes excludes the approximately 15% of adult Virginians without smartphones or reliable data plans [1]. 

By positioning SMS as an equity layer rather than just a technological novelty, Richmond can deliver neighborhood transparency that reaches low-income renters and seniors. The technical feasibility is exceptionally high, with major vendors like Granicus and Accela already supporting two-way SMS APIs [2] [3]. By partnering with a local civic-tech nonprofit to handle the SMS platform and user support, the City of Richmond can bypass lengthy procurement cycles, mitigate public records retention risks, and create a scalable model that can eventually expand to the entire PlanRVA region.

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: City-Nonprofit Hybrid**

To maximize speed, community trust, and grant eligibility, this idea should not be solely city-owned. Instead, it requires a hybrid governance structure:

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond must retain ownership of the underlying data (via the Legistar API) [4], the physical zoning signs, the policy oversight, and the approval of the sign templates. The city is also responsible for ensuring that the data provided to the API is accurate and timely.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A Richmond-area nonprofit (such as a local civic tech collective) should own and operate the resident-facing SMS platform (e.g., Twilio integration), manage the 10DLC registration process [5] [6], handle user support, and maintain the automated archiving pipeline required for public records compliance.
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The city should not own the custom SMS software middleware or the direct carrier contracts for the pilot phase. Municipal procurement for custom software can take over 12 months, whereas a nonprofit can act as a grant-bearing implementer to launch the pilot in 30-90 days.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

Municipalities have attempted to digitize zoning signs, but almost exclusively through QR codes, leaving a gap for SMS innovation.

* **Raleigh, NC:** Deployed QR codes on Public Notice Signs linking to a "Current Development Activity" page [19]. *Lesson:* Quick deployment, but excludes residents without smartphones or data plans. SMS must be the primary accessibility channel.
* **Portland, OR:** Neighborhood Contact sign templates require URLs and email addresses for developers and neighborhood associations, but lack SMS options [20] [21]. *Lesson:* Standardized templates work for compliance, but fail to provide instant, mobile-first engagement for passersby.
* **Durham, NC:** Offers a Planning & Development Public Notification Service via email, but explicitly states that "text messages (SMS) are not available for this subscription due to extra charges" [22]. *Lesson:* Cost perception is a major barrier; securing government/nonprofit 10DLC pricing is critical.
* **GRTC (Richmond, VA):** Successfully operates a text-stop-ID bus tracker where users text "GRTC [stopID]" to receive arrival times [12] [13]. *Lesson:* Richmond residents are already trained to use SMS short codes for place-based civic information.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond neighborhood project limits its potential. 

* **Regional Frame:** If expanded to the PlanRVA region, the service could leverage a shared Legistar-to-GeoJSON ETL pipeline, multiplying its reach to all development projects in the metro area and feeding data into regional public engagement platforms [14] [23].
* **Statewide Frame:** A statewide approach enables uniform sign language and economies of scale in 10DLC registration [5]. It also aligns perfectly with Virginia's Digital Opportunity initiative, positioning the project for larger state and federal (HUD) funding matches [9].
* **Ownership Shift:** If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing SMS layer while the city provides the data, the project isolates technical and compliance risks (like TCPA) away from the city, allowing for rapid iteration and scaling that municipal procurement cannot support.

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **TCPA and 10DLC Compliance:** The FCC's Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) strictly regulates automated texts [24] [25]. Furthermore, carriers require A2P 10DLC registration for applications sending SMS to the US, which adds setup time and costs [5]. *Mitigation:* Use Twilio's government/nonprofit onboarding track and embed explicit opt-in language [6] [26].
* **Public Records Retention:** The Library of Virginia requires retention of public records, including text messages, though there is no single retention period for all texts [17] [27]. *Mitigation:* Build an automated archiving pipeline that stores SMS metadata securely to meet LVA standards.
* **Cost and Procurement:** As seen in Durham, SMS incurs per-message charges [22]. *Mitigation:* Utilize a hybrid model where a nonprofit absorbs the software procurement and carrier costs via grant funding.
