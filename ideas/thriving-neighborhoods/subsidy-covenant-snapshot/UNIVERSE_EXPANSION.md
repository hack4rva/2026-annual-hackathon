# Affordable-Housing Covenant Radar: Expanding the Subsidy-Covenant-Snapshot Beyond Richmond

## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Subsidy & Covenant Snapshot is a high-potential civic innovation that can transcend Richmond's city limits by tapping into regional housing ecosystems and national data infrastructure. Currently framed as a local compliance tool, its universe expands significantly when repositioned as a regional "expiration radar" for affordable housing preservation. By leveraging national datasets like the National Housing Preservation Database (NHPD), which already tracks "Earliest End Date" and "Latest End Date" for subsidized properties [1] [2], Richmond can bypass the bottleneck of manual county recorder searches [3]. 

To ensure long-term viability and avoid the maintenance pitfalls that have doomed similar projects in other cities [4], the tool should not be solely city-owned. Instead, it requires a hybrid ownership model where the City of Richmond provides the foundational GeoHub parcel data [5], while a regional nonprofit or quasi-public entity like PlanRVA or Enterprise Community Partners manages the public-facing portal. This shift not only unlocks state-level capacity-building grants from Virginia Housing [6] but also aligns with regional goals to secure $18.5 million in annual federal-aligned housing funds [7].

## 2. EXPANDED OWNERSHIP RECOMMENDATION

**Recommended Model: Hybrid City/Nonprofit Partnership**

* **What the City Should Own:** The City of Richmond should own the underlying data infrastructure and API integrations. This includes the GeoHub parcel and assessor layers [5], as well as the automated data pipelines that extract compliance records from internal HCD systems. The City Auditor, who recently highlighted gaps in the Affordable Housing Trust Fund [8] [9], should act as an internal champion and primary consumer of the compliance data.
* **What Partners Should Own:** A regional nonprofit partner (such as Enterprise Community Partners Mid-Atlantic or LISC Virginia) should own the public-facing application, user interface, and community engagement strategy. They should also manage the integration of national datasets like the NHPD and HUD's Multifamily Assistance and Section 8 (MAH) database [10] [11].
* **What Should NOT be City-Owned:** The resident-facing "expiration radar" and advocacy tools should not be city-owned. City ownership of the advocacy layer creates a conflict of interest and exposes the tool to political risks, especially given recent audits showing the city fell short on required revenues for affordable housing [8]. Furthermore, relying solely on city IT for maintenance risks the tool becoming abandoned due to shifting municipal budgets.

## 5. EXISTING EFFORTS / PRIOR ART

* **DC Preservation Catalog (Urban Institute / CNHED):** 
 * *What was attempted:* A digital mapping tool to help the District and residents identify assisted housing with upcoming subsidy end dates [33] [34].
 * *What failed:* The website was retired in September 2020 [4].
 * *Lesson:* Standalone civic tech projects die without sustainable, long-term maintenance funding. The Richmond tool must secure ongoing operational grants (e.g., via Virginia Housing) rather than relying on one-time hackathon momentum.
* **New York City ACRIS:** 
 * *What was attempted:* An online system allowing users to search property records (including Regulatory Agreements) by Borough-Block-Lot (BBL) [35] [36].
 * *What worked:* Provides definitive, parcel-level legal truth.
 * *Lesson:* While ideal, relying on local recorders is risky. For example, Los Angeles County does not provide online access to real estate records [3] [37]. Richmond should bypass the recorder initially by using national datasets (NHPD) as a proxy.
* **National Housing Preservation Database (NHPD):** 
 * *What was attempted:* A deduplicated, address-level inventory of federally assisted rental housing [32].
 * *What worked:* Successfully aggregates earliest and latest end dates for subsidies [1] [2].
 * *Lesson:* Do not rebuild the wheel. The Richmond tool should ingest NHPD data via API to establish the baseline "expiration radar" before layering on local HCD data.

## 6. SCALE SHIFT ANALYSIS

Framing this solely as a Richmond City project makes it too small to attract major philanthropic or federal funding. Housing markets and displacement pressures do not stop at city lines. 

If the frame becomes regional (Greater Richmond/PlanRVA), the project aligns with the $18.5 million in annual federal funding targeted by the regional housing ecosystem [7]. PlanRVA already collaborates across jurisdictions for fair housing [24], making them a natural scaling partner. If a nonprofit owns the resident-facing layer, the tool transforms from a bureaucratic compliance dashboard into a public accountability and advocacy platform. This allows the city to focus purely on data provision (via GeoHub) while the partner scales the tool to neighboring counties, eventually creating a statewide preservation radar that could be adopted by HousingForward Virginia [27].

## 7. RISK AND CONSTRAINT ANALYSIS

* **Maintenance Burden & Sustainability:** The biggest technical risk is the tool becoming obsolete, exactly as the DC Preservation Catalog did in 2020 [4]. *Mitigation:* Secure a Virginia Housing Capacity Building Grant [6] specifically earmarked for multi-year maintenance by a nonprofit partner.
* **"False Expirations" and Data Misinterpretation:** Expiration dates in national databases are risk flags, not definitive outcomes. Many HUD Section 8 properties have renewal options [38]. *Mitigation:* The UI must include a "Renewal Probability" indicator to prevent advocates from panicking over routine contract renewals.
* **Political Feasibility & Trust:** A recent audit revealed the City of Richmond fell short on paying required revenues into the Affordable Housing Trust Fund [8] [9]. Exposing compliance data publicly may cause political friction. *Mitigation:* Frame the tool as a proactive preservation asset for Mayor Avula's "Thriving Neighborhoods" pillar, emphasizing that it helps the city secure HUD PRO Housing grants [13].
* **Data Integration Blockers:** Local real estate records and covenants are often not digitized or easily accessible online [3]. *Mitigation:* Rely on the NHPD [39] and HUD MAH [11] databases for the MVP, adding local HCD data only as it becomes structured.
