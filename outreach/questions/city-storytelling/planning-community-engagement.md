# Outreach: City Planning + Community Engagement — Resident Stories to Policy Decisions — Research & Questions

### What we've confirmed through public sources

- **Diamond District engagement data:** The City collected **1,300 survey responses**, held four community meetings, and received **150 comments** on the draft plan. There is no published evidence of how qualitative comments influenced the final scope — collection happened, but the decision-linkage is opaque.
- **No demographic tracking of commenters:** Richmond does not publish demographic data on who participates in Planning Commission hearings, public comment periods, or engagement surveys. There is no baseline to measure whether new outreach tools are reaching underrepresented residents.
- **59% renter notification gap:** Richmond's renter population is approximately 59%. Zoning notification is mailed to **property owners** within the required radius — renters are structurally invisible to the notification system unless they happen to see a posted sign or attend a meeting.
- **Eviction rate context:** Richmond's eviction rate is **11.44%** — second-highest nationally. The most displacement-vulnerable residents are the least likely to appear in any formal engagement channel because housing instability makes sustained civic participation nearly impossible.
- **Staff time allocation:** Planning staff spend approximately **60% of time on application review** and only **5% on community communication**. Engagement is a legal compliance activity, not a resourced function.
- **City cultural arts pages:** Richmond's Cultural Arts program pages on rva.gov **lack ICS or RSS feeds**, meaning city-produced arts and cultural events don't propagate to partner calendars (CultureWorks, VisitRichmond, Style Weekly). City events are effectively invisible to aggregators.
- **Richmond 300 Priority Neighborhoods:** The Richmond 300 Master Plan identifies specific **Priority Neighborhoods** for focused investment and planning attention. No mechanism currently connects resident stories to the Priority Neighborhoods framework so that planners can query stories by the neighborhoods they're actively making decisions about.
- **Richmond 300 survey demographics:** The Richmond 300 community engagement survey drew **72% White respondents** and **20% Black respondents** — in a city that is approximately 46% Black. The survey is the best available proxy for participation demographics but doesn't reflect routine engagement patterns.
- **Santa Monica Wellbeing Index precedent:** Santa Monica's Wellbeing Project successfully tied **qualitative resident data to departmental workplans and budgets**. All City Departments must include strategies to improve resident wellbeing in their annual plans. The project created a **Chief Data Officer role** and partnered with RAND Corporation and LA County. This is the closest precedent to a functioning story-to-policy feedback loop in a US city.
- **No public comment tracking system:** No US city that we found in our research operates a public-facing system that tracks how specific resident comments or stories influenced specific planning decisions. "Stories informing decisions" is aspirational everywhere — but Santa Monica shows the infrastructure is buildable.
- **Digital divide in target neighborhoods:** In key East End census tracts, up to **66.2% of households lack broadband** and **47.7% lack smartphones**. Black residents are 115% more likely to lack internet access. Any engagement tool that requires internet access will reproduce the same participation gaps the City already has.
- **Planning Commission hearing dynamics:** Public hearings are dominated by repeat participants — neighborhood association leaders, developers, and attorneys. Three-minute time limits force residents to compress multi-generational neighborhood experience into soundbites. RVA311 confines input to predefined categories. The existing engagement infrastructure prioritizes quantitative aggregation over qualitative depth.
- **Oral History Association consent standards:** Rolling consent (ongoing assent, right to withdraw, review before release) is the professional standard for collecting stories about displacement, eviction, and urban renewal. Even a minimal implementation is feasible within a prototype and protects vulnerable contributors from re-traumatization.

### What we haven't been able to determine — and where we need your help

We have **five specific questions** that only Planning & Development Review and City Communications can answer:

1. **Story-to-decision tracking interest:** Has the City considered any mechanism — even informal — for linking resident engagement input (stories, comments, survey responses) to specific planning decisions? The Santa Monica Wellbeing Index shows this is buildable; would Richmond's Planning department be interested in exploring a lightweight version that tracks whether resident voices were reviewed before specific commission votes?

2. **Notification system reform:** Are there any active plans to address the renter notification gap — expanding notification beyond property owners to reach the 59% of Richmond households that rent? Even a digital notification opt-in (email/SMS for residents who want to know about zoning changes near them) would dramatically expand who participates.

3. **Resident voice in active planning cases:** Would Planning Commission staff find it useful to receive a curated feed of resident stories relevant to specific cases on their docket — alongside the standard staff report — so commissioners hear directly from affected residents who can't attend hearings? We're trying to understand whether this would be welcomed or seen as outside the formal process.

4. **Richmond 300 integration:** Is there an API, data export, or even a GIS layer that maps Richmond 300 Priority Neighborhoods in a way that a civic tool could use to automatically tag and route resident input to the relevant planning context? Or is the Priority Neighborhoods framework primarily a policy document without a corresponding data layer?

5. **Language access for engagement:** Beyond the Language Access Policy (AR 5.24) requirement for professional translation of vital documents — what is the City's current capacity for multilingual community engagement on planning cases? Are public comment forms, surveys, or hearing notices available in languages beyond English? What languages would the City prioritize if a tool offered multilingual story submission?

### Why this matters

We've been generating and scoring new solution ideas grounded in the research. The highest-scoring concepts for connecting resident stories to City decisions are:

- **Planning Commission Story Feed** (scored 93/105 Pillar Award) — a curated feed that delivers relevant resident stories alongside the standard staff report for each case on the Planning Commission docket. Commissioners hear directly from affected residents — including renters, non-English speakers, and displacement-vulnerable residents who never attend hearings. No US city currently delivers resident stories alongside staff reports to commissioners.
- **Story-to-Policy Citation Tracker** (scored 90/105 Pillar Award) — a public-facing tool that tracks when specific resident stories or community narratives are reviewed, referenced, or cited in planning decisions, budget allocations, or commission votes. Modeled on the Santa Monica Wellbeing framework but focused on individual story attribution. Closes the feedback loop that makes collection without use feel performative.
- **Neighborhood Change Narrative Map** (scored 90/105 Pillar Award) — a temporal overlay connecting resident stories to physical and demographic change data (RRHA demolition timelines, eviction rates, census shifts) at the neighborhood level. Useful for newcomers ("what was this block?") and planners ("who lived here before this rezoning?"). Designed to make invisible history visible in the exact places where planning decisions are being made.

All three ideas build on the City's existing engagement infrastructure rather than replacing it. They add a qualitative depth layer — resident voices anchored to place and time — on top of the quantitative data the City already collects.

## Context for the SME
The fundamental gap identified across all three JTBD for this problem statement is that Richmond collects resident input (surveys, comments, hearing testimony) but has no mechanism to connect that input to specific decisions. Collection without visible use erodes trust and depresses future participation — particularly from displacement-vulnerable residents who have the most to lose and the least reason to believe their voice matters. The Diamond District process collected 1,300+ inputs with no public evidence of decision-linkage. Planning Commission hearings structurally exclude renters, non-English speakers, and working parents.

The three prototypes address this at different points in the pipeline: the Story Feed inserts voices at the decision moment, the Citation Tracker makes the feedback loop visible after the fact, and the Narrative Map provides spatial/temporal context that staff reports lack. All three require some level of City staff cooperation — the bottleneck is political will, not technology.

## Why This Person
The Planning & Development Review Director controls the commission workflow and can speak to whether a story feed alongside staff reports is feasible within the existing process. City Communications / Public Engagement leads manage the notification system and community engagement strategy — they would know about any planned reforms to renter notification and multilingual outreach capacity. Together they represent the institutional decision-makers for how resident voices enter the planning process.
