# Outreach: Capital Region SBDC + Richmond Economic Development — Research & Questions

### What we've confirmed through public sources

- **SBDC scale:** The Capital Region SBDC has served 1,500 clients since 2020 and assisted 111 new business starts — meaningful throughput, but every engagement is one-to-one. Services include business plan development, market research, assistance with raising capital, and financial management.
- **SCORE mentoring:** SCORE Richmond provides free counseling for first-time entrepreneurs, with workshops including topics like "Selling to the Federal Government." The SBDC and SCORE serve overlapping but not identical populations with no shared intake or referral protocol.
- **1717 Collective:** The 1717 Innovation Center coordinates ecosystem organizations but lacks visibility among entrepreneurs who don't already know it exists. It is not functioning as a public-facing "front door" for the startup ecosystem.
- **Tacit knowledge gap:** Staff knowledge about the real startup process — the actual sequence, the gotchas, the workarounds, which agencies are slow, which forms trip people up, which requirements are commonly missed — lives in the heads of experienced SBDC counselors, SCORE mentors, and OMBD staff. Research on organizational knowledge suggests roughly 80% of operational knowledge is tacit and undocumented. None of this is captured in any public-facing system.
- **Five-system gauntlet:** Starting a business in Richmond requires navigating at least five distinct government systems — SCC (entity formation), IRS (EIN), Virginia Tax (state registration), Zoning Administration (CZC), and Finance (BPOL) — with no cross-referencing, no shared data, and no documented sequencing. The City's own 21-item Business License Checklist is an admission that no unified process exists.
- **Undocumented dependencies:** Hard dependencies are not surfaced — the CZC must precede BPOL, but this is discovered only mid-application. The business startup process functions as a "journey of surprises" where each step reveals requirements the entrepreneur didn't anticipate.
- **OMBD centralized downtown:** OMBD's documented service delivery is centralized at City Hall without recurring satellite offices in the East End, Southside, or surrounding areas. Entrepreneurs from those communities must travel downtown for in-person assistance unless pop-up or partner-hosted events occur outside City Hall.
- **No Virginia wizard:** No Virginia city or the state itself has deployed a personalized step-by-step business startup wizard. Richmond's Online Permit Portal (EnerGov) handles permit transactions but doesn't help entrepreneurs identify which permits they need.
- **Virginia Business One Stop is a directory:** VBOS claims 33,000+ users but functions as a resource aggregation hub, not an interactive guide. It references a future "New Business Wizard" but has not shipped one. No active RFP or roadmap for the upgrade was found on SBSD or DPB websites.
- **No neighborhood business managers:** Richmond has not implemented a model comparable to Boston's Neighborhood Business Managers, where named staff are assigned to specific geographic districts. Support is centralized counters, the SBDC network, EDA, and OMBD.
- **What makes tools stick:** Research on successful business navigation tools (NYC MyCity, Chicago Business Direct) identifies three factors that distinguish sustained adoption from stalled projects: transactionality (apply/pay/track in one place), cross-agency user profiles (reducing data re-entry), and executive sponsorship tied to a government reform initiative. Static link directories and resource aggregators do not achieve sustained adoption.
- **311 as business intake:** RVA 311 handles approximately 13,500 calls per month and the BPOL FAQ explicitly directs prospective businesses to call 311. However, 311 is a general-purpose municipal help desk, not a business startup navigator, and does not publish a breakdown of business-related inquiry types.
- **Digital equity context:** Richmond's Digital Equity Implementation Plan mandates non-digital outreach (signs, kiosks, ambassadors, pamphlets). Spanish is the most spoken language after English citywide (ACS data), but no data exists on the specific languages of aspiring entrepreneurs or the availability of multilingual support through the startup process.

### What we haven't been able to determine — and where we need your help

We have **five specific questions** that only your teams can answer:

1. **Percentage of unguided entrepreneurs:** What share of first-time entrepreneurs in Richmond attempt the startup process without any SBDC, SCORE, or OMBD counseling? We found no published statistic. Even a rough estimate would help us understand how large the "self-service" population is that a digital tool would primarily serve — and how much demand your counselors are currently absorbing that could be deflected.

2. **Common abandonment points:** At what point in the startup journey do entrepreneurs most commonly give up or start operating without proper licensing? Is it the CZC step, the multi-agency registration sequence, industry-specific permits, or the cost and complexity of the overall process? The City publishes no analytics on registration drop-off, but your counselors see the pattern every day.

3. **Geographic reach outside downtown:** Does the SBDC or OMBD have documented or recurring programming in the East End, Southside, or other areas outside downtown? We confirmed OMBD's services are centralized at City Hall. Understanding whether entrepreneurs in underserved neighborhoods have practical access to in-person support is critical for designing tools that complement — not duplicate — what already exists.

4. **Language support gaps:** Beyond English and Spanish, what languages do your clients or callers most commonly speak? Is there a meaningful population of aspiring entrepreneurs who need startup guidance in Vietnamese, Arabic, Amharic, or other languages? The ACS gives us general population data but not entrepreneur-specific language needs.

5. **What would make a digital guide stick:** Your counselors have the deepest firsthand knowledge of what entrepreneurs actually need. In your experience, what would make a self-service startup guide something entrepreneurs actually use — and keep using — rather than another government website they visit once and abandon? Is it mobile-first design, SMS-based step reminders, integration with a real person when they get stuck, or something else?

### Why this matters

We've been generating new solution ideas grounded in the research. The highest-scoring concepts for the small business navigation problem are:

- **Richmond Business Launch Wizard** (Pillar Award: 90/105, Mayor's Choice: 92/105) — a personalized, sequenced, Richmond-specific step-by-step tool that asks 5–8 questions about the business (type, location, employees, industry) and generates the complete ordered checklist of registrations, permits, costs, and timelines. Modeled on NYC's MyCity Business. No Virginia city has built one. The research shows that what distinguishes tools that achieve sustained adoption from those that stall is transactionality and cross-agency integration — not just information.
- **Total Startup Cost Calculator** (Pillar: 82, Mayor's Choice: 86) — a consolidated cost and timeline estimator that aggregates currently fragmented fee information (SCC filing + EIN + CZC + BPOL rate + industry permits) into a single, business-type-specific estimate. No government source currently provides this.

We also explored a **SBDC Knowledge Capture** concept — a tool that would document the tacit operational knowledge your counselors carry (real sequences, common gotchas, workarounds) into a structured, searchable resource that both entrepreneurs and new staff could use. This scored lower on weekend-buildability (the knowledge takes months to collect), but the underlying insight remains: the most valuable data for a business startup guide exists in your counselors' heads, not in any published system. Any prototype that succeeds will need your input to be accurate.

We're not asking for any commitment — just trying to understand what's technically possible so we can give the Mayor's office a realistic assessment of which prototypes could move to a pilot. Happy to share the prototypes or our full research.

## Context for the SME
The SBDC and economic development ecosystem sits at the intersection of both Thriving Economy problem statements. Their counselors are the de facto "front door" for entrepreneurs who don't know where to start — but every hour spent on a "where do I start?" call is an hour not spent on a complex MBE certification case or a food entrepreneur navigating HACCP requirements. A self-service startup wizard that handles the common path would free counselor time for the cases that genuinely need human judgment. The knowledge capture dimension is what makes this email different from the Finance/Zoning email: SBDC counselors know the real process (including the informal workarounds), not just the documented one.

## Why This Person
The Capital Region SBDC Director oversees the primary free counseling pipeline for Richmond entrepreneurs (1,500 clients since 2020). The Economic Development Director shapes the broader ecosystem strategy and has visibility into 1717 Collective coordination, neighborhood business development, and the gap between centralized City Hall services and underserved communities. Together they can validate the "unguided entrepreneur" population size, the common failure points, and whether a digital tool would complement or conflict with existing support channels.
