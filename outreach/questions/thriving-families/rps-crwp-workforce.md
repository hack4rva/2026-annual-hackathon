# Outreach: RPS + CRWP — Youth Workforce Pipeline — Research & Questions

### What we've confirmed through public sources

- **RPS demographics:** Richmond Public Schools reports 21.3% English Learners across 60+ languages. The Language Instruction Education Program (LIEP) dashboard provides school-level demographic breakdowns, but no public citywide ranked language list or neighborhood-level map has been published.
- **CRWP WIOA youth contracts:** CRWP's in-school youth (ISY) contracts are awarded to Peace of Mind and Charles City County — not RPS. This means Richmond Public Schools lack a formal, scaled in-school WIOA navigator presence, creating a gap for RPS students who need workforce referrals during the school year.
- **CRWP performance and funding pressure:** CRWP youth "skills gained" metrics fell to 78% in FY24. The FY26 proposed budget shows a 3% decrease — suggesting fragility in the funding base for youth workforce programming in the Capital Region.
- **No co-enrollment protocol:** YES/OCWB and CRWP both serve 18–24 out-of-school youth, but no formal co-enrollment protocol or shared data system connects them. Referrals between programs appear to be verbal or informal.
- **No Summer Bridge handoff:** Youth Works RVA is an 8-week seasonal program with no structured handoff to fall apprenticeships, WIOA WEX/OJT, or other year-round pathways. Post-summer churn risk is high and untracked.
- **Project SEARCH gap:** No Project SEARCH site exists within Richmond city limits in partnership with RPS. Metro host sites are Bon Secours St. Francis (Chesterfield), Bon Secours St. Mary's (Henrico), and Bon Secours Memorial (Hanover) — all partnered with their respective county school districts, not RPS.
- **Recruitment calendar fragmentation:** The ecosystem's enrollment timelines are siloed — Girls For A Change secures its cohort in December–January, Youth Works RVA closes applications in March, Partnership for the Future recruits freshmen in spring — each with different eligibility criteria and no shared timeline or intake.
- **FERPA permits integration:** Under FERPA's "school official" exception, a third-party youth employment platform can receive student PII if the vendor operates under a written agreement with direct district control, limiting use and redisclosure. This is a permissible pathway — not a blanket block.
- **Youth Works RVA program structure:** The program has a two-track model: a Virtual Earn and Learn Program for ages 14–15 (career training and workforce readiness) and in-person work-based learning and internship opportunities for ages 16–24 through employer partners.
- **1717 Collective:** This youth-serving coalition brings together organizations across the Richmond region. It represents a coordination mechanism but does not operate a shared data system or unified referral platform for workforce programs.
- **National adaptable features:** NJ Career Navigator's ML-driven career recommendations and WA-style plain-language screeners, document checklists, and referral workflows can be adapted for a lightweight teen platform using open data and simple matching models — no enterprise infrastructure required.
- **Sustained national models:** Baltimore YouthWorks (city MOED), Hire LA's Youth (city EWDD), and Philadelphia WorkReady (nonprofit PYN intermediary) demonstrate that two-sided youth/employer matching platforms can survive beyond a hackathon when institutionally owned — either by a city department or a designated nonprofit partner.
- **Document barriers cause major drop-off:** Boston SuccessLink data shows up to 15% of youth who are matched to a job fail to complete onboarding, with the primary barrier being the need to locate and upload work permits, SSN cards, and proof of residency. This is likely comparable in Richmond given similar permit requirements.

### What we haven't been able to determine — and where we need your help

We have **five specific questions** that only your teams can answer:

1. **RPS Work-Based Learning enrollment data:** How many RPS students are currently enrolled in CTE-linked Work-Based Learning experiences (internships, apprenticeships, cooperative education)? Is there a breakdown by grade level, school, or program area — and is this data reportable without FERPA constraints since it's aggregate?

2. **FERPA implementation willingness:** The legal path for FERPA-compliant integration exists (school official exception + written agreement). Has RPS ever executed such an agreement with a workforce development partner — CRWP, YES/OCWB, or a nonprofit? If so, what was the scope, and what data elements were shared? If not, what would the approval process look like?

3. **Summer-to-fall transition tracking:** When Youth Works RVA's 8-week summer program ends, is there any tracking of what happens to participants afterward? Do CRWP or RPS have visibility into whether summer participants enroll in fall WEX/OJT, return to school-year WBL, or drop off entirely? What does the handoff look like in practice?

4. **Language support gaps:** RPS serves 60+ languages, but the LIEP dashboard is school-level only. Which languages beyond English and Spanish are most concentrated in neighborhoods with the highest youth unemployment or lowest workforce program participation? Does CRWP have language access data from its intake process that could identify which communities are underrepresented?

5. **Neighborhood NEET concentrations:** No published Richmond tract-level map of NEET (Not in Employment, Education, or Training) youth exists. Does RPS or CRWP have internal data — even proxy data like chronic absenteeism overlaid with non-enrollment — that could identify which neighborhoods have the highest concentration of disconnected 16–24-year-olds? DHCD's Commonwealth Connection broadband map and GRTC service maps exist for infrastructure gaps, but there's no youth data layer to pair them with.

### Why this matters

We've been generating new solution ideas grounded in the research. The highest-scoring concepts relevant to the school-to-workforce pipeline are:

- **Richmond Youth Hub** (Pillar Award: 90/105, Mayor's Choice: 92/105) — A unified program navigator that consolidates all 7+ youth employment programs (YES, CRWP, RPS WBL, PFF, BGCMR, GFAC, CIS) into one searchable directory, filtered by age, neighborhood, language, and eligibility. Fills the #1 gap: ecosystem fragmentation. Directly answers the problem statement's "one clear place" requirement. Top-scoring youth employment idea across both award tracks.
- **First Job SMS Kit** (Pillar Award: 87/105, Mayor's Choice: 91/105) — A text-message-based step-by-step guide through the entire first-job process: permits, documents, transit, schedules. Designed for the 9.7% of Richmond residents without broadband and RPS's 21.3% English Learner population. Highest equity score (5/5) of any youth employment idea. Could be deployed in multiple languages using existing GRTC GTFS data for transit step guidance.

Both ideas build coordination layers on top of existing programs — they don't replace RPS WBL, CRWP programs, or YES. They address the discovery and navigation gaps that cause teens to fall through cracks between strong but siloed program pillars.

We're not asking for any commitment — just trying to understand what data and coordination pathways exist so we can give the Mayor's office a realistic assessment of which prototypes could move to a pilot. Happy to share any of the prototypes or our full research.

## Context for the SME
The research identifies a specific structural gap: RPS lacks a formal, scaled in-school WIOA navigator (ISY contracts went to Peace of Mind and Charles City County), and CRWP's youth programs have no data-sharing connection to YES/OCWB or RPS WBL. The result is that a Richmond teenager's path from "interested in working" to "placed and retained" depends on which program they happen to hear about first, and there is no tracking of what happens between programs or after summer programs end. The prototype ideas don't try to merge these programs — they create a shared discovery and navigation layer that respects each program's autonomy while making the ecosystem legible to a 15-year-old.

The FERPA question is critical: the legal path exists (school official exception), but practical willingness and precedent within RPS will determine whether any tool can verify enrollment or eligibility without requiring teens to manually re-enter information their school already has.

## Why This Person
The RPS CTE/WBL Coordinator would know enrollment figures, FERPA agreement precedents, and the practical relationship between school-year WBL and summer programs. CRWP's Youth Programs Lead would know WIOA ISY/OSY intake data, the referral relationship with YES, language access patterns in their intake process, and whether any summer-to-fall transition tracking exists. Together, they can validate whether the "unified navigator" concept is operationally feasible within current institutional relationships.
