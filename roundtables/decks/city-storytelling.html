A City That Tells Its Stories

Roundtable Evaluation — RVA Hacks 2026

Ten ideas evaluated across arts discovery and resident storytelling.

The Problem

Richmond has a rich cultural life — live music, gallery walks, community festivals, oral history projects — but no single place to discover what's happening or whose stories are being told.

Residents piece it together from Instagram posts, word of mouth, and scattered event calendars.

Two Challenges, One Pillar

Arts & Cultural Event Discovery: Aggregate Richmond's arts and cultural events into one discoverable place.

Resident Stories as Civic Insight: Give residents a trusted way to share lived experiences so community narratives can inform City decisions.

How We Evaluated

We didn't just score these ideas. We put each one through a simulated roundtable with seven distinct perspectives — from the Mayor worrying about political risk to the Citizen asking who gets left out.

The Seven Voices

The Innovator finds the non-obvious product wedge. The Activist asks who benefits and who is harmed. The Citizen tests whether real people would use it. The Mayor weighs political risk and implementation readiness.

The Architect checks technical feasibility against Richmond's real stack. The Frontliner asks if the people doing the work would trust it. The Funder asks who pays after year one.

The Process

Each idea went through four phases: independent persona assessment, tension identification and resolution, direct challenge debates, and a final pass-or-fail validation against five fatal flaw tests.

What Kills an Idea

Does the required data exist? Does a vendor wall block the path? Would anyone own it after demo day? Could it harm residents? Would it embarrass the city?

If the answer is wrong on any one of these, the idea doesn't ship.

The Headlines

All ten ideas earned a "Ship with Conditions" verdict — but the conditions range from a single partnership conversation to multi-institutional governance charters.

Three themes dominate: every idea depends on partnerships the City doesn't control, consent and governance are universal preconditions, and not one of the five structural IT walls blocks anything in this pillar.

Weekend Culture SMS Ship w/ Conditions

Every Friday, your phone buzzes with a curated text: five arts events this weekend, no app required. This is the simplest idea in the pillar — and the highest-scoring, because simplicity is what makes it shippable.

Hackathon Judge "This is one of the easiest ideas to prove live on demo day: judges' phones buzz with a real weekend sample."

Ranked #22 of 73 ideas overall for implementation readiness (Tier A).

Weekend Culture SMS — Verdict

Ship as a pilot with two conditions: a formal CultureWorks data-use agreement and a published curation charter that ensures neighborhood rotation and spotlight slots for smaller organizers.

It passed all five fatal flaw tests — no City IT walls, manageable TCPA compliance, and clear demand for push-over-pull arts discovery.

With a nonprofit operator and disciplined editorial governance, this becomes Richmond's simplest path to arts visibility.

Displacement Memory Archive ★ Ship w/ Conditions

While SMS solves weekend discovery, this idea asks a deeper question: whose stories are missing from the city's memory? It proposes a federated portal linking displacement narratives already held at VCU, the Library of Virginia, and JXN Project — not a new collection, but a discovery layer timed to Reconnect Jackson Ward.

The Mayor "The narrative is 'memory informing reconnection' — not only retrospective guilt."

Displacement Memory Archive ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: letters of intent from at least three institutional partners, a rights audit of every clip in the MVP, and a descendant advisory body chartered before any public launch.

Ownership and harm potential are real risks, but they are governance problems — not technical walls.

If those conditions are met, this could become the nation's most compelling model for tying displacement memory to live infrastructure policy.

Fulton Oral History ★ Ship w/ Conditions

Where the Displacement Memory Archive spans the whole city, this idea zooms into one neighborhood. An interactive map surfaces VCU's 17-interview Historic Fulton collection on the landscape — pinning voices of displacement to the streets where homes once stood.

The Activist "Displacement memory is political: publishing quotes and locations without descendant governance repeats the harm pattern."

Fulton Oral History ★ — Verdict

Ship this prototype as a credible discovery surface, but only after securing explicit VCU and Valentine rights for clips, establishing a descendant advisory review step, and documenting a consent posture for any new submissions.

The corpus exists and the stack is proven — the risk is not technology but whether governance keeps pace with ambition.

If descendant authority is centered from day one, this becomes a model for how cities activate dormant archives without extracting from them.

Community Story Popup Kit ★ Ship w/ Conditions

From preserving existing archives, this idea turns to capturing new ones. A borrowable recording kit — Pelican case, printed consent forms, multilingual prompts — goes wherever community events already happen, modeled on Boston Public Library's lendable oral history backpacks.

The Frontliner "Organizers already run events — they need borrowable gear and a clear return path, not another unfunded platform."

Community Story Popup Kit ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: a documented partnership path with Richmond Public Library, a consent and moderation process tested on real participants, and pilot recordings in both a noisy festival and a quiet indoor venue.

All five fatal flaw tests pass — no blocked City APIs, no impossible data dependencies — but volunteer moderator burnout is the 12-month cliff.

If the library signs on and stories route to a real archive, this is civic storytelling infrastructure that costs less than a single kiosk rental.

Public Comment Atlas Ship w/ Conditions

Public comment is another form of civic storytelling that rarely gets mapped. This idea geocodes Legistar planning comments onto Richmond's neighborhoods — showing not just where people spoke up, but where no one did.

The Innovator "The provocative bit is the atlas of silence — hotspots and deserts — not just another sentiment dashboard."

Public Comment Atlas — Verdict

Ship with conditions: pilot validation of Richmond's Legistar eComment fields, a privacy and aggregation policy before any launch of commenter-origin data, and a named sustaining partner for refresh and accuracy audits.

Legistar OData and GeoHub are both public — no structural walls block this — but maps can mislead if participation deserts are read as apathy rather than systemic exclusion.

If normalization and equity framing are built into the product, this becomes a transparency tool that serves organizers and council members alike.

Vecina ★ Ship w/ Conditions

From civic participation, we return to arts discovery. Vecina filters CultureWorks' regional calendar through a neighborhood-first, map-forward lens — so a resident in Jackson Ward can ask "what's happening near me this weekend" without scrolling past 400 citywide listings.

Arts-Curious Resident "I do not want another app that lists 400 citywide events. Filtering by Jackson Ward and 'this weekend' matches how I actually decide to go out."

Vecina ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: formal CultureWorks authorization to use Localist feeds, an accessibility review for the map-heavy interface, and a roadmap for embeddable neighborhood widgets rather than just another standalone calendar site.

The fatal flaw test has zero unconditional failures, but without CultureWorks' written permission the entire product is a layer on data it cannot legally refresh.

If syndication becomes the strategy — civic associations embedding "this weekend in our neighborhood" — Vecina outlives its own homepage.

Richmond Stories Online ★ Ship w/ Conditions

Where Vecina narrows the lens to neighborhoods, this idea proposes a citywide living archive where residents submit oral histories, photos, and place-based memories on a geospatial map. The ambition is large: a "front door" to Richmond's collective voice.

Valentine Museum Educator "We already operate Richmond Stories as an educational program. A separate site risks brand confusion unless we co-own the narrative."

Richmond Stories Online ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: resolve the naming conflict with The Valentine's existing "Richmond Stories" program, establish a preservation MOU with a museum or university partner, and budget reuse programming so the archive doesn't become another retired oral history silo.

The tech is commodity — Omeka S, Survey123, or StoryMaps can all serve — but the real risk is the cold-start problem and the gap between collecting stories and actually using them.

If the Valentine partnership materializes and reuse events are funded, this becomes the resident on-ramp into accessioned civic memory.

Planning Commission Story Feed ★ Ship w/ Conditions

From broad story collection, this idea turns to a very specific civic narrative: what happened at last night's Planning Commission meeting? AI-drafted, human-verified plain-language summaries are geocoded to affected neighborhoods and delivered by subscription.

The Citizen "I do not have two hours on a Tuesday to decode a 47-page packet. I want 'what changed near me' in plain English with a map pin."

Planning Commission Story Feed ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: a named City owner for QA and corrections, DIT review under Administrative Regulation 2.13 for any AI in production, and no publication without human verification of motions, votes, and legal excerpts.

Legistar OData and GeoHub are public and proven — but one wrong vote summary on a hot rezoning case would destroy trust permanently.

If the hybrid AI-plus-human model holds and a clerk's office champions it, this becomes the template for how Richmond translates bureaucratic process into neighborhood-readable narrative.

What's On RVA Ship w/ Conditions

Returning to arts discovery, this idea adds an AI-powered conversational layer: browse a unified calendar, filter by neighborhood, or ask a chatbot "what should I do this weekend?" The ambition is a resident-first cultural concierge.

The Maintainer "Aggregators die from stale data, ghost events, and unfunded curation."

What's On RVA — Verdict

Proceed as a partner-aligned pilot with strict AI and data boundaries: RAG only against verified event listings, AR 2.13 disclosure if city-branded, and a named post-demo curator responsible for feed health.

The chat interface is compelling for demos but dangerous in production — one hallucinated event destroys trust faster than no tool at all.

If guardrails hold and CultureWorks provides the verified feed, the conversational interface could differentiate this from every other Richmond calendar.

ExploreRVA ★ Ship w/ Conditions

Our final idea takes a different approach: not another calendar but curated cultural trails. StoryMaps, QR waypoints, and spoken-word poetry guide residents through Richmond's neighborhoods — blending event discovery with the oral history of who and what came before.

The Citizen "Search feels like 'echoes,' not a living city."

ExploreRVA ★ — Verdict

Ship with conditions: an anchor institution or merchant association for content and QR maintenance, an explicit relationship to CultureWorks for event dates, and a consent and attribution policy before any oral history content goes live.

The spoken-word pitch is distinctive and the trail model avoids competing with calendars head-on, but without a content steward the QR signs decay and the StoryMaps go stale.

If piloted on one corridor with documented analytics and partner sign-off, ExploreRVA becomes proof that cultural discovery works better as a walk than a search.

What Should Ship First

Weekend Culture SMS — the fastest path to arts visibility, requiring only one partnership conversation and a curation charter.

Displacement Memory Archive — the pillar's strongest vision, strategically timed to Reconnect Jackson Ward.

Fulton Oral History — a real archive, a real neighborhood, and the most emotionally grounded prototype in the set.

What Needs More Work

Richmond Stories Online must resolve a brand conflict with The Valentine before launch, or risk confusing the very community it hopes to serve.

What's On RVA faces a crowded field of existing calendars and must prove its AI layer adds trust rather than risk.

ExploreRVA has a beautiful pitch but needs a content strategy and stewardship plan more than it needs technology.

What This Pillar Tells Us

Not one of Richmond's five structural IT walls — 311, EnerGov, OnBase, B2GNow, DPW Fleet GPS — blocks any idea here. The barriers are human: partnerships, consent protocols, descendant governance, and who pays the curator after year one.

The simplest ideas scored highest because they can ship without solving problems the City hasn't solved yet. And CultureWorks is the critical shared infrastructure — four of ten ideas depend on the same data feed.

This pillar's hardest truth: Richmond doesn't lack stories. It lacks the institutional trust and sustained commitment to treat those stories as civic infrastructure.
