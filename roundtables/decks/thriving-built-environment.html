RVA Hacks 2026 — Post-Event Roundtable Evaluation

The Problem

Richmond residents encounter road work, detours, and service disruptions daily — but rarely know what's happening, why, or when it will end.

DPW supervisors track fleet operations with paper and radio, while infrastructure projects live in GIS layers that residents never see.

Two Challenges, One Pillar

Infrastructure Project Visibility: Make it easier for residents to find and understand what's being built, fixed, or blocked on their streets.

Fleet Operations & Street Service Tracking: Help DPW supervisors and residents track the progress of services like snow removal and street cleaning.

The Roundtable Model

We didn't just score these ideas.

We put each one through a simulated roundtable with seven distinct perspectives — from the DPW supervisor worried about stale data to the wheelchair user asking who gets stranded when a sidewalk closes.

The Seven Voices

A Concerned Resident who encounters construction daily. A DPW Supervisor who runs shifts by radio. A DIT Architect who knows which APIs actually work. An Equity Advocate who asks who gets left out.

A Transit Rider dependent on paratransit. A Legal Advisor measuring liability. And a Judge asking: could the City ship this in 90 days?

The Process

Each idea went through four phases: independent assessment, convergence debate, direct challenges between personas, and a final pass-or-fail validation.

No idea advanced without surviving all seven perspectives.

What Kills an Idea

We applied five fatal flaw tests to every idea.

If the data doesn't exist, if a vendor wall blocks the MVP, if no department would own it, if it could harm residents, or if it would embarrass the City — the idea doesn't ship.

The Headlines

Of nine ideas evaluated, one is ready to ship, seven advance with conditions, and one needs a significant pivot.

The defining pattern: ideas that avoid the DPW Fleet GPS wall and work with data Richmond already publishes score highest on feasibility.

Three Walls Shaped Every Verdict

DPW Fleet GPS is not exposed — blocking real-time vehicle tracking. EnerGov has no public API — blocking permit-level detail. AvePoint 311 has no public API — blocking service request integration.

The highest-scoring ideas found ways to deliver civic value without crossing any of them.

GRTC Construction Conflict Detector Advance

When construction blocks a bus stop or CARE paratransit pickup point, riders may be marked no-shows for trips they couldn't physically reach — and face suspension from the service they depend on.

This tool runs a nightly spatial join between construction zones and transit stops to flag conflicts before they strand anyone. Tier A — #41 of 73

CARE Rider "If construction blocks the curb and I cannot reach it, I may be marked a no-show even though I showed up."

GRTC Conflict Detector — Verdict

The panel recommends advancing this as a dual-anchor pilot: City supplies construction geometry, GRTC consumes alerts and owns transit policy.

Pattern A — a nightly batch join using public GTFS and open geodata — needs no City IT budget and can ship in 90 days.

Full CARE rider alerts and automated no-show waivers require a GRTC data-sharing agreement, which is a 6-to-18 month effort separate from the core value.

Snow Tier Compass Ship

Richmond publishes snow plow priority tiers on its website, but no resident can type their address and learn which tier their block is on.

Snow Tier Compass closes that gap with a GeoHub geocoder and digitized DPW priority polygons — no fleet GPS required. Tier S — #16 of 73

Concerned Resident "I still cannot type my address and know my block's tier without squinting at a PDF-style map."

Snow Tier Compass — Verdict

The panel recommends shipping with three conditions: DPW validates the digitized tier geometry, the UX never implies live plow positions, and VDOT-maintained roads are explicitly distinguished from city streets.

It passed all five fatal flaw tests — deliberately avoiding the Fleet GPS wall by design.

If paired with seasonal operational status updates, this could become Richmond's first public snow information service that actually reduces 311 call volume.

What's On My Street? Conditions

While Snow Tier Compass solves one seasonal question, this idea asks: what if residents could answer that question for every type of infrastructure project, year-round?

It aggregates CIP projects, VDOT work, and paving data into a text-first, WCAG-aligned address lookup — accessible by design, not as an afterthought. Tier A — #26 of 73

Accessibility Advocate "Leading with a sortable, screen-reader-friendly list turns the prototype into a plausible Title II posture, while map-only civic clones remain structurally weak."

What's On My Street? — Verdict

The panel recommends shipping a pilot after validating that the GeoHub CIP FeatureLayer is actually queryable without authentication — the tech stack says it may not be.

Stale Socrata datasets must be either quarantined or prominently labeled, and the tool must never imply complete coverage of all street work.

If the access question is resolved, this is one of the most legible demos in the pillar: type an address, see a list, know who to call.

StreetLights ★ Conditions

Where What's On My Street aggregates many sources, StreetLights takes a simpler path — a high school team from J.R. Tuggle built a clean, plain-English front door to data Richmond already publishes.

The city's existing CIP dashboard gets roughly 0.38 views per thousand residents per month — this tool's address-first design aims to change that.

Concerned Resident "I already have the city dashboard link somewhere but never use it because it feels like work."

StreetLights ★ — Verdict

The panel recommends proceeding with scope discipline: ship the address-first CIP lookup, but defer SMS alerts, community upvotes, and anything that implies 311 integration.

The name "StreetLights" implies streetlight assets, but the product is really about capital projects — a subtitle like "infrastructure near you" would sharpen the pitch.

Built by high schoolers on proven public GIS patterns, this is a strong example of what civic tech education can produce.

ADA Detour Publisher Advance

StreetLights makes capital projects visible to everyone — but this idea asks: what about the people for whom a sidewalk closure is not an inconvenience, but a crisis?

It converts permit data into screen-reader-friendly detour pages with grade estimates and turn-by-turn text. No U.S. city currently publishes routable, ADA-attributed pedestrian detour geometries.

Wheelchair User "Living with a mobility disability means construction is not an inconvenience — it is route failure."

ADA Detour Publisher — Verdict

The panel recommends advancing this as a high-equity pilot with manually verified data for three to five real corridors, not automated permit scraping.

EnerGov has no public API, so the MVP must rely on field-walked verification and DPW partnership — automation is downstream of a future permit system replacement.

If inaccurate detour data routes someone into active construction, the harm is not reputational — it is physical, which makes verification guardrails non-optional.

Storefront Work-Window Brief Conditions

The ADA Detour Publisher centers pedestrians with disabilities — this idea asks the same visibility question from a different angle: what about the small business owner watching foot traffic vanish?

It generates a printable one-page brief for merchants: project dates, which side of the street is open, and who to call at DPW. Tier A — #27 of 73

Storefront Owner "I do not live in ArcGIS; I live next to orange barrels."

Storefront Brief — Verdict

The panel recommends proceeding only after validating a public CIP data path — the tech stack says the dashboard may require authentication.

The real distribution channel is not a URL but trusted corridor organizations like the Carytown Merchants Association, who can print and hand-deliver briefs.

Peer cities show that digital tools work when paired with human outreach — a PDF generator alone is a complement, not a substitute for a liaison program.

RideShift RVA ★ Advance

While the previous ideas focus on what the city builds, RideShift asks whether better information could change how residents move through Richmond.

It pairs multimodal commute nudges with local MBE business rewards and deep-links to Richmond Connects for civic engagement — available in Spanish, with no-password magic-link login.

Transit-Dependent Rider "Self-reported 'did you meet your goal?' does not fix my real pain: knowing before I leave if Broad Street will eat 20 minutes."

RideShift RVA ★ — Verdict

The panel recommends shipping the behavior-and-engagement loop, but warns that the IDEA and the Innovation Analysis currently read like two different products.

The rewards concept is shippable on commodity hosting, but sustained partner trust requires a verification roadmap so honor-system check-ins do not erode credibility at scale.

Barrier reports need a named intake pathway — a nonprofit partner or GRTC customer service — or they become data that no one acts on.

Stay Informed Conditions

RideShift nudges commuters toward alternatives — this idea tackles the other challenge in the pillar: helping residents know when DPW services like sweeping and plowing will reach their street.

It deliberately sidesteps the Fleet GPS wall by using supervisor-authored status updates and existing Socrata street sweeping zones rather than live vehicle telemetry.

Mayor's Choice Judge "The idea sidesteps the DPW Fleet GPS structural wall for residents by design — aligned with national 'schedule plus status plus optional delayed AVL' playbooks."

Stay Informed — Verdict

The panel finds this conditionally strong — credible as a roadmap once synthetic demo telemetry is replaced by supervisor-backed or Cityworks-sourced status data.

Email-only alerts skew toward people with stable inboxes; SMS, voice, and in-language channels are needed before this can claim equitable reach.

If a named DPW champion steps forward and the public layer uses real Socrata zones with honest timestamps, this is a viable pilot within months.

Road Maintenance Fleet Tracker ★ Pivot Needed

Stay Informed wisely sidesteps the GPS question — this idea did not, and that design choice defined its verdict.

The prototype demonstrated rich vehicle-level dashboards, but ran entirely on synthetic telemetry because Richmond's DPW fleet GPS data is not publicly available.

DPW Supervisor "If this tool expects second-by-second GPS from every plow, it is fantasy until telematics contracts and IT pipes exist."

Fleet Tracker ★ — Verdict

The panel recommends a significant pivot: rename this a "DPW Service Status Dashboard" and adopt the supervisor-toggle model that avoids the Fleet GPS wall entirely.

Under the GPS-first interpretation, this idea hits a fatal flaw — the data simply does not exist in any accessible form.

Under the service-delivery interpretation it becomes viable, but overlaps substantially with Snow Tier Compass and Stay Informed, which scored higher and arrived at that insight first.

What Should Ship

Snow Tier Compass is ready now — address-level snow priority lookup using proven GeoHub patterns, no GPS required.

What's On My Street and StreetLights should follow once public CIP API access is validated — both solve the "what's happening on my block?" question.

GRTC Construction Conflict Detector deserves a 90-day pilot — the nightly batch join can flag transit stop conflicts using entirely public data.

What Needs Work

Road Maintenance Fleet Tracker must pivot away from GPS dependence or be absorbed by ideas that already made that design choice.

ADA Detour Publisher, Storefront Brief, and RideShift RVA all need agency partnerships and validated data paths before they can leave prototype stage.

Stay Informed needs real operational data and multi-channel alerts to replace its synthetic demo.

What This Pillar Tells Us

The strongest ideas succeed by working around Richmond's infrastructure walls, not by pretending they don't exist.

Accessibility is not an edge case — the ADA Detour Publisher and GRTC Conflict Detector revealed that construction harms disabled residents in ways most civic tech completely ignores.

Richmond publishes more infrastructure data than residents realize — the gap is not data, but a usable front door that meets people where they are.
