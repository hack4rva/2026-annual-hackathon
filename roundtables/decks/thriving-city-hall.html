Making City services findable and procurement manageable.

RVA Hacks 2026 — Post-Event Roundtable Evaluation

The Problem

When Richmond residents need help from the City — paying a bill, reporting a pothole, finding the right office — they face a maze of departments, phone trees, and outdated websites.

On the procurement side, City staff hunt through PDFs to track contracts worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

Two Challenges

Resident Service Navigation: Help residents find the right City service or department quickly, so requests get routed correctly the first time.

Procurement Risk & Opportunity Review: Help City staff identify valid, compliant contracts across City, state, and federal sources without manual PDF hunting.

The Roundtable Model

We didn't just score these ideas.

We put each one through a simulated roundtable with seven distinct perspectives — from the Mayor worrying about political risk to the Citizen asking who gets left out.

The Seven Voices

The Mayor asks what's politically viable. The Innovator pushes for bolder framing. The Architect checks the tech stack. The Frontliner says whether staff will actually use it.

The Citizen asks who benefits and who's excluded. The Activist scans for prior art. The Funder asks who pays after the hackathon ends.

The Process

Each idea went through four phases: independent assessment from each persona, convergence debate, direct challenges between perspectives, and a final pass-or-fail validation.

Every claim was tested against real evidence — published research, peer city precedents, and Richmond's actual technology stack.

What Kills an Idea

We applied five fatal flaw tests to every idea.

If the data doesn't exist, if a vendor wall blocks the MVP, if no department would own it, if it could harm residents, or if it would embarrass the City — the idea doesn't ship.

The Headlines

This pillar produced 15 ideas — the most of any pillar — including four Tier S ideas ready to ship immediately.

The Socrata Contracts Pipeline, a cluster of procurement tools tapping the same public dataset, dominated the top rankings with three of the top eight ideas across all 73 evaluated.

The Socrata Contracts Pipeline

Five procurement ideas share a single foundation: Richmond's public contracts dataset on Socrata, with zero missing expiry dates and monthly refresh.

The top three — Expiry Dashboard, Alerts, and Drift Scanner — rank #1, #3, and #8 of all 73 ideas for implementation readiness.

Together they form a pipeline: see what expires, get notified, and catch payment anomalies.

The 311 / AvePoint Wall

Richmond's 311 system runs on AvePoint with no public API for reading or writing service requests.

Every resident-facing service navigation idea was forced to work around this wall, limiting them to link directories and referral tools rather than true intake integration.

The Procurement Cluster

Seven ideas, one dataset, and a clear winner.

Seven ideas tackled how City staff track, review, and manage contracts worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

The strongest were radically simple — reading public data that already exists.

Contract Expiry Dashboard CONDITIONS

A filterable timeline of every City contract approaching expiry, built on public Socrata data for zero dollars on GitHub Pages with no City IT involvement.

Ranked #1 of 73 ideas for implementation readiness. TIER S

Innovator "A countdown without a default next action is just informed anxiety. A countdown with a default next action is a system."

Contract Expiry Dashboard — Verdict

Ship with conditions: a 90-day handoff plan with a named City steward, staleness signals in the product, and Phase 1 limited to read-only transparency.

Passed all five fatal flaw tests. The risk is organizational abandonment, not technical failure.

If Procurement adopts it within 90 days, this becomes civic infrastructure for contract oversight.

Expiry Alerts CONDITIONS

The behavioral complement to the dashboard — weekly email digests warning procurement staff which contracts approach expiry. Same Socrata foundation, push instead of pull.

Ranked #3 of 73 ideas. TIER S

Funder "The funder's job isn't to fund the code — it's to fund the 90-day window between 'it exists' and 'someone owns it.'"

Expiry Alerts — Verdict

Ship with conditions. The fatal flaw is organizational: without an authorized email list and Procurement buy-in, the product stays a demo that sends to personal Gmail.

Procurement must name a champion and test delivery against City spam filters before any wide rollout.

If adopted, push beats pull — alerts create urgency that a dashboard alone never will.

Contract-Payment Drift Scanner CONDITIONS

Joins the contracts dataset with Richmond's Payment Register to flag overruns, post-term payments, and spending anomalies — an audit intelligence layer built entirely on public data.

Ranked #8 of 73 ideas. TIER S

Mayor "Low integration cost, high oversight signal."

Drift Scanner — Verdict

Ship with conditions. Flags must be framed as triage, not findings — a "possible anomaly" is not a "proven violation."

Gate zero: validate the join key between the two datasets and document the match rate before going live.

If the join works cleanly, this becomes the fiscal watchdog layer that contract transparency has been missing.

The Procurement Cluster — Below the Line

While the top three procurement ideas scored high on simplicity and public data, the remaining four attempted more ambitious approaches — AI extraction, federated search, vendor portals.

Each ran into the same wall: OnBase and RAPIDS have no public API for the contract PDFs where the real value lives.

RVA Contract Lens ★ RE-SCOPE

Pitched as a federated AI engine delivering eight-second procurement verdicts across city, state, and federal sources simultaneously.

The roundtable found the full architecture blocked by vendor walls, API terms of service, and AI governance gaps.

Frontliner "An eight-second verdict without performance memos, budget, and legal review would be ignored or dangerous."

RVA Contract Lens ★ — Verdict

Re-scope required. The full AI verdict engine is not viable without City systems integration and legal sign-off.

A narrower Socrata search with deep links to cooperative portals could ship, but overlaps with the Expiry Dashboard without adding a distinct lane.

The most distinctive long-term direction is OCDS publishing, following Portland's lead.

CivicPulse AI ★ CONDITIONS

A dual-pane tool using AI to extract fields from contract PDFs and display them alongside the source document for human verification.

Aligned with emerging norms for governed procurement AI, but blocked by the OnBase wall for any citywide corpus.

City Attorney "Wrong expiry or dollar figure from an LLM is not a UX bug — it is potential fiscal or legal exposure."

CivicPulse AI ★ — Verdict

Promising demo with a conditional path to pilot. Must choose one wedge — cooperative search or governed extraction — and defer claims of parity with the official contract repository.

Production blocked by OnBase access and AR 2.13 governance review, neither of which is a weekend solve.

If paired with a DIT charter and Procurement co-design, the extraction layer could save real staff hours.

Mira ★ PILOT

An Azure-based AI assistant that reads contract PDFs, extracts structured fields, and offers per-contract chat — built on Azure Document Intelligence with a full RAG stack.

The chat feature is where hallucination risk lives, and no accuracy metrics exist for Richmond documents.

Auditor "Until you publish precision and recall on Richmond PDFs, the product is a demo, not an evidence-based procurement system."

Mira ★ — Verdict

Pilot with scope cuts. Lead with extraction and a human-confirmed checklist; disable the generative chat until field-level accuracy is measured on real City documents.

Dual failure mode: an authority gap (no governed OnBase path) and a generative trust gap (no measured accuracy).

If scoped to a checklist accelerator with source citations, this fits the IDP pattern proven in peer cities.

Vendor Contract Mgmt ★ RE-SCOPE

A two-sided Next.js portal where City staff and vendors each log in to view contracts, with AI-generated summaries — creating what the roundtable called "a second narrative of contract truth."

Lowest-ranked procurement idea in the pillar. TIER B

Frontliner "I need one system of record, not a fourth place."

Vendor Contract Mgmt ★ — Verdict

Re-scope to a thin Socrata explorer with no uncited AI. The dual-portal vendor login has no institutional mandate, no business owner, and no path to becoming the system of record.

Finance and Audit will not treat a Firebase-authenticated shadow repository as authoritative.

The team's energy would be better directed toward the Socrata pipeline tools that already have momentum.

The Service Navigation Cluster

Five ideas, one wall, and a clear lesson.

Five ideas asked the same question: how do you help a Richmond resident find the right City service on the first try?

All five collided with the 311/AvePoint wall — and their answers reveal very different strategies for working around it.

Service Wizard CONDITIONS

The highest-scoring idea in this pillar. A deterministic, no-AI decision tree that guides residents to the right form, phone number, or office through plain-language questions — modeled on GOV.UK Smart Answers.

Ranked #7 of 73 ideas. TIER S

Civic Innovator "The most boring pattern may be the most trustworthy."

Service Wizard — Verdict

Ship with governance conditions. The fatal flaw is taxonomy drift: without a named owner refreshing the decision tree when departments reorganize, the wizard becomes confidently wrong routing.

No 311 API required, no AI governance risk, and a strong equity story through human-translatable Spanish.

If adopted by 311 or digital services, this is the safest path to "route right the first time."

Hey804 ★ REFINE

An AI-powered widget for the rva.gov homepage, using Claude to classify resident questions and return verified links — never generating answers, only routing to official sources.

The team's key design choice: refuse to answer when confidence is low; escalate to phone or 311 instead.

Innovator "Value equals what it refuses to synthesize, not eloquence."

Hey804 ★ — Verdict

Refine before shipping. Needs a named City owner under AR 2.13, a curated knowledge base with freshness discipline, and a path to 311 voice for residents who can't use the web.

No single fatal flaw, but conditional on ownership, curation, and inclusion.

The "classify, don't generate" architecture is the right instinct in a post-NYC-MyCity world.

Council Routing Memo PILOT

A lightweight form that helps Council district office staff generate a structured routing memo when residents call — standardizing the handoff from "switchboard" to the right department.

Ranked #24 of 73 ideas. TIER A

Frontliner "Resident on the line — I need the right department in under two minutes."

Council Routing Memo — Verdict

Pilot first with one Council office. Validate the taxonomy against 20 real cases before expanding — if the map misroutes high-stakes issues, the memo becomes confidently wrong infrastructure.

Pattern A ships without the AvePoint wall; ticket integration stays on the roadmap until DIT opens a gateway.

Low cost, low risk, and it directly solves a pain every district office experiences daily.

Text-311 ★ REFINE

An SMS channel for 311 requests, targeting residents without smartphones or broadband — the strongest equity argument of any service navigation idea. TIER C

The demo claimed real-time integration with Dynamics 365, but the tech stack says there is no public API.

Citizen "Highest fit for any-phone, no-broadband residents — but intake-only feels like a black hole."

Text-311 ★ — Verdict

Refine. The demo's integration claims are false against the tech stack, and that gap must be corrected before any credible pilot.

An honest version exists: Twilio with structured menus and a manual handoff to 311 operations, paired with City endorsement.

The equity case is real — this is the only idea that reaches residents with just a basic phone — but it cannot ship on false premises.

RVA Help ★ REFINE

An AI-assisted tool that helps residents describe their issue clearly, suggests the top three matching service categories, and links to the correct submission channel — a translator between resident language and City taxonomy.

Activist "Misclassification can route homelessness-related inquiries toward enforcement workflows."

RVA Help ★ — Verdict

Refine to a wall-aware referral tool. Ship top-three suggestions with human escalation for sensitive topics — not a shadow CRM that claims to manage the ticket lifecycle.

The full ticket system is blocked by the 311 wall and AR 2.13 governance, but the referral layer is viable.

Strongest when paired with the Service Wizard as complementary tools: deterministic routing plus AI-assisted intake.

The Transparency Cluster

Three ideas, three different views of City Hall.

Three ideas stepped back from specific workflows to ask a broader question: can the City make itself legible to the people it serves?

Each answers with a different lens — budget, strategy, or operations.

City Budget Dashboard ★ CONDITIONS

A Tableau Public dashboard built on Socrata budget data, with drill-down to agency line items and an LLM feature mapping spending to MAP pillar priorities.

Strong execution, but addresses fiscal transparency rather than the pillar's assigned problem statements.

Budget Analyst "The fatal flaw is not Tableau — it is absence of a committed owner and pipeline for authoritative, current numbers."

Budget Dashboard ★ — Verdict

Ship as a civic layer if Budget & Strategic Planning formalizes the refresh pipeline and owns the truth claim.

Without that, the dashboard drifts from official figures and becomes narrative decoration — numbers that look authoritative but aren't current enough to trust.

The LLM pillar-mapping feature triggers AR 2.13 expectations and needs DIT review.

MAP Tracker ★ REFINE

A progress dashboard for the Mayor's Action Plan, tracking KPIs across all seven pillars — and the only idea where department heads actually participated during the hackathon.

Ranked #23 of 73 ideas. TIER A

Innovator "Not a website but a governance OS — the meeting cadence matters more than the dashboard."

MAP Tracker ★ — Verdict

Refine to Go. Ship under the Mayor's Office with a mandated update cadence and a path to AchieveIt integration for automation.

Without executive discipline, quarterly data entry atrophies and the site becomes stale. Software is cheap; governance is not.

Does not address service navigation or procurement — but fits the MAP transparency mission directly.

City Activity Transparency CONDITIONS

A Boston CityScore-style dashboard aggregating KPIs across City operations into one public view with data freshness indicators — the most ambitious transparency play in the pillar.

Also the most exposed to Richmond's data gaps.

Citizen "If half the tiles say 'data unavailable,' the page feels like an excuse."

Activity Transparency — Verdict

Ship with conditions if scoped to Pattern A: curated tiles from Socrata, Legistar, and public GeoHub only, with a gap register pointing at the walls it cannot cross.

The full real-time common operating picture is blocked by 311, fleet GPS, and EnerGov. Dashboards without meeting cadences become wallpaper.

If paired with a governance charter and named owner, this could become Richmond's version of CitiStat.

What Should Ship

Contract Expiry Dashboard — #1 of 73. Public contract timeline, zero cost, zero IT dependency.

Service Wizard — No-AI routing that's trustworthy because it's boring.

Expiry Alerts — Push beats pull for procurement attention.

Drift Scanner — Audit intelligence from joining two public datasets.

What Shouldn't — and Why

RVA Contract Lens — Full AI federation blocked by vendor walls, API terms, and governance gaps.

Vendor Contract Mgmt — Shadow system without institutional mandate or business owner.

Text-311 (as demoed) — Claims real-time 311 integration that doesn't exist. An honest pilot version is viable.

What This Pillar Tells Us

Procurement ideas can ship because the data is public and clean. Service navigation ideas are walled off from the system residents actually use.

The Socrata Contracts Pipeline proves that open data plus commodity hosting can produce Tier S civic tools at zero cost — if someone commits to owning them past demo day.

The 311/AvePoint wall is not a hackathon problem to solve — it's a procurement and partnership conversation the City needs to have.
