Thriving & Inclusive Communities

Building trust and reducing friction for immigrant, refugee, and reentry communities

RVA Hacks 2026 — Post-Event Roundtable Evaluation

The Trust Paradox

Richmond's immigrant, refugee, and reentry communities face a cruel paradox — the people who need services most are the ones least able to navigate the system.

They're asked to create accounts, share identifying information, and repeat their story to every agency they visit. Trust is the scarcest resource.

Two Problem Statements

Immigrant Service Discovery — Help residents find and connect to trusted community services without creating accounts or sharing identifying information.

Cross-Agency Service Navigation — Help residents navigate housing, workforce, and reentry services without repeating their story across every agency.

How We Evaluated

We didn't just score these ideas. We put each one through a simulated roundtable with seven distinct perspectives — from the Mayor worrying about political risk to the Citizen asking who gets left out.

The Seven Voices

Seven personas tested every idea: the Mayor on political feasibility, the Innovator on creative reframing, the Frontliner on day-to-day operational reality, the Citizen on who benefits and who's excluded, the Architect on technical viability, the Funder on sustainability, and the Activist on ecosystem fit.

Four Phases of Review

Each idea went through independent assessment from all seven perspectives, convergence debate to find common ground, direct challenges between personas with conflicting views, and a final pass-or-fail validation.

What Kills an Idea

We applied five fatal flaw tests. If the required data doesn't exist, if a vendor wall blocks the MVP, if no one would own it post-hackathon, if it could harm the residents it claims to serve, or if it would embarrass the city — the idea doesn't ship.

What Emerged

Of eight ideas evaluated, one is ready to ship, four need targeted pilots with named partners, and three require significant refinement before they can serve residents.

Trust dominated every conversation. The strongest ideas put power in residents' hands rather than asking them to trust another system.

The Wall That Became a Feature

Help1RVA and findhelp have no free public API — blocking any idea that depends on a centralized service directory.

But the highest-scoring ideas turned that wall into a design principle: paper artifacts, WhatsApp channels, and curated local lists that work around closed systems rather than through them.

Cross-Agency Handoff Memo GO

Instead of connecting databases, this idea gives clients a printed summary they carry from one agency to the next — their story, once, on their terms. It costs nothing to host, hits no API walls, and ranked #6 of 73 ideas for implementation readiness.

The Innovator "The problem is not missing software but missing portable legibility at handoffs."

Tier S — 22/25 survivability

Handoff Memo — The Verdict

The panel says GO — pilot with paired send-and-receive agencies, measuring whether intake staff reference the memo in the first five minutes.

It passed all five fatal flaw tests. The risk is low, the experiment is nearly free, and no City IT integration is required for the core product.

If one intake point accepts the memo, this becomes a model other cities will study.

WhatsApp Service Finder ★ Pilot

While the Handoff Memo solves the arrival moment, discovery begins earlier — on the channel newcomers already use. This idea puts a verified service-finder on WhatsApp with multilingual menus, no logins, and anti-scam education.

The Newcomer "If the number comes from a mosque or clinic flyer, I will try it. If it is a random forward, I will assume scam."

Tier A/B — commodity stack; partnership-dependent

WhatsApp Finder — The Verdict

The panel recommends a targeted pilot with a named CBO coalition, not a city-wide launch. The fatal flaw is not the technology — it is unowned trust and an unsustainable human escalation loop.

If community organizations co-sign the number and fund response capacity, this becomes the most accessible front door in the pillar.

Reentry 72-Hour Navigator ★ Pilot

The first 72 hours after release from incarceration are the highest-risk window for homelessness, overdose, and re-arrest. This idea provides a sequenced checklist — ID, shelter, transit, first appointment — designed to survive a parking lot and a bus ride.

The Citizen "Success looks like leaving the gate with one credible next step completed or scheduled, not a 12-item fantasy list."

Tier B — tech is easy; partnerships are hard

Reentry Navigator ★ — The Verdict

The panel recommends piloting under nonprofit leadership with an anchor organization committed to distribution at release. The fatal flaw is operational false certainty — a polished plan that assumes shelter beds and transit passes exist when they may not.

This idea scored highest for Moonshot potential across all seven pillars. If paired with real capacity commitments, it could set a national standard for reentry navigation.

Core Eligibility Pack Builder ★ Pilot

Residents applying for SNAP, Medicaid, and housing repeat the same information at every desk. This tool builds a single eligibility packet entirely in the browser — no data leaves the device — and prints it for every appointment.

The CBO Caseworker "Assisted flows reach 95.6% submission in seven days versus 60.6% unassisted. The caseworker is the realistic completion engine."

Tier A — static hosting; zero API dependency

Eligibility Pack — The Verdict

The panel recommends piloting with agencies first. The gating question is not whether the PDF can be generated, but whether intake workers will accept it as legitimate input.

If agencies treat the packet as a head start rather than noise, this becomes a low-cost, high-dignity tool for multi-program applicants — and it needs zero City IT integration to work.

Neighborhood Anchor Map Pilot

Instead of building another directory, this idea maps the places immigrants already trust — libraries, mosques, clinics — and layers GRTC transit routes to show what's reachable by bus. It converts trust into geography using entirely public data.

The Innovator "The non-obvious pitch is not prettier pins — it is converting trust into reachable geography."

Tier S — 21/25, ranked #14 of 73

Anchor Map — The Verdict

The panel says pilot, but privacy and partners must come first. The fatal flaw is that mapped immigrant-serving locations without community governance could become a targeting surface — repeating the failure that shut down FindHello nationally in 2026.

Ship 20 anchors with library and CBO co-design and a published privacy tier. Do not ship a comprehensive sensitive-service layer in v1.

RVA Helps ★ Refine

The most ambitious vision in the pillar: a true resource data utility with Open Referral standards, freshness badges, and a metric that measures whether people actually got help — not just whether they clicked a link.

The Funder "Connection rates fell from 65% to 38% when funding waned in a cited study. Maintenance funding is the product."

Tier B — 13/25 survivability

RVA Helps ★ — The Verdict

The panel recommends refining, not shipping. Two fatal flaws remain: no named institutional steward and no funding line for ongoing verification.

Richmond needs a resource data utility, not another stale directory. But without a signed owner and a data maintenance contract, this becomes exactly the thing it criticizes.

804Me ★ Refine

A privacy-first resource hub with no accounts, multilingual access, and physical QR codes at transit stops and clinics. The design intent — navigator profiles, sanctuary-grade privacy, non-digital on-ramps — is among the best-aligned to the immigrant discovery problem.

The Frontliner "I will only share this with clients if Sacred Heart, IRC, or ReEstablish Richmond visibly co-sign it."

804Me ★ — The Verdict

The panel recommends against shipping as documented. The fatal flaw is an unverified directory with no accountable steward — precisely the wrong failure mode for a population whose primary barrier is trust, not search UX.

With published provenance, a named maintaining organization, and CBO co-branding, the same design becomes a high-leverage complement to 211 and Help1RVA.

Circle Trust ★ Refine

Circle Trust asks a different question: what if the platform isn't for residents, but for the 300+ organizers who coordinate behind the scenes? Built by Pollinators for Change, it offers invitation-only coordination circles with trust and accountability by design.

The Judge "Score it against the problem statement and you see a mismatch. Score it as community coordination infrastructure and you see traction."

Tier B — 11/25 survivability

Circle Trust ★ — The Verdict

The panel recommends nurturing this idea but relabeling its scope. As submitted under Cross-Agency Navigation, no resident-facing flow or agency handoff was demonstrated — the stated problem goes unaddressed.

As community governance infrastructure, the trust model and network scale have real value. The path forward requires connecting pollinators to the residents they serve.

What Should Move Forward

Cross-Agency Handoff Memo — a paper passport that costs nothing and sidesteps every API wall in Richmond's stack.

WhatsApp Service Finder — meets newcomers on the channel they already use, with anti-scam education built in.

Reentry 72-Hour Navigator — addresses the highest-risk window with sequenced, printable logistics designed for the parking lot.

What Needs Work — And Why

Neighborhood Anchor Map, Core Eligibility Pack Builder, and RVA Helps each have strong foundations but need agency partnerships, privacy governance, or institutional ownership before they can responsibly serve residents.

804Me and Circle Trust need to resolve fundamental questions — data provenance and problem-statement fit, respectively — before the strong design work underneath can deliver on its promise.

What This Pillar Tells Us

Trust is not a feature — it is the entire product. Every winning idea in this pillar puts control in residents' hands rather than asking them to trust another system.

The closed Help1RVA API forced creativity: the best ideas work around infrastructure walls with paper, WhatsApp, and curated local knowledge. The technology that ships is the technology that doesn't depend on anyone's permission.
